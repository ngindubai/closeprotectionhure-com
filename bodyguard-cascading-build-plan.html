1. Business Overview

The Opportunity

When a high-net-worth individual, executive, journalist or NGO worker needs personal security in a foreign country, they search online. There is no global platform for hiring vetted bodyguards or executive protection teams across borders. The information asymmetry is extreme: how do you know which security firm in Lagos or Bogota is legitimate?

Scored 7.4/10 in the cross-border opportunity report. Ranked #8 overall. Lower page count than pet transport or bus hire, but the highest value per lead of all three builds ($1,000-$10,000+ per day).

Market Size

Global executive protection market: $15-20B annually (Allied Market Research). Growing with geopolitical instability, corporate travel to emerging markets, and increased risk awareness post-COVID.

Deal Value

$1,000-$10,000+ per day. An executive protection detail in Lagos: $2,500/day. A 5-day trip with driver and bodyguard in Bogota: $8,000. Corporate team protection in Kabul: $15,000/week. The highest per-engagement value of all three builds.

Competition

No global SEO player at city level. Major security firms (G4S, Securitas, GardaWorld) focus on corporate contracts, not individual SEO. Smaller firms are local. Nobody owns "bodyguard hire [city]" at global scale.

2.6 Client Segments

Segment 1: Corporate

Multinational companies sending executives to high-risk locations. Budget: $2K-$15K per trip. Repeat bookings. Decision maker: corporate security director or travel risk manager. Long sales cycle but high lifetime value.

Segment 2: HNWI / Celebrity

High-net-worth individuals travelling for leisure, property viewings, or events. Budget: $1K-$5K per day. Often want discreet, unarmed protection in luxury settings. Decision maker: personal assistant or family office.

Segment 3: Media / Journalism

Journalists, documentary crews, news agencies working in conflict or high-risk zones. Budget: $2K-$10K/day (often funded by broadcaster). Need hostile environment trained operators. Decision maker: news desk security advisor.

Segment 4: NGO / Aid

NGO workers, aid organisations operating in conflict zones. Budget: varies. Need security drivers, compound security, movement planning. Often require operators with humanitarian experience.

Department C: Content Creation

✍️
The Wordsmith
Security Content Writer (Authority Voice)

Writes with authority and discretion. The tone is professional, knowledgeable, and measured. Not fearmongering ("Lagos is DANGEROUS!") but factual and practical ("Lagos presents specific security challenges for business travellers, particularly in certain areas"). The writing should sound like it comes from an experienced security professional, not a marketing agency.

Tone Rules

  • Never fearmongering. State facts, not scare stories.
  • Never guarantee safety. "We reduce risk" not "We keep you safe".
  • Professional, not military. Accessible to civilian clients.
  • Discreet. No operational details that could compromise client security.
  • Factual about risk. "This city has a crime index of X" not "This city is terrifying".
  • Confident but honest. "Our operators are experienced in this environment" not "Nothing can go wrong".
❓
The Interrogator
FAQ & Risk Question Generator

Generates city-specific FAQs: "Can I hire an armed bodyguard in Colombia?", "How much does a security driver cost in Lagos?", "Do I need executive protection in Dubai?", "Is it safe to visit the Ikoyi neighbourhood?"

Backlinks from: ASIS International, security industry publications (Security Management magazine), travel risk management platforms, corporate travel associations, insurance companies. Links FROM government advisory pages TO your content would be the ultimate authority signal.

Builds with security in mind. The site itself must be secure (HTTPS, no data leaks, encrypted contact forms). Clients submitting enquiries may be high-profile. The enquiry system must not expose client details. Clean, professional, dark design. Think: government contractor website, not e-commerce.

Manages two databases: (1) City risk profiles with crime data, travel advisories, regulation lookups. (2) Vetted operator database: security firms per city with licensing, insurance, and vetting status.

Tracks lead quality by city. In this business, one high-value corporate client is worth 100 casual enquiries. The Analyst identifies which cities generate the highest-value leads and prioritises content investment accordingly.

5. Google Compliance & Legal Standards

YMYL Classification: Highest Standards Required

Security content is classified as YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) by Google. Content about personal safety is held to the highest E-E-A-T standards. This means:

  1. Author expertise signals: Pages should reference real security professionals. Author bios with verifiable credentials.
  2. Accuracy: Every claim about firearms laws, licensing, risk levels must be verified against official sources.
  3. No safety guarantees: Never state or imply that hiring security eliminates risk. Language: "reduces risk" / "mitigates exposure" / "provides experienced support".
  4. Liability language: All pages should include appropriate disclaimers. No promise of outcomes.
  5. Citations: Link to FCDO advisories, State Dept pages, OSAC reports for risk data.

17. SEO & LLM Optimization Plan (Parallel Workstream)

Why This Runs in Parallel

The original build plan treats SEO as a stage within each phase (task 1.12, 5.2-5.6). That is not enough. SEO and LLM optimization must run continuously from the moment Phase 1 pages go live. While Phase 2 content is being built, Phase 1 pages should already be indexed, ranking, and being refined. Every week a live page sits without monitoring is a week of lost ranking signal.

LLM ranking (Generative Engine Optimization / GEO) is entirely absent from the original plan. As of 2026, a significant and growing share of security research queries are answered by ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews, and Gemini. If the site is not optimised for LLM citation, it misses an entire acquisition channel.

Workers responsible: The Optimiser (on-page SEO + LLM structure), The Scout (monitoring + intelligence), The Connector (authority signals + backlinks).

17.1 LLM Ranking Strategy (Generative Engine Optimization)

What LLMs Need to Cite Your Content

LLMs (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Claude) cite sources when they find content that is: (1) authoritative and well-sourced, (2) structured clearly enough to extract clean answers, (3) unique data not available elsewhere, and (4) consistently entity-rich. Security content is ideal for LLM citation because users ask specific questions ("Can I hire an armed bodyguard in Nigeria?") and expect factual, sourced answers.

LLM Optimization TechniqueImplementationWorkerApply From
Definitive Answer Format Every FAQ uses "Question > Direct 1-sentence answer > Supporting detail with named source" pattern. LLMs extract the first sentence as the citation. No vague lead-ins. Interrogator + Optimiser Phase 1 (retroactive)
Entity-Rich Content Every city page explicitly names: city, country, service type, local licensing body, relevant government advisory source, cost context. Entities must appear in structured, parseable patterns, not buried in prose. Wordsmith + Optimiser Phase 1 (retroactive)
Unique Data Tables Create comparison tables LLMs cannot find elsewhere: risk rating vs service availability per city, licensing requirements comparison across countries, typical cost ranges by city tier. Tables are highly parseable by LLMs. Geographer + Optimiser Phase 2
Source Chain Authority Cite FCDO, US State Dept, OSAC, SIA by name with dates. LLMs already trust these sources. Content that references them inherits citation authority. Every factual claim gets a named, dated source. Wordsmith + Auditor Phase 1 (already active)
Conversational Query Optimization Optimise for natural language questions: "Where can I hire a bodyguard in Lagos?", "Do I need security in Bogota?", "Can bodyguards carry guns in Nigeria?". These are how people prompt LLMs. H2s and FAQ questions should mirror these patterns. Interrogator + Scout Phase 2
Topical Authority Depth For each city, cover every sub-angle: regulations, risk profile, cost, what to expect, traveller tips, FAQ. LLMs prefer citing sites that cover a topic exhaustively over sites that mention it briefly. The 11-section city page structure already supports this. Wordsmith + Geographer Phase 1 (already active)
Brand Entity Building Consistent brand mentions across all pages with consistent entity description. Schema.org Organization markup site-wide. "About" page with verifiable credentials. Build a knowledge graph entity that LLMs associate with "global security services". Optimiser + Builder Phase 2
Structured Data for LLM Parsing Beyond Schema.org: use definition lists for regulations, consistent table formats for comparisons, bullet-point lists for requirements. LLMs parse structured HTML far better than long prose paragraphs. Optimiser + Builder Phase 1 (retroactive)
LLM Citation Monitoring Weekly: search target queries in ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews. Document: (1) who gets cited, (2) what content format gets cited, (3) whether our pages appear. Track over time. Scout Phase 2 (ongoing)
