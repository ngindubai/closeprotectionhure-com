Eindhoven Airport's IATA code, EIN, appears on far more budget-carrier boarding passes than executive itineraries, and that fact shapes how a secure transfer service in this city has to operate: the terminal itself is small, single-building, and built around high passenger throughput on short-haul routes rather than a dedicated business-traveller flow. A pre-arranged transfer resolves the gap. Drivers hold a chauffeurskaart from the Rijksdienst voor het Wegverkeer (RDW) for the commercial passenger function, with WPBR 1997 registration and Justis approval covering any protective element added to the journey, and position themselves inside the terminal with a name board so the principal never needs to assess the public forecourt, where FCDO Netherlands (2024) notes petty theft as the general transport-hub risk.

The 7km route into the city via the N2 and A2 is short and low-risk by any measure, and the same applies to the longer run north to Amsterdam Schiphol for principals connecting internationally, where the choice between a 75-minute direct train and a similar-length road transfer usually comes down to whether the principal wants to stay in one vehicle for the whole journey. Eindhoven's own arrival points carry more relevance to route planning than the roads themselves: Eindhoven Centraal is a documented pickpocketing location, so rail arrivals are met on the platform rather than the concourse, and drop-offs at Strijp-S, the redeveloped Philips site now home to offices and event venues, or at the ASML and NXP-adjacent Veldhoven campuses each follow a different approach road and, in the case of the technology campuses, a separate visitor-registration step arranged in advance with the principal's host.
