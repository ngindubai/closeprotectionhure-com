Robert Gabriel Mugabe International Airport (HRE) sits approximately 12 kilometres from Harare's central business district, with a transfer corridor that the FCDO Zimbabwe advisory (updated 2026) places in an area of documented carjacking risk, compounded by Zimbabwe's chronic load-shedding programme that regularly removes street lighting from the airport road. Managing this specific combination of threats requires generator-equipped vehicles, inside-terminal collection, and route planning that accounts for both criminal and infrastructure variables.

## The Harare arrival window

The arrivals hall at HRE is a single-channel facility where passengers exiting customs emerge directly into an area occupied by informal taxi operators. For business principals who do not have a pre-confirmed collection, this creates pressure to accept an unvetted vehicle, which is the primary point of exposure at this airport. The kerb area is uncontrolled and the absence of a dedicated, credentialled taxi zone makes distinguishing licensed from unlicensed operators difficult under normal arrival conditions.

The 12-kilometre road transfer to Borrowdale or Highlands passes through areas of Harare where carjacking has been recorded, with the airport road itself flagged in the FCDO Zimbabwe advisory. Night transfers carry an elevated risk because load-shedding removes street lighting across extended stretches of the corridor, reducing visibility for both driver and potential threat actors and making approaches to stationary vehicles harder to detect early.

Zimbabwe's security environment also includes the possibility of police checkpoint delays, which can create static vehicle exposure on the transfer route. Drivers in our Harare network are briefed on checkpoint compliance procedures and on the distinction between official ZRP checkpoints and the less formal arrangements that can occur on certain routes.
