Gdansk Lech Walesa Airport sits about 12km northwest of the city, and the transfer itself is usually the easy part. What takes more planning is timing: Dlugi Targ and the Motlawa riverfront, the city's principal tourist zone, see their heaviest crowds during cruise-ship arrivals, and drivers build that seasonal pattern into drop-off timing rather than treating every old-town run the same.

Drivers track the inbound flight and are positioned in the terminal before the principal clears customs, and hold valid Polish licences, with the operator carrying a Ministry of Internal Affairs and Administration concession under the Act of 22 August 1997 where the transfer includes a protective dimension. For visits touching Gdansk's working port or shipyard facilities, access needs advance coordination given the independent site accreditation both require, and Nowy Port gets standard industrial-district caution after dark.

Gdansk's history adds one more planning layer few Polish cities carry: anniversary events tied to the 1980 Gdansk Agreement can bring elevated official security presence around the European Solidarity Centre each mid-August, and drivers confirm current arrangements with the host institution before any travel connected to those dates.
