Why does a European Parliament session week change how event security gets planned in Strasbourg? Because the city's institutional profile is not theoretical. Strasbourg hosts the Parliament's plenary sessions roughly monthly alongside standing Council of Europe and European Court of Human Rights business, and any protective work near these sites is coordinated with the institutions' own security perimeter rather than run as a stand-alone operation, with our advance work concentrating on approach routes and the handover points between institutional and public space.

The Christkindelsmarik adds a second, entirely different planning problem. One of France's oldest and largest Christmas markets, it draws around two million visitors annually across 300 or more chalets in the Grande Ile, and Strasbourg's documented 2018 attack history, a gunman killed five people and wounded eleven before being killed by police after a three-day manhunt, means market-period security gets treated as its own exercise rather than a seasonal variation of routine cover.

CNAPS licenses both firms and individual officers under the Code de la securite interieure, and France's Vigipirate Urgence Attentat level, its highest tier, applies nationally. Neuhof and Hautepierre, peripheral districts with documented elevated crime, are excluded from all route planning, while periodic demonstrations tied to national strike calendars can intersect with Parliament session weeks and affect the Grande Ile with limited notice.
