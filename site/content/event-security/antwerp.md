What does event security look like in a city whose defining venue is a diamond trading floor rather than an exhibition hall? Antwerp's Diamond District, adjacent to Antwerpen-Centraal, handles a very large share of the world's rough and polished diamond trade, and its trading buildings sit under a permanent, tightly controlled access regime run jointly by the Antwerp World Diamond Centre's security office and the Antwerp Local Police diamond department. Any trade event held inside works with that existing structure, with the district's documented theft history, including the 2003 Antwerp diamond heist, informing the level of coordination required.

A second, quite different calendar runs through the Port of Antwerp-Bruges, home to Europe's largest integrated chemical cluster. Corporate conferences and site briefings here need formal accreditation and defined routes agreed with site security well ahead of arrival. FCDO Belgium notes that organised crime groups linked to the port's cocaine trafficking role have carried out violent incidents in the city, a risk that overwhelmingly affects those in the trade rather than event attendees, but one that still shapes the wider security posture around any port-adjacent gathering.

Belgian private security runs under the 2017 law on private and particular security, overseen by the SPF Interieur. Antwerpen-Centraal and the surrounding De Keyserlei and Pelikaanstraat area see a concentration of pickpocketing tied to high footfall, and Belgium's OCAM/CUTA threat level has held at 3 out of 4 through early 2026, with reinforced patrols confirmed at locations including the Jewish quarter.
