Hungary's presence on this network is anchored by Budapest, a genuinely significant regional business centre rather than a secondary stop: the Budapest Stock Exchange, a deep pharmaceutical and financial services sector, and a growing technology footprint all draw executive travel here. The overall advisory picture for 2026 is low risk. FCDO calls for normal precautions and the US State Department rates Hungary Level 1.

Licensing runs through a single national authority, which simplifies verification considerably. Act CXXXIII of 2005 on Security Services puts oversight in the hands of the ORFK, the National Police Headquarters, and every operator, foreign or domestic, needs to be inside that framework before doing protective work in the country. Firearms sit under a separate Act on Firearms and Ammunition, and armed deployment stays rare: the standard is unarmed protection built around planning rather than a weapon.

What actually happens to corporate visitors in Budapest is mostly opportunistic, not violent. Budapest Metropolitan Police annual statistics point to tourist-targeted scams, unlicensed taxis and overcharging near Vaci Street and the Erzsebetvaros ruin bar district as the recurring pattern, and Hungarian Police data flags the Keleti station metro corridor for pickpocketing specifically. Political demonstrations near Parliament are a real but manageable disruption, not a security threat in the conventional sense; a route alternative built into the day's plan handles it.
