Budapest carries essentially the whole of Hungary's business and diplomatic security profile, and the underlying picture is a low-crime capital where scams, not violence, are the actual visitor concern.

## Act CXXXIII of 2005: the licensing framework

Hungary's private security sector is regulated under Act CXXXIII of 2005 on Security Services, together with the Protective Services Act, with ORFK, the National Police Headquarters, as the licensing authority. Every legitimate operator holds an ORFK licence. Checking that status directly, rather than taking a firm's word for it, is the one piece of due diligence that actually matters before signing a contract.

## Firearms: legally possible, operationally rare

The Act on Firearms and Ammunition sets the framework for armed private security in Hungary, but authorisation for corporate close protection work is genuinely uncommon. What that means for a client is straightforward: expect unarmed protection as the default across Budapest, built instead on protest awareness, route alternatives and disciplined movement planning around known trigger points like Parliament Square.

## Where the real risk sits

Hungary sits at a US State Department Level 1 advisory, its lowest tier, and FCDO advises normal precautions. The gap between that low national rating and what visitors actually encounter is scams, not crime in the traditional sense. Budapest Metropolitan Police statistics repeatedly flag unlicensed taxis, deliberate overcharging in bars and clubs around Vaci Street, and shell-game operators working the tourist sites. Separately, Keleti station and the M1, M2 and M3 metro lines carry the city's highest documented pickpocketing activity, purely a transit-hub pattern rather than anything specific to Budapest as a destination. Political demonstrations, concentrated around Parliament Square and Heroes Square, are usually peaceful but worth building route alternatives around.
