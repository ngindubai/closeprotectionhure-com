Hungary's close protection market is, in practice, Budapest. It is the single city on this network for the country, but it carries real volume: government and diplomatic business, a growing corporate and tech presence, and one of Central Europe's busiest tourism sectors, all generating distinct security demand.

## ORFK licensing and the unarmed default

Private security in Hungary runs on Act CXXXIII of 2005 on Security Services, with ORFK, the National Police Headquarters, handling licensing and oversight. There isn't a separate close protection credential layered on top of the general security licence, which makes direct verification of an operator's current ORFK registration the meaningful check, rather than assuming licensing status from a company's general standing.

Firearms fall under separate legislation, and armed private security deployments are the exception rather than a routine option. The model across Budapest, including for high-profile corporate principals, is unarmed protection built around planning, liaison, and disciplined movement.

## Budapest's actual risk picture

The pattern here is consistent and well documented rather than mysterious: tourist-targeted scams. Unlicensed taxis overcharging at tourist pickup points, inflated bills in bars and clubs around Vaci Street and the Erzsebetvaros ruin-bar district, and shell-game operators working the main squares are the recurring issues flagged by Hungarian Police statistics. Layer on pickpocketing concentrated at Keleti station and the M1 to M3 metro lines, and you have most of what an unbriefed corporate visitor is actually exposed to. Political demonstrations around Parliament Square and Heroes Square happen periodically and are generally peaceful, though they can disrupt planned routes without much warning.

None of this pushes Budapest above a low overall rating. It is a manageable, well-understood risk profile, and the practical response is the same one that works in most European capitals: pre-arranged transport, a briefed principal, and a route plan with a fallback option.

## Foreign firms: work through an ORFK partner

International operators need to work with an ORFK-licensed Hungarian firm and give the required ORFK notification before a deployment begins. Setting that partnership up in advance of a principal's arrival, rather than after, is the difference between a smooth engagement and a scramble.
