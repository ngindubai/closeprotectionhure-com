Hungary's corporate security demand is a Budapest story almost in its entirety. The Budapest Stock Exchange, a concentrated pharmaceutical sector and a steady flow of investor delegations give the city a genuinely distinct client base from the tourist-driven demand seen in some of Central Europe's other capitals.

## Act CXXXIII of 2005 and the ORFK

Hungary licenses its private security sector through Act CXXXIII of 2005 on Security Services, administered by the National Police Headquarters, ORFK. Both companies and individual officers need current ORFK authorisation, and foreign operators face an additional step: ORFK notification before any deployment starts. Skipping that step is the most common compliance gap for international clients bringing their own security arrangements into Hungary rather than working through a local partner.

## Weapons remain a background option, not a default

General firearms regulation sits under Hungary's Act on Firearms and Ammunition, and while licensed armed private security does exist, it is not how most corporate close protection in Budapest is delivered. Unarmed teams built around route planning and liaison with local police cover the large majority of assignments, including for high-profile investor and executive visits.

## Parliament demonstrations versus the everyday risk

Budapest's most visible risk factor, periodic political demonstrations concentrated near Parliament and Kossuth Square, is almost entirely a route-planning problem: these events are typically announced ahead of time and rarely turn confrontational. What actually affects a visiting executive day to day is more mundane. Hungarian Police data identifies Keleti station and metro lines M1, M2 and M3 as pickpocketing hotspots, and tourist-targeted overcharging around Vaci Street and the Erzsebetvaros nightlife district is a documented, ongoing pattern that pre-arranged transport largely removes.
