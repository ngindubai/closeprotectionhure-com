Finland's close protection market is Helsinki, the capital and the single city on this network for the country. It is also one of the calmest security environments we cover: a low baseline crime rate, effective policing, and a security profile shaped more by geography and current geopolitics than by any conventional threat picture.

## The Private Security Services Act and Poliisihallitus

Private security in Finland runs on the Private Security Services Act (1085/2015), with the National Police Board, Poliisihallitus, issuing licences. The Finnish Security Association, SPEK, represents the licensed industry. There is no distinct close protection qualification layered above the general licence, so verifying an operator's current Poliisihallitus status is the direct, meaningful check for a client before engaging them.

Firearms sit under the separate Firearms Act (1/1998), and armed private security is simply not the model here. Corporate close protection in Helsinki runs unarmed as standard, not as a fallback.

## What actually matters in Helsinki: weather, not crime

Ask a Finnish security provider what shapes their planning and the honest answer is rarely "crime." Helsinki has a genuinely low crime rate by any international standard. What does matter is winter: icy roads, short daylight hours for much of the year, and the practical need to build contingency time into a principal's schedule around weather-related delays. That is a logistics question as much as a security one, and it is the kind of thing a generic "Finland is safe" statement misses entirely.

There is one more current factor worth naming: Finland's 2023 NATO accession has brought a noticeable increase in defence, diplomatic, and government-adjacent visits to Helsinki. That does not change the country's low baseline risk rating, but it has added a distinct category of protective demand alongside the corporate and tech-sector work that made up most of Helsinki's security market previously.

## Foreign operators: the National Police Board route

International firms need to partner with a Finnish, Poliisihallitus-licensed operator and give the required National Police Board notification before a deployment starts. Doing that groundwork ahead of a principal's arrival is the only sensible order of operations.
