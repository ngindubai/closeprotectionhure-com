The Czech Republic's business travel security demand runs through one city almost exclusively. Prague hosts the country's financial services, technology and manufacturing-supply-chain activity, and it is where the regulatory picture below plays out in practice.

## Act 69/2006: the licensing framework

Private security in the Czech Republic answers to Act 69/2006, with operators licensed jointly by the Czech Police and the Ministry of Interior. That dual oversight is worth knowing before hiring: a legitimate provider should be able to point to standing with both bodies, not just one. Foreign firms operate under a separate track, the Czech Trade Licensing Act, and short-term EU service provision is possible with the correct notification, though most sustained work goes through a Czech-licensed local partner instead.

## Weapons stay in the background

The Act on Firearms and Ammunition (156/2000) governs weapons generally, and while armed licensing for private security does exist through the Czech Police, it isn't the default choice for corporate work. Close protection in Prague is typically unarmed, and given the country's low baseline risk environment, that's rarely a limitation clients notice.

## A genuinely low-risk environment, with specific local exceptions

FCDO rates the Czech Republic as normal precautions and the US State Dept holds it at Level 1, both readings that put it among the calmer countries on this site. The exceptions are narrow and well documented: Old Town Square and the area around Charles Bridge see tourist-targeted pickpocketing, nightlife venues near Wenceslas Square have a known drink-spiking pattern, and unregulated taxis at tourist pickup points have a history of overcharging visitors who don't pre-arrange transport.
