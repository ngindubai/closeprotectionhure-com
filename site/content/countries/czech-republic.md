One city carries most of the country's business and diplomatic weight, and Prague's risk profile is almost entirely a function of tourist footfall rather than any deeper structural problem.

## Act 69/2006: the licensing framework

Private security in the Czech Republic runs on Act 69/2006, administered by the Czech Police alongside the Ministry of Interior. Operators need a current licence, checked and re-checked rather than assumed to still be valid from a prior engagement. The framework covers everything from static guarding through to close protection, and clients hiring for principal movement in Prague should confirm the specific licence class an operator holds.

## Firearms: available, but not the default

Armed private security is legally possible under the Act on Firearms and Ammunition (156/2000), with the Czech Police as the licensing authority. Grant is not automatic, and what does get authorised tends to concentrate on cash-in-transit runs and fixed-site guarding rather than personal protection. Close protection delivered for visiting executives in Prague is, in practice, unarmed almost across the board: careful routing, liaison with local police where needed, and disciplined movement planning do the job instead.

## Where the actual exposure sits

The FCDO advises normal precautions for the Czech Republic, and the US State Department holds it at Level 1, its lowest advisory tier. What that low national rating conceals is a genuinely concentrated pickpocketing problem: Prague City Police statistics single out Old Town Square, Charles Bridge and the metro network as the environments where opportunistic theft is most common, purely a function of tourist density rather than crime trend. Separately, drink-spiking incidents recorded in the Wenceslas Square nightlife district are worth planning around for evening events, and unregulated taxis operating from tourist areas have a documented history of overcharging that pre-arranged transfers simply remove from the equation.
