The Czech Republic's close protection market runs almost entirely through Prague. It is a small country by the standards of this network, one city rather than a spread of regional hubs, but Prague itself pulls a genuine volume of corporate, tech-sector, and tourism-linked security demand.

## Licensing: one act, two regulators

Private security in the Czech Republic operates under Act 69/2006, with the Czech Police and the Ministry of Interior sharing oversight. There is no separate close protection credential layered on top the way there is in some neighbouring countries. What this means for a client is straightforward: confirm the specific operator's current licence status rather than treating a company's general registration as sufficient proof that the named officers are covered.

Firearms sit under the separate Act on Firearms and Ammunition (156/2000). Armed private security licensing exists and is available through the Czech Police, but it is not the default model. Prague-based close protection, including for high-profile principals, is conducted unarmed in the overwhelming majority of cases.

## What actually goes wrong in Prague

Prague's security profile is genuinely low by European capital-city standards, and the FCDO's normal-precautions advisory reflects that. What does happen, reliably enough to plan around, is tourist-area pickpocketing concentrated at Old Town Square and Charles Bridge, drink-spiking incidents documented in the Wenceslas Square nightlife district, and unlicensed taxi operators overcharging tourists, an issue the US Embassy in Prague has flagged separately from the country's overall Level 1 State Department rating. None of this amounts to a dangerous city. It is the specific, well-documented friction of a heavily touristed capital, and pre-arranged professional transport removes most of it.

## Foreign firms: the partnership path

International security companies fall under the Czech Trade Licensing Act, and short-term EU service provision is possible with the right prior notification. For anything sustained, though, partnering with a Czech-licensed operator is genuinely the faster route, not just the legally cautious one.
