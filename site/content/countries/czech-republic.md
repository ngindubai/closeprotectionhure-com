The Czech Republic's coverage on this network currently runs through one city: Prague, which does the heavy lifting for the country's corporate travel and investment footprint. It is a low-risk environment by European standards, and the operational picture is narrow and well documented rather than diffuse.

Act 69/2006 on private security services is the governing statute, and it puts licensing authority jointly in the hands of the Czech Police and the Ministry of Interior. That dual oversight means a client checking an operator's credentials has two official channels to confirm against, not one. Firearms sit separately, under the Act on Firearms and Ammunition (156/2000), and while armed licensing is available through the Czech Police, it is not what most corporate close protection in Prague looks like day to day.

Prague's own risk profile is concentrated, not broad. Prague City Police statistics consistently name Old Town Square and Charles Bridge as the pickpocketing hotspots that matter operationally, and drink-spiking incidents have been recorded around the Wenceslas Square nightlife strip specifically, a detail the FCDO's 2026 travel advice calls out directly rather than folding into a generic warning. A third, less obvious issue: unregulated taxis working the tourist ranks have a documented overcharging history, flagged separately by the US Embassy in Prague. Pre-arranged transfers solve that problem outright.
