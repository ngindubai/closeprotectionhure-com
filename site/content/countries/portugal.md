Portugal's two covered cities split the market cleanly: Lisbon carries the volume, the financial and tech-sector demand, and the country's highest pickpocketing rates, while Porto runs a calmer, lower-crime profile built around its own and increasingly significant financial district.

## Lei 34/2013: one national licensing system

Portugal's private security industry answers to a single national statute, Lei n.º 34/2013 de 16 de Maio, supervised by the PSP, Policia de Seguranca Publica. Every operator needs a current PSP licence, and close protection specifically requires the PSP-recognised CPO qualification on top of the general licence. Unlike Italy's province-by-province issuance, this is one system covering both cities, which makes provider verification straightforward: one licence check, not a Prefettura-by-Prefettura patchwork.

## Unarmed is the practical norm

Lei n.º 5/2006 keeps firearms controls strict, and armed private security requires specific PSP authorisation tied to a demonstrated need rather than issued as routine practice. For close protection clients in Lisbon or Porto, that means the deployed team is planning-led and unarmed by default. Vetted transport, pre-surveyed routes and briefed drivers do most of the practical risk reduction.

## Lisbon's pickpocketing problem is real and specific

The single biggest practical risk difference between the two cities is opportunistic theft, not violent crime and not terrorism. PSP annual crime statistics place the Tram 28 corridor, Alfama and Praca do Comercio among the highest-concentration pickpocketing zones in Western Europe, and motorcycle-based bag snatching along the Tagus waterfront is a separately documented pattern. Porto's equivalent risk sits meaningfully lower; its more notable issue is weekend alcohol-related disorder around the Galerias de Paris and Clerigos nightlife strip after 23:00, a different problem requiring different mitigation.
