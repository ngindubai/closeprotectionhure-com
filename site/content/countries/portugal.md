Two cities, one national licensing regime. Lisbon and Porto sit at different points on the risk scale, driven almost entirely by tourist density rather than anything structural about Portugal itself.

## Lei n.º 34/2013: the licensing backbone

Portugal regulates private security through Lei n.º 34/2013 de 16 de Maio, the Private Security Activities Act, administered by PSP, the Policia de Seguranca Publica. Every operator needs a valid PSP licence. Close protection officers need something further: a recognised CPO qualification layered on top of the base guard licence, because personal protection work is treated as a distinct discipline from static or mobile guarding.

That distinction matters for clients. A guard licence alone does not qualify someone to run close protection. Checking which specific licence an operator holds, not just whether they hold one, is the actual due-diligence question.

## Firearms: authorised only against a documented threat

Lei n.º 5/2006, Portugal's Firearms and Ammunition Law, sets a genuinely high bar for armed private security. PSP grants authorisation case by case, against a specific and documented threat assessment, not as a routine add-on to a service contract. In practice this means close protection across both cities is delivered unarmed almost without exception. Route planning, advance reconnaissance and disciplined positioning carry the operational weight instead.

## Where the risk actually sits

FCDO Portugal travel advice notes normal precautions nationally, and the country's underlying crime rate is unremarkable by European standards. What separates Lisbon from Porto is footfall-driven petty theft. PSP crime data identifies Tram 28, Alfama and Praca do Comercio as Lisbon's highest-density pickpocketing corridors; drive-by bag snatching along the Tagus waterfront is a repeated pattern in GNR and PSP operational reporting, not an isolated event. Porto's exposure is smaller and more concentrated, mainly the Ribeira and Aliados tourist core during the day and the Galerias de Paris strip after dark on weekends.

For visiting executives, the planning effort is proportionate to that difference: Lisbon itineraries benefit from more deliberate route and timing choices around the historic centre, while Porto's Boavista financial district carries a genuinely lower baseline.
