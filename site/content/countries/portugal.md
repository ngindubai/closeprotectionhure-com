Portugal's close protection market runs on two cities with quite different profiles: Lisbon, the capital and the country's main draw for government, diplomatic, and tech-sector visitors, and Porto, smaller and calmer, anchored by the port-wine trade and a growing startup scene. Both sit under the same national licensing regime, administered by the same regulator, but they are not the same risk environment.

## The PSP framework and the CPO qualification

Private security in Portugal runs on Lei n.o 34/2013, overseen by the PSP, the Policia de Seguranca Publica. A general private security licence is the baseline requirement for any operator. Close protection specifically needs the additional CPO qualification, and that distinction is worth checking rather than assuming. A firm can be entirely properly licensed for standard guarding work and still not have the specific credential the assignment actually needs.

Firearms sit under separate, stricter legislation, Lei n.o 5/2006. Armed private security requires case-specific PSP authorisation, and it is not the standard model in either Lisbon or Porto. Most close protection here, including for genuinely high-profile principals, is unarmed.

## Lisbon versus Porto: not the same city twice

Lisbon's risk profile is shaped almost entirely by tourism volume. Tram 28, a genuine transport route as much as a tourist attraction, and the narrow lanes of Alfama see organised, well-practised pickpocketing that has become something of a fixture of Lisbon travel advice generally. It is not a violent crime problem. It is a numbers problem: high visitor density plus narrow, crowded spaces equals a productive environment for distraction theft.

Porto does not carry that same intensity. It is a working port and business city with a calmer street-crime profile, and its security demand looks different too: cruise-terminal transfers, port-wine industry visitors, and a smaller but real tech and startup presence. A client moving between the two cities in a single trip should expect the security posture to shift accordingly, not stay identical.

## Foreign operators: the partnership route

International firms cannot deploy their own personnel directly under Portuguese law. PSP notification is mandatory before any protection assignment, and while EU-registered operators have some mutual-recognition advantage, the practical path for most foreign companies, EU or otherwise, is a formal partnership with a PSP-licensed Portuguese provider.
