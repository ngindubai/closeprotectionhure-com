Two cities, one licensing regime, two very different risk pictures. Lisbon's pickpocketing statistics sit among the worst in Western Europe, concentrated on Tram 28 and the Alfama quarter. Porto, 300km north, runs one of the lower crime profiles of any major European business destination outside a narrow late-night window. What doesn't change between the two is the law: Lei n.º 34/2013 governs every operator in the country, and the PSP issues and polices the licence.

Lisbon's threat picture is dominated by opportunistic theft rather than violence. PSP annual crime statistics (2025) identify the historic tram and heritage corridors as the country's sharpest pickpocketing hotspots, and motorcycle-borne bag snatching along the Tagus waterfront is well documented by GNR and PSP operational reporting. None of this changes the underlying advisory position: FCDO Portugal travel advice (2026) calls for normal precautions, and the US State Department rates the country Level 2, driven by petty crime rather than any elevated violent-crime trend.

Porto is a different proposition operationally. PSP Crime Statistics (2024) place it among the safer major European cities for corporate visitors, with theft risk narrowing down to Ribeira during peak season and the Galerias de Paris nightlife strip after 23:00 at weekends. A client running meetings in Boavista's financial district or Foz do Douro is, on the numbers, dealing with a materially lower baseline risk than the same itinerary in Lisbon's historic centre.
