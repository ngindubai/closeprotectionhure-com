Ireland's close protection market runs through Dublin, the capital and the country's entire footprint on this network. What makes Dublin distinctive is not its crime profile, which is unremarkable by European standards, but its business mix: a genuinely outsized concentration of technology, financial services, and pharmaceutical company headquarters for a city its size.

## The PSA: one regulator, every licence type

Ireland's Private Security Authority, the PSA, is an independent statutory regulator set up under the Private Security Services Act 2004. It licenses security guards, door supervisors, and close protection officers under a single framework, which makes the verification step simple in principle: confirm the operator's current PSA licence before engaging them, rather than relying on general reputation.

Armed private security sits well outside standard practice here. It requires authorisation beyond ordinary PSA licensing, and the default model for corporate close protection in Dublin, regardless of how senior the principal, is unarmed.

## The post-Brexit wrinkle UK firms need to know

Here is a specific point worth flagging clearly: a UK security company's licensing does not automatically transfer into Ireland. Post-Brexit, PSA compliance is required independently of any UK credentials a firm holds, even though the two markets are geographically and culturally close. The PSA has a defined application pathway for firms that want to pursue direct licensing, but in practice, engaging an already PSA-licensed Irish partner remains the faster and more reliable route, for UK operators just as much as for firms from further afield.

## Why Dublin's security demand looks the way it does

Dublin's client base is shaped heavily by its corporate tenants. A significant share of major technology, financial services, and pharmaceutical companies run their European or international operations out of the city, and that concentration, plus the visiting executives and corporate events it generates, is the real engine of close protection demand here, more than any conventional street-crime or terrorism concern. The practical issues that do come up, general organised crime awareness as a due diligence matter and ordinary city-centre petty theft, are the kind that standard vetted transport and sensible route planning handle without difficulty.
