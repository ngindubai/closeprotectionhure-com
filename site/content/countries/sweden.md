Sweden's close protection market runs through Stockholm, the capital and the single city we cover for the country. It combines a genuinely low baseline crime environment with two specific, well-reported factors that shape how a serious security plan handles the city: gang-related crime in certain suburbs, and a general European terrorism awareness context.

## The Security Companies Act and Polismyndigheten

Private security in Sweden operates under the Security Companies Act, Lag om bevakningsforetag (1974:191), with the Swedish Police Authority, Polismyndigheten, issuing licences. Sakerhetsbranschen, the Swedish Security Association, represents the licensed sector. There is no separate close protection credential distinct from the general licence, so confirming an operator's current Polismyndigheten status is the direct and useful check before any engagement.

Firearms regulation does not permit routine armed private security. It exists only as an exceptional authorisation, and the standard, expected model for corporate close protection in Stockholm is unarmed.

## What the headlines get half right

Stockholm's gang-crime problem is real and has been widely reported internationally, but it is also geographically specific: it concentrates in particular peripheral suburbs, not the central business districts, government quarter, or tourist areas a corporate visitor would typically move through. That distinction matters for planning. A blanket "Sweden has a gang violence problem" headline and an accurate security brief for a Stockholm executive visit are two different documents, and conflating them either causes unnecessary alarm or, worse, misses the specific areas actually worth avoiding.

Sweden's general terrorism awareness sits in line with the broader European picture, shaping standard precautions around large public events and crowded venues rather than anything more unusual. Combined, these factors keep Stockholm at a low overall rating, with the caveat that "low" still means planning matters, not that it can be skipped.

## Foreign operators: licence directly, or partner

International firms can pursue Swedish Police Authority licensing directly, though in practice most engagements run through partnership with an already Sakerhetsbranschen-affiliated, Polismyndigheten-licensed Swedish company. That is the standard route, not a compromise, for supporting a Stockholm deployment.
