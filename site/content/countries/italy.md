Nine Italian cities carry live coverage on this site, and every one of them sits under the same regulatory backbone even though the risk picture varies sharply between them. Rome and the Vatican draw diplomatic and government-adjacent traffic; Milan runs on fashion and finance; Genoa's protection demand is shaped by shipping, not tourism. What doesn't change city to city is the law.

## TULPS and the GPG qualification

Italy's private security sector runs on the Testo Unico delle Leggi di Pubblica Sicurezza, TULPS, supplemented by Legislative Decree 153/2009. A security company needs Prefettura licensing to operate at all, and an individual close protection officer needs the Guardia Particolare Giurata, GPG, qualification specifically, a general security guard licence is not enough on its own. Because both are administered at the provincial level rather than centrally, a firm properly licensed in Milan may not automatically be registered to work in Genoa or Palermo. Confirming local registration, not just national qualification, is worth the five minutes it takes.

## Armed protection stays the exception

Firearms authorisation for private escorts requires case-specific Prefettura or Questura approval tied to a documented threat assessment. It is not issued routinely, and it is not the norm even for high-profile corporate principals. Most Italian close protection, in Rome's diplomatic quarter as much as in Palermo's more complex environment, is delivered unarmed by officers who lean on advance planning and police liaison instead.

## One country, several different risk pictures

FCDO rates Italy's overall terrorism threat as high, a figure shaped by the country's symbolic weight, Rome's government seat and the Vatican in particular, rather than by recent attack history; Italy's AISE and AISI intelligence services have an active and effective disruption record. Layered under that national rating sits real variation: Palermo's organised-crime context and elevated petty-crime environment, Bologna's motorway vehicle-crime exposure on the A1 and A14, Verona's seasonal event spikes around Vinitaly and the opera season. A single national risk figure would flatten all of that into something less useful than treating each city on its own terms.
