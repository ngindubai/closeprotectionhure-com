Italy runs nine cities on this network, more than any other single country we cover, and the spread says something about how corporate and HNWI travel here actually works: Rome for government and diplomatic business, Milan for finance and fashion, and seven further cities each carrying a distinct commercial reason to visit. The regulatory backbone is national. The practical risk picture, and even the correct security posture, is not the same in Palermo as it is in Turin.

## TULPS: one framework, two separate licences

Italy's private security industry sits inside TULPS, the Testo Unico delle Leggi di Pubblica Sicurezza, administered through the Prefettura in each province. It draws a hard line between two things that clients sometimes assume are the same: a company's Prefettura licence to operate, and an individual officer's Guardia Particolare Giurata (GPG) qualification to actually work close protection. Neither substitutes for the other. A properly licensed Italian security firm can still propose an officer whose GPG certification has lapsed, so asking to see it, not just the company's paperwork, is a five-minute check that matters.

Foreign operators cannot simply turn up and deploy. Every international firm works through an Italian-licensed partner, and the Prefettura enforces that requirement with more than nominal attention, especially around Rome and Milan where diplomatic and luxury-sector engagements draw scrutiny.

## Armed protection stays the exception

Firearms authorisation for private close protection requires specific Prefettura sign-off tied to a documented threat. It is not something a client requests as a matter of preference. The overwhelming majority of assignments across all nine of our Italian cities, from G7-adjacent diplomatic work in Rome to luxury-sector protection during Milan Fashion Week, run unarmed, built instead on advance route planning, local liaison, and a driver who actually knows the terrain.

## Nine cities, nine different risk profiles

FCDO rates Italy's national terrorism threat as high, driven by the country's symbolic weight rather than any specific current plot, and Rome and Milan carry that weight most visibly because of the Vatican, the diplomatic community, and high-profile events. Set that macro rating against the day-to-day reality, though, and the picture shifts city by city. Florence and Verona see intense seasonal tourist pickpocketing tied to the Uffizi and the opera season respectively. Turin and Bologna see periodic demonstration activity linked to industrial and student politics. Genoa's steep, narrow Caruggi lanes are as much a logistics problem as a security one. And Naples and Palermo both carry an organised-crime backdrop, documented annually by Italy's DIA anti-mafia commission, that is a due-diligence question for anyone doing business in construction or logistics rather than a direct threat to a visiting executive.

That variation is exactly why we treat each city as its own briefing rather than one generic "Italy" assessment. What works as a security plan in Rome does not automatically transfer to Palermo.
