Venezuela's two cities on this network both sit at the severe end of this site's risk scale, and the country's security picture is unusual for combining extremely high conventional crime with a politically fractured armed-actor landscape and, in Maracaibo's case, genuine infrastructure collapse as an active security variable.

## Kidnapping at a scale most destinations don't approach

Venezuela has one of the highest kidnapping rates anywhere, spanning express kidnapping (short-duration, cash-focused) and planned kidnap-for-ransom operations. What sets it apart from other high-kidnap destinations on this network is actor diversity: FCDO reporting notes that both organised criminal groups and government-aligned colectivos have been implicated in kidnap operations, the latter a politically driven armed presence operating with de facto state tolerance rather than a purely criminal one. In Maracaibo, foreign oil-sector personnel are specifically flagged by InSight Crime as a recognised target profile.

## Licensing that formal registration doesn't fully capture

Private security nationally sits under SENAS registration, administered through the Ministerio del Poder Popular para Relaciones Interiores, Justicia y Paz. That sounds like a conventional licensing chain, and structurally it is. What differs from most jurisdictions this site covers is enforcement: institutional pressure on Venezuelan state functions means SENAS registration alone is not a reliable signal of operational quality. Verifying a provider's actual track record and corporate references carries more weight here than a licence check would in a more institutionally stable country.

## Profile and infrastructure as compounding factors

In Caracas, colectivos have targeted foreign journalists, activists and individuals perceived as opposition-linked, making personal and organisational profile a material input into risk assessment, not a background detail. In Maracaibo, prolonged blackouts and water shortages, described in FCDO Venezuela travel advice, disable CCTV and electronic access controls that a security plan might otherwise depend on, so contingency planning has to assume functioning infrastructure will not always be there.
