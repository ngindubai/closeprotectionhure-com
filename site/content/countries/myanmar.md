Myanmar's three cities on this network sit at the more severe end of this site's risk scale, and for good reason. The military coup of February 2021 replaced a cautiously reforming state with active civil conflict, and every operational question here, licensing, physical security, communications, sanctions compliance, has to be answered against that fact rather than against Myanmar's pre-2021 profile.

## A junta-controlled licensing environment

Private security in Myanmar runs through the Private Security Business Law, administered by the Myanmar Police Force. On paper that sounds like a normal regulatory chain. In practice, the licensing authority itself now sits under the State Administration Council's control, so a company's pre-coup licence is not evidence of its current standing. Several established international security firms withdrew from Myanmar after 2021 specifically because of this ambiguity. Verifying an operator's live relationship with the licensing authority, not just its paperwork, is the first real due-diligence step.

## Detention risk ahead of physical risk

Across Yangon, Mandalay and Naypyidaw, the recurring theme in FCDO and OSAC guidance is that arbitrary detention, not street crime or even the active conflict itself, is the defining risk for most foreign visitors. The SAC has detained NGO workers, journalists and business people on charges including unlawful association and support for organisations it labels as terrorist. Consular access is not guaranteed. A profile-risk check, media connections, civil-society links, prior public statements on Myanmar, belongs at the start of pre-deployment planning, not somewhere in the appendix.

## Sanctions as a parallel workstream

UK, US and EU sanctions on the SAC and affiliated entities mean commercial activity anywhere in the country carries legal exposure that sits alongside, and is genuinely separate from, physical security risk. Organisations that keep a Myanmar presence run ongoing sanctions screening on counterparties and suppliers as a matter of course; a security team's job includes flagging where physical planning might create inadvertent proximity to a sanctioned actor.

## The narrow band of who still operates here

The organisations with a legitimate case for maintaining Myanmar operations are few: humanitarian bodies providing essential services, energy-sector companies with existing legal obligations, a small number of development finance institutions, and limited diplomatic missions largely based in Yangon rather than the capital. Naypyidaw's tightly controlled government zone and Mandalay's position on the China trade corridor each carry their own operational logic, but neither changes the country-level baseline: this is conflict-environment planning, not standard business travel.
