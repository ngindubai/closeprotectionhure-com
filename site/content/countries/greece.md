Athens and Thessaloniki share a single national licensing framework but split on risk in a specific way: protest and disorder patterns, not street crime, are what actually separate the two cities.

## Law 2518/1997: the licensing framework

Private security in Greece runs on Law 2518/1997, as updated by Law 3707/2008, with the Hellenic Police as the licensing authority. The Greek Private Security Association represents operators holding that licence. Verifying an operator's current standing is the practical safeguard for any client, since the licence is what makes the deployment lawful.

## Firearms: licensed, but not the operational default

Armed private security carries a genuine legal route under Law 2168/1993, again administered by the Hellenic Police, but authorisation for commercial close protection work is not routine. Both cities see the majority of executive protection delivered unarmed, with protest monitoring, route planning and liaison work carrying the operational load instead of a weapon.

## Where the risk actually diverges: Athens versus Thessaloniki

Athens' security picture centres on Syntagma Square demonstrations, tourist-area pickpocketing concentrated in Monastiraki and Plaka, and a genuine seasonal wildfire risk across the Attica region between July and September that can disrupt road access and airport contingency planning. Thessaloniki, rated slightly higher at low-moderate, carries FCDO-documented demonstrations around Aristotle Square that have turned confrontational with little warning, plus football-related disorder around major fixtures that brings a heavier police presence and knock-on movement disruption. Neither city's underlying violent-crime rate drives that difference; it is almost entirely a function of how predictably each city's protest and disorder events escalate.
