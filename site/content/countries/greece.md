Athens and Thessaloniki share a licensing regime and a national character trait: frequent public demonstrations. What separates them is how those demonstrations behave. FCDO's 2024 Greece advice notes that protests are common nationally, but singles out Thessaloniki's, centred on Aristotle Square, as carrying a documented risk of turning violent with little warning. That single distinction accounts for the gap between the two cities' risk ratings.

Law 2518/1997, updated by Law 3707/2008, is the national licensing statute, administered by the Hellenic Police across both cities equally. Firearms sit apart, under Law 2168/1993, and require Ministry of Public Order approval granted case by case rather than routinely; commercial close protection in Greece is, in practice, an unarmed discipline built on movement planning and liaison rather than weapons.

Athens carries the country's shipping, EU institutional and energy sector weight, anchored by Piraeus, Europe's busiest cruise port. Hellenic Police 2024 data points to Monastiraki and Plaka as the operational pickpocketing concern, a genuinely minor issue set against the bigger planning factor: Syntagma Square protests, frequent enough that route contingency is simply part of every transfer brief rather than an exception. Summer adds a further seasonal wrinkle, wildfire season between July and September can affect road access to Athens International Airport, and contingency routing accounts for that too. Thessaloniki, 500km north, runs the Thessaloniki International Trade Fair and a strong academic and Balkan-trade economy, but the FCDO's specific violence warning around its demonstrations is the reason its baseline rating sits a notch above the capital's.
