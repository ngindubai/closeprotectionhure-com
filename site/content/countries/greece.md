Greece runs two cities on this network: Athens, the capital and by far the larger security market, and Thessaloniki, the northern port and trade hub. Both operate under the same national licensing law, but the two cities are not carbon copies of each other's risk profile.

## Law 2518/1997 and the Hellenic Police

Private security across Greece runs on Law 2518/1997, updated by Law 3707/2008, with the Hellenic Police handling licensing. The Greek Private Security Association represents the licensed sector nationally. A straightforward, worthwhile step before any engagement is confirming a specific operator's current Hellenic Police licence rather than assuming general market reputation is sufficient proof of compliance.

Armed private security exists under Law 2168/1993 but sits well outside the standard model. Most close protection in Athens and Thessaloniki alike is unarmed, built on planning and coordination with Greek police rather than on carrying weapons.

## Athens and Thessaloniki: related but not identical

Athens carries the bulk of Greece's corporate and diplomatic activity: government, major shipping company headquarters, and a substantial tourism sector all concentrated in one city. Its main practical security issues are periodic protest activity around Syntagma Square, which can disrupt central routes without much warning, and pickpocketing in the busiest tourist areas.

Thessaloniki, Greece's second city, sits a step above Athens at a low-moderate rating, driven by its own particular mix of local protest activity and street-level petty crime as a major northern port and trade centre. Neither city carries an elevated terrorism profile relative to the other. The distinction is genuinely local rather than national, and it is exactly why treating each city as its own briefing, rather than one blanket "Greece" assessment, produces a more useful security plan.

## Foreign operators and the partnership route

International security firms can provide short-term services in Greece with prior EU notification to Hellenic Police, but any sustained deployment needs a formal partnership with a Greek-licensed operator. That is the standard, dependable route for international clients bringing protection details into either city.
