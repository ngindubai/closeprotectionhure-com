Bangladesh's two cities on this network, Dhaka and Chittagong, both sit at high risk, driven less by everyday violent crime and more by a combination of terrorism risk, political volatility, and infrastructure constraints that compound each other during any actual security incident.

## A regulatory system still maturing

Private security agencies register under Ministry of Home Affairs rules, administered through Bangladesh Police, with both companies and individual operators required to hold registration. The framework itself is a reasonable structure on paper. What makes due diligence more important here than in a more established market is that the system is still developing: verifying a specific company's actual registration status and the quality of its operators is worth doing directly, rather than accepting registration as a given.

## Terrorism risk that hasn't gone away

The July 2016 Holey Artisan Bakery attack in Dhaka's Gulshan district killed 22 people, including foreign nationals, and confirmed that IS-affiliated groups have both the intent and the capability to target foreigners in Bangladesh. Security at Gulshan venues frequented by foreign visitors has been substantially upgraded since. The FCDO's continued high terrorism rating for the country reflects that the underlying threat, not just the 2016 incident, remains current.

## Political volatility as an operational variable

Both cities are exposed to hartals, general strikes that can block major roads and shut down transport with little warning, and Chittagong in particular has seen routes to its port and industrial districts cut by blockades tied to the political restructuring that followed the 2024 fall of the Hasina government. Political violence between party affiliates is documented and difficult to predict precisely, which is exactly why movement planning needs a contingency for sudden disruption rather than a single fixed route.

## Traffic as a force multiplier for other risks

Dhaka's traffic congestion ranks among the worst globally and materially extends emergency response times during any incident, security-related or otherwise. That interacts directly with the hartal and terrorism risks above: a city that is already hard to move through quickly becomes considerably harder during a strike or in the aftermath of an incident, which is why route planning and pre-identified alternative paths matter as much as the underlying threat assessment itself.
