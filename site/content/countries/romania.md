Bucharest carries the weight of Romania's corporate security demand, and its risk profile looks different from most European capitals on this site: the biggest practical hazard for a visiting executive isn't terrorism or violent crime, it's the road.

## Law 333/2003 and Gendarmerie oversight

Romania's private security sector operates under Law 333/2003, with the Inspector General of Gendarmerie and the Ministry of Interior sharing oversight responsibility, an arrangement distinct from the police-only licensing models seen in the Czech Republic or Hungary. The Romanian National Association of Private Security Firms, ANSSI, represents licensed operators, and while membership isn't itself the legal bar, it's a reasonable additional signal when evaluating a provider.

## Firearms stay with the state, not private operators

Law 295/2004 keeps armed private security tightly restricted, requiring specific authorisation that isn't issued as routine practice. Romanian police and military remain the country's primary armed presence. Close protection for corporate clients in Bucharest is unarmed almost without exception, which puts more operational weight on planning, vetted transport and counterparty screening than on physical intervention capability.

## The road is the risk that actually matters

Here's the thing most generic country guides miss: Eurostat's 2024 figures place Romania among the EU's highest road fatality rates, which makes professionally driven, vetted transport a genuinely material risk-reduction step rather than a convenience upgrade. Layer on petty theft concentrated around Gara de Nord and the Obor market, plus due-diligence exposure in sectors with organised-crime adjacency such as EU-funds administration and energy, and the picture that emerges is low-drama but specific. FCDO rates Romania as normal precautions and the US State Dept holds it at Level 1 for 2026, both consistent with that assessment.
