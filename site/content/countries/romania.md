Romania's coverage here centres on Bucharest, and the security picture is more about logistics than menace. The FCDO advises normal precautions for 2026 and the US State Department rates the country Level 1. The two things that actually shape an operational plan here are road safety and, for specific sectors, counterparty due diligence.

Law 333/2003 is the governing statute for private security, with the Inspector General of Gendarmerie and the Ministry of Interior jointly overseeing the licensed sector, and ANSSI representing operators at industry level. Firearms sit separately under Law 295/2004, and armed private security stays the exception, not the rule: routine executive protection in Bucharest runs unarmed.

The road safety point deserves more weight than it usually gets in a security brief. Eurostat 2024 data puts Romania among the European Union's highest traffic-fatality-rate countries, a fact that has more day-to-day bearing on a visiting executive's safety than most conventional crime statistics. That's precisely why professional, vetted drivers matter more here than in most comparable European capitals; it isn't boilerplate advice, it's the single most effective precaution available. Petty theft, meanwhile, concentrates predictably around Gara de Nord station and the Obor market, and organised crime adjacency is a due diligence issue for specific sectors, energy and EU funds administration especially, rather than a direct personal threat to most corporate visitors.
