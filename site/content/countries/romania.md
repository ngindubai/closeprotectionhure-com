Romania's close protection market centres on Bucharest, the single city we cover here, but the underlying business reasons to be there, energy, EU-funded infrastructure, and joint-venture manufacturing, generate a genuine and specific security demand rather than incidental tourist traffic.

## Law 333/2003 and Gendarmerie oversight

Private security in Romania is governed by Law 333/2003, with the Inspector General of Gendarmerie and the Ministry of Interior sharing regulatory oversight. The Romanian National Association of Private Security Firms, ANSSI, represents the licensed industry. There is no separate close protection qualification carved out from general private security licensing, so the practical check for a client is confirming an operator's current Gendarmerie registration rather than assuming licensing from reputation alone.

Firearms fall under the distinct Law 295/2004, and armed private security requires its own specific authorisation. It is not standard, and the routine model for corporate close protection in Bucharest is unarmed, structured around planning and liaison with Romanian police rather than weapons.

## Road risk, not street crime, is the headline issue

Ask what actually threatens a business visitor to Bucharest and the honest answer is Romania's roads before its streets. Eurostat data has repeatedly placed Romania among the EU's higher road traffic fatality rates, and that is exactly the kind of risk a professional driver genuinely mitigates, rather than a box to tick. Petty theft around Gara de Nord station and the Obor market area is real but unremarkable by regional standards, and organised crime's visibility has fallen considerably even as it remains a due diligence factor for anyone entering energy sector deals or EU funds administration, where local counterparty checks matter.

The US State Dept's Level 1 advisory and FCDO's normal-precautions guidance both track with this picture: Bucharest is a manageable environment, and the specific things worth planning for are vetted transport and standard commercial due diligence, not personal security in the conventional sense.

## Foreign operators: the notification requirement

International firms need Romanian Police notification before any protection deployment and, in practice, a partnership with a Law 333/2003-licensed local company. That applies to short assignments as much as extended ones, so setting it up ahead of a principal's arrival avoids delay once they land.
