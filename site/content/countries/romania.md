Bucharest carries the country's business, government and energy-sector security profile, and the road, not the street, is where the sharpest risk differential from comparable European capitals actually sits.

## Law 333/2003: the licensing framework

Romania regulates private security under Law 333/2003, with the Inspector General of Gendarmerie and the Ministry of Interior providing oversight. The Romanian National Association of Private Security Firms, ANSSI, represents the licensed sector. An operator's standing under this law is the entire legal basis for the engagement, which is why verifying it directly, rather than assuming it, is the correct starting point for any client.

## Firearms: restricted, not standard

Law 295/2004 keeps armed private security tightly controlled, and it simply is not standard practice for close protection engagements. Romanian police and military hold the armed presence nationally. What visiting executives get instead across Bucharest is unarmed protection built on route discipline, vetted transport and, where relevant, coordinated liaison with local authorities for larger corporate movements.

## Where the real risk sits: the road, not the street

The US State Department holds Romania at a Level 1 advisory, its lowest tier, and FCDO advises normal precautions. Bucharest's street-level crime, concentrated around Gara de Nord station and the Obor market area, is genuinely modest by European standards. The number that should actually shape a security plan is Eurostat's 2024 road traffic fatality data, which places Romania among the EU's worst performers. That single fact does more to justify a vetted, professionally trained driver than any street-crime statistic does, particularly for intercity movement beyond Bucharest's ring road. Separately, corporate visitors working joint ventures, energy deals or EU funds administration should run real due diligence on local counterparties, since organised crime networks, while less visible than a decade ago, have not disappeared.
