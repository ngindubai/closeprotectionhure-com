Norway's close protection market runs through Oslo, the capital and the single city we cover for the country. It is one of the lowest-crime environments on this network, and its security demand is shaped less by street-level risk than by two specific factors: the oil and gas sector's weight in the Norwegian economy, and Norway's NATO membership.

## Vaktvirksomhetsloven and the NSR framework

Private security in Norway operates under the Security Services Act, Vaktvirksomhetsloven (2018), with Norwegian Police as the licensing authority. Most established operators also sit within the Norwegian Security Association, NSR, framework, and Stiftelsen NSR offers a further quality-verification layer beyond the base police licence. Checking both before engaging an operator is a reasonable, not excessive, level of diligence.

Armed private security requires specific authorisation through the PST, Norway's domestic security service, and the police framework, and it simply is not standard for commercial work. Corporate close protection in Oslo runs unarmed by default.

## Why Oslo's demand doesn't look like a typical capital's

Ask what actually drives security bookings in Oslo and the answer is not tourism or general crime concern, both of which are genuinely low here. It is oil and gas sector executives, whose industry dominates the Norwegian economy, and a steady flow of defence, diplomatic, and government-adjacent visits tied to Norway's NATO membership. That combination produces a corporate security market that looks meaningfully different from, say, a finance-and-tourism European capital, even though Oslo's underlying crime statistics would rank it among the calmest cities this site covers.

The practical planning factor that matters most day to day is winter logistics: limited daylight for a large part of the year, icy road conditions, and the contingency time that needs building into any schedule as a result. It is a logistics discipline more than a security one, but it is the difference between a smooth Oslo engagement and one that runs into avoidable delay.

## Foreign operators: NSR partnership is the standard route

International firms rarely hold Norwegian Police licensing directly. The standard, reliable approach is partnering with an NSR-member Norwegian company for the specific engagement, which covers both the legal requirement and the practical local knowledge a foreign firm would otherwise lack.
