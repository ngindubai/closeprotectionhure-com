Ministries, not motorcycle thieves, set the tone for most executive visits to Rabat. Corporate travel here more often means a ministry meeting, an embassy function, or a government-linked engagement than the finance-sector schedule typical of Casablanca. That changes what a protection detail spends its time on: checkpoint navigation, diplomatic liaison, and access protocol matter more than street-crime awareness, though the latter hasn't vanished entirely.

Souissi, the diplomatic quarter housing most of Morocco's foreign embassies, is the clearest example. It carries a heavier, more visible state security presence than anywhere in Casablanca, and each mission runs its own screening for visitors. A protection team attending an embassy function there coordinates with the host's security office in advance; turning up and expecting a standard corporate walk-in doesn't work. The same caution extends to the government quarter around parliament and the royal palace at Dar al-Makhzen, both symbolic-value sites that shape Moroccan security posture and, by extension, route planning.

Getting principals to Rabat at all usually means Casablanca Mohammed V International Airport (CMN) first, then a 70-minute-to-two-hour transfer, unless the itinerary uses Rabat-Sale Airport (RBA) directly. Licensing sits under Law 27-06, and protection here, as everywhere in Morocco, runs unarmed as standard.
