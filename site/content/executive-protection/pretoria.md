Pretoria's executive protection profile is shaped by what the city is: South Africa's administrative capital, seat of the Presidency at the Union Buildings, and home to the largest concentration of foreign embassies on the continent. A meaningful share of the principal movement here is government-adjacent or diplomatic rather than purely commercial, which shifts venue access and liaison requirements away from a standard corporate protection framework and toward one that shares more with diplomatic-security practice.

None of that softens the underlying threat picture. SAPS Q3 2025/26 crime statistics, released 20 February 2026, name Johannesburg and Tshwane, the metropolitan area covering Pretoria, jointly as the country's leading centres for armed robbery and kidnapping, and Gauteng province accounts for more than half the nation's carjackings. The US State Department's South Africa advisory, Level 2 as of 27 May 2025, separately flags kidnapping risk to foreign nationals. A Pretoria detail runs on the same provincial threat assessment as one in Johannesburg, whatever the calmer surface character of Brooklyn or Waterkloof suggests day to day.

FCDO's July 2026 advice adds a Pretoria-specific wrinkle: protests can escalate with little warning, and given the city's role as the seat of government, demonstrations occasionally gather near the Union Buildings and the wider administrative precinct. Movement planning for any government-facing itinerary builds in route flexibility for that possibility rather than treating the precinct as static. Pretoria has no significant international airport, so every arrival routes through OR Tambo, shared with Johannesburg, followed by a road transfer of roughly 50 to 60 minutes or the Gautrain link via Marlboro to Hatfield station.
