Corporate visits to Brasilia have a different centre of gravity than Sao Paulo or Rio de Janeiro. Rather than banking, industry, or tourism, the pull here is government relations, regulatory affairs, and diplomatic engagement, and the Setor de Embaixadas Sul and Norte, the purpose-built diplomatic quarter housing most of Brazil's foreign missions, is where a large share of executive protection work actually happens.

The threat picture reflects that specialisation. The US State Department's Level 2 advisory for Brazil (2026 update) applies nationally, citing violent crime and kidnapping risk, but Brasilia's Plano Piloto runs below Sao Paulo and Rio de Janeiro on most of those measures. What Brasilia does carry, distinctly, is government-district risk: on 13 November 2024, a lone attacker set off explosives at the Praca dos Tres Poderes, outside the Supreme Federal Court. He died. No one else was hurt. It's a low-frequency risk, and not one that shows up in a standard national crime rating, which is exactly why it needs naming directly rather than folded into generic guidance.

Officers here hold Policia Federal registration under Portaria 3233/2012, and most engagements run unarmed given the comparatively calm baseline inside the Plano Piloto. Brasilia International Airport (BSB), about 11 kilometres from the centre, gives a genuinely straightforward arrival, a 15 to 25 minute transfer rather than Sao Paulo's longer, more congested run from Guarulhos.
