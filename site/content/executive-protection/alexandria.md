Alexandria's business case is different from Cairo's, and executive protection planning follows that difference closely. The city handles the large majority of Egypt's seaborne trade and hosts six refineries and three petrochemical complexes, a meaningful share of national refining capacity. Executive visits here skew toward energy, shipping, and industrial business, with site access to port or petrochemical facilities operating under active security protocols and photography restrictions that extend to mobile phones. A protection team working this corridor needs advance site clearance arranged before the executive arrives, not on the day.

The threat picture carries a factor Cairo doesn't share in quite the same way. Two shootings, in May 2023 and October 2024, targeted individuals with Israeli association in public locations in Alexandria itself, both documented in FCDO travel advice and unconnected to the Sinai-based terrorism threat that shapes most of Egypt's national risk assessment. Any executive itinerary with an Israeli-linked element warrants its own threat review rather than folding into a general Egypt briefing. Beyond that specific risk, Alexandria's day-to-day environment is comparatively calm, and most visits combine an industrial-sector meeting with a shorter stop at the Corniche, Bibliotheca Alexandrina, or Qaitbay Citadel.

Getting an executive to Alexandria usually means routing through Cairo first. Borg El Arab (HBE) carries limited scheduled international traffic, so Cairo International (CAI) followed by the desert road, roughly 220km and 2.5 to 3 hours, or rail, is the standard journey, and protection planning treats Cairo airport as the real arrival point. Licensing follows Egypt's national Ministry of Interior framework, with commercial details running unarmed and police liaison arranged where the engagement requires it.
