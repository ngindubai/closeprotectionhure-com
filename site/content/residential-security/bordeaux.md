A wine estate in the Medoc or Saint-Emilion is not a suburban house with a bigger garden. It is a working commercial property with a chai holding stock worth far more than the building itself, seasonal staff numbers that swing sharply around harvest, vehicle gates used by delivery lorries and tasting-room visitors alike, and, in parts of rural Gironde, a mobile signal that drops out well before the drive reaches the road. Residential security for [Bordeaux](/cities/bordeaux/) has to treat that estate property and a Chartrons townhouse as genuinely different problems, not variations on the same checklist.

The city side of the picture is shaped by where Bordeaux's ambient risk actually sits. Chartrons and Cauderan are quiet, well-policed, residential and professional districts; Place de la Victoire, Sainte-Croix, and the corridor around Gare Saint-Jean and the Bastide south bank carry more disorder, more petty crime, and, during periods of national unrest such as the 2018-19 Yellow Vest protests or the 2023 pension-reform demonstrations, more disruption. French police data also flags a specific pattern worth planning around: acquisitive crime targeting HNWI households and luxury guests tends to rise around en primeur season and major wine trade gatherings, when the concentration of wealthy buyers and collectors through the region is at its highest.

Every operative and firm working on a Bordeaux property must hold current CNAPS authorisation under Loi 83-629, administered locally through the CNAPS Nouvelle-Aquitaine delegation; armed guarding is tightly controlled and rarely used outside specific commercial contexts, so the working default is unarmed.
