A Medoc chateau and a Triangle d'Or apartment share almost nothing in terms of physical security needs, yet both fall under the same French regulatory framework and both belong to owners who often live between the two. Bordeaux's residential security landscape is defined by this split: a compact, historic city centre with copropriete access constraints on one side, and a rural estate circuit running through Pauillac, Saint-Julien, and Saint-Emilion on the other, where the nearest gendarmerie brigade might be twenty minutes away rather than five. Our assessments start from the property type rather than a generic template, because the two environments genuinely need different answers.

The estate side of the work is shaped heavily by the wine trade calendar. En primeur week temporarily turns part of a working chateau into a public-facing venue, and DCPJ data referenced on our [Bordeaux city security guide](/cities/bordeaux/) documents targeted acquisitive crime against HNWI visitors during exactly this kind of event. A cellar holding a season's stock is also, in plain terms, one of the most valuable single assets many estate owners hold, and insurers are increasingly asking for documented security assessments before extending high-value cover. City residences, by contrast, sit closer to the petty-crime and tourist-density issues that affect the Saint-Pierre quarter and the area around Gare Saint-Jean, and the access-control questions are about shared stairwells and facade constraints rather than gate perimeters.
