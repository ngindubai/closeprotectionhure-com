West of the Bordeaux ring road, the villas of Caudéran sit behind hedges and low walls that give privacy from the street but little physical delay to anyone determined to get past them. That gap between the look of security and the substance of it is the starting point for most residential surveys we carry out in Bordeaux, whether the property is a Caudéran family home, a Chartrons apartment above a former wine warehouse, or a chateau residence in the Médoc appellation with several hundred metres of vineyard frontage to account for. Chartrons in particular presents a specific pattern: many of its apartment buildings began as merchant houses and storage floors for the wine trade, and their conversion to residential use has left a mix of original stone stairwells, later-added digicode entry, and shared courtyard access that a generic property-security checklist tends to miss. Each survey is written against the building it actually assesses, not a template.

Chateau properties in the Médoc, Pomerol, and Saint-Emilion circuit raise a different set of questions again. A vineyard estate needs controlled access for contractors and, each September, a larger seasonal workforce brought in for the vendange, without turning the residence itself into a place strangers can walk up to. Staff vetting for these properties runs alongside the household's formal employment arrangements, whether that is a CESU declaration for a Caudéran housekeeper or a direct contract for a chateau's resident estate manager, and is supplemented by criminal record and reference checks rather than relying on the paperwork alone. Outbuildings such as the chais, where barrels and bottled stock are held during ageing, are treated as a distinct security zone from the main residence, since their contents can represent a substantial part of an estate's annual value and are accessed by a wider circle of staff and contractors than the house itself.
