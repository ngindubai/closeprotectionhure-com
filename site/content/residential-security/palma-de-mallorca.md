Son Vida's gated streets sit in stark contrast to the old town's narrow lanes around the cathedral and Born, and that contrast runs through nearly every residential security decision in Palma de Mallorca. A hillside villa already benefits from estate-level access control before its own perimeter is even considered; an apartment above a shared stairwell near the Born does not, and needs a different answer built around door and intercom security instead.

The FCDO's Spain travel advice (2024) ties the city's theft risk to specific locations, the cruise terminal on arrival days, the cathedral and Born tourist core, and the Playa de Palma and s'Arenal nightlife strip, rather than to residential neighbourhoods generally. Recurring 'Menys Turisme, Mes Vida' protests since 2024 add occasional route disruption around the centre but are not, on the evidence available, a threat directed at private homes.

Superyacht owners arriving through Real Club Nautico de Palma or STP Shipyard Palma bring a household staffing need that moves on the boat's schedule rather than a fixed calendar, and vetting is structured to keep pace without cutting corners on any individual check. Son Vida's established position as the island's HNWI address means most of that staffing and monitoring work concentrates there, though seasonal properties elsewhere on the island carry their own case for remote monitoring while owners are away.
