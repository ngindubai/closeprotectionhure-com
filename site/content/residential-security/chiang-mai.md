Chiang Mai has built one of Southeast Asia's largest long-stay expatriate and remote-work communities, and residential security work here reflects that population more than it reflects any crime concern. Most requests involve condominiums and houses in Nimmanhaemin or the eastern suburbs, both well-infrastructured districts with an established international resident base and low pedestrian density outside peak hours.

The genuinely distinctive factor is air quality. During the 2026 burning season, coverage of the crisis recorded a PM2.5 reading of 188 micrograms per cubic metre on 29 March, more than four times the WHO's 24-hour guideline, with northern Thai authorities logging over 2,090 fire hotspots in the first three months of the year. For a household planning to be in Chiang Mai from roughly mid-February through April, indoor air purifiers and PM2.5 sensors aren't optional comfort items; they're a standard part of the residential security brief here, alongside a ventilation and outdoor-activity plan built around daily air-quality forecasts.

The Old City is a workable residential option outside its busiest hours, but the density that comes with the Sunday Walking Street market and general tourist footfall makes it a less predictable daily environment than the newer districts for a family or corporate household. Vetting for domestic staff follows the Security Guard Business Act framework used across Thailand, and given how much of Chiang Mai's relocating population arrives on flexible, remote-work or retirement arrangements rather than fixed corporate postings, onboarding here is often set up with that shorter time horizon in mind.
