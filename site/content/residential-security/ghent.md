Executives posted to Ghent for an industrial or pharmaceutical assignment rarely stay for a single week. ArcelorMittal, Volvo Cars Belgium, UCB, and Ablynx (now part of Sanofi) all draw senior staff into the city for months at a time, and that changes the security question entirely. A hotel solves the problem for three nights. It does very little for a family settling into a rented house near the canal zone, or an executive assistant left to manage a household while the principal is on-site. Our residential security work in Ghent starts from that longer time horizon: what does the property actually need, and what does the household around it need, over the full length of the posting. For the wider threat picture behind these recommendations, see our [Ghent city security guide](/cities/ghent/).

Two areas come up repeatedly in our property surveys: Citadelpark and Leopoldspark to the south of the historic centre, and Drongen to the west, both offering a lower crime density than the tourist core and, in Drongen's case, quick E40 access towards Brussels and Antwerp. We steer clients away from the Graslei and Korenlei waterfront, where Federal Police data record concentrated pickpocketing, and from the Overpoort student entertainment street, which sees elevated disorder on weekend evenings. Domestic staff vetting sits alongside the physical survey; household access is a genuine vulnerability, and Belgium's data protection framework, GDPR as implemented through the Belgian Law of 30 July 2018 and overseen by the Gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit, sets clear limits on how any personal data gathered during that process must be handled. CCTV brings its own Belgian wrinkle: the Camera Act requires declaration of any camera facing a public space to the local police zone, on top of ordinary GDPR compliance, and we build that step into installation rather than leaving it as a surprise.
