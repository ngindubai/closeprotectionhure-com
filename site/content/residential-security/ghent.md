Ghent's economy runs on port, steel, and pharmaceutical industry, and most of the city's residential security demand outside the historic centre comes from households connected to those sectors, sitting in conventional suburban districts with standard perimeter needs. The picture changes markedly for a few weeks each July, when the Gentse Feesten transforms the historic quaysides at Graslei and Korenlei into the centre of a festival that City of Ghent figures put at up to one million visitors over its run. Belgian Federal Police data document a corresponding rise in pickpocketing around those areas during the festival, a pattern driven entirely by crowd volume rather than any change in the city's underlying residential crime profile.

A well-built residential security plan for a Ghent household treats the Gentse Feesten as exactly what it is: a predictable, calendar-based spike that calls for temporary measures, stepped-up exterior monitoring, a specific access-control review for properties near the festival route, and awareness for household members moving through the crowds, rather than a permanent addition to the property's baseline security posture. Firms operating in Ghent should hold a current SPF Interieur agrement under the Loi du 2 octobre 2017, and any temporary staff brought on for the festival period should still go through a proportionate vetting process.
