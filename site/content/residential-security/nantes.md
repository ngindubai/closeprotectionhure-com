A Loire Valley chateau an hour from Nantes and a period apartment near the Parc de Procé have almost nothing in common physically, yet both sit under the same French regulatory framework, and both often belong to the same owner. That split defines residential security work in the region: a compact city centre with shared-stairwell access questions on one side, and an estate circuit running towards Saumur and Chinon on the other, where the nearest gendarmerie post might be thirty minutes away rather than five. We start every assessment from the property type, not a generic template, because a chateau and an apartment genuinely need different answers.

The demonstration history that shapes Nantes' reputation, well documented on our [Nantes city security guide](/cities/nantes/), rarely touches residential properties directly. Cours des 50-Otages sees Yellow Vest and pension-reform crowds; a Graslin apartment three streets over does not. What does carry across both property types is staff vetting. Whether a household in the Ile de Nantes hires a cleaner through an agency or a chateau brings on a groundskeeper by word of mouth, the check is identical: identity verification, a criminal record extract where permitted, and a reference call that actually happens rather than one taken on faith. CCTV, wherever it's installed, has to satisfy CNIL-supervised data protection rules regardless of setting, and skipping that step tends to surface as a problem only once it's too late to fix quietly.
