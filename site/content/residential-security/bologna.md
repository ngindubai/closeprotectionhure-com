A residential security review in Bologna has to start by asking which Bologna the client actually lives in. A pharmaceutical executive renting a flat behind the porticoes near Piazza Maggiore faces an entirely different set of considerations to a family who has bought a farmhouse in the Colli Bolognesi. Both are common patterns among the international residents we work with here, and neither should be handled with a template. Full detail on the city's wider security picture, including the trade-fair calendar and the university district, is set out on the [Bologna city page](/cities/bologna/).

City-centre flats generally come with a shared street door, an intercom, and stairwells used by several households; the security question is less about the individual apartment and more about who controls the portone and what happens once someone gets past it. Hillside estates south of the city raise a different set of problems entirely: long driveways, distant neighbours, and connectivity that can drop out without warning. We size perimeter and monitoring plans, including cellular-backup systems where fixed lines are unreliable, to the property in front of us rather than to a generic checklist.

Staff vetting deserves its own line of attention, particularly where a household relies on informal referral rather than a domestic agency, which is common in this part of Emilia-Romagna.
