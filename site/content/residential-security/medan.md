Corporate and expat housing in Medan concentrates in two districts: Medan Polonia, built around the site of the city's former airport, and the newer Setia Budi corridor further south. Both carry a well-established, comparatively low-risk residential profile, and both are the practical starting point for a household security survey here, whether the tenant is on a permanent posting or a project tied to North Sumatra's palm oil and agribusiness sector.

Two factors shape Medan residential planning more than they would in most Indonesian cities. Seasonal haze, typically June to September, from agricultural and forest fires elsewhere in Sumatra can affect air quality and visibility for extended periods, so verified air filtration on home climate systems is worth checking as a household item rather than assuming it. North Sumatra's genuine volcanic and seismic setting is the second, and while Medan itself sits well outside the 5km exclusion zone FCDO advice sets around Mount Sinabung's crater in Karo Regency, structural checks on perimeter walls and outbuildings are a sensible addition to a standard survey.

Domestic staff vetting runs through Indonesia's national POLRI and Ministry of Manpower framework, and because Medan's relocating population is smaller and more project-based than Jakarta's, vetting and staffing timelines are often built around a defined plantation or corporate assignment window rather than an open-ended posting.
