Maersk, Vestas, Arla Foods, Salling Group, and Jysk are all headquartered in Aarhus, and between them they bring a steady flow of relocating executives to Denmark's second-largest city. Add a technology and life-sciences cluster forming around Katrinebjerg and the Agro Food Park, and the residential security picture in Aarhus is shaped almost entirely by where these employers and Aarhus University, with its 44,000-plus students, sit relative to the harbour.

Aarhus C, the business core around the harbour, and Aarhus Ø, the newer harbourfront development, are both well-suited to executive and family accommodation: modern stock, straightforward access control, and a police presence that matches the city's low overall risk profile. Gellerup, in the Brabrand district, is the one area to flag rather than consider: it has featured on Denmark's official disadvantaged-area list since 2014, with a partial relisting in 2017, and sits well outside the business and university areas that most relocating families use day to day.

Aarhus does record the second-highest volume of reported offences of any Danish city after Copenhagen, a figure that tracks the city's size rather than any specific threat to Aarhus C, Aarhus Ø, or the Katrinebjerg area. FCDO guidance notes that a terrorist attack cannot be ruled out anywhere in Denmark, consistent with the general baseline across Western Europe rather than a local Aarhus concern.
