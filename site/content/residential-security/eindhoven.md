A semiconductor-sector assignment in Eindhoven rarely lasts a single week. Engineers, programme managers and executives rotating through ASML, NXP Semiconductors or the Philips Innovation Campus often hold accommodation for months at a stretch, and that duration changes what "residential security" needs to mean. A one-off hotel stay tolerates gaps that a lived-in home should not: default building access codes, an unverified cleaner, a ground-floor window nobody checked. Our approach to Eindhoven residential security starts from the assumption that the property will be occupied long enough for small gaps to matter, and works through the survey, staff vetting and monitoring accordingly.

Location choice does most of the risk-reduction work before a single camera goes up. Strijp-S and Woensel-Noord are the two districts we steer clients towards, both offering lower crime density and, in Woensel-Noord's case, straightforward road access to the ASML campus for a daily commute. Stratumseind, one of the Netherlands' longest bar streets, generates weekend disorder that has nothing to do with a principal's employer but everything to do with where they sleep, so we treat it as a district to avoid for accommodation rather than a citywide concern. Beyond location, the Eindhoven brief carries one dimension that would be unusual in most residential security work: an information-security thread running through staff vetting and monitoring, reflecting AIVD's documented findings on foreign state interest in Dutch high-technology firms and their personnel.

None of this requires treating Eindhoven as anything other than what it is, a low-risk Dutch city with a genuinely distinctive sector-specific threat profile.
