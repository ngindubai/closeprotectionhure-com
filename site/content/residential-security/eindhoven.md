Eindhoven's residential security landscape is unusual for a mid-sized European city because its dominant employer is not a bank or a government department but a chipmaker whose equipment underpins the global semiconductor supply chain. ASML, the sole manufacturer of EUV lithography systems anywhere in the world, sits alongside NXP Semiconductors and the Philips Innovation Campus as the anchor institutions shaping who lives in the city's residential districts and what their security needs look like. That concentration of strategically significant technology work is precisely why the AIVD's 2023 annual report singles out foreign state targeting of the Dutch semiconductor sector as a documented concern.

For most Eindhoven households, that AIVD finding translates into a proportionate, not exceptional, set of measures: awareness of counter-surveillance basics, careful attention to visitor and delivery access at the property boundary, and staff vetting that accounts for confidentiality where a principal's work touches commercially sensitive material. This sits on top of, rather than instead of, the standard Dutch residential security baseline under the WPBR framework, with Justis screening firms and Politie clearing individual operatives. General residential burglary risk in Eindhoven does not stand out from comparable Dutch cities, so a security plan here is really about layering sector-specific awareness onto an otherwise conventional foundation.
