Rabat's residential security picture starts with a fact that shapes almost everything else: this is the seat of government, and its most secured residential district, Souissi, is also its diplomatic quarter. A large share of the households needing property security here are diplomatic postings or government-linked corporate roles rather than the finance-sector relocations that dominate Casablanca's market.

That changes the practical work. Souissi properties often sit near, or effectively within, a broader zone of state security presence built around the embassies themselves, which is a genuine asset for perimeter planning, but only if it's formally coordinated rather than assumed to extend automatically to a private residence next door. Agdal and the newer Hay Riad development offer a calmer, more conventional residential security picture: standard gating, perimeter walls, and controlled access, well suited to family accommodation.

The old medina is a different story. It's a real UNESCO-listed heritage site, not a risk zone in any meaningful sense, but its daytime density and tourist footfall make it a poor fit for family residential placement, the same pattern seen in historic old towns elsewhere in Morocco and across the Mediterranean. Staff vetting follows Law 27-06 nationally, and posting lengths in Rabat often track a defined diplomatic or government contract term rather than an open-ended relocation.
