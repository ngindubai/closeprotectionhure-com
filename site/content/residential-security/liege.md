A logistics executive relocating to Liege for a role connected to the airport's cargo operations, the fifth-largest in Europe by volume, needs two quite different things from residential security: a household placed well away from the district-specific crime pattern documented in Sainte Marguerite, and staff vetted against a start date that matches the posting rather than an open-ended search.

Cointe, a hillside district south of the centre, and Angleur, along the Meuse to the east, both carry lower crime density than the historic core and support fairly conventional access control and perimeter planning. Sainte Marguerite, by contrast, is flagged in local safety guidance as an area to avoid after dark, and the area around Liege-Guillemins station and the Carre nightlife district sees elevated pickpocketing and disorder, particularly at weekends. Neither reaches Cointe or Angleur.

Belgium's national OCAM/CUTA threat level has held at 3 out of 4 through 2026, and Liege's industrial character gives it a higher documented crime rate than smaller Belgian cities, but this is background context rather than a reason to change how a Cointe or Angleur property is assessed. Staff vetting, covering identity verification and employment history review, runs under the Loi du 2 octobre 2017, with firms required to hold an SPF Interieur agrement.
