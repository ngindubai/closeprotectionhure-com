Several hundred Japanese companies maintain a presence in Dusseldorf, concentrated around Immermannstrasse near the Hauptbahnhof in an area known locally as Little Tokyo. That community brings a meaningful share of the city's residential security demand: households relocating on a corporate posting need staff vetted to a timeline, not an open-ended search, and identity verification and employment history review are structured accordingly.

Where a household lives changes the picture more than who lives there, though. Oberkassel's elegant left-bank streets and Kaiserswerth's historic village layout are both well-policed and support conventional gate and lighting specification, while the area around the Hauptbahnhof and the Altstadt nightlife district carry documented petty crime that rises further during Rosenmontag, when several hundred thousand attendees pass through the Altstadt and Konigsallee.

None of that petty crime pattern extends into Oberkassel or Kaiserswerth, and a property survey there is a fairly conventional exercise. CCTV and alarm installation follows the Bewachungsverordnung framework, updated with a revised Sachkundeprüfung scoring system from 1 July 2025, which sets the qualification bar for anyone installing or monitoring residential security systems in the city.
