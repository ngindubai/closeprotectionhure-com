Rabat is not Casablanca with a different postcode. It is Morocco's administrative capital: seat of parliament, home to the royal palace at Dar al-Makhzen, and host to the largest concentration of foreign embassies anywhere in the country. That distinction matters for security planning. Where Casablanca's risk picture centres on commercial-district street crime, Rabat's centres on government-quarter access, diplomatic-security protocols, and a heavier, more visible state security presence around ministries and embassies.

The city's UNESCO listing, granted in 2012 for "Rabat, Modern Capital and Historic City: a Shared Heritage," covers the historic medina, the 17th-century Kasbah of the Oudayas, and the Chellah necropolis, a walled site layering Roman ruins beneath a Marinid-era royal cemetery. These sites draw real tourist volume, and with it the ordinary pickpocketing risk that follows dense foot traffic anywhere. It is a minor risk next to Casablanca's motorcycle drive-by theft problem, not an absent one.

Morocco's national terrorism threat, rated likely by the FCDO, applies to Rabat without adjustment. What changes is the target profile. Government buildings, the royal palace, and foreign missions in the Souissi diplomatic quarter carry symbolic value that ordinary commercial premises don't, and Moroccan security services station accordingly. Static checkpoints and a visible police and gendarmerie presence around these sites are routine, not a sign of an active incident. Visitors moving through the government quarter should expect and plan for this, rather than treat it as unusual.

Access to Casablanca Mohammed V International Airport (CMN), Morocco's principal hub, remains the practical entry point for most international visitors, with a road or ONCF rail transfer to Rabat running roughly 70 minutes to 2 hours depending on method and traffic. Rabat-Sale Airport (RBA) offers a direct alternative, with 118 weekly international flights to 43 destinations including Paris, Madrid, and Istanbul, though its route network is narrower than Casablanca's.
