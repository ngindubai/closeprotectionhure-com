Chiang Mai is northern Thailand's largest city and a well-established hub for digital-nomad and remote-work communities, regional business and logistics, and long-stay expatriates. Its security profile differs sharply from Bangkok's: political unrest and terrorism, the defining risks in the capital, are largely absent here, while the Old City's moat district, night markets and mountain surroundings bring their own, quieter set of planning considerations.

The city's most distinctive risk factor is not crime but air quality. Every February through April, agricultural burning across northern Thailand and neighbouring Myanmar and Laos fills the mountain basin Chiang Mai sits in with smoke that has nowhere to disperse. During the 2026 season, IQAir-sourced readings put PM2.5 levels at 188 micrograms per cubic metre in late March, well over four times the WHO's guideline, with authorities recording thousands of fire hotspots and running an extended open-burning ban. For any principal, expatriate household or event planner based in Chiang Mai during those months, this is a real operational factor rather than a footnote, and it needs to be built into scheduling in the same way heat or flooding would be planned around in other markets.

Road safety is the second consistent concern. Thailand's national road death rate sits among the highest the WHO records worldwide, and Chiang Mai's combination of scooters, songthaews and winding routes to outlying attractions such as Doi Suthep adds local texture to that national picture. A trained security driver remains the standard mitigation, just as in Bangkok, though the underlying risk here is accidents and congestion rather than the political unrest that shapes Bangkok assignments.

Crime against visitors is comparatively low-key: pickpocketing and scams cluster around the Old City's markets, much as they do in any major Thai tourist centre, but violent crime targeting foreign business travellers is uncommon. Chiang Mai also sits well clear of both the Cambodian border tensions and the deep south insurgency that the FCDO flags separately for far-southern Thailand; those risks belong to a different part of the country and should not be read across onto a Chiang Mai itinerary.
