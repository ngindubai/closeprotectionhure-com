Moscow is Russia's commercial capital and, before 2022, one of the largest business hubs in the former Soviet space. For the small number of foreign executives who continue to travel there on essential business, the security picture has changed fundamentally.

**The primary risk in Moscow today is political and legal, not physical.** Arbitrary detention of foreign nationals, including executives, has occurred. Surveillance of foreign business visitors is comprehensive. Exit from Russia has become legally complicated for some foreign nationals. These are the risks that dominate security planning — not street crime or terrorism.

## The current operating environment

From 2022 onward, Western governments have issued travel advisories against all non-essential travel to Russia. Companies with ongoing Russian commercial obligations operate in a sanctions-constrained environment where legal exposure can translate into personal risk for travelling executives.

The physical security environment in Moscow — street crime, terrorism, kidnapping — is not the primary concern for most corporate visitors. The city's ambient crime level for business travellers in established areas is lower than many other cities in our network. What has changed is the political risk: the Russian state's willingness to use legal mechanisms against foreign nationals as instruments of geopolitical pressure.

## Communications and operational security

Assume comprehensive electronic and physical surveillance. Hotel rooms used by foreign executives, vehicles, and standard meeting venues are routinely monitored. Any Moscow engagement must include a pre-travel communications security protocol: clean devices, encrypted communications, no sensitive material stored on any Russian-jurisdiction system.

Meeting venue selection matters. Avoid informal conversations about commercially sensitive matters in hotel rooms, restaurants, or vehicles without counter-surveillance precautions.
