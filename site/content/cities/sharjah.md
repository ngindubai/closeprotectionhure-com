Executives who know Dubai often assume Sharjah, twenty minutes up the coast, runs on the same rules. It doesn't. Sharjah is the only emirate in the UAE that bans alcohol outright, and its public-conduct standards, set out in rules the Sharjah government issued back in 2001, are enforced with more consistency than in Dubai or Abu Dhabi. That legal distinction, not crime, is the real planning issue for anyone moving between the two.

Beyond the legal environment, Sharjah has a character of its own. UNESCO named it Cultural Capital of the Arab World in 1998, and the emirate has leaned into that identity ever since, building out museums, heritage districts, and cultural institutions rather than competing with Dubai's skyline. Economically, it plays a different role too: manufacturing, logistics, and trading firms cluster around the Sharjah Airport International Free Zone (SAIF Zone), drawn by industrial space and running costs well below Dubai's. Sharjah International Airport itself, the base for budget carrier Air Arabia, is one of the region's busiest, carrying close to 20 million passengers a year to more than 90 destinations.

Sharjah's police force dates to 1967 and has run under the federal Ministry of Interior since the UAE's formation in 1971, but private security licensing in the emirate does not follow Sharjah Police directly. It runs instead through the Private Security Business Department (PSBD), an Abu Dhabi Police unit, which covers Sharjah alongside Abu Dhabi and the four smaller Northern Emirates. Dubai's separate SIRA licence has no standing here: any operator working legally in Sharjah needs PSBD certification specifically.

A large share of Dubai's corporate workforce actually lives in Sharjah, drawn by cheaper housing, and commutes daily across the Al Ittihad Road border corridor at Al Nahda. That commute is congested and predictably so, a fact that matters more for planning a Sharjah visit than any crime statistic does.
