Riyadh is the seat of Saudi Arabia's government, the headquarters of Saudi Aramco, and the primary hub for Vision 2030-related business investment. Corporate visits to Riyadh have increased significantly as the Kingdom opens to foreign investment and tourism. The security environment is structured and in many ways controlled — but several risks require specific planning.

**Close protection in Riyadh is unarmed as standard, and all security must operate through Saudi-licensed entities.** The physical security risk for executives in the main business districts is manageable; the more significant concerns are the Houthi missile and drone threat, the legal environment for foreign nationals, and the regulatory requirements for security operations.

## The security landscape

The terrorism threat has a long history in Saudi Arabia — the 2003 Riyadh compound bombings, subsequent attacks on security forces and infrastructure — and AQAP remains a listed concern for government threat assessments. In practice, Saudi Arabia's domestic security architecture is extensive, and the visible security presence in Riyadh is substantial.

The Houthi threat from Yemen is the more operationally relevant current risk. Ballistic missile and drone attacks on Saudi Arabia have been persistent since 2015. Several have reached Riyadh, including impacts near King Khalid International Airport. Saudi air defences intercept the majority, but not all. Executives should treat the hotel briefing on air attack procedures as operational information, not a formality.

The legal environment for foreign nationals is the third major consideration. Saudi Arabia's laws differ materially from Western norms on a range of personal and business conduct matters. What is legal elsewhere may result in detention in Saudi Arabia. Any executive travelling to Riyadh for the first time should receive a legal environment briefing as part of their pre-travel preparation.

## Business districts and security context

The **Al Olaya / King Fahd Road** corridor is Riyadh's established international business district — luxury hotels, multinational offices, controlled-access buildings. The **King Abdullah Financial District (KAFD)** is a modern controlled-access zone north of the city centre, designed from the outset with international business security standards. The **Diplomatic Quarter** has the highest security density of any residential/commercial area in the city.

Airport transfers between King Khalid International Airport (RUH) and the central business districts are long (30–60 minutes depending on traffic) and should be pre-arranged with a vetted driver.
