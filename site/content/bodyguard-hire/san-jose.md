Costa Rica's stability is a genuine asset for the executive traveller. The country has maintained a stable democracy since 1948, has no standing army, and is consistently rated the safest country in Central America. San Jose's growing technology and medical devices sector has made it a regional hub for multinational operations, and the Escazu district hosts the majority of international corporate offices and senior executive residences.

That stability does not eliminate the need for professional close protection. Petty crime, including pickpocketing, phone theft and vehicle break-ins, is documented across central districts. Armed robbery is active in peripheral areas. Drug trafficking networks operating through the Caribbean coast corridor generate spillover risk that, while not the dominant threat picture, features in operational planning for principals moving beyond the corporate zone.

Costa Rica's Ministry of Security operates the most developed private security regulatory framework in Central America through its Department of Private Security Services. Licensing is consistently enforced, armed operators require individual firearms permits in addition to company authorisation, and foreign operators work through locally licensed companies. This regulatory clarity is an operational advantage: it provides clients with greater confidence in operator standards and supports the due diligence requirements of multinational compliance teams.

For most corporate principals in San Jose, the close protection requirement is for discreet, professional coverage that does not create an overt security profile in what is largely a low-threat business environment.
