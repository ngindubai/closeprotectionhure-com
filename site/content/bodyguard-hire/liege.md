Europe's fifth-largest cargo airport by volume runs around the clock in Liege, with no noise curfew, and that single fact says more about who books close protection here than anything to do with tourism or diplomacy. There are effectively two Liege: the passenger city, and the cargo one. That freight economy, layered on top of the city's steel and manufacturing base, is what drives most bookings.

The Loi du 2 octobre 2017 sets the national framework, with the SPF Interieur issuing both firm agrements and individual identification badges, and armed work staying behind a high ministerial bar that most commercial clients never clear. What pushes Liege into the low-to-medium risk category, above several of its Belgian neighbours, is a genuinely higher industrial-city crime rate: Sainte Marguerite is specifically flagged in local safety guidance as an after-dark risk, and OCAM/CUTA has held Belgium's national threat level at 3 out of 4 through 2026.

None of that changes the basic transport logic. LGG has almost no scheduled passenger service, so most executives fly into Brussels or Charleroi and drive in, roughly 97km or 78km respectively, with a vetted driver for the onward run. The exception is anyone with a genuine reason to access LGG's cargo side directly, which requires advance coordination rather than a casual arrival.
