Hiring a bodyguard in Lagos is usually a short-notice decision tied to a specific trip: a board visit, an energy-sector site inspection, or a period of heightened personal risk. This page covers how close protection hire works, what an NSCDC-licensed officer does across a day of movements, and how quickly cover is arranged. ## The Lagos threat environment

The FCDO maintains a travel advisory for Nigeria that reflects the specific risk picture of different regions. Lagos itself is not on the advise-against-all-travel list, but the FCDO and US State Department both note an elevated risk environment that includes violent crime, kidnapping for ransom, armed robbery, and vehicle crime. For corporate visitors, the operationally relevant risks are kidnapping (particularly for executives in financial services, energy, or with publicly visible profiles), armed robbery during transfers, and the difficulties created by Lagos's traffic environment in any emergency scenario.

The Niger Delta security situation, while geographically distinct from Lagos, materially affects the threat picture for executives associated with the upstream oil and gas sector. Visitors with that profile carry an elevated targeting risk that shapes the close protection plan from the moment of arrival.

## The NSCDC framework

Commercial private security in Nigeria is licensed by the Nigeria Security and Civil Defence Corps (NSCDC) under the Private Guard Companies Act. Armed close protection requires additional firearms authorisation through the Nigeria Police Force. Foreign close protection personnel cannot lawfully bear arms in Nigeria; all armed cover is provided by Nigerian nationals with current licensing. The verification step for clients is: ask for the NSCDC licence number of the operating company and of the officers proposed for your detail.
