Brasilia's most reported security incident in recent years wasn't a robbery or a kidnapping attempt. It was a lone attacker with explosives outside the Supreme Federal Court on 13 November 2024, in the Praca dos Tres Poderes, the square that anchors Lucio Costa's original city plan. He died; no one else was hurt. That single fact says more about Brasilia's protective needs than any generic city overview could: this is a government capital first, and bodyguard work here follows that logic.

Officers working Brasilia hold Policia Federal registration under Portaria 3233/2012, the same national standard applied across Brazil, with Distrito Federal state oversight layered on top. Most details run unarmed. The working environment is the Praca dos Tres Poderes government sector, the Esplanada dos Ministérios, and the Setor de Embaixadas diplomatic quarter, where access protocol and liaison matter more than the armoured-vehicle countermeasures that define Sao Paulo assignments. Pedestrian theft around the central bus station, flagged in FCDO Brazil travel advice (2026), is the more routine concern, and it responds to ordinary discretion rather than anything more elaborate.

Satellite cities including Ceilandia, Santa Maria, Sao Sebastiao, and Paranoa sit outside the Plano Piloto and carry a different after-dark risk profile, according to the OSAC Brasilia crime and safety report. None feature in a standard itinerary, but route planning accounts for the distinction as a matter of course.
