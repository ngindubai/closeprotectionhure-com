Little Tokyo, the local name for the Immermannstrasse district near Dusseldorf's Hauptbahnhof, is the fastest way to understand why bodyguard hire here looks different from most German cities. Several hundred Japanese companies maintain a presence there, the largest concentration in the country, and the close protection work built around that client base leans on business etiquette as heavily as it does on physical security planning.

Section 34a of the Gewerbeordnung and the Bewachungsverordnung set the legal framework, with IHK Dusseldorf issuing the Sachkundeprufung qualification every officer needs for higher-risk protective work. Armed cover stays rare, gated behind specific police authorisation that few commercial clients ever require. What does change the calculus twice a year is Messe Dusseldorf, running more than fifty international fairs including boot, drupa, K, and ProWein, and Rosenmontag, which pushes several hundred thousand people through the Altstadt and Konigsallee in a single afternoon.

Neither event calls for a heavy-handed response. A vetted driver covering airport and venue transfers handles most exhibitor and delegate traffic, with close protection reserved for high-profile principals during the busiest fair weeks or for anyone moving through central Dusseldorf during Karneval itself, when opportunistic theft rises sharply in the densest crowd areas.
