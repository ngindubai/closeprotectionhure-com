A Canberra bodyguard assignment runs on a different logic to Sydney or Melbourne. This is the federal capital by design, purpose-built after the 1911 Griffin design competition, and its geography reflects that: Parliament House, the High Court, and Government House sit inside a formal Parliamentary Triangle, with the diplomatic corps concentrated a short distance away in Yarralumla. Over 100 foreign embassies and high commissions operate from Canberra, more than any other Australian city, according to the National Capital Authority, and that density of protected sites shapes how a close protection detail plans movement and access far more than any elevated crime risk.

Licensing is the first practical check. Canberra's Class 1B bodyguard licence runs under the ACT's own Security Industry Act 2003, administered by Access Canberra rather than a police licensing body, and it has no automatic reciprocity with the NSW licence that covers the surrounding state. Anyone hiring a bodyguard for a Canberra visit should confirm ACT accreditation specifically, particularly if the operator's usual work is in Sydney. Policing itself works differently too: the Australian Federal Police delivers community policing here under contract as ACT Policing, since the territory has no separate police force of its own.

Client work tends to follow the city's economic base. Government contracts, public-service engagements, and diplomatic-adjacent business dominate over the corporate and retail settings that define Sydney or Melbourne assignments, reflecting an Australian Public Service workforce in the ACT that reached 70,221 in 2025, roughly 35 per cent of the entire federal public service. Seasonal factors round out the planning picture: bushfire smoke risk peaks in the December to February fire season, and magpie swooping season runs roughly July to November across Canberra's leafy government precincts, a minor but genuine consideration for any walking route.
