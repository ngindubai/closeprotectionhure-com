Wroclaw counted 234 modern business-service centres as of May 2025, according to a KPMG Poland report on the country's shared-services sector, with Google, HP, Nokia, IBM, UBS and Volvo IT all named among the operations based here. That's a serious concentration of corporate footprint for a city of its size, and it's the main reason bodyguard hire in Wroclaw looks less like classic threat mitigation and more like discreet, logistically smooth executive travel into a campus environment.

It's worth noting that footprint moves. UBS's Wroclaw headcount reportedly fell from around 2,200 to between 1,400 and 1,600 by the third quarter of 2024 amid sector-wide restructuring, which is a useful reminder that confirming a client's current office location and access arrangements before a visit matters more here than in a city with a more static corporate base.

Away from the campuses, the Rynek market square and its tram stops carry the highest tourist footfall in the city, and travel guidance consistently flags pickpocketing and scam risk there, including a documented fake-petition approach used against visitors. Poland's role as NATO's principal logistics hub for Ukraine support has added a genuine hybrid-threat dimension nationally, tracked by the ABW security agency, though it sits at a strategic level well above the everyday corporate visitor experience. Licensing runs through the Act of 22 August 1997, with Ministry of Internal Affairs and Administration concessions required for any commercial CP work and armed authorisation rarely granted.
