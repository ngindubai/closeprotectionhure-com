Ask a Dubai-based security provider about Sharjah and you'll sometimes get an answer that assumes the two emirates run on the same rulebook. They don't. Sharjah is the one emirate in the UAE with a total alcohol ban, no licences at all, and its public-conduct rules, in force since 2001, are enforced with a consistency that surprises visitors coming from Dubai's more relaxed hotel culture. Getting a principal properly briefed on that distinction before arrival is the actual job here, more than any physical protective posture.

Licensing follows a different chain too. Dubai's SIRA framework has no authority in Sharjah. Officers working legally in the emirate hold Private Security Business Department (PSBD) certification instead, issued through Abu Dhabi Police under the federal Ministry of Interior, the same body that also covers Abu Dhabi, Ajman, Umm Al Quwain, Ras Al Khaimah, and Fujairah. A bodyguard with a valid Dubai licence cannot simply carry that credential across the Al Ittihad Road border and keep working.

Most Sharjah engagements don't stay in Sharjah. Business visits typically combine a Sharjah leg, perhaps a SAIF Zone site visit or a meeting near the cultural district, with a Dubai leg for accommodation or onward travel, so officers need working knowledge of both legal environments and the traffic realities of the border crossing itself.
