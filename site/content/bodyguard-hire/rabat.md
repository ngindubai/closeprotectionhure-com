Rabat runs on a different logic to Casablanca. It's the seat of government, the King's primary residence, and home to the largest concentration of foreign embassies anywhere in Morocco. A close protection detail here spends as much time thinking about government-checkpoint access and diplomatic liaison as it does about street crime.

That's not to say street-level risk disappears. It doesn't. The medina and the Kasbah of the Oudayas, both folded into Rabat's 2012 UNESCO listing alongside the Chellah necropolis, pull in dense tourist crowds, and pickpocketing follows. It's a smaller problem than Casablanca's motorcycle drive-by thefts, but a real one. What's genuinely distinct is the Souissi diplomatic quarter: heavy static security, embassy-specific access protocols, and a symbolic-target profile that shapes route planning around the royal palace and parliament in a way Casablanca's commercial districts simply don't require.

Officers here operate unarmed, as they do across Morocco, under Law 27-06's licensing framework. Foreign firms work through our accredited Moroccan partner, and any assignment brushing government or embassy premises usually needs a further layer of coordination.
