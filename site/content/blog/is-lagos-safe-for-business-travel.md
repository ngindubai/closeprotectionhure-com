Ask whether Lagos is safe and you get two honest answers that both matter. Yes, tens of thousands of business visitors move through the city every month without incident. And no, the risks documented by the FCDO and OSAC are not exaggerated: armed robbery, kidnapping and civil unrest are real, current, and specifically shaped by how the city works, not abstract travel-advisory boilerplate.

## What the official sources actually say

FCDO travel advice for Nigeria is updated regularly rather than static, and it consistently names armed robbery on roads and kidnapping as the primary threats. OSAC's Crime and Safety Report for Lagos goes further, documenting carjacking, armed vehicle robbery and the kidnapping of business travellers as recurring categories, not one-off incidents. Neither source treats Lagos as uniquely dangerous among major African commercial capitals. Both treat it as genuinely elevated, and worth planning around rather than shrugging off.

## Geography decides more than luck does

Lagos's risk is not evenly spread. Victoria Island, Ikoyi and Lekki Phase 1 carry the city's highest security presence, its international hotel infrastructure, and a materially lower incident rate. The mainland, and Apapa, Oshodi and Mushin specifically, present a different environment: higher robbery rates, congested transit corridors, and little reason for a corporate visitor to be there at all. The practical rule most operators apply is simple. If the itinerary does not have a specific business reason to leave the Island corridor, it should not.

## Why the traffic itself is a security variable

This is the detail visitors underestimate most. Lagos's congestion is not just an inconvenience, it is the mechanism through which most robbery and express-kidnapping risk actually materialises, because stationary or slow-moving vehicles are predictable and exposed. That is the whole logic behind treating armoured transport, closed windows and route variation as baseline practice rather than a premium add-on, and why arriving after dark in heavy traffic is one of the specific things security advisers tell clients to avoid scheduling.

## Unrest changes the picture fast

The October 2020 EndSARS protests are the reference point here, and for good reason. They began as a response to police brutality and escalated into widespread violence and property destruction, including inside Victoria Island itself, the district most visitors assume is insulated from that kind of disruption. It wasn't. Contingency planning for fast-moving unrest, not just routine crime, is now a standard part of any extended Lagos assignment.
