Nairobi is East Africa's primary business hub. It is also a city where the question of how you move between venues is a security question, not just a logistics one. The carjacking environment is real, documented, and has specific geographical and temporal patterns that professional security drivers understand and that most Uber drivers and conventional transport services do not.

This is not about fear. It is about accurate risk awareness and proportionate response.

## What the Carjacking Risk Actually Is

Carjacking in Nairobi is not random. The incidents that professional security advisers track, from OSAC Crime and Safety Reports to Kenya Police data, reveal consistent patterns.

**Geographic concentration:** The areas with the highest frequency of carjacking incidents in Nairobi are not the business districts. They are the transition zones between the business and diplomatic districts and the broader city: the approaches to and from JKIA airport along Mombasa Road, the outer sections of Thika Road, areas around Eastleigh, and movement through Mathare and Kayole. Karen, Westlands, and Lavington are materially different from these corridors.

**Temporal concentration:** Night movement significantly elevates all vehicle crime risk in Nairobi. The reduction in witnesses, the lower police visibility, and the improved approach opportunities for attackers all compound after dark. Late-night arrivals at JKIA are the scenario that most concerns security advisers for Nairobi visitors.

**Target profile:** Vehicles that read as high-value, driven by non-local drivers who are visibly uncertain about their environment, are higher-risk targets. A locally inconspicuous vehicle driven by a driver who moves with local confidence and knowledge creates a different risk profile.

## The JKIA Airport Run

Jomo Kenyatta International Airport is 18 kilometres from the Westlands and CBD business districts. The Mombasa Road corridor connecting them has documented carjacking incidents, particularly at the forced-stop points where traffic concentrates.

This is the journey that most Nairobi business visitors make without thinking about it. They land, grab the first available transport, and head to their hotel. A professional security driver changes the approach:

- Collection is coordinated inside the terminal before the passenger exits to the exposed vehicle area
- The vehicle is identified to the passenger by registration plate and driver photo before they approach it
- The route is planned, with the specific Mombasa Road pinch points accounted for
- The driver maintains escape space at forced stops and knows which sections to transit quickly

## What a Security Driver Does Differently

The comparison is not about the vehicle. It is about training and operational practice.

A vetted security driver in Nairobi has been trained in: defensive and evasive driving techniques; threat recognition at static and moving positions; route planning and alternatives; anti-surveillance (understanding whether a vehicle has been noted and is tracking the principal); protocols for approaching and leaving venues; first aid; and communication with an operations controller throughout the assignment.

An Uber driver's job is to get from A to B efficiently. That is genuinely useful but it is a different function.

## Nairobi's Terrorism Context

The carjacking risk is the primary vehicle security concern for most business visitors. But it is worth noting that Nairobi's security environment also includes the terrorism threat from Al-Shabaab. The DusitD2 attack in 2019 involved vehicle-borne operatives moving through the city. Security drivers in Nairobi with counter-terrorism awareness are trained to notice surveillance behaviour that is relevant to both criminal and terrorism threat scenarios.

## When You Do Not Need a Security Driver

Not every Nairobi visit requires a full-time vetted security driver. If you are staying and working exclusively within the Westlands-Gigiri-Karen triangle, travelling during daylight only, on a short visit with a low profile, and using Uber or a hotel transfer for movement between known points, the risk is materially lower than the factors above might suggest.

The profile that warrants a security driver: late-night airport arrivals or departures, movement between Nairobi's business district and locations on the Mombasa Road corridor or beyond, visits to sites outside the primary business districts, or any principal whose sector or profile creates elevated targeting interest.
