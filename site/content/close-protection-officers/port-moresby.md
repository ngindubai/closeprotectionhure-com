Port Moresby presents one of the most demanding security environments in the Pacific region. The city is the commercial and political capital of Papua New Guinea, a country rich in extractive resources that draws a significant international business community, yet urban infrastructure and policing capacity have not kept pace with the city's expansion. FCDO travel advice rates Papua New Guinea as requiring a high degree of caution, noting that serious violent crime, including carjacking and robbery, occurs regularly across the city and that the situation can deteriorate quickly.

The Raskol gang phenomenon, a network of loosely affiliated street gangs operating across Port Moresby's informal settlement belt, is a documented feature of the city's security landscape noted in both FCDO and OSAC reporting. These groups are opportunistic and target perceived wealth, making foreign business travellers with predictable routines, visible equipment, and unfamiliarity with the environment a higher-risk profile. Kidnapping incidents involving foreign nationals have been recorded and tend to follow periods of predictable movement, making route variation and low-profile travel disciplines particularly important.

Close protection officers working in Port Moresby must combine personal protection skills with a detailed working knowledge of the city's geography, gang territories, and police liaison channels. The Security (Protection) Act 2004 provides the licensing framework for commercial security in PNG, administered by the PNG Police Force. Clients commissioning security support should confirm operator licensing status before any deployment commences, and should treat the security briefing on arrival as an essential operational step rather than a formality.
