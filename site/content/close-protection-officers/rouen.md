Rouen's officers work a split profile few French assignments require in one city. On one side is Vieux Rouen, the medieval core where Joan of Arc was executed in 1431, well-policed but pedestrianised enough that vehicle access needs planning ahead of time. On the other is the Seine valley's industrial corridor, anchored by the Port of Rouen, Europe's leading grain-export port under HAROPA PORT, where the September 2019 Lubrizol Seveso-site fire remains the standing reference point for petrochemical risk. Any site visit into that corridor gets coordinated in advance through the host organisation rather than approached as open ground.

French Ministry of Interior data reported in April 2024 placed Rouen fourth of 368 cities over 22,500 residents for recorded crime per capita, ahead of both Lyon and Paris, though the Ministry's own methodology note describes the figure as informational rather than a complete measure. An officer here treats that ranking as a genuine reason for tighter station-area vigilance around Gare de Rouen-Rive-Droite, alongside the standard nationwide Vigipirate terrorism posture and the documented history of gilets jaunes and pension-reform unrest in the city since 2018.

Every officer working Rouen holds CNAPS authorisation under Loi n. 83-629, a legal requirement rather than a formality, since providing security services without it is a criminal offence in France. Rouen has no major international airport, so officers meet the principal at a Paris airport and stay with them through the onward leg, most often the roughly 1 hour 15 minute SNCF run from Paris Saint-Lazare.
