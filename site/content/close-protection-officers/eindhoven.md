Eindhoven is a low-risk city with a threat profile that does not read like most low-risk cities. AIVD, the Dutch intelligence service, stated plainly in its 2023 annual report that foreign state actors are targeting Dutch semiconductor and high-technology firms, and Eindhoven is where that finding becomes concrete: ASML, the sole global manufacturer of extreme ultraviolet lithography systems, sits alongside NXP Semiconductors and the Philips Innovation Campus as the city's defining commercial presence. A close protection officer working an Eindhoven assignment needs to hold both truths at once, that the city is genuinely safe by any conventional measure, and that visitors connected to its technology cluster carry a specific, documented interest from actors most cities never have to think about.

That combination shapes what a WPBR-registered officer actually does here. Route planning and venue liaison look much like they would in any well-run European city: steer principals towards Strijp-S or Woensel-Noord rather than the Stratumseind corridor, manage the standard station awareness at Eindhoven Centraal, keep a written emergency reference to Catharina Ziekenhuis and the Politie non-emergency line close at hand. Layered on top of that is a counter-surveillance discipline that would be unusual to build into a low-risk city deployment: care around what gets discussed in a hotel lobby, awareness of who might be observing a laptop screen, basic habits around device security during transit between meetings at ASML or NXP. None of this assumes an individual principal has been targeted. It assumes that operating in a cluster AIVD has already flagged publicly calls for a baseline most other business-travel assignments do not need.
