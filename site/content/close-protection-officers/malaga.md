Malaga is a low-risk city, but "low risk" is not the same as "nothing to plan for," and the officers we deploy here are trained to work that distinction rather than ignore it. The threat picture is specific and documented: pickpocketing concentrated around Calle Larios and the Alcazaba approach, vehicle crime linked to AGP airport collection areas and A-7 motorway service stations, and a scooter-assisted bag-snatching technique on the Paseo del Parque seafront that has been reported by Spanish police. None of it changes Malaga's standing alongside Madrid, Barcelona, and Seville as a comparatively safe Spanish city, but a close protection officer who does not know these local patterns is planning against the wrong picture.

Licensing is the other half of the job. Spain runs a tighter framework than many jurisdictions: companies need SES registration under Ley de Seguridad Privada 5/2014, and individual officers need the Escolta Privado qualification plus a TIP credential from the Direccion General de Policia, a step beyond the standard guard licence that some buyers mistakenly assume is sufficient. Non-EU security professionals cannot simply deploy on their own; they work through a Spanish SES-registered firm, full stop. We treat credential verification as a fixed part of every assignment, not an optional add-on, and we can put the documentation in a client's hands before an officer sets foot on the job.

Most assignments move between two circuits: the city and the Malaga TechPark, where executives from companies like Vodafone, Google, and Amazon generate steady business travel, and the Marbella, Puerto Banus, and Costa del Sol corridor further west, home to one of Europe's densest concentrations of HNWI property.
