An officer assigned to Aarhus spends most of a detail with the corporate sector rather than the street. Maersk runs logistics and services functions from the city, Vestas anchors Denmark's wind-turbine industry here, and the Port of Aarhus is the country's largest container hub, handling over half of Denmark's container traffic. Arla Foods, Salling Group and Jysk round out a headquarters presence that gives Aarhus a genuine corporate weight most visitors underestimate next to Copenhagen.

Security-wise, the city is straightforward. Aarhus does record the second-highest volume of reported offences nationally after Copenhagen, but that tracks its size as Denmark's second city rather than any specific threat, and nothing in the data reviewed points to violent crime concentrated in the business core, university district, or harbourfront areas a corporate visitor actually uses. Gellerup, part of the Brabrand district and on Denmark's official disadvantaged-area list since 2014, sits well outside that itinerary and gets noted as a geographic point of awareness rather than a coverage priority.

Every officer working Aarhus, foreign or Danish, needs personal Rigspolitiet approval and an identification card under the Guard Act, and unarmed deployment is the standard model given Danish firearms rules. Aarhus Airport's limited international service means officers typically meet the principal at Billund or Copenhagen instead, with the fastest direct rail connection running about 3 hours.
