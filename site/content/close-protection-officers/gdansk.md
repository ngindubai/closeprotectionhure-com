Gdansk asks its officers to carry more history in mind than most European assignments. The Gdansk Shipyard strike that began on 14 August 1980 led within weeks to the Gdansk Agreement and the formal registration of the Solidarity trade union that November, and the European Solidarity Centre beside the original shipyard gates keeps that history active. Anniversary periods each mid-August can bring elevated official security presence and institutional visits, and officers coordinate any delegation access through the host institution well ahead of travel rather than assuming standard entry.

Day to day, the picture is calmer. General travel guidance points to the crowded Dlugi Targ and Motlawa riverfront, busiest during cruise-ship arrivals, as the main pickpocketing zone, and old-town nightlife venues carry a reported touting and overcharging problem that vetted, pre-booked venues sidestep. Nowy Port, close to Gdansk's still-active port and shipyard operations, gets standard industrial-district caution after dark, a routine precaution rather than a documented crime hotspot.

Every officer holds a Ministry of Internal Affairs and Administration concession under the Act of 22 August 1997, and unarmed deployment with Polish Police liaison is the norm. Gdansk is also a genuine hub for the amber trade, hosting the Amberif and Ambermart fairs, which bring their own commercial-visitor segment alongside the port, shipping and institutional traffic.
