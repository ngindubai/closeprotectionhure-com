Charleroi's protection work sits astride two very different environments. Gosselies, home to Sonaca's aerospace-structures operations and the A6K digital-innovation hub backed by European Recovery and Resilience Plan funding, is a modern, access-controlled business campus that officers treat on its own terms. The city centre, postal code 6000, is a different picture: Federal Police-sourced reporting identifies it as accounting for a significant share of the wider police zone's vehicle-related theft, which is why standard vehicle-security awareness and vetted transport after dark form the baseline for any detail here.

Charleroi's reputation as one of Belgium's tougher cities is real but not deteriorating. Local reporting on vehicle theft and burglary trends found the pattern broadly stable through 2022-2023, described by the reporting itself as neither better nor worse, a legacy of coal and steel industrial decline and a Wallonia unemployment rate running above the national figure rather than a live crime spike. Belgium's OCAM/CUTA national threat level of 3 out of 4 through early 2026 applies here as everywhere in the country.

Every officer working Charleroi holds the specific CPO qualification under the Act of 2 October 2017, distinct from a general security agent card, and unarmed deployment with Federal Police coordination is the standard model. Brussels South Charleroi Airport, Ryanair's continental base and Belgium's second-busiest airport at a record 10.5 million passengers in 2024, is the entry point for most visits, and officers factor in routine peak-period congestion there and on the roughly 46km run to Brussels.
