Katowice's protection work tracks a genuine economic transition. For most of the twentieth century this was Upper Silesia's coal-mining and heavy-industry capital; today the Katowice Special Economic Zone, established in 1996, has drawn more than PLN 50 billion in investment and over 100,000 jobs, named Europe's best special economic zone in a 2024 fDi Intelligence assessment. Officers assigned to Katowice increasingly cover financial-services and business-process visitors rather than purely industrial delegations, reflecting a business-services sector that grew nearly 70 percent in four years.

That transition is visible at the Katowice International Congress Centre, which hosted COP24, the UN Climate Change Conference, in December 2018 alongside the adjacent Spodek Arena, drawing some 20,000 attendees from 190 countries. Any institutional or corporate event of that scale gets a dedicated advance security survey from an assigned officer rather than a standard venue check. Away from major events, the city is straightforward: crime levels have reportedly fallen in recent years, and the one recurring caution point is the main railway station and bus depot area after dark, a pattern common to most major Polish transit hubs.

Every officer holds a Ministry of Internal Affairs and Administration concession under the Act of 22 August 1997. The one genuine logistics quirk is Katowice Airport itself: at roughly 30 to 35km from the centre, it sits notably farther out than comparable European business destinations, so officers confirm vetted transport well ahead of arrival given there is no closer alternative.
