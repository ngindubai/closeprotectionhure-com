Heat is the thing that catches unprepared visitors out in Cordoba, not crime. AEMET, Spain's state meteorological agency, recorded the country's all-time temperature record here, 47.4C at La Rambla in August 2021, and any officer working this city treats verified air conditioning and shade planning as core to the job, on the same footing as route assessment.

The rest of the picture is familiar Andalusian territory. Officers hold the Escolta Privado qualification under Ley 5/2014, on top of the standard guard licence, and armed work stays confined to narrow, regulated contexts, so protection here runs unarmed. The Mezquita-Catedral ticket queue and the Juderia's pedestrianised lanes carry the city's heaviest tourist footfall, and pickpocketing is the recurring, if unofficial, concern travel-advisory sources flag there. May adds the Patios Festival and the Feria de Cordoba, both genuinely dense old-town crowd events that call for tighter movement discipline across the month.

Cordoba has no working airport of its own worth the name. Officers meet principals at Madrid or Seville and stay with them through the AVE rail leg into Santa Justa, keeping continuous protective coverage rather than a handover partway through the journey.
