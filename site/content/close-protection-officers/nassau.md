    description: "Private security in the Bahamas is governed by the Private Security (Licensing) Act (Chapter 199 of the Laws of the Bahamas) and administered by the Bahamas Security Industry Regulatory Authority (BSIRA). BSIRA licences both security companies and individual security officers; all personnel providing close protection services must hold a valid BSIRA individual licence in addition to their employing firm holding a company licence. Armed close protection requires a specific weapons authority from the Royal Bahamas Police Force (RBPF) beyond the standard BSIRA individual licence; this authorisation is not routinely granted for all commercial assignments and the process should be initiated well in advance. Foreign security personnel wishing to provide close protection services in the Bahamas require a work permit from the Department of Immigration in addition to any BSIRA recognition of their qualifications; the immigration process typically takes two to three weeks from a complete application. Foreign security licences are not directly recognised in the Bahamas, and each assignment involving foreign CPOs therefore requires prior immigration and BSIRA compliance planning. International firms operating in Nassau regularly do so through established Bahamian-licensed partners who hold the necessary corporate and individual licences. BSIRA's contact details and licence verification process should be confirmed before any assignment commences."

Nassau is one of the most naturally stratified close protection environments in the Caribbean: the gated HNWI communities of Lyford Cay and Albany, the managed resort infrastructure of Paradise Island and Baha Mar, and the higher-risk inner-city districts of New Providence exist within a very small geographic area and require fundamentally different protection postures. The HNWI zones are designed environments with perimeter control, private roads, and in-house security that a close protection detail complements rather than replaces. The inner-city districts require the full standard CPO methodology but are typically not on the itinerary of the clients who engage close protection services. More context on Nassau's commercial and social geography is on the [Nassau city page](/cities/nassau/).

The transit risk, particularly the route from the airport through Nassau city to Paradise Island, is the point at which HNWI and corporate principals are most exposed. This is the zone where the managed resort environment ends and the general New Providence road network begins, and it is the route that a determined adversary would most easily profile over multiple visits. CPOs should apply unpredictable routing, time variation, and vehicle selection discipline to this transfer above all other movements in the Nassau brief. Private charter arrivals at NAS, which permit vehicle-side collection and bypass the public arrivals hall, substantially reduce this exposure.

The Bahamas' growing role as a financial services and private wealth hub, alongside its long-established HNWI leisure appeal, means that the Nassau protection brief increasingly combines the requirements of a resort assignment with those of a financial district visit. Clients attending meetings at Bay Street banking institutions in the morning and returning to a Paradise Island villa in the evening have a dual-environment brief that requires planning across both zones.
