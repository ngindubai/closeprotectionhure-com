Immermannstrasse, the heart of Dusseldorf's Japanese business district and often called Little Tokyo locally, generates a steady, predictable stream of corporate ground-transport bookings tied to several hundred Japanese companies with a presence in the city, the largest such community anywhere in Germany. It is one of the more distinctive features of security driving in Dusseldorf, alongside a trade fair calendar that runs to more than fifty international events a year at Messe Dusseldorf.

Licensing is standard German practice: Section 34a of the Gewerbeordnung and the Bewachungsverordnung, with a Sachkundeprufung certificate held under IHK Dusseldorf qualification. The BewachV's Sachkundeprufung scoring system was revised from 1 July 2025, and drivers working higher-risk protective transport hold the certificate under the current standard. Armed driving is rare in Germany and requires specific police authorisation; Dusseldorf deployments run unarmed as a matter of course.

Two dates on the calendar demand the most route planning: any week Messe Dusseldorf is hosting a major fair, when the A3 and A44 approaches to the exhibition halls see significant congestion, and Rosenmontag, the Rose Monday carnival parade, which draws several hundred thousand people through the Altstadt and Konigsallee with phased police road closures. Outside those periods, Dusseldorf's low overall risk profile means route planning is more about efficiency than threat management, though Hauptbahnhof and the Altstadt bar district retain the city's usual concentration of documented petty crime after dark.
