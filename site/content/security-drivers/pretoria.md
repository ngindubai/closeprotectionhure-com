Pretoria has no meaningful international airport, so every security driver assignment here starts at OR Tambo International Airport, the same arrival point used for Johannesburg. From there, the choice is a road transfer of roughly 50 to 60 minutes or the Gautrain rail link, which requires a transfer at Marlboro station before reaching Hatfield in Pretoria, a total journey of around an hour to an hour and a quarter. Neither option is a default; the right one depends on traffic conditions, the principal's schedule, and whether continuous vehicle security matters more than platform-to-platform speed.

Once in the city, drivers work two very different environments. Brooklyn and Waterkloof, the diplomatic quarter that hosts most of Pretoria's foreign embassies, offer straightforward, well-policed routing. The Union Buildings and the surrounding government precinct add a layer that Johannesburg's financial district doesn't: FCDO's 2026 advice notes that protests in South Africa can escalate quickly, and route planning near administrative buildings accounts for that possibility as a matter of course, not an exception.

None of this changes the underlying provincial risk. Gauteng, the province containing both Pretoria and Johannesburg, accounts for more than half the country's carjackings according to SAPS Q3 2025/26 statistics, so closed-window, locked-door discipline at every intersection applies here exactly as it does across the rest of the province. PSIRA registration under the Private Security Industry Regulation Act is the baseline licensing check for any driver working the route.
