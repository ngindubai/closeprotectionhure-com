Getting into Chiang Mai is one of the more straightforward legs on our Southeast Asia network. Chiang Mai International Airport (CNX) sits close to the city, and a private transfer to the Old City typically takes 15 to 20 minutes under normal traffic, a short run by regional standards. The harder planning starts once a driver is working inside the city itself.

The Old City's moat district is a tight grid of narrow, largely one-way lanes, and during the Sunday Walking Street market those lanes fill with pedestrians. Drivers stage pickups on the edge of the district rather than trying to push through market crowds. Routes out to Doi Suthep and other hillside spots are a different challenge again: steep, winding roads that carry a materially higher accident risk than the flat city centre, feeding into a national picture where Thailand's road death rate, 25.4 per 100,000 people according to the WHO, sits among the highest recorded anywhere.

Burning season adds a seasonal layer to that. From roughly mid-February to April, agricultural burning across the region can drive PM2.5 well past WHO guidance; a 188 microgram-per-cubic-metre reading on 29 March 2026, cited in reporting on that year's crisis, sat at more than four times the guideline. On the worst days this affects visibility as well as air quality, so drivers factor both into journey timing and cabin ventilation settings.

Licensing runs through Thailand's Security Guard Business Act, with drivers taking on protective work holding the relevant Royal Thai Police personnel licence.
