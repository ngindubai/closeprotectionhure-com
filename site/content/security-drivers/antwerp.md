A driver collecting a diamond trade principal near Antwerpen-Centraal is working under a second layer of security beyond standard Belgian licensing: the Diamond District runs its own access-control regime, jointly managed by the Antwerp World Diamond Centre's security office and the Antwerp Local Police diamond department, and visitors need advance arrangement through a host firm before they ever reach a trading building.

The underlying legal framework is the same across Belgium: the Loi du 2 octobre 2017 reglementant la securite privee et particuliere, an SPF Interieur agrement for the employing firm, and an SPF-issued identification badge for the individual driver. Armed work needs specific Ministry of Interior authorisation and is not standard commercial practice, so Antwerp deployments run unarmed.

The Port of Antwerp-Bruges, home to Europe's largest integrated chemical cluster, is Antwerp's other major driver of corporate ground transport, and Belgian federal authorities have repeatedly identified the same port as Europe's leading entry point for cocaine trafficking. FCDO Belgium travel advice (2026) is direct about this: violent organised crime incidents connected to the trade overwhelmingly affect those involved in it rather than corporate visitors, but the wider security posture, including a national OCAM/CUTA threat level that sat at 3 out of 4 through early 2026, reflects that environment. Day to day, the more routine concern is pickpocketing around Antwerpen-Centraal station, De Keyserlei, and Pelikaanstraat, standard for any busy European transport hub.
