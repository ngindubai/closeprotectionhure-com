Is a security driver enough for a European Parliament visit to Strasbourg, or does the city call for something more? For most delegates and corporate representatives attending routine institutional business, a vetted driver with current CNAPS registration and institutional-quarter familiarity is the standard baseline. For senior principals, the calculation shifts, and it shifts because of a specific, documented event rather than abstract risk scoring.

On 11 December 2018, a gunman attacked the Strasbourg Christmas Market, killing five people and wounding eleven before being killed by police two days later after a manhunt involving around 700 officers. That history, combined with France's Vigipirate Urgence Attentat level and Strasbourg's role hosting the European Parliament and Council of Europe, gives the city a genuine institutional target profile that most French regional centres simply do not carry. It is part of why Strasbourg sits at low-moderate rather than low risk in our city assessments.

Day to day, the operational picture is more familiar: CNAPS-authorised drivers, executive vehicles suited to the Petite France canal quarter and the wider Neustadt district, and seasonal planning for the roughly two million visitors the Christkindelsmarik market draws to the Grande Ile each December. Neuhof and Hautepierre, both carrying documented elevated crime, are excluded from any principal's route as a matter of course.
