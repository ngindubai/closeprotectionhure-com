Moscow presents one of the most complex security driver operating environments of any major global city. The FCDO's against-all-travel advisory, the risk of arbitrary detention, sanctions-driven operational constraints, and a high-risk criminal environment for foreign nationals require that any security driver engagement in Moscow be planned as part of a comprehensive risk management framework rather than as a standalone logistics arrangement.

## Regulatory clarity in a high-risk environment

The CHOP licensing system under Federal Law 2487-1 provides a formal regulatory structure that operators in Moscow must work within. Rosgvardia licensing is verifiable and provides a baseline of accountability. For foreign organisations, the challenge is not identifying the regulatory framework but conducting reliable due diligence on operators within it, given the limitations on independent verification that apply under current Russia conditions. Engaging operators with established track records and references from comparable foreign clients is the practical mitigation.

## Operational planning for Moscow movements

Effective security driver planning for Moscow requires pre-movement route reconnaissance, contingency routing for the city's severe traffic conditions (particularly on the MKAD ring and the radial arterials into central Moscow), and active communication protocols between the driver, an operations controller, and the principal's organisation. The city's size, the unpredictability of peak-hour travel times, and the current political environment all argue for conservative time planning with substantial buffers for all movements.
