Tokyo's security driver market is defined by the inverse of most other high-demand markets: the ambient personal security risk for foreign executives is negligible, but the operational complexity of driving for senior executives in Tokyo is among the highest of any major business city. The expressway network requires real-time traffic monitoring to navigate effectively; Japanese language competency is genuinely operationally necessary; the Tokyo hotel and corporate venue environment has specific vehicle access constraints; and the dual licensing requirement (second-class driving licence plus Security Business Law registration) sets a high professional standard.

## Security Business Law licensing

The Security Business Law (Keibigyo Ho, 1972, as amended) requires security companies to register with the National Public Safety Commission. Drivers performing security functions operate under their employing company's registration. The second-class driving licence (Daini-shu Menkyo) is required for all commercial passenger transport in Japan. Both are confirmable; operating companies should provide both licence details before engagement.

## Haneda versus Narita as the Tokyo arrival airport

The choice between Haneda and Narita has a direct operational consequence for transfer quality. Haneda's proximity to central Tokyo (20 km, 30 to 50 minutes) makes it materially preferable for senior executive arrivals. Narita's 60 km distance creates transfer times that are sensitive to expressway congestion in ways that Haneda transfers are not. Where the principal has a choice of Tokyo arrival airport, the security driver recommendation is Haneda.
