Mercedes-Benz Group AG and Porsche AG both keep their headquarters inside Stuttgart's city limits, at Untertürkheim and Zuffenhausen, and that single fact shapes more of the city's security driver work than any crime statistic does. Formal site accreditation, vehicle checks, and defined approach routes apply at both addresses, and a driver arranging an executive transfer to either site is really coordinating with the client company's own industrial security procedures as much as running a route.

Licensing follows the standard German pattern: Section 34a Gewerbeordnung, Bewachungsverordnung, registration through the Stuttgart Ordnungsamt, and IHK Region Stuttgart qualification, with a Sachkundeprüfung certificate for higher-risk protective work. A private Waffenschein is difficult to obtain, so armed security driving is not the commercial norm in Stuttgart. The BfV's annual reporting on industrial espionage risk in Germany's automotive sector is a real, named consideration for automotive executives, and it shapes device and meeting security briefings as much as physical transport planning.

Day to day, Stuttgart is a genuinely low-risk city. Stuttgart Hauptbahnhof's long-running Stuttgart 21 construction project is the most consistent source of pickpocketing opportunity, and Messe Stuttgart's exhibition calendar, sitting directly beside the airport, is the most consistent source of transfer-timing pressure. Killesberg and Degerloch offer straightforward, low-crime accommodation options for extended assignments.
