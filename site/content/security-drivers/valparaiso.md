Valparaiso doesn't have a working international airport, so every security-driver assignment here starts with the same leg: Santiago's Arturo Merino Benitez International Airport, followed by an overland transfer of roughly 1.5 to 2 hours depending on traffic and route. Planning that journey properly, rather than treating Valparaiso as a standalone arrival point, is the first job.

Once inside the city, the challenge shifts from distance to terrain. The cerros that define Valparaiso's UNESCO-listed character are served by narrow, steep streets, some pedestrian-only stairways, and a handful of surviving heritage funicular elevators, so drivers confirm vehicle access to a specific address before arrival rather than assuming a standard saloon will reach the door. Add Chile's documented seismic history, including major earthquakes affecting Valparaiso directly in 1906 and 1985, and a confirmed alternative route off any hillside address becomes a genuine operational consideration, not a formality.

FCDO Chile travel advice (2026) records muggings occurring in Valparaiso, sometimes in daylight, more frequently in the port district and lower old town than in the well-visited heritage hills. Large-scale protests are also documented here, at times connected to sessions of Chile's National Congress, which has sat in Valparaiso rather than Santiago since 1990. Drivers pad journey times accordingly around known sitting dates or demonstration patterns.
