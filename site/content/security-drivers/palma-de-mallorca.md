A superyacht owner clearing customs at STP Shipyard Palma expects a driver already positioned at the quay, not one still finding a parking space. That expectation shapes much of what security driving looks like in Palma de Mallorca: an island market built around marina arrivals, a single airport, and a compact but topographically varied road network running from the flat Bay of Palma coast road up into the Serra de Tramuntana foothills.

Licensing sits on two separate tracks. The driver's personal security function falls under Spain's Ley de Seguridad Privada 5/2014, administered through SES registration and an individual TIP from the Direccion General de la Policia. The vehicle-hire function is a distinct Balearic VTC authorisation, a category the regional government has tightened in recent years as part of wider tourism-pressure measures. A provider operating in Palma without both elements in place is not operating within the law, whatever the vehicle looks like.

Route familiarity matters more here than the island's low overall risk level suggests. The FCDO's 2024 travel advice documents organised distraction-theft activity around the cruise terminal and the cathedral and Born tourist core, both places a driver may be asked to wait close to during a principal's day. Recurring anti-tourism demonstrations since 2024 add a second, less predictable variable: road closures that can appear in central Palma with little warning. Add the switchback roads toward Son Vida, Valldemossa, and Deia, and route planning becomes a genuine skill rather than a formality.
