Operational detail for Cairo

Egyptian Private Security Regulatory Framework

Private security in Egypt is regulated by the Ministry of Interior, which licenses security companies through the Central Administration for Private Security. Security companies must obtain operating permits from the Ministry of Interior; individual security personnel undergo background screening and receive authorisation from the relevant security authority. Egypt's private security sector operates under a framework that integrates closely with the Ministry of Interior's broader security architecture, and licensed private security drivers work within parameters established by the relevant governorate security directorate. Clients operating in Cairo should confirm the operating company's Ministry of Interior licence and the individual driver's authorisation documentation before engagement, particularly for assignments involving government premises or diplomatic principals.

Terrorism Awareness and FCDO Assessment

The FCDO advises that terrorists are likely to carry out attacks in Egypt, and that the threat is highest in North Sinai. Cairo has experienced attacks on Coptic churches and security force targets; in 2024 and 2023, FCDO-documented shootings of Israeli-connected individuals occurred in Alexandria. Cairo's principal terrorism risk for corporate principals centres on crowded locations, tourist infrastructure, and sites associated with Western or Israeli connections, rather than routine corporate district movement. Heightened security is maintained at tourist sites, Coptic churches, and major government buildings. Security drivers plan routes to avoid prolonged static exposure near high-profile target categories and maintain awareness of FCDO updates on the current threat level.

Road Safety and Cairo Traffic Conditions

Cairo road conditions are among the most hazardous in the region. FCDO Egypt travel advice (2024) documents aggressive driving, poorly maintained road surfaces, and minimal traffic law enforcement as the primary road safety risks for foreign visitors. The city's extensive ring road network, including the Cairo Ring Road, 6th of October Bridge, and 15th of May Bridge, provides the principal high-speed arterial links between districts; individual road safety on these routes requires alert, defensive driving. Cairo's peak traffic periods see extreme congestion that extends security driver journey times significantly. Security drivers with current Cairo route knowledge reduce journey-time risk through departure timing and alternative corridor selection.

Principal Districts and Airport Transfer Coverage

Cairo security driver operations cover the principal corporate and residential districts: Maadi (the primary expatriate and corporate residential district, home to most multinational company offices and many embassies), Zamalek (the Nile island district with a concentration of embassy residences and premium hotels), the Downtown and Garden City embassy belt, and New Cairo (5th Settlement, with newer corporate campuses and residential compounds). Cairo International Airport (CAI) is 22 kilometres northeast of the city centre. Airport transfers are planned with sufficient time buffer to account for CAI terminal approach congestion and peak-hour Ring Road delays. The Fairmont Nile City, Four Seasons Nile Plaza, and Kempinski Nile Hotel are the principal luxury venues for corporate arrivals.

State Surveillance and Communications Security

Egypt's security services maintain extensive domestic surveillance capability. FCDO Egypt travel advice (2024) and UK government guidance document the risk of detention for social media posts critical of the Egyptian government, and advise that photography near military installations, government buildings, the Suez Canal, and even from mobile phones in sensitive areas is prohibited by Egyptian law. For corporate and government principals visiting Cairo with sensitivity around their business activities, security drivers are briefed on communications security protocols relevant to the Cairo environment: the avoidance of sensitive conversations in vehicles, the handling of device security in hotel environments, and awareness of surveillance indicators in high-profile meeting venue approaches.
