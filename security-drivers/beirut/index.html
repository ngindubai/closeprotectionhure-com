What this covers

Operational detail for Beirut

Lebanon Ministry of Interior Licensing

Security drivers and close protection operators in Lebanon must hold Ministry of Interior operational authorisation. The Lebanese private security regulatory framework is less standardised than Western licensing systems; diligence on operator registration and driver vetting documentation is essential before any Beirut engagement. Our Beirut partners hold Ministry of Interior registration; all driver documentation is provided to clients before the engagement commences.

Rafic Hariri International Airport Transfer Routing

Rafic Hariri International Airport (BEY) is located approximately 8 kilometres south of central Beirut, directly adjacent to the Dahieh southern suburbs, which are under Hezbollah territorial control. The airport exit requires specific routing to avoid the Dahieh district on the journey north to Hamra, Downtown Solidere, and Achrafieh. Security drivers operating from BEY are briefed on the current safe routing corridors and avoid the Dahieh approaches on every transfer. Journey time to the hotel zone under normal conditions is 20 to 35 minutes; checkpoint activity can extend this.

Zone Avoidance Route Management

Beirut's security driver route planning is built around explicit zone avoidance: the Dahieh southern suburbs (Hezbollah-controlled), the areas adjacent to the Palestinian refugee camps (Sabra, Shatila, Bourj el-Barajneh), and the southern Lebanon highway approaches. East-west cross-city transfers use the Hamra-Ashrafieh corridor or the Corniche coastal route depending on real-time traffic and checkpoint conditions. Route assessment is updated before each principal movement given Beirut's fluid security environment.

Vehicle Security and Hardening

Beirut's critical-risk environment requires specific vehicle selection and hardening beyond the standard Gulf or European security driver specification. Vehicles are pre-checked before every movement; parking at unsecured locations for extended periods is avoided; and alternate vehicles are available for the engagement. The economic collapse has increased the prevalence of armed robbery targeting vehicles; window-up and door-locked management applies in all districts, not only in historically higher-risk areas.

Intra-City Transfer Management

Beirut's road network was not fully repaired following the 2020 port explosion, and certain routes through the Mar Mikhael and port district remain in degraded condition. The city's traffic management is further complicated by frequent power cuts that disable traffic lights. Security driver route planning for Beirut incorporates real-time assessment of alternative routes, allows generous time for cross-city transfers, and does not rely on traffic signal infrastructure that is routinely non-functional.

Medical and Emergency Transfer Protocol

Given Lebanon's degraded ambulance system and reduced hospital capacity following the economic collapse, security drivers in Beirut are briefed on the direct transfer route to the American University of Beirut Medical Centre (AUBMC) and Hotel-Dieu de France as primary medical facilities. Emergency transfer capability without reliance on ambulance response is a distinct requirement of the Beirut security driver brief, reflecting the specific infrastructure environment.
