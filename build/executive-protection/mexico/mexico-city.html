Security landscape

Mexico City security overview for corporate principals

Mexico City carries an overall risk rating of 4/5, with crime at 4/5 and kidnapping at 4/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; OSAC). The US State Department rates Mexico at Level 2 (increased caution). OSAC classifies the threat environment as CRITICAL. For corporate principals, the risk profile is distinct from Mexico's cartel warfare in the north. Here, threats are urban, targeted, and designed to exploit routine patterns.

Express kidnapping is the primary concern for executives. Criminals force victims into vehicles, often disguised as licensed taxis, and spend hours moving between ATMs draining accounts. Virtual kidnapping runs alongside it: phone calls claiming a colleague or family member has been taken, demanding immediate wire transfers. These scams specifically target foreigners and corporate visitors who are likely to pay quickly rather than verify (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). For a delegation of four or five principals arriving at AICM without a security plan, the airport taxi rank is where exposure begins.

Meeting security in CDMX is complicated by the city's protest culture. Teacher union demonstrations, political rallies, and Ayotzinapa marches regularly close Paseo de la Reforma and the Zocalo without warning. A delegation moving between venues in Polanco and Santa Fe can find itself trapped on blocked arterial routes, static in traffic, in a vehicle that is identifiably corporate. That is exactly the kind of predictable exposure that EP advance work eliminates.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup adds pressure. Mexico City is a host venue. The surge in foreign visitors creates a larger target pool for express kidnapping, taxi fraud, and street-level crime. Corporate travel during match periods requires earlier planning and larger teams (OSAC).

Regulations

Security regulations in Mexico

Private security in Mexico falls under dual regulation: the federal Direccion General de Seguridad Privada and state-level licensing authorities. Companies must hold both federal and state licences under the Ley Federal de Seguridad Privada (2006). Firearms control sits with SEDENA (Ministry of Defence), which operates the only legal firearms outlet in the country, located in Mexico City. Licensed security firms apply for weapons permits through a military vetting process with ongoing compliance checks.

Foreign security operators cannot carry weapons in Mexico. Full stop. Mexican military authorisation for foreign nationals is not practically obtainable. Direct foreign operation is prohibited under federal law. International firms must establish a Mexican legal entity or partner with a locally licensed company. Consulting and advisory roles are the only workable entry point. All EP personnel require background checks, psychometric evaluation, drug screening, and SEDENA-certified training (Ley Federal de Seguridad Privada, 2006; SEDENA).

Traveller guidance

Security guidance for corporate visitors to Mexico City

Concentrate activity in colonias with established security infrastructure. Polanco is the embassy district and the default for corporate hotels. Condesa and Roma Norte are manageable in daylight hours. Santa Fe is a gated business district with controlled access. Lomas de Chapultepec rounds out the safer operating area. Outside these zones, the threat picture changes quickly (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Transport discipline is not optional. The FCDO warns that unlicensed taxis in Mexico City are directly used for robbery and kidnapping. At AICM, use only the official taxi desk inside the arrivals hall, or better still, a pre-booked vehicle through your EP team. Ride-hailing apps reduce exposure but do not eliminate it. For corporate delegations, all ground transport should run through the security detail.

Tepito is gang-controlled territory. Do not enter under any circumstances. Doctores after dark, Iztapalapa, and Ecatepec are high-threat areas that should be avoided without a security detail. The metro system is a pickpocketing corridor, particularly Lines 1, 2, and 3, and the threat profile escalates sharply after 10pm.

Drink spiking is documented in Zona Rosa bars and nightlife venues. Victims are drugged and robbed, sometimes assaulted (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). Virtual kidnapping phone scams target foreign numbers: callers claim to hold a colleague and demand immediate payment. Standing protocol is to not engage. Notify your EP team leader and verify through direct contact with the named person.
