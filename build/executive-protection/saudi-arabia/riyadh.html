Security landscape

Riyadh security overview

Riyadh carries a low overall risk rating of 2/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; US State Dept Level 2; OSAC Low). Crime sits at 2/5. Kidnapping at 1/5. Civil unrest at 2/5. Terrorism is the exception at 3/5, driven entirely by the Houthi campaign from Yemen. For executive protection planning, that asymmetry matters: the daily operational environment is calm, but the background threat is ballistic rather than criminal.

Street crime barely registers. Petty theft is rare. Fraud and road rage are the most commonly reported incidents. Islamic law enforcement acts as an effective deterrent at a level that surprises most Western visitors. Riyadh does not have the pickpocketing corridors, express kidnapping routes, or nightlife crime patterns that dominate EP planning in Latin American or African capitals.

The Houthi threat is concrete. Long-range ballistic missiles and explosive drones have been launched at Riyadh from Yemen. Saudi air defences have intercepted projectiles over the city. Targets are military installations and oil infrastructure, not commercial districts. But the proximity of defence sites to urban areas means the risk is not contained to remote locations. IS remnants maintain a residual capacity, historically targeting government buildings and critical infrastructure (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; US State Dept advisory).

Road conditions are the primary daily risk for visiting executives. Saudi Arabia's road fatality rate is among the highest in the Middle East. Riyadh's highways see routine speeds above 140km/h, aggressive lane changes, and tailgating. For EP teams, transport planning is where the real operational hours are spent, not counter-surveillance.

What to expect from executive protection in Riyadh

EP in Riyadh starts with cultural calibration. Saudi business culture operates on hierarchies, personal relationships, and protocol that an EP team unfamiliar with the environment will disrupt. Your team leader briefs your principals on dress code, greeting conventions, prayer time schedules, and the behavioural boundaries that apply under Saudi law. This is not cultural sensitivity training for its own sake. A misstep in a Saudi government meeting or royal-adjacent engagement creates operational problems that no amount of physical security can fix.

On the ground, your EP team manages movement between Riyadh's key commercial zones: KAFD, the Diplomatic Quarter, Al Olaya, and venue locations for conferences or bilateral meetings. Advance work is lighter than in high-crime cities because the street-level threat is low. The focus shifts to route planning (construction zones from Vision 2030 projects change road access weekly), scheduling around prayer times, and managing the transition between air-conditioned environments and outdoor exposure in temperatures that can exceed 50C in summer months.

Unarmed EP is the standard. Armed components require MOI authorisation and are reserved for principals whose profile or engagement type warrants it. For most corporate delegations visiting Riyadh, the correct configuration is a team leader, EP operatives matched to principal count, and a secure transport package. The threat model is vehicle-based risk and cultural/legal exposure, not armed confrontation.

Regulations

Security regulations in Saudi Arabia

Private security operates under the Ministry of Interior (MOI). The General Authority for Private Security is expanding its regulatory scope under Vision 2030 as the sector grows to meet new demand from tourism, entertainment, and international business. Companies require an MOI licence with capital, staffing, and facility thresholds (Ministry of Interior; Vision 2030 framework).

Firearms are prohibited for civilians. Licensed companies can obtain weapons permits through the MOI for critical infrastructure and high-profile engagements, but most EP in Riyadh operates unarmed. Foreign security companies cannot operate independently. A Saudi partner holding at least 51% ownership is required. Saudization quotas mandate increasing Saudi national employment across the security sector. Foreign personnel require specific visa arrangements under the kafala sponsorship system, with MOI background checks as standard (Ministry of Interior).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for executives visiting Riyadh

Road transport is where visiting executives face the highest daily risk. Riyadh's highway network operates at speeds and with driving behaviour that would be considered reckless in Europe. Saudi Arabia's road fatality rate reflects this. Use a professional driver for all ground movements. Do not rely on ride-hailing apps for unmonitored travel, particularly on inter-city routes (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Respect the legal environment. Alcohol is illegal. Drug offences carry the death penalty. Social media posts critical of the Saudi government, royal family, or Islam can result in arrest, even if the posts were made before entering the country. During Ramadan, eating, drinking, and smoking in public during daylight hours is prohibited for everyone, including non-Muslims. These are enforced laws, not cultural suggestions (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

KAFD, the Diplomatic Quarter, and Al Olaya are the primary business zones with strong security infrastructure. Most of Riyadh is safe for business visitors. Industrial areas on the outskirts carry slightly elevated risk after dark, but incidents remain uncommon. The Riyadh Metro (opened 2024) provides a controlled transport alternative for some routes.

Be aware of employment dispute risks. Foreign nationals involved in contractual disagreements have been subject to travel bans preventing departure from Saudi Arabia for extended periods. If your visit involves negotiations, joint ventures, or employment matters, legal counsel should review the risk before arrival (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).
