Security landscape

Lagos security overview

Lagos sits at the intersection of extreme wealth and extreme poverty. That combination, on a scale of 20 million people, creates a security environment where corporate visitors are conspicuous and vulnerable. The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to much of Nigeria, and specifically flags Lagos for armed robbery, carjacking, and kidnapping (FCDO Nigeria, April 2026).

The kidnapping threat has evolved. Express kidnapping, where victims are driven to ATMs and forced to withdraw cash, is increasing across the Lekki corridor and Third Mainland Bridge approaches. Traditional ransom kidnapping targets HNWIs and expatriates. Maritime kidnapping in the Gulf of Guinea affects commercial shipping but also extends to Lagos waterways. Nigeria has one of the highest kidnapping rates globally.

Terrorism risk comes from ISWA (Islamic State West Africa), Boko Haram/JAS, and ISWAP. While the primary conflict zones are in northeastern Nigeria, intelligence warnings for soft-target attacks in Lagos persist. Hotels, transport hubs, and crowded markets are identified target types. Security forces maintain checkpoints throughout the city.

Civil unrest is triggered by economic protests, fuel subsidy disputes, and the ongoing EndSARS movement. Demonstrations can escalate to violence with security forces sometimes responding with lethal force. Communication blackouts are imposed during significant unrest. For an EP team, this means monitoring local intelligence feeds and having contingency extraction plans in place.

Regulations

Security regulations in Nigeria

The NSCDC (Nigeria Security and Civil Defence Corps) regulates private security operations. All security companies must register with NSCDC and the Corporate Affairs Commission, with annual licensing renewals. Licensed firms can apply for firearms permits. Armoured vehicles require specific permits and are legal for licensed operations (NSCDC regulations, April 2026).

Foreign security companies must partner with Nigerian-registered firms. Direct foreign operation is not permitted without local incorporation. Foreign nationals cannot carry firearms in Nigeria. EP teams from abroad commonly operate in advisory and coordination roles, with locally licensed Nigerian operators providing the armed close protection element on the ground.

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Lagos

Airport arrivals are a known vulnerability. Criminals conduct surveillance at Murtala Muhammed International, identifying targets by luggage quality, clothing, or vehicles. Your EP team should collect you airside or at a pre-arranged secure point. Never exit the terminal and look for transport. Baggage theft in the arrivals area is documented by OSAC.

Accommodation should be within Victoria Island, Ikoyi, or Lekki Phase 1. These areas have established private security networks. Avoid establishing patterns in your movements. If you visit the same office daily, your team should rotate routes and timing. Lagos traffic can extend journey times dramatically, so build buffer time into every schedule.

Do not carry large amounts of cash. Do not display expensive watches, phones, or jewellery in traffic. If confronted at an unauthorized checkpoint, do not resist. Your EP team will know the difference between a police roadblock and a criminal operation, and will advise accordingly.
