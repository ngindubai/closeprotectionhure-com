Security landscape

Nairobi security overview

Nairobi is a city of contradictions. The UN's Africa headquarters sits in Gigiri, surrounded by diplomatic compounds and manicured gardens. A few kilometres east, Eastleigh is one of the most densely packed and poorly policed neighbourhoods on the continent. The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to parts of Kenya, citing terrorism and violent crime (FCDO Kenya advisory, April 2026). The US State Department rates Kenya at Level 2.

The terrorism threat is specific and documented. Al Shabaab attacked the Westgate shopping mall in September 2013, killing 67 people. In January 2019, they hit the DusitD2 hotel complex on Riverside Drive, killing 21. Both targets were popular with foreign nationals. The UK government assesses further attacks as "very likely" (FCDO Kenya, April 2026). Hotels, shopping centres, transport hubs, and government buildings are all identified target categories. For EP teams, this means venue selection is not just about convenience. It is a security decision.

Street crime is the more common daily threat. Mugging, carjacking, armed robbery, and home invasion affect every part of Nairobi, including the wealthier suburbs. Carjacking spikes on Mombasa Road and Thika Road after dark. The CBD becomes unpredictable once offices close. Express kidnapping, where victims are driven between ATMs, operates on a smaller scale than in West Africa but is increasing. Criminal ransom kidnapping targets wealthy Kenyans and expatriates. Al Shabaab has also conducted kidnappings in border regions.

Civil unrest follows a pattern tied to elections, cost-of-living protests, and political tensions. Demonstrations can turn violent quickly, particularly in central Nairobi and informal settlements. Tear gas and live rounds have been used by security forces during recent protests. An EP team working Nairobi monitors multiple intelligence channels and maintains contingency extraction plans at all times.

Regulations

Security regulations in Kenya

The Private Security Regulatory Authority (PSRA), established in 2016, regulates all private security operations in Kenya. Licensed firms apply for group firearms certificates through the PSRA and Kenya Police. The Central Firearms Bureau controls individual licensing under the Firearms Act (Cap 114). All security operators must be registered and carry valid PSRA identification.

Foreign security companies must register locally. Joint ventures with Kenyan-registered firms are the standard operating model. Foreign nationals working in security roles need Class G work permits. Direct foreign armed operations are not permitted. International EP teams typically provide coordination, advance planning, and team leadership, with locally licensed Kenyan operators handling the armed close protection element.

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Nairobi

Jomo Kenyatta International Airport (JKIA) is a known surveillance point. Criminals identify targets by luggage, clothing, and the vehicles that collect them. Your EP team should meet you at a pre-arranged secure point inside the terminal. Do not exit and hail transport. JKIA transfers after dark carry elevated carjacking risk on Mombasa Road, and your driver should be briefed on alternate routes.

Stay within Westlands, Karen, Lavington, Kilimani, or the Gigiri/Village Market area. These neighbourhoods have established security infrastructure and are where most diplomatic and corporate personnel are based. Avoid Eastleigh entirely. The CBD is workable during business hours but becomes unpredictable after dark. Kibera and Mathare are off-limits for unescorted visitors. South B and South C carry elevated risk from late afternoon onward.

Nairobi traffic is severe and unpredictable. A 10-kilometre journey can take over an hour. Build buffer time into every schedule. Do not walk between venues, even short distances. Snatch theft and mugging happen in broad daylight. Your EP team should handle all movement by vehicle with pre-planned routes.
