Security landscape in Johannesburg

Carjacking is the baseline threat. Not a possibility, a probability for anyone moving through the city in a visible vehicle. The SAPS 2024/25 crime statistics recorded over 20,000 carjackings nationally, with Gauteng province accounting for the largest share (SAPS Annual Crime Statistics, 2024/25). Attackers follow targets from OR Tambo International along the M3 and N2, waiting for the vehicle to slow at a traffic light or residential gate. The window between airport and hotel is where most incidents occur.

Smash-and-grab attacks at intersections remain common. Windows down, phone visible, laptop bag on the seat. That is an invitation. Armed robbery at residences, particularly in the northern suburbs, often involves surveillance over several days before execution.

Kidnapping is increasing. What was once primarily a syndicate-on-syndicate problem has shifted. Business executives, foreign nationals, and high-net-worth individuals are now targeted for ransom (OSAC South Africa Country Security Report, 2025). Express kidnappings, where victims are held for hours while ATM withdrawals are made, have become more frequent in Gauteng.

Load shedding introduces a layer of vulnerability that is unique to South Africa. When Eskom cuts power, traffic lights go dark, electric fences drop, CCTV systems fail, and access gates stop working. Criminals know the schedule. They plan around it. Entire suburbs become softer targets during outage windows, and response times for private security companies increase when their control rooms switch to backup power.

Xenophobic violence flares periodically, particularly in informal settlements and CBD areas, creating unpredictable disruptions to movement (FCDO South Africa Travel Advice, April 2026).

What to expect from EP in Johannesburg

Effective executive protection in Joburg starts before your flight lands. Advance teams confirm routes from OR Tambo, identify choke points, check gate access at accommodation, and verify backup power status. Nothing about the airport transfer is left to chance.

Armoured vehicles are standard, not optional. B4 to B6 rated depending on the threat assessment. Drivers are anti-hijack trained. They know which intersections to avoid, when to run a red light safely, and how to execute a J-turn if a vehicle blocks the road ahead. Route variation is daily. The same path to the same office twice in a row is a pattern, and patterns get exploited here.

Counter-surveillance is active throughout the assignment. Joburg criminals conduct their own reconnaissance. A good EP team spots the watcher before the watcher spots the principal. Close protection operatives maintain low-profile positioning in public. Visible security draws attention. Discretion keeps the principal off target lists in the first place.

Medical contingency planning accounts for both trauma and the reality that public hospitals in Gauteng are overwhelmed. Private hospital locations, blood type records, and air evacuation options are confirmed before the first movement.

Regulations and licensing

All private security operators in South Africa must register with PSIRA (Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority). This is law, not a recommendation. Close protection operatives carry firearms on duty under business licences issued to their registered security company through the Firearms Control Act 2000. Individual carry permits are separate from business licences, and PSIRA audits compliance actively.

Foreign security personnel cannot simply arrive and operate. Registration with PSIRA is mandatory. Specialists may enter on critical skills visas, but standard work permits are strictly enforced (Private Security Industry Regulation Act, 2001; Immigration Act, 2002). Arriving with unregistered foreign operatives exposes both the team and the principal to criminal liability. The standard approach: pair your international team with locally registered operators who hold current PSIRA credentials.

Practical safety guidance for Johannesburg

The highest-risk moment is the airport transfer. If you are not being met by a vetted driver in a pre-arranged vehicle, you are exposed. Do not take unmarked taxis from OR Tambo. Share your flight details only with your security team. The M3 and N2 corridors between the airport and Sandton are where follow-from-airport hijackings occur most frequently.

Windows up. Doors locked. At every intersection, every time. Leave a car-length gap to the vehicle ahead so you have room to manoeuvre if someone approaches. Do not stop for minor fender-benders in quiet areas. Drive to the nearest police station or petrol station.

Monitor the Eskom load shedding schedule daily. During outages, avoid travel through areas with non-functioning traffic lights. Confirm that your accommodation has generator backup and that electric fencing has battery failover. Do not walk between buildings during power cuts, even in Sandton.

Use toll roads (Gauteng Freeway) where possible. They are better lit, better monitored, and harder for hijackers to stage ambushes compared to surface routes through residential areas.
