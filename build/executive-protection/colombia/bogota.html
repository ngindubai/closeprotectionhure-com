Security landscape

Bogota security overview

Bogota carries a high overall risk rating (4/5) across crime, kidnapping, and civil unrest. The UK FCDO advises against all but essential travel to parts of Colombia, citing kidnapping, terrorism, and armed groups (FCDO Colombia advisory, April 2026). The US State Department maintains a Level 2 advisory urging increased caution.

Half a century of internal conflict, guerrilla warfare, and cartel violence left permanent marks on the threat landscape. The armed groups have fragmented, not disappeared. The ELN (National Liberation Army) car-bombed a Bogota police academy in January 2019, killing 22 cadets and officers. FARC dissident factions continue to operate. Both retain the capacity to strike inside the capital, even as most fighting concentrates in rural departments.

Street-level threats are what visiting executives encounter most. Scopolamine (burundanga) is a chemical compound that criminals use to incapacitate victims, slipped into drinks or applied through physical contact. Under its effects, victims become compliant and suggestible, emptying bank accounts and handing over valuables with no memory afterwards. The FCDO specifically warns about scopolamine attacks in Colombia (FCDO Colombia advisory, April 2026).

Express kidnapping (the "paseo millonario") is the other defining threat. Victims enter unvetted taxis and are driven between ATMs over several hours, forced to withdraw everything their accounts hold. This is not theoretical. It is the primary reason every international advisory warns against hailing street taxis in Bogota.

Motorcycle-borne crime adds a constant operational factor. Phone snatching, armed robbery at traffic lights, and targeted attacks from pillion passengers are daily occurrences, particularly in congested traffic. Bogota's gridlock makes vehicles stationary targets.

The same conflict that created these threats also created the solution. Colombia's private security sector has operational depth built over decades of real-world experience. That is the foundation EP programmes here are built on.

Regulations

EP regulations in Colombia

Private security in Colombia is governed by the Estatuto de Vigilancia y Seguridad Privada (Decreto 356 de 1994) and regulated by SuperVigilancia (Superintendencia de Vigilancia y Seguridad Privada). All companies providing executive protection must hold a current SuperVigilancia licence covering the escolta (personal protection) category. Weapons are registered and controlled through INDUMIL, the state military industry body. Armed escorts are legal and common.

Foreign security firms cannot operate directly. They must establish a Colombian legal entity or partner with a locally licensed company (SuperVigilancia regulations). Foreign EP advisors can work in consulting and coordination roles while Colombian-licensed operators handle armed, on-the-ground protection. All foreign personnel require work permits through Migracion Colombia. In practice, most international programmes run through a hybrid model: foreign team leader coordinating locally licensed, locally experienced operators.

Traveller guidance

Security tips for executives visiting Bogota

Never hail a taxi from the street. This is the single most repeated piece of advice in every international advisory on Bogota (FCDO Colombia advisory, April 2026; OSAC). Fake taxi drivers are the primary vector for express kidnapping. Use pre-booked vehicles, rideshare apps, or your security driver. No exceptions.

Scopolamine awareness is non-negotiable. Do not accept drinks from strangers, including in upscale bars and restaurants in Zona Rosa. The drug can be applied through physical contact, not only spiked beverages. If someone approaches you with an unusual request, a map, a pamphlet, maintain distance. Your EP operative is trained to manage approach vectors before they develop.

Zona T, Zona Rosa, Usaquen, Chapinero Alto, and Parque 93 are where most corporate travellers and expatriates operate. Hotels, restaurants, and offices concentrate in northern Bogota, and the security picture is significantly better there than in the south (FCDO Colombia advisory, April 2026). La Candelaria draws tourists during the day but the threat changes after dark. El Bronx, Los Martires, and Ciudad Bolivar should not be entered without a security detail. TransMilenio stations are pickpocketing and robbery hotspots, particularly at night.
