Security landscape

Karachi security overview for corporate principals

Karachi carries an overall risk rating of 5/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; US State Dept Level 3; OSAC Critical). Crime, terrorism, and kidnapping each score 5/5. Civil unrest runs at 4/5. Police reliability sits at 2/5. For corporate principals planning EP in Karachi, these are not abstract numbers. They describe a city where mass casualty terrorism, targeted kidnapping, and street crime operate simultaneously across overlapping geographies.

The terrorism threat requires specific attention from EP planners. TTP, TJP, BLA, ISKP, and al-Qaida all maintain operational presence in or near Karachi. Their target selection includes the exact locations that corporate delegations use: airports, hotels adjacent to government buildings, and commercial areas near security force installations. The October 2024 suicide attack near Jinnah International Airport killed four people, including two Chinese nationals working on CPEC-related projects. BLA specifically targets Chinese nationals as part of its campaign against Chinese investment in Balochistan (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). Any delegation from a Chinese-affiliated company operates under an elevated threat that goes beyond the general Karachi baseline.

Kidnapping is the second critical planning factor. Express kidnapping mirrors the Latin American model, while longer-term kidnap for ransom targets anyone perceived as having access to foreign money. British nationals of Pakistani origin are specifically singled out (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). For a corporate delegation that includes British-Pakistani executives, the EP plan needs anti-kidnapping protocols built into every phase: airport arrival, meeting venues, hotel security, and especially evening schedules.

Civil unrest adds an unpredictable layer. Political rallies, sectarian violence, and economic protests block major roads without warning. The government cuts mobile data and internet during sensitive periods, which strips away real-time communication for any team not using satellite phones or offline messaging protocols. Election periods escalate the risk further. EP plans must account for communications blackouts as a realistic scenario, not a theoretical one.

Regulations

Security regulations in Pakistan

Pakistan has no single national licence for private security. Regulation is provincial. Sindh province governs Karachi. Punjab operates under the Punjab Private Security Companies Ordinance (2002), which served as the model for other provinces. KP and Balochistan each maintain separate legislation. A licence valid in Sindh does not automatically cover operations in Punjab or Balochistan. Travel between provinces may require No Objection Certificates, adding a logistical layer that EP planners must factor into any cross-provincial itinerary (Provincial Home Departments).

Firearms licensing goes through district authorities and is more permissive than most countries. Armed security is the standard configuration for corporate and VIP protection. Security personnel require background verification through police and arms training certification. Foreign operators must register locally, comply with provincial regulations, and partner with a licensed Pakistani company. Government authorities routinely assign police escorts for foreign clients, particularly for travel outside major commercial zones (Provincial Home Departments; Pakistan Security Guard Ordinance).

Traveller guidance

Security guidance for corporate visitors to Karachi

Concentrate all activity within DHA and Clifton unless your business requires otherwise. Defence Housing Authority is gated, military-secured, and operates checkpoints at all entry points. Clifton is upscale with its own private security infrastructure. Bahria Town functions as a gated community. Outside these enclaves, the security picture shifts dramatically. Lyari is gang-controlled. Orangi Town is high-threat. Saddar's commercial centre is manageable in daylight but deteriorates after dark (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

All public transport should be avoided entirely. The FCDO explicitly advises against all public transport in Pakistan, including Metro Bus. Railways are documented terrorist targets: the BLA Jaffar Express hijacking in March 2025 killed 64 people. E-taxis and online taxi apps do not guarantee driver vetting. All corporate ground transport should run through your EP team using pre-vetted, armoured vehicles.

Internet and mobile data shutdowns during political unrest are common. If your delegation depends on real-time communications with a home office, ensure your EP team has satellite phone capability or VHF radio backup. Standard mobile networks may go dark without warning during protests, elections, or security operations. Do not assume connectivity.

British-Pakistani executives face a specific kidnapping risk. They are perceived as wealthier and more likely to have access to foreign currency. Do not display expensive personal items, discuss travel plans in public, or share hotel details with anyone outside the immediate delegation. If a ransom demand is received, contact your EP team leader and the British High Commission before responding.

Pakistan has no national security licence. Each province (Sindh, Punjab, KP, Balochistan) regulates separately. A company licensed in Sindh to operate in Karachi is not automatically authorised in Punjab (Lahore, Islamabad) or KP (Peshawar). Cross-provincial operations require coordination between separate regulatory frameworks, and armed personnel may need additional clearances to carry weapons across provincial boundaries. The Pakistan Rangers are a paramilitary force that supplements police in Karachi. They maintain a heavier armed presence than conventional police and operate checkpoints, patrols, and rapid response units throughout the city. Rangers personnel are better equipped and generally better trained than the Karachi police force, which scores 2/5 for reliability. For EP operations, Rangers liaison provides faster response times and a more credible security presence at checkpoints and during movement through sensitive areas. Your EP team maintains pre-established contacts with Rangers officers in the operating areas (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Political strikes (hartals) can paralyse Karachi with no notice. MQM, PPP, and religious parties all call strikes that shut down transport, close businesses, and empty roads. During a hartal, any vehicle that moves becomes visible and potentially a target for stone-throwing or worse. Enforcement can be violent. Your EP team monitors political intelligence and, when a strike is called, advises the principal to remain at a secure location until the strike lifts. If movement is essential, a police escort or Rangers coordination is arranged. Attempting to maintain a normal schedule during an active hartal is not advisable.

Pakistan's mobile networks are unreliable outside major cities, and the government periodically shuts down cellular networks during security operations, religious events, and periods of heightened threat.
