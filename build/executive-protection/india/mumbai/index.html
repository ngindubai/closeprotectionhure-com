Security landscape

Mumbai security overview

The 2008 Mumbai attacks changed everything. Ten Lashkar-e-Taiba operatives struck the Taj Mahal Palace hotel, the Oberoi Trident, CST railway station, and other sites across the city. 166 people died over three days. Hotels used by international business travellers were specifically targeted. That operational reality shapes every EP assignment in Mumbai today (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Terrorism remains the headline threat. The FCDO assesses attacks as "very likely" across India. LeT, Jaish-e-Mohammed, AQIS, and IS-inspired cells all maintain intent. Targets include hotels, commercial centres, transport infrastructure, and places of worship. Post-2008 hotel security improved substantially, but the threat has not diminished (US State Dept Level 2; OSAC crime rating: High).

Day-to-day, the picture is more mixed. Street crime is moderate. Pickpocketing clusters around crowded railway stations. Scams target tourists. The FCDO warns specifically about sexual assault risk, including against foreign nationals. Civil unrest flares regularly: religious tensions, caste disputes, and political bandh (shutdown) protests can close roads and freeze the city's transport network with limited warning.

For corporate visitors, the combination of high terrorism risk, unpredictable civil disruption, and India's challenging transport infrastructure creates a complex operating environment. Professional EP is not a luxury here. It is an operational requirement for organisations with duty-of-care obligations.

Regulations

Security regulations in India

India regulates private security through the Private Security Agencies Regulation Act (PSARA, 2005). Licensing is administered state by state. A firm operating in Maharashtra (Mumbai) needs a separate PSARA licence from one covering Delhi or Karnataka. Multi-state operations require registrations in each jurisdiction.

The Arms Act (1959) makes armed private security extremely rare. Firearms are restricted to government-authorised forces: the Central Industrial Security Force (CISF), state police, and similar bodies. The standard executive protection model across India is unarmed operatives supported by police liaison when armed response is assessed as necessary.

Foreign operators face additional barriers. Direct operation is not permitted. A foreign firm must establish an Indian subsidiary or enter a joint venture, then register under PSARA. FDI caps apply to the security sector. All personnel undergo police-verified background checks. India's private security workforce exceeds 9 million people, making it the largest in the world, but training standards and professionalism vary widely (PSARA, 2005; Arms Act, 1959).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Mumbai

Book airport transfers in advance. Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj International (BOM) has licensed taxi counters, but pre-arranged secure transport eliminates the risk of unlicensed vehicles. Do not use Mumbai's local trains. They carry 7.5 million passengers daily in carriages designed for far fewer. People fall from moving trains. This is not a transport option for security-conscious travellers.

Road safety in India is a genuine concern. The country records one of the highest road accident death rates globally. Mumbai's traffic includes potholes, erratic lane changes, pedestrians crossing highways, and the occasional cow. A driver with local knowledge and defensive training is not optional for business travel.

Colaba and Nariman Point operate under heightened security since 2008. Bandra-Kurla Complex is a modern business district with controlled access. Powai and Juhu are relatively secure residential areas. After dark, avoid Dharavi, Kamathipura, and unfamiliar areas. The FCDO's sexual assault warning applies particularly to women travelling alone in less-secured neighbourhoods (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Watch for bandh announcements. Political and communal tensions trigger city-wide shutdowns with minimal notice. Your EP team should maintain a rolling 48-hour political risk assessment and have contingency plans ready for schedule disruptions.
