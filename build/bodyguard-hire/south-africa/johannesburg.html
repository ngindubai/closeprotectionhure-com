Security landscape

Johannesburg security overview

Carjacking defines the threat picture in Johannesburg. Not as a secondary risk. As the primary one. South Africa recorded over 20,000 carjackings in the 2022/23 reporting year (South African Police Service annual crime statistics, 2023), and Gauteng province, where Johannesburg sits, accounts for the largest share. Criminals target vehicles at traffic lights, in driveways, at shopping centre parking areas, and on the approach roads from OR Tambo International Airport. The UK FCDO specifically warns about follow-from-airport robberies along the M3 and N2 corridors near OR Tambo (FCDO South Africa travel advice, April 2026).

Armed robbery is the second constant. Home invasions in the northern suburbs. Smash-and-grab at intersections. ATM muggings across the city. Hillbrow and Joubert Park are extremely dangerous on foot and after dark. The CBD empties after business hours and becomes high risk. Even Sandton, the financial district where most international travellers stay, sees incidents on approach roads once the sun goes down.

Kidnapping is increasing. Criminal ransom kidnapping, express kidnapping, and carjack-kidnapping (where a hijacked vehicle leads to a secondary kidnapping) are all documented and rising. Targets are frequently followed from airports. OSAC rates Johannesburg's overall crime environment as critical (OSAC South Africa report, 2025).

Then there is load shedding. When Eskom cuts power, which happens without reliable advance schedules, electric fences go dead, security gates stop working, traffic lights go dark, and CCTV systems drop offline. Criminals know the schedules better than the residents. Load shedding creates a predictable window of increased vulnerability across the entire city, and opportunistic crime spikes every time the grid drops.

Regulations

Security regulations in South Africa

South Africa's private security industry is regulated by PSIRA (Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority). All security operators, companies, and individual close protection officers must register with PSIRA. The Firearms Control Act 2000 governs weapons licensing. Security companies obtain business firearms licences, and registered CP operatives can carry firearms on duty under these licences. Armoured vehicles are legal and widespread (PSIRA registration requirements, current as of April 2026).

Foreign security personnel must register with PSIRA before operating on South African soil. A Critical Skills visa is available for specialist executive protection work. Foreign nationals can be employed by a South African security company under company authorization, but work permits are strictly enforced. If you are bringing an international security team, they will need to coordinate with a PSIRA-registered local firm.

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Johannesburg

The airport corridor is your first vulnerability. Criminals station spotters at OR Tambo arrivals to identify high-value targets, then follow them along the M3 or N2 towards Sandton. Pre-arrange your collection. Your driver or CPO should meet you inside the terminal or at a pre-designated pickup point. Never accept unsolicited transport offers.

Keep vehicle windows up and doors locked at all times. At traffic lights, leave enough space between your vehicle and the one ahead to pull out if needed. Smash-and-grab attacks happen in seconds. Do not place bags, phones, or laptops on passenger seats where they are visible from outside.

Track load shedding schedules through your operator or apps like EskomSePush. When power drops, stay indoors if possible. Electric fences, automated gates, and alarm systems will be offline. Traffic lights will be out, creating confusion at intersections that criminals exploit. Your CPO will factor load shedding windows into route planning and timing.
