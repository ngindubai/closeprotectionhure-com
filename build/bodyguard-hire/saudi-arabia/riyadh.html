Security landscape

Riyadh security overview

Riyadh carries a low overall risk rating of 2/5, with crime at 2/5, terrorism at 3/5, kidnapping at 1/5, and civil unrest at 2/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; US State Dept Level 2; OSAC Low). That terrorism score is the outlier, and it deserves attention. The rest of the numbers tell a story of a city where strict Islamic law enforcement functions as the primary crime deterrent.

Petty crime is rare. Fraud and road rage incidents are the most commonly reported offences. There are very few genuine hotspots within the city itself. Riyadh's crime profile is closer to Singapore or Tokyo than to any other city on our Middle East roster. The penalties for theft, assault, and drug offences under Saudi law are severe enough to suppress opportunistic crime at a level most Western visitors find unfamiliar.

Terrorism is the category that breaks the pattern. Houthi rebels in Yemen have launched long-range ballistic missiles and explosive drones at Riyadh on multiple occasions. These are not theoretical threats. Saudi air defences have intercepted projectiles over the capital. Islamic State remnants also maintain a residual presence, with historical targeting of military installations, government buildings, and oil infrastructure (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; US State Dept advisory). The targets are not typically civilian, but the proximity of military sites to urban areas means collateral risk exists.

Civil unrest is effectively non-existent. Demonstrations are illegal in Saudi Arabia. Security forces maintain strict control, and dissent is suppressed. Vision 2030 has introduced social reforms, but political expression remains tightly restricted. You will not encounter protests blocking roads or sudden crowd surges in Riyadh.

Police reliability scores 4/5. The force is well-resourced, response times are good, and corruption is low by regional standards. The catch: Saudi law enforcement applies Islamic law with zero tolerance. Drug offences carry the death penalty. Alcohol is prohibited. Dress codes are enforced. Social media posts critical of the government or religion can result in arrest. Foreign workers face travel bans over employment disputes, sometimes lasting years (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Regulations

Security regulations in Saudi Arabia

Private security in Saudi Arabia falls under the Ministry of Interior (MOI), with the General Authority for Private Security expanding its regulatory role as part of Vision 2030 reforms. Companies must obtain an MOI licence, which carries capital, staffing, and facility requirements. The sector is growing rapidly as Saudi Arabia opens to tourism, mega-events, and international business at a scale not seen before (Ministry of Interior; Vision 2030 framework).

Firearms are prohibited for civilians. Licensed companies can apply for weapons permits through the MOI, but armed security is restricted to critical infrastructure protection and high-profile client engagements. Most executive protection in Riyadh operates unarmed. Foreign security operators cannot work independently. A Saudi partner with minimum 51% local ownership is required. Saudization quotas mandate increasing proportions of Saudi nationals in security roles. Foreign personnel require specific visa arrangements under the kafala sponsorship system, with background checks through the MOI (Ministry of Interior).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Riyadh

Road safety is the biggest daily risk. Riyadh's highways see speeds that would be illegal in most countries. Aggressive lane changing, tailgating, and high-speed driving are standard. The road fatality rate in Saudi Arabia is among the highest in the Middle East (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). Do not drive yourself unless you are experienced with local conditions. Use a professional driver.

Cultural and legal compliance is not optional. Alcohol is illegal. Drug offences carry the death penalty. Dress conservatively, particularly women, though Vision 2030 reforms have relaxed enforcement in some areas. Public criticism of the royal family, government, or Islam on social media can result in arrest and prosecution. These are not theoretical risks. Foreign nationals have been detained for social media posts made before entering the country (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

The Diplomatic Quarter and KAFD are the safest areas for business visitors. Al Olaya, Riyadh's main commercial strip, has good security infrastructure. Most of the city is relatively safe at street level. Industrial areas on the outskirts present slightly elevated risk after dark, but incidents are uncommon. The Riyadh Metro opened in 2024 and offers a safer alternative to road transport for some routes.

Employment disputes can trigger travel bans. Foreign workers, including contractors, have been prevented from leaving Saudi Arabia for months or years over unresolved business disagreements. If your visit involves contractual negotiations or employment matters, seek legal advice before arrival (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).
