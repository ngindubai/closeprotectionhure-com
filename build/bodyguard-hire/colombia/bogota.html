Security landscape

Bogota security overview

Bogota carries a high overall risk rating (4/5) across crime, kidnapping, and civil unrest. The UK FCDO advises against all but essential travel to parts of Colombia, citing kidnapping, terrorism, and armed groups (FCDO Colombia advisory, April 2026). The US State Department maintains a Level 2 advisory urging increased caution. OSAC rates the crime threat as critical.

Two threats define the Bogota security picture more than any other: scopolamine and express kidnapping. Scopolamine, known locally as burundanga, is a chemical compound that criminals use to incapacitate victims. It gets slipped into drinks, rubbed onto handshakes as powder residue, or blown directly into someone's face. Under its effects, victims become compliant and suggestible. They empty their bank accounts, hand over valuables, open their homes. They remember nothing afterwards. The FCDO specifically warns about scopolamine attacks in Colombia (FCDO Colombia advisory, April 2026).

Express kidnapping (the "paseo millonario") is the other signature threat. Victims are picked up in fake taxis, driven between ATMs over several hours, and forced to withdraw everything their accounts hold. This is not rare. It happens routinely, and it is the primary reason every international advisory tells you never to hail a street taxi in Bogota.

Terrorism remains a factor. The ELN (National Liberation Army) car-bombed a police academy in Bogota in January 2019, killing 22 people. FARC dissident factions maintain operational capacity. Most violence concentrates in rural areas, but the ELN has demonstrated the ability and willingness to strike urban targets (US State Dept advisory). Criminal armed groups add organised violence to the mix.

Fifty years of conflict left their mark on this city. But they also built something useful: a private security sector with depth and operational experience that few countries can match. That is the context you are operating in.

Regulations

Security regulations in Colombia

Private security in Colombia is governed by the Estatuto de Vigilancia y Seguridad Privada (Decreto 356 de 1994) and administered by SuperVigilancia (Superintendencia de Vigilancia y Seguridad Privada). All security companies must hold a current SuperVigilancia licence, with separate categories for vigilancia (guard services), escolta (personal protection), and blindajes (armoured vehicles). Civilian firearm carry is restricted, but licensed security companies can arm operators through INDUMIL registration and SuperVigilancia authorisation.

Foreign security firms cannot operate directly in Colombia. They must establish a Colombian legal entity or partner with a locally licensed company (SuperVigilancia regulations). Foreign personnel require work permits through Migracion Colombia. In practice, foreign EP advisors commonly work in consulting roles while Colombian-licensed operators handle the armed, on-the-ground protection. Armoured vehicles are legal and extremely common throughout Bogota.

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Bogota

Never hail a taxi from the street. This is the single most repeated piece of advice in every international advisory on Bogota (FCDO Colombia advisory, April 2026; OSAC). Fake taxi drivers are the primary vector for express kidnapping. Use pre-booked vehicles, rideshare apps, or your security driver. No exceptions.

Scopolamine awareness is non-negotiable. Do not accept drinks from strangers, including in upscale bars and restaurants. Be aware that the drug can be applied through physical contact, not only spiked beverages. If someone approaches you with an unusual request, a map they want you to look at, a pamphlet, maintain distance. Your CP operative is trained to manage these approach vectors.

Zona T, Zona Rosa, Usaquen, Chapinero Alto, and the Parque 93 area are where most visiting executives and expatriates operate. Hotels, restaurants, and corporate offices concentrate in northern Bogota, and the security picture here is considerably better than the south (FCDO Colombia advisory, April 2026). La Candelaria draws tourists during the day, but the threat changes sharply after dark. El Bronx, Los Martires, and Ciudad Bolivar should be avoided entirely without a security detail. TransMilenio stations are pickpocketing and robbery hotspots, particularly at night.
