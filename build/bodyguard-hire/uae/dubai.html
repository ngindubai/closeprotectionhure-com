Regulations

Security regulations in the UAE

SIRA (Security Industry Regulatory Agency) regulates all private security activity in Dubai. Abu Dhabi operates under ADSSSA. Each emirate sets its own licensing framework, meaning a licence in one does not automatically cover another. All CP operatives working in Dubai must hold current SIRA certification, which requires background checks, medical fitness assessments, and completion of SIRA-accredited training programmes.

Firearms are prohibited for private security. Full stop. No civilian carry permits. No weapons in vehicles. Even replica weapons are illegal. Private security in the UAE is overwhelmingly unarmed, with armed operations limited to government-authorised activities. This applies without exception to close protection assignments (SIRA regulatory framework).

Foreign operators cannot work directly in Dubai. They must establish a local entity, secure a free zone licence, or partner with a SIRA-registered company. UAE national hiring preference applies. Our Dubai operatives work through locally registered, SIRA-licensed companies that meet both regulatory requirements and our internal vetting standards.

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Dubai

Almost all of Dubai is safe for visitors. Downtown, Marina, JBR, Palm Jumeirah, DIFC: the security infrastructure across these areas is visible and effective. Deira's older commercial districts have a different feel after dark, but crime remains rare even there. Labour camps in industrial areas like Sonapur and Al Quoz are not unsafe for visitors, just a different environment entirely.

Road safety is the primary physical risk. Sheikh Zayed Road carries fast, aggressive traffic. Tailgating at 120 km/h is routine. Lane discipline is poor. If you are unfamiliar with the driving culture here, hire a professional driver. Road accidents injure and kill more visitors than any other cause in the UAE (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Legal awareness is not optional. Zero tolerance on drugs means exactly that. Trace amounts on clothing or in bloodstreams from use abroad have resulted in custodial sentences. Alcohol is legal only in licensed venues. Public intoxication leads to arrest. Social media criticism of the UAE, its government, or its judicial system is a criminal offence. During Ramadan, do not eat, drink, or smoke in public during daylight hours. These rules apply to visitors without exception.

Regional tensions add a layer. The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to the UAE as of April 2026. Within Dubai, daily life continues as normal, but principals should maintain awareness of the broader situation and have contingency plans for schedule disruption if regional events escalate.
