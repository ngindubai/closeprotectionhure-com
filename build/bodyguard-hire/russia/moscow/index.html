Security landscape

Moscow security overview

Moscow sits at the intersection of every threat category. Terrorism. State surveillance. An active armed conflict that reaches the city itself through drone and missile strikes. And a legal environment that, since December 2024, criminalises a broad range of activities by foreign nationals deemed "against Russian interests" (FCDO travel advisory, Against All Travel, April 2026).

The terrorism threat is severe. In March 2024, gunmen attacked the Crocus City Concert Hall on Moscow's outskirts. 145 people were killed. IS claimed responsibility. It was the deadliest attack in Russia in two decades. Six months later, coordinated strikes hit a synagogue and churches in Dagestan, killing more than 20 (US State Dept Level 4, Do Not Travel). The conflict with Ukraine brings regular drone attacks on Moscow itself. Residential areas, airports, and infrastructure have all been hit. Flight disruptions from Sheremetyevo and Domodedovo are now routine.

Street-level crime is moderate but carries particular risks for foreigners. Drink spiking at bars leads to robbery; victims left unconscious outdoors face life-threatening hypothermia in winter months. Fake police demand to inspect documents, then steal wallets. Railway stations and tourist areas around Red Square and Old Arbat are the primary hotspots. The FCDO flags racial discrimination and attacks against African and Asian visitors as a documented pattern (OSAC crime rating: High).

Then there is the state itself. The FCDO warns explicitly: British nationals have been arrested on charges that would not constitute offences in the UK. Social media posts expressing support for Ukraine have triggered prosecution. Consular access for detained foreign nationals is extremely limited. This is not a standard operating environment.

Regulations

Security regulations in Russia

Russia's private security industry is regulated by Rosgvardia (the National Guard) under Federal Law No. 2487-1 (1992, amended). Licensed private security companies (CHOPs) can employ armed personnel carrying firearms registered with the regulator. This is one of the few markets where armed close protection through private firms is legally structured, though heavily monitored.

Foreign security operators face conditions that are effectively impossible to meet. Western sanctions, the geopolitical situation, and December 2024 legislation expanding criminal liability for activities by foreigners "against Russian interests" have closed the door for Western companies. Russian citizenship is required for most security roles. FSB and MVD background checks are mandatory. The domestic industry is large, with significant expansion of private military company activity during the Ukraine conflict.

For practical purposes, any bodyguard hire in Moscow must use Russian-licensed operators. There is no pathway for a Western security firm to deploy its own personnel. Our model works through established regional contacts who hold valid Rosgvardia licences and meet our vetting standards (Federal Law No. 2487-1, 1992 as amended; FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Moscow

Do not post anything on social media about the conflict in Ukraine. Since December 2024, Russian law criminalises a wide range of activities by foreigners considered "against Russian interests." Social media posts have triggered arrests. The FCDO confirms that British nationals have been detained on charges that would not constitute offences in the UK. Delete any Ukraine-related content from your devices before travelling.

Drink spiking is a documented pattern in Moscow bars and clubs. Victims are robbed and left unconscious outdoors. In winter, this is life-threatening: hypothermia sets in rapidly at Moscow temperatures. Never leave drinks unattended. Travel with someone you trust. Avoid bars in unfamiliar areas, and have your hotel address written in Russian for emergency taxi use.

Watch for fake police. Individuals in uniform (or plain clothes claiming authority) demand to inspect passports and wallets, then steal cash and cards. Genuine police can request ID, but you are within your rights to insist on going to a police station. Carry a photocopy of your passport separately from the original.

There are no direct flights between Moscow and the UK or EU. Travel routes go through Istanbul, Dubai, or other transit points. Flight disruptions from Sheremetyevo and Domodedovo are frequent due to drone attacks. Build flexibility into your departure plans. Have a backup route out of the country that does not depend on a single airport or airline.

Central Moscow (Red Square, Kremlin area, Tverskaya district, upscale hotel zones) is relatively secure in daylight. Railway stations are high-risk at any hour. Avoid the outskirts and industrial areas. During drone or missile alerts, follow local shelter guidance immediately.
