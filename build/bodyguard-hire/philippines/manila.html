Manila security overview

Manila holds a high overall risk rating of 4/5, with crime at 4/5, terrorism at 4/5, and kidnapping at 4/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; OSAC Critical). The US State Department rates the Philippines at Level 2 (exercise increased caution). The FCDO advises against all travel to western and central Mindanao and the Sulu archipelago. For Metro Manila, the threat profile centres on armed street crime, terrorist targeting of soft targets, and kidnap-for-ransom operations that have historically included British nationals.

Terrorism is not a hypothetical concern. Abu Sayyaf Group, IS-Philippines, and the Bangsamoro Islamic Freedom Fighters maintain active capability. In December 2023, four people were killed in an explosion during a Catholic mass in Marawi. Previous attacks have targeted shopping malls, public transport hubs, and entertainment venues. Multiple groups retain the ability to strike in Manila itself (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). Every major building in the city operates metal detectors and bag checks at the entrance. That is the baseline security posture here.

Armed robbery on public transport is documented and sometimes fatal. Jeepneys and buses are regular targets. Phone snatching is endemic across the city, and taxi-related robbery remains a persistent pattern for foreign visitors. Tondo district is densely populated, violent, and firmly outside any tourist perimeter. Quiapo draws pickpockets. The Manila CBD empties after dark and the threat level rises accordingly. Ermita and Malate shift into red-light district territory at night, attracting a different category of crime (OSAC).

Typhoon season adds a variable that most security planning overlooks. Infrastructure collapse, flooding that submerges entire districts, power failures, and transport network shutdowns. When a major storm hits, the security equation changes completely. Evacuation logistics become the priority, and operators need contingency routes that account for impassable roads and overwhelmed emergency services.

Civil unrest is periodic. Political protests, labour disputes, and anti-government demonstrations do occur. The Philippine government warns that foreign nationals who participate face deportation. Protests can disrupt traffic on major routes without notice.

Regulations

Security regulations in the Philippines

Private security falls under the Supervisory Office for Security and Investigation Agencies (SOSIA), a division of the Philippine National Police. Companies require a PNP-SOSIA licence, financial qualification, and organisational structure approval. The governing legislation is RA 5487 (Private Security Agency Law), significantly amended by RA 11917 in 2022. The Philippines has over 600,000 licensed security guards, making it one of the largest private security workforces in the world.

Firearms are managed through PNP-FEO (Firearms and Explosives Office). Licensed security agencies can arm their personnel, and armed guards are a standard feature at malls, banks, hotels, and corporate offices throughout Metro Manila. This is not a country where armed protection draws attention. It is expected.

Foreign operators cannot directly provide security services in the Philippines. Constitutional ownership limits impose a 60-40 rule: Filipino nationals must hold at least 60% of any security company. Foreign firms must partner with a Filipino-owned entity. All individual security personnel require PNP-SOSIA licensing, NBI clearance, drug testing, and neuro-psychiatric examination. Pre-licensing training courses and annual in-service training are mandated by PNP standards (RA 5487; RA 11917, 2022; PNP-SOSIA).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Manila

Stick to the areas where security infrastructure matches your profile. Makati is the primary business district with good private security coverage. Bonifacio Global City (BGC) is modern, purpose-built, and well-secured. Ortigas Center functions as a secondary business hub. Alabang offers gated residential compounds in the south of Metro Manila. These areas operate at a fundamentally different security level than the rest of the city (OSAC).

Avoid Tondo. It is the most densely populated area of Manila, violent, and there is no business reason for a corporate visitor to enter it. Quiapo is a pickpocketing corridor. The Manila CBD thins out after dark, and the threat environment shifts. Ermita and Malate attract a different crowd at night, with associated risks. During typhoon season, any area of Manila can become dangerous when infrastructure fails and flooding blocks evacuation routes.

Do not use jeepneys or public buses. Armed hold-ups on these vehicles are documented and have resulted in fatalities (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). The MRT and LRT systems are overcrowded and present pickpocketing risk but are lower-threat than road-based public transport. For any ground movement, use pre-arranged secure vehicles or reputable ride-hailing services as a minimum.

At NAIA airport, use only pre-arranged transfers. The unlicensed taxi zone outside arrivals is a documented risk area for robbery. Phone snatching is endemic across Manila, particularly in crowded market areas and on foot in commercial districts. Keep devices secure and maintain awareness of your immediate surroundings at all times.
