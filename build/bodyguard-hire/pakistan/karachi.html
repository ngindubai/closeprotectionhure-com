Security landscape

Karachi security overview

Karachi holds a critical overall risk rating of 5/5, with crime at 5/5, terrorism at 5/5, and kidnapping at 5/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; US State Dept Level 3; OSAC Critical). The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to parts of Pakistan, and the US State Department rates the country at Level 3: Reconsider Travel. This is not cautionary language. It reflects a city where the threat environment is active, layered, and persistent.

Terrorism drives the top-line risk. TTP, Tehrik-i-Jihad Pakistan (TJP), the Balochistan Liberation Army (BLA), Islamic State Khorasan Province (ISKP), and al-Qaida all maintain operational capability in or near Karachi. Targets include airports, government buildings, security forces, places of worship, railways, and public gatherings. In October 2024, a suicide attack near Jinnah International Airport killed four people, including two Chinese nationals. In March 2025, BLA fighters hijacked the Jaffar Express train and killed 64 passengers. In January 2026, coordinated BLA attacks across Balochistan killed 48 or more. In February 2026, a mosque bombing in Islamabad killed 36 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). The pattern is clear. These are not historical footnotes. They are the operating tempo.

Kidnapping is a parallel industry. Terrorist groups, criminal gangs, and opportunistic criminals all engage in kidnapping for ransom throughout Karachi. Express kidnapping follows the same model seen in Latin America: grab the target, drain their accounts at gunpoint, release or escalate. British nationals of Pakistani origin are specifically targeted because they are perceived as wealthier (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). The British government does not pay ransoms as a matter of policy, which every visitor should factor into their risk calculation before arrival.

Street crime fills in whatever gaps the larger threats leave. Armed robbery, carjacking, target killings linked to political and gang rivalries, and extortion. Lyari district is effectively a war zone, controlled by competing gangs with access to military-grade weapons. Orangi Town and Korangi present similar threats after dark. The old city around Saddar becomes unpredictable once businesses close for the evening.

Civil unrest is regular. Political rallies, sectarian violence during religious periods, and economic protests can shut down major roads without warning. Mobile data and internet are cut by the government during sensitive periods, eliminating real-time communication for anyone not operating on satellite phones or offline protocols. Election periods are particularly volatile (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Regulations

Security regulations in Pakistan

Private security in Pakistan falls under provincial regulation. There is no single national licence. Sindh province (which covers Karachi) has its own legislation governing security companies. Punjab operates under the Punjab Private Security Companies Ordinance (2002), the model that other provinces have adapted. KP and Balochistan maintain separate frameworks. Travel between provinces may require No Objection Certificates (NOCs), and a licence valid in Sindh does not automatically cover operations in Punjab or Balochistan (Provincial Home Departments).

Firearms licensing goes through district authorities. Arms licences are more accessible than in most countries, and armed security is the standard for corporate and VIP protection. Security personnel require background verification through police and arms training certification. Companies must register with the relevant provincial Home Department. Foreign operators must register locally, comply with provincial regulations, and work through a Pakistani partner. Government authorities may assign police escorts for foreign clients, particularly when travel involves areas outside major commercial zones (Provincial Home Departments; Pakistan Security Guard Ordinance).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Karachi

Stay within DHA and Clifton wherever possible. Defence Housing Authority is gated, military-secured, and operates its own checkpoint system. Clifton is upscale with private security and police checkpoints at key junctions. Bahria Town is a gated community with controlled access. These areas bear little resemblance to the rest of Karachi. Outside them, the threat picture changes block by block (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Avoid Lyari entirely. It is not a neighbourhood going through a rough patch. It is gang-controlled territory where armed factions fight for dominance with automatic weapons and grenades. Orangi Town and Korangi Industrial Area after dark present similar dangers. The old city around Saddar becomes unpredictable once shops close. Anywhere near political or religious gatherings should be treated as a potential mass casualty site.

The FCDO explicitly advises avoiding all public transport in Pakistan, including the Metro Bus system. Railways are a documented terrorist target: BLA hijacked the Jaffar Express in March 2025, killing 64 people. Avoid e-taxis and online taxi apps, which do not guarantee driver vetting or vehicle security. All ground transport should go through your security provider or a pre-arranged hotel vehicle (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

British nationals of Pakistani origin face a specific kidnapping risk. They are perceived as wealthier than local residents and are targeted accordingly. Do not advertise British connections, display expensive watches or phones in public, or share travel plans with anyone outside your immediate circle. If you receive a ransom demand, contact your security detail and the British High Commission before taking any action.
