Security landscape

Nairobi security overview

Nairobi is East Africa's largest city and its primary business hub. It is also the city where the Westgate mall attack (2013, 67 dead) and DusitD2 hotel attack (2019, 21 dead) demonstrated that Al Shabaab maintains both the intent and capability to strike. The FCDO rates terrorism in Nairobi as "very likely" and advises heightened vigilance in public places (FCDO Kenya advisory, April 2026).

Street crime is the day-to-day threat. OSAC rates Nairobi crime as critical. Violent muggings happen in broad daylight in the CBD. Carjacking is common, particularly on Mombasa Road and Thika Road after dark. Criminals use machetes, knives, and firearms. Eastleigh, a densely populated area northeast of the city centre, has both high crime rates and documented links to Al Shabaab recruitment.

Kidnapping is a real threat. Al Shabaab has conducted kidnappings of Western nationals in Kenya. Criminal kidnapping for ransom also occurs, with express kidnapping incidents in Nairobi and its outskirts. The FCDO notes that British nationals specifically may be targets.

Civil unrest is periodic, triggered by election disputes, cost-of-living protests, or political opposition rallies. Kenyan police have used tear gas and live ammunition at demonstrations. Post-election violence remains a significant risk factor during political cycles.

Regulations

Security regulations in Kenya

Kenya's private security sector is regulated by the Private Security Regulatory Authority (PSRA), established in 2016. All security firms must hold a PSRA licence with annual renewal and compliance inspections. Firearms are available to licensed security companies through group firearms certificates administered by the Central Firearms Bureau under the Firearms Act (Cap 114). The process is strict but obtainable for established firms (PSRA Act, 2016).

Foreign security firms must register locally and comply with PSRA requirements. Joint ventures with Kenyan companies are common. Foreign nationals cannot independently carry weapons and must operate under a local firm's firearms certificate. Work permits (Class G) are required under Kenyan immigration law for foreign security professionals.

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Nairobi

Use pre-arranged hotel transfers from JKIA. Do not use unlicensed taxis at the airport. Carjacking risk is elevated on Mombasa Road and the airport approach, particularly after dark. Your hotel or security provider should arrange collection.

Stay in Westlands, Karen, Lavington/Kilimani, or the Gigiri area (near the UN compound) where security infrastructure is strongest. Avoid the CBD after dark, Eastleigh entirely, and Kibera unless you have a specific operational reason with security support. Uhuru Park after dark is also flagged by OSAC.

Be aware of your surroundings at shopping malls and hotels. Post-Westgate and DusitD2, most major malls and hotels have screening and armed guards, but the terrorism threat has not disappeared. Avoid predictable patterns. Matatus (minibuses) are overcrowded, accident-prone, and targeted by robbers. The SGR train to Mombasa is a safer intercity option.
