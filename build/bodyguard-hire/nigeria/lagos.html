Security landscape

Lagos security overview

Lagos, with over 20 million people, is Nigeria's commercial engine and one of the most complex security environments in West Africa. The OSAC (US Overseas Security Advisory Council) rates crime in Lagos as critical. The UK FCDO advises against all travel to parts of Nigeria and against all but essential travel to others, citing armed robbery, carjacking, and kidnapping (FCDO Nigeria advisory, April 2026).

Armed robbery is the baseline threat. Criminals set up unauthorized checkpoints on highways, particularly after dark. Carjacking happens at traffic lights, intersections, and on the Lekki-Epe Expressway. Oshodi, Mile 2, and the Apapa port corridor are high-density crime zones. Even Victoria Island, the commercial heart where most business travellers stay, sees incidents.

Kidnapping rates in Nigeria are among the highest globally. Express kidnapping, where victims are held briefly and forced to make ATM withdrawals or mobile transfers, is increasing in Lagos proper. Traditional ransom kidnapping, once concentrated in the Niger Delta and northwest states, now occurs across Lekki, the Third Mainland Bridge approaches, and the city's outskirts. The Gulf of Guinea also sees maritime kidnapping, though that primarily affects shipping crews.

Police reliability is poor. The NSCDC and Nigerian Police Force maintain a visible presence, but response times are slow and corruption is pervasive. Roadblocks where officers solicit bribes are routine. This is why the private security sector in Lagos is large and well-established. For business travellers and HNWIs, a close protection officer is standard operating procedure.

Regulations

Security regulations in Nigeria

Private security in Nigeria is regulated by the Nigeria Security and Civil Defence Corps (NSCDC). All security companies must register with NSCDC and the Corporate Affairs Commission. Licensed firms can obtain firearms permits, though the application process is bureaucratic. Armoured vehicles are legal with specific permits and commonly used by banks, oil companies, and HNWIs (NSCDC regulations, current as of April 2026).

Foreign security firms cannot operate directly in Nigeria. They must partner with a Nigerian-registered company. Foreign nationals cannot carry firearms under any circumstances. If you are travelling with your own security team, they can operate in an advisory role but the armed, licensed personnel on the ground will be Nigerian operators working under a local firm's licence.

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Lagos

Use pre-arranged transfers for all airport movements. Murtala Muhammed International has documented issues with baggage theft, taxi scams at arrivals, and follow-from-airport robbery. Never hail a taxi outside the terminal. Your hotel or security provider should arrange collection.

Stick to Victoria Island and Ikoyi for accommodation and meetings where possible. These are the areas with the best private security coverage. Oshodi, Mushin, Ajegunle, and Mile 2 are high-crime areas that should be avoided unless there is a specific operational reason. Night travel on the Third Mainland Bridge and Lekki-Epe Expressway carries elevated robbery risk.

Keep vehicle windows up and doors locked at all times. Do not resist if confronted at an unauthorized checkpoint. Vary your routes. Do not establish patterns. Avoid displaying phones, watches, or valuables in traffic. Communication blackouts can be imposed during civil unrest, so keep your security provider informed of your schedule.
