Security landscape

Mumbai security overview

Mumbai's security profile is defined by one event. On 26 November 2008, ten operatives from Lashkar-e-Taiba carried out coordinated attacks across the city, targeting the Taj Mahal Palace hotel, the Oberoi Trident, Chhatrapati Shivaji Terminus railway station, and several other locations. 166 people were killed over three days. The attacks reshaped security protocols across India's commercial capital and remain the reference point for threat planning in Mumbai (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

The terrorism threat has not diminished. The FCDO rates terrorist attacks in India as "very likely," citing continued intent from groups including LeT, Jaish-e-Mohammed, AQIS, and IS-inspired cells. Hotels, transport hubs, commercial centres, and places of worship remain primary targets. Hotel security improved after 2008, but the underlying threat persists (US State Dept Level 2; OSAC crime rating: High).

Street-level crime is moderate by comparison. Pickpocketing, scams, and petty theft cluster around crowded train stations (CST, Dadar) and tourist areas after dark. The FCDO issues specific warnings about sexual assault, including against foreign nationals. Violent crime directed at tourists is relatively rare, but the sexual assault risk is serious enough to warrant operational precautions for female travellers and mixed groups.

Civil unrest is a regular feature. Religious tensions between Hindu and Muslim communities, caste disputes, and political rallies can escalate quickly. Bandh (shutdown) protests can paralyse the city with little warning, closing roads and public transport for hours or days. These events are rarely violent toward foreigners but can strand people in unsafe areas if not anticipated.

Regulations

Security regulations in India

Private security in India is governed by the Private Security Agencies Regulation Act (PSARA, 2005), administered at the state level. Each state issues its own licences. Operating across multiple states (Mumbai in Maharashtra, for example, then Delhi) requires separate PSARA registrations for each jurisdiction.

Firearms are heavily restricted under the Arms Act (1959). Armed security is limited to government-authorised agencies such as the Central Industrial Security Force (CISF) and state police units. Private armed security is exceptionally rare. The standard model for executive protection is unarmed operatives with police liaison arrangements for situations requiring armed response.

Foreign security firms cannot operate directly. They must establish an Indian subsidiary or joint venture and register under PSARA at the state level. Foreign direct investment caps apply. All personnel require police-verified background checks. Quality varies significantly across India's 9+ million-strong private security workforce, the largest in the world (PSARA, 2005; Arms Act, 1959).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Mumbai

Use pre-booked transport from Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj International Airport (BOM). Do not use unlicensed taxis. Mumbai's local trains are extremely overcrowded. People fall from moving carriages regularly. They are not recommended for security-conscious travel. Autorickshaws and app-based taxis are safer alternatives for short distances.

India has one of the highest road accident death rates globally. Mumbai traffic is unpredictable: potholes, erratic driving, pedestrians in traffic lanes, and animals on roads. Defensive driving skills and local road knowledge are not optional. They are operational requirements.

Stick to well-known areas after dark. Colaba and Nariman Point have increased security presence since 2008. Bandra-Kurla Complex is a controlled business district. Powai's gated compounds and Juhu's residential streets are relatively secure. Avoid Dharavi, Kamathipura, and isolated areas at night. The FCDO warns specifically about sexual assault risk, including against foreign nationals. Women should avoid travelling alone after dark in unfamiliar areas (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Monitor for bandh (shutdown) announcements. These can be called with minimal notice and shut down transport and commerce across the city. Your security team should have contingency plans for rapid schedule changes during periods of political or communal tension.
