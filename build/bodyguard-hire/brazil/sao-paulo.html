Security landscape

Sao Paulo security overview

Sao Paulo holds an overall risk rating of 4/5, with crime at the maximum 5/5 and kidnapping at 4/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; OSAC). The US State Department rates Brazil at Level 2 (increased caution), while OSAC classifies the threat environment as CRITICAL. This is one of the most dangerous cities for street crime in our portfolio. The threat is constant, varied, and often violent.

Express kidnapping (sequestro relampago) is the defining crime. Victims are forced into vehicles and driven between ATMs for hours, sometimes through the night, while criminals drain every account they can access. Violence during these incidents is standard, not exceptional. The FCDO warns that attackers in Brazil are frequently armed and often under the influence of drugs, making their behaviour unpredictable (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). Do not resist. Virtual kidnapping runs alongside it: callers claim to hold a family member and demand immediate transfers. Traditional kidnapping of high-value targets still operates at a lower frequency but with higher stakes.

Street crime fills every gap. Armed robbery. Carjacking at traffic lights after dark and in tunnels. Arrastao (coordinated group robberies on beaches and public spaces). Phone snatching peaks between 4pm and 9pm. Dating apps used to lure foreigners to locations where they are robbed at gunpoint. The crime is opportunistic but it follows patterns, and those patterns are what trained operatives read.

Cracolandia sits near the centre of the city. It is not a favela. It is an open crack cocaine market where criminal activity is continuous and any outsider entering is a target. Favelas across the city are absolute no-go zones. Police operations inside favelas involve live gunfire, armoured vehicles, and collateral damage to bystanders. The military police (Policia Militar) handle street patrols but corruption is high and reliability is low (OSAC).

Civil unrest is regular. Workers' strikes, economic protests, and political demonstrations close major arteries including Avenida Paulista. Police respond with rubber bullets and tear gas. These events disrupt movement and create exposure windows that criminals exploit when transport is static in gridlocked traffic.

Regulations

Security regulations in Brazil

Private security in Brazil falls under the Federal Police (Policia Federal) Private Security Division, with state police providing additional oversight. The governing legislation is Law 7.102/1983, updated by Portaria 3.233/2012, which establishes categories including vigilancia patrimonial, transporte de valores, seguranca pessoal (personal security), and escolta armada (armed escort). Licensed companies can arm personnel. All weapons are registered with the Federal Police. Armed executive protection is not just permitted in Brazil. It is the norm.

Foreign operators cannot work in Brazil without local incorporation. Establishing a Brazilian entity (LTDA or S.A.) and obtaining Federal Police registration are mandatory before any security operations can begin. There is no route for a foreign national to operate independently. All security personnel must pass Federal Police background checks, psychological evaluations, and weapons proficiency testing, with periodic renewal required. Specialist EP training comes from Federal Police-certified academies. Brazil's private security industry is one of the largest in the world, with over 600,000 registered professionals and mature industry associations including ABESE and FENAVIST (Federal Police; Law 7.102/1983; Portaria 3.233/2012).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Sao Paulo

Stay within the districts where the security infrastructure supports your profile. Jardins is upscale with a visible private security presence. Itaim Bibi is the business district. The Faria Lima corridor and Pinheiros both hold during business hours. Vila Madalena is manageable in daylight. Once you step outside these areas, or stay beyond dark, the threat picture changes fast (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Favelas are absolute no-go zones. No exceptions, no guided tours, no curiosity visits. Police operations in favelas involve live ammunition and armoured personnel carriers. Bystanders get caught in crossfire. Cracolandia, near Luz station, is an open drug market. Criminal activity is constant. Do not walk through it, do not drive through it, do not go near it.

Carjacking peaks after dark, particularly at traffic lights and in tunnels. Keep doors locked, windows up, and leave space ahead of your vehicle to allow an escape route. Do not stop for anyone flagging you down. Phone snatching runs from about 4pm to 9pm on foot, especially around metro stations and pedestrian areas. Carry a decoy phone if you must walk. Dating app robbery targets foreigners specifically: criminals use fake profiles to lure victims to controlled locations (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; OSAC).

The Bras and Luz districts are high-risk at all hours. Centro after dark is dangerous. Avenida Paulista is generally safe during daylight but becomes a protest corridor when demonstrations shut it down. Metro is relatively safe with security during peak hours, but theft and robbery on buses remain common throughout the system.
