Security landscape

Jakarta security overview

Jakarta holds a medium overall risk rating of 3/5, but that average obscures a pronounced terrorism threat scored at 4/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; OSAC High). The US State Department rates Indonesia at Level 2 (exercise increased caution). The FCDO advises against all travel to parts of Papua, Central Sulawesi, and Maluku, though Jakarta itself falls outside those restrictions. The threat picture in the capital is shaped by Islamist extremism, street crime, severe flooding, and a police force that operates on informal terms.

Terrorism is the defining risk. Jemaah Islamiyah remnants and IS-affiliated cells have carried out attacks against hotels, bars, places of worship, police posts, and shopping malls carrying international brands. A suicide bomber struck a police post in Bandung in 2022. A suicide attack hit Makassar cathedral in 2021. Indonesian authorities regularly disrupt plots before execution, but the FCDO warns that further attacks are likely and that Western interests are specifically at risk (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). Risk escalates around Christmas, New Year, Ramadan, and Independence Day.

Street crime sits at 3/5. Pickpocketing, bag snatching, credit card fraud, drink spiking, and scams targeting foreigners are all documented. Kota Tua (Old Town) after dark, crowded markets, public transport hubs, and nightlife areas carry the highest exposure. Sexual assault occurs. Methanol poisoning from counterfeit alcohol has killed tourists across Indonesia; only sealed, branded spirits from reputable venues should be consumed (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Civil unrest is occasional but unpredictable. Elections, events in the Middle East, religious tensions, and student protests can trigger demonstrations that turn violent without warning. Protests have shut down arterial roads across the city on multiple occasions. Jakarta's police score 2/5 for reliability. Corruption is well documented. Officers may seek informal payments after traffic incidents. Drug enforcement is exceptionally strict: trafficking carries the death penalty, and British nationals have received extended sentences. Police regularly raid venues popular with foreigners to check for drug use (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Regulations

Security regulations in Indonesia

Private security in Indonesia operates under dual oversight from the Ministry of Manpower and Polri (Indonesian National Police). Companies require a business licence from the provincial government and police approval for security operations. Chief of Police Regulation No. 24/2007 sets the regulatory framework, with additional requirements under Government Regulation on Industrial Security.

Firearms are prohibited for civilians. Only military, police, and specifically authorised government officials may carry weapons. Armed private security does not exist in Indonesia. When armed protection is required, police provide it through formal liaison. This arrangement is standard for high-profile corporate clients, resource sector operations, and elevated threat scenarios.

Foreign security operators face significant barriers. Establishing an Indonesian entity (PT PMA) is required, and the negative investment list may restrict foreign participation in the security sector. A local partner is typically necessary. Indonesian citizens are preferred for all security personnel roles, with background checks conducted through police channels and physical fitness requirements enforced. Training standards are set by Polri, though quality varies. Multinational corporations operating in Indonesia often apply higher internal standards above the regulatory minimum (Chief of Police Regulation No. 24/2007; Ministry of Manpower).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Jakarta

Stay in districts where the security infrastructure matches your profile. Menteng is Jakarta's diplomatic and upscale residential quarter. Sudirman CBD houses the major business towers. Kuningan is the embassy district. Kemang caters to the expatriate dining and social scene. These areas have visible security, functioning CCTV, and proximity to police and embassy support. Move outside them with a plan (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Avoid Kota Tua after dark. The area draws tourists during the day but the threat profile changes sharply at night. Poorly lit backstreets, industrial areas, and zones affected by flooding should be treated as no-go without a security detail. During the rainy season, flooding can cut off the main toll road to Soekarno-Hatta airport entirely. If your trip falls between November and March, build contingency time into every airport transfer.

Methanol poisoning is a real and documented killer across Indonesia. Counterfeit alcohol, particularly locally produced spirits sold in bars and beach clubs, has caused multiple tourist deaths. Buy only sealed, branded spirits from reputable hotels and restaurants. Do not accept homemade cocktails or unlabelled spirits under any circumstances (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Drug enforcement in Indonesia operates on a zero-tolerance basis. Trafficking carries the death penalty. Possession carries long prison sentences. British nationals have been convicted and sentenced to extended terms. Police regularly raid bars and clubs popular with foreigners, conducting on-the-spot urine tests. This is not an abstract policy. It is an operational reality that your security team must factor into venue selection and evening schedule planning (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).
