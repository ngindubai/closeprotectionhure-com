Security landscape

Istanbul security overview

Istanbul carries a medium overall risk rating (3/5), but the individual threat categories tell a more complicated story. Terrorism scores 4/5. That is the highest single-category rating for this city, and it shapes every security decision here. The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to parts of Turkey and specifically warns that terrorist attacks are "likely" throughout the country (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). The US State Department maintains a Level 2 advisory with OSAC assigning a High crime rating.

The terrorism threat is not theoretical. In November 2022, a bomb on Istiklal Avenue killed 6 people and injured 81. In 2024, attackers struck a courthouse, a church, and an aerospace facility in separate incidents. PKK and affiliated Kurdish groups, Daesh (IS), and the far-left DHKP/C all maintain operational capability in Turkey. Transport hubs, tourist areas, government buildings, military installations, and foreign embassies are identified target categories (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Crime scores 3/5. Pickpocketing and bag snatching are persistent along Istiklal Avenue, in Sultanahmet, and around the Grand Bazaar. Drink spiking occurs in Beyoglu nightlife spots. Overcharging scams target tourists at restaurants and taxis. These are not violent crimes in most cases, but they create distraction opportunities that a trained operative eliminates.

Civil unrest scores 3/5. Protests related to the Kurdish issue, political opposition, and social movements can erupt around Taksim Square with limited warning. Post-2016 coup security forces maintain a heavy presence across the city, and demonstrations can escalate quickly when police respond. Kidnapping is low at 2/5 and not a significant factor in Istanbul itself, though south-eastern border areas carry higher risk (US State Dept advisory).

Add to this the 6.2 magnitude earthquake in the Sea of Marmara in April 2025. Istanbul sits on an active fault line. Earthquake preparedness is not optional here. It is a legitimate operational planning factor for any security detail (OSAC).

Regulations

Security regulations in Turkey

Private security in Turkey is governed by Law No. 5188 (2004), the Private Security Services Act. The Ministry of Interior, through the General Directorate of Provincial Administration, oversees all licensing. Companies require permission from the provincial governor, and all personnel must complete a minimum 120 hours of certified training, pass background checks, and undergo medical examinations. Annual refresher training is mandatory.

Civilian firearm carry is restricted. Licensed security companies can arm their operatives with specific provincial governor approval, and all weapons must be registered. Armoured vehicles are legal for licensed operators. Foreign security personnel cannot work directly in Turkey. They must establish a Turkish legal entity and are subject to additional scrutiny with limited reciprocity arrangements (Law No. 5188).

Since the 2016 coup attempt, regulations have tightened. State security presence expanded across Istanbul, and emergency powers can override private security operations. Our operators in Istanbul hold current licences under Law No. 5188 and work through locally incorporated, governor-approved security companies.

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Istanbul

Beyoglu (specifically Nisantasi), Besiktas, and Bebek along the Bosphorus are the areas most used by visiting executives and expatriates on the European side. Kadikoy on the Asian side offers a calmer environment with good restaurants and hotels. Stay in these districts when possible, particularly for evening engagements.

Istiklal Avenue requires careful consideration. It is Istanbul's most famous pedestrian street and also a documented terrorism target (the November 2022 bombing). Avoid peak crowd times if you can. The Grand Bazaar and Sultanahmet attract persistent scam operators and pickpockets. Keep valuables secured and be alert to distraction techniques.

Tarlabaasi, immediately behind Istiklal, changes character sharply after dark. The back streets of Fatih carry a similar profile. These areas are best transited with a security detail or avoided entirely at night. Crowded trams between Sultanahmet and Kabatas are pickpocketing hotspots. Istanbul traffic is aggressive. Pre-arranged vehicles with vetted drivers are significantly safer than street taxis, and ride-hailing apps offer a middle ground if a security driver is not available (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).
