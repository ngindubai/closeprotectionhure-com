Security landscape

Mexico City security overview

Mexico City holds a high overall risk rating of 4/5, driven by crime at 4/5 and kidnapping at 4/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; OSAC). The US State Department rates Mexico at Level 2 (increased caution), while OSAC classifies the threat environment as CRITICAL. CDMX sits apart from the cartel warfare that tears through northern border states. The threats here are urban, opportunistic, and often targeted at people who look like they have money.

Express kidnapping is the signature crime. Criminals pull victims into vehicles, usually fake taxis, and drive them between ATMs for hours, draining every account they can access. Virtual kidnapping runs in parallel: callers claim to hold a family member and demand immediate wire transfers. The FCDO warns specifically that unlicensed taxis in Mexico City are used for robbery and kidnapping (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). This is not an edge case. It is a persistent, documented pattern that catches foreign visitors who do not know which vehicles to trust.

Street crime fills in the gaps. Armed robbery, carjacking on highways leaving the city, ATM robberies, and drink spiking in Zona Rosa bars where victims are drugged, robbed, and sometimes assaulted (OSAC). Tepito is gang-controlled territory and effectively a no-go zone. Doctores, Lagunilla, and the metro system after dark present consistent threats at street level.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup adds a new variable. Mexico City is a host city, and the influx of foreign visitors is expected to increase targeting by opportunistic criminals. Express kidnapping, taxi scams, and drink spiking incidents are all projected to rise around match dates (OSAC).

Civil unrest is routine in CDMX. Teacher union protests, Ayotzinapa disappearance demonstrations, political marches, and women's rights rallies regularly shut down Paseo de la Reforma and the Zocalo. These are generally non-violent but they block arterial routes without warning, trapping traffic and creating unpredictable exposure windows for anyone caught in the wrong position.

Regulations

Security regulations in Mexico

Private security falls under dual regulation: the federal Direccion General de Seguridad Privada and state-level authorities. Companies must hold both federal and state licences under the Ley Federal de Seguridad Privada (2006). Firearms control sits with SEDENA (Ministry of Defence), which operates the only legal gun shop in the country, located in Mexico City. Licensed security firms can apply for weapons permits, but the process involves military vetting and ongoing compliance checks.

Foreign security operators cannot carry weapons in Mexico under any circumstances without Mexican military authorisation, which is essentially impossible to obtain. Direct foreign operation is prohibited. Foreign firms must establish a Mexican legal entity or partner with a locally licensed company. Consulting and advisory roles are the only practical route for international operators. All security personnel require background checks, psychometric testing, drug screening, and certified training (SEDENA; Ley Federal de Seguridad Privada, 2006).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Mexico City

Stick to the colonias where the security infrastructure matches your profile. Polanco (upscale, embassy district), Condesa and Roma Norte (relatively safe in daylight), Santa Fe (gated business district), and Lomas de Chapultepec are where corporate travellers and expatriates concentrate. The security environment in these areas bears little resemblance to the east and south of the city (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Never use unlicensed taxis. This is not general caution. The FCDO warns that unlicensed taxis in Mexico City are directly used for robbery and kidnapping. At AICM airport, purchase taxi vouchers only from the official desk inside the arrivals terminal. Better yet, use a pre-booked vehicle through your security provider or hotel. If you are already on the ground, Uber operates widely but does not eliminate all risk.

Avoid Tepito entirely. It is gang-controlled and presents extreme danger even during daylight. Doctores after dark, Iztapalapa, and Ecatepec should not be entered without a security detail. The metro is a pickpocketing corridor, especially Lines 1, 2, and 3. After 10pm the threat profile on the metro escalates. Women-only carriages are available during peak hours.

Drink spiking is a documented problem in Zona Rosa bars and nightlife spots. Victims are drugged, robbed, and sometimes assaulted (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). If you are out at night, manage your own drinks and do not accept beverages from strangers. Virtual kidnapping phone scams target foreigners: if you receive a call claiming a family member has been taken, do not pay. Contact your security detail or call 911 first.
