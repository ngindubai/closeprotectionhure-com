Security landscape

Sao Paulo transport security overview

Sao Paulo holds an overall risk rating of 4/5, with crime at 5/5 and kidnapping at 4/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; OSAC). The US State Department rates Brazil at Level 2 (increased caution). OSAC classifies the threat environment as CRITICAL. For anyone moving through this city by road, the threat is constant and directly tied to how you travel.

Carjacking defines the transport risk in Sao Paulo. After dark, traffic lights become ambush points. Criminals approach stationary vehicles, weapons drawn, and take the car, its contents, and sometimes the occupants. Tunnels are worse: enclosed, low visibility, no escape route. The FCDO warns that attackers in Brazil are frequently armed and under the influence of drugs, making resistance extremely dangerous (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). Do not resist. The vehicle can be replaced. You cannot.

Express kidnapping (sequestro relampago) is the second transport threat. Unvetted vehicles, whether fake taxis or ride-hails with compromised drivers, are the entry point. Once inside, victims are driven between ATMs for hours while criminals drain their accounts. Violence during these events is standard. For corporate travellers arriving at Guarulhos International (GRU), the 25-kilometre corridor into the city passes through zones where both carjacking and express kidnapping are documented (OSAC).

Protest blockages add an unpredictable layer. Workers' strikes and economic demonstrations close Avenida Paulista and connecting arteries. Police respond with rubber bullets and tear gas. A vehicle caught in that gridlock is static, exposed, and unable to manoeuvre for an unknown duration. Route variance and real-time protest intelligence are the only counters.

Road accident rates are high. Sao Paulo's traffic is aggressive and chaotic. Bus robberies are common across the public transport network. The metro has security during peak hours, but it is not a viable option for corporate passengers. Congonhas domestic airport (CGH) sits within the city and presents a lower transfer risk than Guarulhos, but carjacking patterns still apply on surrounding roads after dark.

Regulations

Security regulations in Brazil

Private security in Brazil falls under the Federal Police (Policia Federal) Private Security Division, with state police providing additional oversight. The governing legislation is Law 7.102/1983, updated by Portaria 3.233/2012. Licensed companies can arm personnel, with all weapons registered with the Federal Police. The escolta armada (armed escort) category specifically covers armed security transport operations. Armed security driving is not just legal in Brazil. It is the industry norm.

Foreign nationals cannot operate security vehicles in Brazil without local incorporation. A Brazilian entity (LTDA or S.A.) with Federal Police registration is mandatory. All security drivers require Federal Police background checks, psychological evaluations, weapons proficiency testing, and periodic renewal. Training comes from Federal Police-certified academies. Brazil's private security industry employs over 600,000 registered professionals (Federal Police; Law 7.102/1983; Portaria 3.233/2012).

Traveller guidance

Transport safety guidance for Sao Paulo visitors

Do not use unvetted transport. Express kidnapping in Sao Paulo starts when the target enters an uncontrolled vehicle. At Guarulhos, use only a pre-booked security driver or a vetted hotel transfer. At Congonhas, the same rule applies. Ride-hailing apps reduce some exposure but do not eliminate it: compromised drivers and vehicle swaps have been documented. The safest transport is a pre-positioned armoured vehicle with a driver confirmed through your security provider (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Keep to districts where the road infrastructure supports your security. Jardins, Itaim Bibi, Faria Lima corridor, and Pinheiros have road conditions and private security presence that reduce transport risk. Once you cross into Bras, Luz, Centro after dark, or any road that borders a favela, the threat profile changes. Cracolandia, near Luz station, is an active drug market. Routes that pass through or near it should be avoided entirely.

Carjacking peaks after dark. Traffic lights and tunnels are the primary ambush points. Keep doors locked, windows up, and leave space ahead of your vehicle at lights. Do not stop for anyone flagging you down on the roadside. These are documented setups. If you must drive without a security detail, avoid routes through transitional neighbourhoods after sunset. The road between Guarulhos and the city centre is high-risk for carjacking, particularly in the tunnel segments (OSAC).

Avenida Paulista closes for protests without warning. When it shuts down, connecting roads gridlock. Do not attempt to navigate through or around an active demonstration. If your vehicle is caught, stay inside, lock doors, and wait for the route to clear or for your security detail to direct an alternative.
