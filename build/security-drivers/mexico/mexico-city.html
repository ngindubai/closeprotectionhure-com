Security landscape

Mexico City transport security overview

Mexico City holds an overall risk rating of 4/5, with crime at 4/5 and kidnapping at 4/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; OSAC). The US State Department rates Mexico at Level 2 (increased caution). OSAC classifies the threat environment as CRITICAL. For anyone moving through this city by road, the threat picture is direct and immediate.

Express kidnapping defines the transport risk in CDMX. The method is simple and effective: criminals operating as fake taxi drivers pick up passengers, usually at airports, hotel ranks, or street hails, then drive them between ATMs for hours, draining every account. The FCDO warns specifically that unlicensed taxis in Mexico City are used for robbery and kidnapping (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). This is not a rare event. It is a documented, recurring pattern that targets anyone who negotiates transport with an unvetted driver.

Carjacking is a persistent threat on highway corridors leaving CDMX, particularly the roads toward Puebla and Queretaro. Armed groups stop vehicles at road blockades or in slower traffic segments. Within the city, carjacking patterns shift to intersections in transitional colonias where traffic lights create predictable stopping points. Armed robbery at ATMs, parking garages, and petrol stations fills in the gaps (OSAC).

Protest blockages add an unpredictable layer. Teacher union marches, political demonstrations, and Ayotzinapa rallies regularly shut down Paseo de la Reforma, the Zocalo, and connecting arteries. A vehicle caught in a protest blockage is static, visible, and unable to manoeuvre. For corporate passengers, that exposure window is exactly what route variance and real-time intelligence monitoring are designed to eliminate.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup will increase congestion around the Estadio Azteca corridor and concentrate foreign visitors in predictable zones. Opportunistic criminals adapt to event schedules. Taxi fraud, express kidnapping, and street crime are all projected to rise around match dates (OSAC).

Regulations

Security regulations in Mexico

Private security falls under dual regulation: the federal Direccion General de Seguridad Privada and state-level licensing authorities. Companies must hold both federal and state licences under the Ley Federal de Seguridad Privada (2006). Firearms control sits with SEDENA (Ministry of Defence). Licensed security firms apply for weapons permits through military vetting with ongoing compliance checks. Armed security drivers operate under the same SEDENA authorisation framework as close protection operatives.

Foreign nationals cannot carry weapons in Mexico. This is absolute. Mexican military authorisation for foreign operators is not practically obtainable. Foreign security companies cannot operate directly. They must establish a Mexican legal entity or partner with a locally licensed firm. All security drivers require background checks, psychometric evaluation, drug screening, and certified training under federal standards (Ley Federal de Seguridad Privada, 2006; SEDENA).

Traveller guidance

Transport safety guidance for Mexico City visitors

Never use unlicensed taxis. The FCDO is unambiguous on this point: unlicensed taxis in Mexico City are directly used for robbery and kidnapping (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026). At AICM, purchase taxi vouchers only from the official desk inside the arrivals terminal. Outside the airport, use your security driver or a pre-booked hotel vehicle. Ride-hailing apps offer some protection but do not eliminate all risk. The safest transport is a vetted, pre-positioned vehicle with a driver you have confirmed through your security provider.

Keep to the colonias where security infrastructure matches your profile. Polanco, Condesa, Roma Norte (daylight), Santa Fe, and Lomas de Chapultepec have the road conditions and police presence that reduce transport risk. Once you move east or south, into Iztapalapa, Ecatepec, or the highway corridors beyond the city, the threat profile changes. Tepito is gang-controlled. Do not drive through it, even in transit (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Carjacking patterns in CDMX target intersections with red lights in transitional colonias. If you must drive without a security detail, keep windows up, doors locked, and leave space ahead at traffic lights to allow an escape route. Do not stop for unmarked roadblocks or people flagging you down on highway shoulders. These are known setups.

The metro is a separate risk. Lines 1, 2, and 3 are pickpocketing corridors. After 10pm the system becomes more dangerous. It is not a viable transport option for corporate travellers carrying devices, documents, or anything that marks them as targets.
