Security landscape

Riyadh security overview

Riyadh holds a low overall risk rating of 2/5 (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; US State Dept Level 2; OSAC Low). Crime at 2/5. Kidnapping at 1/5. Civil unrest at 2/5. Terrorism at 3/5 due to Houthi missile and drone attacks from Yemen. For transport planning specifically, the critical number is transport risk at 3/5. That score reflects the reality on the ground: Riyadh's roads are where the danger is.

Saudi Arabia records one of the highest road traffic fatality rates in the Middle East. Riyadh's King Fahd Road, Northern Ring Road, and the network of expressways connecting commercial districts see routine speeds above 140km/h. Lane discipline is poor. Tailgating at high speed is common. Sudden lane changes without signalling are expected, not exceptional. For a visiting executive accustomed to European or North American driving standards, Riyadh's roads are a genuine shock (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Vision 2030 construction compounds the problem. Major infrastructure projects reshape road access across the city on a rolling basis. Diversions appear with minimal warning. Construction vehicles share lanes with passenger traffic. Temporary road surfaces and lane markings add confusion, particularly after dark. A driver who knows this week's road layout is operationally different from one who knew it last month.

Street crime is negligible. Carjacking is virtually unknown. The criminal threat to transport operations in Riyadh is close to zero. What kills people on these roads is physics: high speed, aggressive driving behaviour, and the consequences of a collision at 160km/h.

Regulations

Security regulations in Saudi Arabia

Private security, including secure transport services, operates under the Ministry of Interior (MOI). The General Authority for Private Security is expanding regulatory oversight as part of Vision 2030. Companies must hold an MOI licence. Foreign operators cannot work independently and require a Saudi partner holding at least 51% ownership. Saudization quotas apply across the sector (Ministry of Interior; Vision 2030 framework).

Firearms are prohibited for civilians. Armed drivers are available only through MOI-authorised companies for critical infrastructure or high-profile engagements. Most secure transport in Riyadh operates unarmed because the threat model is road-based, not criminal. The emphasis is on defensive driving certification, route knowledge, and vehicle maintenance rather than ballistic protection. Foreign personnel require kafala sponsorship and MOI background clearance (Ministry of Interior).

Traveller guidance

Transport safety tips for Riyadh

Do not drive yourself unless you have prior experience with Saudi road conditions. The combination of speed, aggressive behaviour, and road infrastructure that changes with construction projects makes self-drive a poor choice for visiting executives. Saudi Arabia allows international driving licences for visitors, but having a legal right to drive is not the same as being operationally safe on these roads (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).

Avoid unlicensed transport. Ride-hailing apps (Uber, Careem) operate in Riyadh and are significantly safer than informal taxi arrangements. But for executives with structured schedules, a dedicated driver eliminates the variability of app-based transport: wait times, unknown drivers, vehicles of inconsistent quality, and routes selected by algorithm rather than local knowledge.

Plan for heat. Riyadh's summer temperatures exceed 50C. Vehicles parked in direct sunlight become dangerously hot within minutes. A professional driver keeps the vehicle running and climate-controlled between stops. If your schedule involves outdoor transitions, construction site visits, or any pedestrian exposure, hydration and shade management are operational concerns, not comfort preferences.

Inter-city routes carry specific risks. The highways between Riyadh and Jeddah, Riyadh and Dammam, and any route south toward the Yemen border region involve long stretches with limited mobile coverage, no service stations, and extreme heat. Breakdowns in these conditions are serious. Satellite phone backup and emergency contingency planning are standard for inter-city secure transport, not optional extras (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026).
