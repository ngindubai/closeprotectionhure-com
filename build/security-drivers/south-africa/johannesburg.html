Transport security in Johannesburg

Johannesburg has a carjacking problem that ranks among the worst on earth. South Africa recorded 22,241 carjackings in the 2023/24 reporting period (South African Police Service Annual Crime Statistics, 2023/24). Gauteng province, which includes Johannesburg, accounts for over 40% of that national total. These are not theoretical risks. They are daily events.

The methods are well documented. Criminals follow targets from OR Tambo International Airport along the M3 and N2, waiting for the vehicle to stop at a traffic light or turn into a residential area. The UK Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office specifically warns travellers about being followed from the airport (FCDO South Africa Travel Advice, April 2026). Smash-and-grab attacks at traffic lights are so common that local drivers treat red lights after dark as advisory rather than mandatory. Armed hijack groups operate at known chokepoints, particularly in the CBD, Hillbrow, and along the M1 corridor.

Load shedding makes everything worse. When Eskom cuts power, traffic lights go dark across entire suburbs. Intersections become uncontrolled. Traffic slows to a crawl. And criminals know the schedule better than most residents do. A darkened intersection at 20:00 in Braamfontein is a different proposition entirely from a lit, controlled junction in Sandton.

Public transport is not an alternative. Minibus taxis are dangerous for visitors: overcrowded, poorly maintained, and the industry itself is plagued by violent turf disputes. Metrorail trains are unsafe at most times of day (OSAC South Africa Crime and Safety Report, 2024). The one exception is the Gautrain, a modern rail system connecting OR Tambo to Sandton, Rosebank, Pretoria, and a handful of other stations. It has private security on platforms and in carriages. For the specific routes it covers, it works. For everything else, you need a vehicle and a driver who knows what they are doing.

E-toll gantries on the N1 and N3 are largely non-functional and widely ignored, so these will not cause unexpected stops. But the highways themselves are not risk-free. The N12 south of the city and sections of the N1 between Johannesburg and Pretoria see regular incidents of vehicles being stopped by objects thrown from overpasses or fake road blocks.

What to expect from a Johannesburg security driver

Your driver is anti-hijack trained. That is the starting point, not a premium add-on. In Johannesburg, anti-hijack driving is a core competency: reading junction approaches for loitering individuals, maintaining vehicle spacing that allows escape routes, and the "rolling through" technique at red lights after dark in high-risk areas where stopping invites approach.

Every journey begins with route planning. Your driver will know which intersections are current hotspots, which suburbs have had recent incidents, and where the nearest response units are positioned along the route. Vehicles carry GPS tracking linked to a 24-hour control room. If a vehicle deviates from its planned route or stops unexpectedly, the control room initiates contact within seconds.

Pattern variation is standard. Same route, same time, same vehicle is a gift to anyone conducting surveillance. Your driver rotates routes between appointments, varies departure times where the schedule allows, and uses counter-surveillance techniques to identify follow vehicles early, particularly on the airport corridor where criminals specifically look for arriving visitors in rental cars or taxis.

Windows stay up. Doors stay locked. Bags go in the boot, not on seats. These are not suggestions. They are standing operating procedures for professional security driving in Johannesburg.

South Africa security driver regulations

Private security in South Africa is regulated by the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA), established under the Private Security Industry Regulation Act 56 of 2001. All security companies and individual operators must hold current PSIRA registration. South Africa has over 2.7 million registered security personnel, making it one of the largest private security industries globally (PSIRA Annual Report, 2023/24).

Armed drivers are available. Firearms are regulated under the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000, and security personnel carrying weapons must hold the appropriate competency certificates and licences. Foreign security personnel can operate in South Africa but require PSIRA registration, valid work permits, and must comply with the same firearms licensing requirements as South African nationals.

Ground transport safety in Johannesburg

Arriving at OR Tambo: confirm your driver's details before you leave the terminal. Name, phone number, vehicle make, colour, and registration. Do not accept approaches from unofficial drivers inside arrivals. Once in the vehicle, the M3 and N2 corridors toward Sandton or the CBD are where follow-from-airport hijackings concentrate. Watch for vehicles that match your turns. Your driver will be doing the same.

Smash-and-grab prevention starts with keeping valuables off the seats. Bags, phones, laptops, anything visible through the window. Criminals target vehicles at traffic lights specifically because they can see what is inside. Windows up, doors locked, and leave enough space to the car ahead to pull out if someone approaches your window.

During load shedding, plan movements around the schedule. Eskom publishes stages and times through the EskomSePush app, widely used across South Africa. When traffic lights are out, intersections become four-way stops. This slows everything down and increases exposure time. If your trip can wait until the lights come back on, wait.

Stick to Sandton, Rosebank, Houghton, Hyde Park, and Melrose Arch where possible. These areas have private security patrols, functioning CCTV, and rapid armed response. The CBD, Hillbrow, Berea, and Yeoville carry significantly higher risk, particularly after dark.
