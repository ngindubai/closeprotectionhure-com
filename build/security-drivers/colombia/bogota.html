Security landscape

Bogota transport security overview

Street taxis are the single biggest threat to foreign visitors in Bogota. That sentence belongs at the top because it drives everything else on this page. The practice is called "paseo millonario" (millionaire's ride): a passenger hails what looks like a normal taxi, the driver picks up accomplices, and the victim is drugged with scopolamine or held at knifepoint while being driven between ATMs to withdraw cash. Sometimes it ends there. Sometimes it escalates to days of captivity. The US State Department and FCDO both flag this specific risk (US State Dept Colombia advisory, April 2026; FCDO Colombia advisory, April 2026).

Scopolamine deserves its own warning. Known locally as "burundanga," it is odourless and tasteless. It can be administered through a drink, blown into your face, or applied to surfaces. Victims lose their will to resist and often have no memory of what happened. Criminals use it in taxis, in bars near El Dorado airport, and in some areas of La Candelaria. This is not urban legend. Colombian police document hundreds of cases annually.

Motorcycle-borne crime is the other constant. Two riders on a motorbike pulling alongside stopped vehicles to grab phones, watches, or bags through open windows. It happens in traffic jams across the city and at traffic lights in less secure neighbourhoods. Bogota's extreme congestion makes this worse because vehicles spend long periods stationary.

El Dorado International Airport arrivals carry specific risks. Unlicensed taxis operate around the terminal. Some are fronts for kidnapping crews. Use only pre-booked transfers from a known provider, or registered rideshare apps (Uber, Didi, InDriver) booked from inside the terminal. Never accept a ride from anyone who approaches you in the arrivals hall.

TransMilenio, the bus rapid transit system, has documented problems with pickpocketing and armed robbery at stations, particularly after dark and on crowded routes. It is not a safe transport option for visitors carrying valuables or travelling outside peak daylight hours.

Regulations

Security regulations in Colombia

Private security in Colombia is regulated by SuperVigilancia (Superintendencia de Vigilancia y Seguridad Privada). All security companies, including those providing driver services, must hold valid SuperVigilancia registration. Armed security personnel require additional licensing. Weapons used in private security operations must be registered through INDUMIL (Industria Militar de Colombia), the state military industry body that controls civilian firearms (SuperVigilancia regulations, April 2026).

Armed drivers are legal in Colombia and common in Bogota's corporate security sector. Foreign security firms must work through Colombian-registered companies. Foreign nationals cannot carry firearms in Colombia without specific authorization, which is rarely granted. If you require armed transport, the driver or accompanying CPO will be a locally licensed Colombian professional.

Traveller guidance

Transport security tips for Bogota

Never hail a street taxi in Bogota. This is the single most important piece of advice on this page. Not at the airport, not outside your hotel, not after dinner, not in an emergency. The express kidnapping risk via fake taxis is real, documented, and ongoing. Use Uber, Didi, InDriver, or a pre-vetted security driver. There is no scenario where hailing a street taxi makes sense for a foreign visitor in Bogota.

Be aware of scopolamine. Do not accept drinks from strangers, even in upscale areas. Do not leave drinks unattended. In taxis or vehicles you did not arrange yourself, keep windows up. Criminals have been known to blow scopolamine powder through open car windows at traffic stops. If you feel suddenly confused, disoriented, or excessively compliant, something is wrong. Get to a public space, call your security contact, or call 123 immediately.

TransMilenio stations are pickpocketing and robbery hotspots, especially on crowded routes and after dark. Avoid the system entirely if you are carrying valuables, electronics, or travelling after sunset. The stations at Portal del Norte and Portal Americas have particularly poor reputations.

For general movement: keep vehicle windows up and doors locked, do not display phones or watches at traffic lights, and avoid La Candelaria after dark, El Bronx, Los Martires, and Ciudad Bolivar entirely. Zona T, Usaquen, Chapinero Alto, and Parque 93 are the safer commercial and dining areas.
