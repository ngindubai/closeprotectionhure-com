Security landscape

Mumbai security overview

India records one of the highest road accident death rates in the world. In Mumbai, that statistic is personal. The city's roads are a collision of overloaded trucks, autorickshaws cutting across lanes, pedestrians crossing highways, potholes that swallow wheels, and the occasional cow standing in the middle of a dual carriageway. For visitors unfamiliar with these conditions, driving in Mumbai is not just uncomfortable. It is genuinely dangerous.

Beyond road safety, Mumbai carries a terrorism risk rated "very likely" by the FCDO. The 2008 attacks targeted locations across the city, including CST railway station, a major transport node. Hotels, commercial areas, and transport infrastructure remain on threat lists. Transport between these locations requires awareness of the security implications, not just the traffic conditions (FCDO travel advisory, April 2026; US State Dept Level 2).

Civil unrest adds unpredictability. Bandh (shutdown) protests can close roads across the city at short notice. Religious and political tensions trigger marches and demonstrations that block key routes. During monsoon season (June to September), severe flooding shuts roads entirely. Some areas become impassable for hours. The city's transport network is fragile in ways that catch visitors off guard.

Mumbai's local trains carry millions daily but are dangerously overcrowded. People fall from moving carriages. For security-conscious travel, they are not an option. A vetted driver with local knowledge is the baseline requirement for corporate visitors (OSAC crime rating: High).

Regulations

Security regulations in India

Private security in India falls under the Private Security Agencies Regulation Act (PSARA, 2005), with licensing administered at the state level. Maharashtra has its own PSARA registration process. Security transport providers operating in Mumbai need state-level compliance, and operating across state lines (Mumbai to Pune in Maharashtra is fine, but Mumbai to Goa crosses a border) may require additional clearances.

Firearms are heavily restricted by the Arms Act (1959). Security drivers do not carry weapons. Armoured vehicles are available in India for banking and high-profile clients through authorised providers, but the standard model for corporate secure transport is a well-maintained vehicle with a trained driver and route planning rather than plating and ballistic glass.

Foreign security companies must establish an Indian subsidiary or joint venture and register under PSARA. All drivers undergo police-verified background checks. India's private security workforce is the world's largest at over 9 million people, but standards range enormously. Vetting is not optional (PSARA, 2005; Arms Act, 1959).

Traveller guidance

Security tips for visitors to Mumbai

Never use unlicensed taxis or accept rides from touts at BOM airport. Pre-booked transport eliminates this risk entirely. If you must use a taxi, only use the prepaid taxi counter inside the terminal.

Avoid Mumbai's local trains. They are the cheapest way to cross the city, and they are dangerously overcrowded. People die falling from moving carriages. Women face particular risk of harassment and assault in crowded compartments. Autorickshaws and app-based ride services are safer for short distances, though neither offers the security awareness of a vetted driver.

Road conditions vary wildly. Main highways between Mumbai and Pune have improved, but city streets feature potholes, no lane discipline, and mixed traffic (trucks, autorickshaws, pedestrians, cattle). During monsoon, roads flood within hours of heavy rain. Low-lying areas near Hindmata and Sion become impassable. Avoid non-essential travel during heavy downpours.

If you are driving at night, especially outside Mumbai on highways, know that many heavy goods vehicles operate without rear lights. Broken-down trucks are often left in traffic lanes with no warning markers. Defensive driving is not just a training certificate. It is a survival skill on Indian highways.
