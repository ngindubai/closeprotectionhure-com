Indonesia security risk overview

Indonesia is Southeast Asia's largest economy and a growing destination for foreign investment, but its security landscape carries real complexity. Jakarta has a moderate overall risk profile, though the terrorism threat remains elevated following historical attacks on hotels and commercial targets. The FCDO advises against all travel to parts of Papua and Central Sulawesi.

Security landscape

Security situation in Indonesia

Indonesia's archipelago of over 17,000 islands creates a fragmented security environment where threat levels vary dramatically between regions. Jakarta itself carries moderate crime risk, but the country's terrorism threat is elevated. The 2002 Bali bombings, the 2003 JW Marriott attack, the 2009 repeat Marriott and Ritz-Carlton bombings, and the 2016 Starbucks/police post attack in central Jakarta demonstrate that Western commercial targets are within the operational reach of Jemaah Islamiyah (JI) affiliates and IS-linked cells. Indonesian counter-terrorism forces (Densus 88) have disrupted dozens of plots since 2016, but the threat has not been eliminated.

Street crime in Jakarta is opportunistic rather than organised. Motorcycle-borne bag snatching is common, particularly in congested traffic. Taxi fraud, petty theft in crowded markets, and occasional ATM skimming affect foreign visitors. Violent crime targeting foreign nationals is rare but not absent. Demonstrations and protests, particularly around government buildings and embassies, can turn unpredictable. Jakarta traffic congestion (routinely ranked among the world's worst by TomTom Traffic Index, 2024) is itself a security factor, significantly lengthening exposure time and complicating evacuation routes.

Outside Java, the picture changes. Papua province has an active separatist conflict with the Free Papua Organisation (OPM) carrying out periodic attacks on security forces and mining operations. Central Sulawesi has seen jihadist activity by the East Indonesia Mujahideen (MIT). Aceh province applies Sharia law, though this affects personal conduct regulations rather than security threat levels for transiting business travellers. Indonesia's zero-tolerance drug laws carry a mandatory death penalty for trafficking, a legal risk that operators must brief all principals on.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Indonesia

Indonesia's private security sector is regulated under Government Regulation No. 43 of 2012 on the Implementation of Security and Public Order (Kamtibmas). Companies must obtain licensing from the Indonesian National Police (Polri) to operate. The market includes a mix of local providers and international security firms with Jakarta offices, though all providers must hold Indonesian licensing regardless of their parent company's origin.

Firearms are not available to private security operators. Executive protection in Indonesia operates on an unarmed basis, similar to Thailand. Protection relies on advance planning, route management, counter-surveillance, and coordination with Polri for high-profile movements. For principals travelling to higher-risk areas outside Java, police escort arrangements can be coordinated through local authorities, though these require advance written requests and are subject to approval timelines.

Foreign security professionals cannot operate independently in Indonesia. Work permits (KITAS) are required, and unlicensed foreign operators risk arrest and deportation. Drug laws carry extreme penalties, including the death sentence for trafficking offences. All EP teams must include this in their pre-deployment legal briefing. The regulatory environment favours established local-international partnerships over fly-in teams attempting to operate independently.

Foreign companies must establish an Indonesian entity (PT PMA) and may face restrictions under the negative investment list. A local partner is typically required.
