Moscow risk assessment: security analysis for travellers and business visitors

The UK FCDO advises against all travel to Russia. That is the starting point, not a footnote. Moscow presents an operating environment where terrorism from multiple vectors (IS, North Caucasus groups, and ongoing Ukrainian military drone strikes on the city), arbitrary state detention, and severe restrictions on civil liberties combine to create a threat picture unlike any other P1 city on this list. The March 2024 Crocus City concert hall attack killed 145 people. Drone strikes on Moscow are now routine. If you must travel, executive protection is not a luxury. It is a baseline requirement.

Crime

Crime in Moscow

Street crime in Moscow is moderate compared to other P1 cities, but it follows specific patterns that catch visitors off guard. Pickpocketing and mugging concentrate around main railway stations, tourist areas near Red Square, and the Moscow Metro late at night. The FCDO flags drink spiking as a particular concern: victims have been left unconscious outdoors, which is life-threatening in Moscow's winter temperatures (FCDO Russia travel advice, April 2026).

Unregistered taxis are a vehicle for robbery. A passenger flags down an unmarked car, a second person gets in at the next stop, and the victim is robbed during the ride. Fake police scams work similarly. Individuals in police-style uniforms approach foreign nationals, demand to inspect documents and wallets, then take cash. Real police may also solicit bribes during random passport checks.

Methanol poisoning from counterfeit alcohol is a documented risk. Buy spirits only from verified retailers. Racial discrimination and physical attacks against African and Asian visitors remain a persistent problem, particularly near football stadiums and in outer residential districts (US State Department Human Rights Report, Russia, 2025). Moscow is not equally dangerous for all visitors. Skin colour changes the threat profile.

Terrorism

Terrorism and political violence

This is the defining risk in Moscow. On 22 March 2024, gunmen attacked the Crocus City Concert Hall, killing 145 people. IS (Daesh) claimed responsibility (BBC News, 23 March 2024). In June 2024, coordinated attacks on synagogues and churches in Dagestan killed more than 20 people. These are not historical footnotes. They represent active, current capability from multiple threat actors.

The ongoing conflict with Ukraine adds a dimension no other European capital faces. Drone attacks on Moscow and the Moscow Oblast are now a recurring event. Flight delays and diversions from Vnukovo, Domodedovo, and Sheremetyevo airports are frequent. Some strikes have reached residential areas. The timing and location of attacks are unpredictable, and there is no reliable warning system for visitors.

North Caucasus insurgent groups retain intent and, as demonstrated, capability. IS-affiliated networks have carried out the largest single attack on Russian soil in two decades. The Russian government's own terror threat level has remained elevated since 2024. For foreign nationals, the risk is compounded by the possibility of being caught in an incident with no clear evacuation route and limited consular support.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Traditional criminal kidnapping for ransom is low in Moscow. That is the good news. The bad news is that the primary detention risk comes from the state itself.

The FCDO warns that foreign nationals in Russia face the risk of arbitrary arrest and prolonged detention (FCDO Russia travel advice, April 2026). Dual nationals and those with any connection to Ukraine are at heightened risk. Social media activity supporting Ukraine, criticising the Russian military, or even sharing news articles that contradict the official narrative can result in prosecution under Russian law.

Once detained, consular access is extremely limited. The FCDO states clearly that its ability to assist detained British nationals in Russia is "extremely limited" (FCDO Russia travel advice, April 2026). Legal proceedings can be opaque, lengthy, and conducted entirely in Russian with no guarantee of translation. This is not a theoretical concern. Multiple Western nationals have been detained on charges that Western governments consider politically motivated.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Public protest in Russia is functionally illegal. Under current legislation, a single individual standing with a blank piece of paper can be arrested for participating in an unauthorised demonstration. The authorities have made the rules clear: dissent is not tolerated.

This matters for foreign nationals because the threshold for "political activity" is extremely low. Attending a gathering that authorities classify as a protest, posting on social media about Russian politics, or even being in the wrong place at the wrong time during a police sweep can result in deportation or criminal prosecution. Foreign nationals have been fined, detained, and expelled for activities that would be unremarkable in any Western democracy.

The practical implication: stay away from any gathering that could be interpreted as political. Do not photograph police or military personnel. Do not discuss politics in public spaces. The surveillance environment in Moscow is extensive, and the authorities have both the capability and the willingness to act on minor infractions by foreigners.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Moscow's Metro is generally safe, operates with airport-style security screening at stations, and is the fastest way to move through the city. That said, late-night travel on the Metro carries pickpocketing risk, and certain interchange stations become crowded in ways that create opportunity for theft.

Road conditions outside Moscow's main highways deteriorate quickly. Traffic police (DPS) regularly stop vehicles for document checks. These stops can turn into requests for cash payments, particularly if the driver is foreign or the vehicle has non-Russian plates. The FCDO warns against flagging down taxis or sharing rides with strangers (FCDO Russia travel advice, April 2026). Use app-based services (Yandex Go) or pre-arranged transport exclusively.

Air travel is disrupted. Drone attacks on Moscow have caused repeated flight delays, diversions, and temporary airport closures. There are no direct flights between Russia and the UK or EU member states. Any itinerary must route through third countries (Turkey, UAE, Serbia), which adds complexity and potential points of failure. Passport theft at Moscow airports has been reported. Keep travel documents secure and carry copies separately.

Areas

Areas of Moscow: where is safer, where is not

Lower-risk areas

  • Central tourist areas in daylight (Red Square, Kremlin vicinity)
  • Tverskaya district (main commercial street, higher police presence)
  • Upscale hotel districts (Marriott, Four Seasons, Ritz-Carlton areas)
  • Moscow City business district (International Business Centre)

Higher-risk areas

  • Main railway stations at night (Kazansky, Leningradsky, Yaroslavsky)
  • Outskirts and industrial districts (limited police coverage)
  • Areas near airports during drone attacks (unpredictable strike zones)
  • Anywhere during active drone or missile strikes (no safe zone guaranteed)
  • Outer residential districts after dark (racial attacks documented)
Local security

Police and local security forces

Russian police operate within an authoritarian framework. They are effective at crowd control and maintaining order in central Moscow, but interactions with foreign nationals carry specific risks. Random passport checks are common, and officers may detain individuals for up to 48 hours without charge (FCDO Russia travel advice, April 2026). Foreign nationals are disproportionately targeted for these checks, and requests for cash to "resolve" the situation are well-documented.

The fake police scam is a variation: individuals wearing convincing uniforms approach foreigners, demand to inspect documents and currency, then confiscate cash. Genuine officers rarely ask to see cash. If stopped, comply calmly, ask for identification, and note badge numbers. Do not hand over your passport; show it but retain physical possession.

For serious incidents, the FCDO's capacity to intervene is minimal. The British Embassy in Moscow operates under severe constraints. Any foreign national detained should expect limited consular access, slow communication, and a legal system that does not prioritise the rights of foreign defendants.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Moscow

The primary recommendation is simple: do not travel to Russia. The FCDO advises against all travel. The US State Department rates it Level 4 ("Do Not Travel"). These are not precautionary suggestions. They reflect an assessment that the risk to foreign nationals is severe and that consular protection is effectively unavailable if something goes wrong.

If travel is genuinely unavoidable (and the threshold for "unavoidable" should be very high), executive protection is not optional. It is a baseline requirement. A close protection team with local knowledge, Russian language capability, and established contacts within Moscow's security infrastructure provides the minimum viable support structure for any foreign national operating in this environment.

The value is not just physical security. It is situational awareness: knowing which areas are affected by drone strikes, which routes are blocked, where police checkpoints are active, and how to respond if detained. A good local team also provides a communication bridge to your embassy and to extraction resources if the situation deteriorates.

No security arrangement removes the risks outlined on this page. The terrorism threat is unpredictable. State detention is arbitrary.
