Security landscape

Security situation in Russia

Russia poses the highest risk level of any country in this assessment series. The FCDO cites the conflict in Ukraine, the risk of arbitrary detention of foreign nationals, and limited consular assistance as primary reasons for the travel ban (FCDO Travel Advice: Russia, updated April 2026). The US State Department echoes these concerns, adding the potential for harassment and detention of US citizens (US State Department Travel Advisory: Russia, February 2026).

Moscow carries a critical overall risk score of 5/5. OSAC rates countrywide crime as HIGH (OSAC Crime and Safety Report: Russia, 2025). Terrorism risk is scored at the maximum 5/5. Drone and missile strikes related to the Ukraine conflict have reached Moscow Oblast. Domestic extremism and state-directed threats to foreign nationals add layers of risk that do not exist elsewhere in this series.

Street crime in Moscow is moderate at 3/5. Petty theft and scams target foreigners, though violent street crime is not the primary concern. The real risks are political. Arbitrary detention, surveillance, and the weaponisation of legal processes against foreign nationals have escalated since 2022. Several Western citizens have been detained on charges widely viewed as politically motivated.

Kidnapping risk is 2/5 in the conventional sense. However, state detention effectively functions as a form of hostage-taking in the current political environment. Western governments have limited ability to secure the release of detained nationals. Consular access has been restricted.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Russia

Russia's private security sector is regulated under Federal Law No. 2487-1 "On Private Detective and Security Activity." Licensed Russian security firms dominate the market. Western security companies have largely withdrawn since 2022 due to sanctions, operational risks, and reputational concerns.

Foreign operators face severe legal restrictions. Independent operation by foreign security personnel is effectively impossible. State surveillance of security operations is pervasive. Communication security is compromised by Russian monitoring capabilities, and encrypted communication tools face legal restrictions under Russian law.

If travel to Russia is unavoidable, engage a vetted Russian-licensed security firm with a verified track record. Assume all communications are monitored. Operate on the basis that extraction options are extremely limited. Pre-travel legal briefings, embassy registration, and a detailed contingency plan are not optional. They are the minimum standard for any visit.

Western sanctions, the current geopolitical situation, and expanding Russian legislation that criminalises activities "against Russian interests" make it unfeasible for Western security firms to operate there. Russian-licensed private security companies (CHOPs) dominate the domestic market.

Rosgvardia (the National Guard of Russia) issues federal licences for private security and detective activities. All security personnel must hold Russian citizenship, pass FSB/MVD background checks, and complete Rosgvardia-certified training. The system is closed to foreign nationals.

Licensed CHOPs (private security companies) can carry firearms registered with Rosgvardia. Armoured vehicles are available for VIP clients. Following the Crocus City Hall attack in March 2024, weapons legislation has tightened and additional security measures apply across the industry.

The FCDO rates a terrorist attack as "very likely." The March 2024 Crocus City Hall attack killed 145 people, with IS claiming responsibility. The ongoing conflict with Ukraine has brought drone and missile attacks to Moscow and western Russia, creating an unpredictable security environment.

The FCDO warns that foreign nationals risk arbitrary arrest and prolonged detention for activities authorities deem hostile to Russian interests. This includes social media posts supporting Ukraine. British consular support is extremely limited. A single person can constitute a "protest" under Russian law.

Police conduct random passport checks and may detain foreign nationals for up to 48 hours without charge. Fake police scams exist where criminals impersonate officers to rob tourists. The FCDO's ability to assist detained British nationals is severely restricted, making self-reliance and advance planning critical.
