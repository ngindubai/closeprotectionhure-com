Crime

Crime in Nairobi

Violent crime in Nairobi is a daily reality, not an occasional risk. Mugging, carjacking, armed robbery and home invasions affect residents and visitors across the city, though frequency varies sharply by neighbourhood (OSAC Kenya Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

The central business district becomes dangerous after dark. River Road and the area around Uhuru Park are consistent hotspots for mugging and bag-snatching. Criminals often work in groups of two or three, and many are armed with machetes or cheap firearms. Resistance typically escalates violence.

Eastleigh, Kibera and Mathare carry the highest risk profiles. These areas see daily armed incidents. Even during daylight hours, foreign nationals attract attention and face a high probability of being targeted. South B and South C become progressively more dangerous after sunset.

Carjacking is a particular problem. Criminals target vehicles at traffic lights, speed bumps and junction queues. The FCDO specifically warns British nationals about carjacking in Nairobi (FCDO Kenya travel advice, February 2026). Vehicles are sometimes followed from shopping centres or restaurants before being ambushed at a quieter point on the route.

Home invasions target properties in wealthier suburbs, including areas like Lavington and Kilimani that are otherwise considered safer. Gangs conduct reconnaissance and strike houses with perceived weak perimeter security.

Terrorism

Terrorism and political violence

Nairobi has experienced two major terrorist attacks in the last decade, both by Al Shabaab. The Westgate shopping mall attack in September 2013 killed 67 people. The DusitD2 hotel complex attack in January 2019 killed 21 (Kenya National Police Service statement, January 2019). Both targeted venues frequented by foreign nationals and affluent Kenyans.

Al Shabaab, the Somalia-based Al Qaeda affiliate, considers Kenya a legitimate target because of Kenya's military presence in Somalia under the African Union mission. The group has repeatedly stated its intent to strike Nairobi again. The FCDO assesses that there is a "heightened threat of terrorism" across Kenya (FCDO Kenya travel advice, February 2026).

Shopping malls, international hotels, transport hubs and government buildings are the most likely targets. The DusitD2 attack demonstrated that even well-secured hotel complexes in upscale areas are not immune. Security screening at major malls has improved since 2013, but the threat remains. Maintain awareness. Know exits. Avoid predictable routines at high-profile locations.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping in Kenya takes three forms, and Nairobi is exposed to all of them. Al Shabaab conducts terrorist kidnappings, primarily in the border areas with Somalia and in coastal Kenya, but the group has operational capacity in Nairobi (FCDO Kenya travel advice, February 2026).

Criminal ransom kidnapping targets anyone perceived as having money. Expatriates, business executives and wealthy Kenyans are all in the target profile. These incidents are planned. Targets are watched and assessed before the grab. Ransom demands vary widely, from tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Express kidnapping follows the same pattern seen across East Africa: the victim is held for hours while being driven to ATMs. This is opportunistic and can happen anywhere in the city, though it is more common in commercial areas and along the Mombasa Road corridor.

Nairobi's outskirts carry the highest kidnapping risk. Areas near the Eastleigh corridor and along the routes toward the Somali border see regular incidents. Within central Nairobi, the risk is lower but present. Varying routes and times of travel is a basic precaution.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Nairobi sees periodic protests driven by election disputes, cost of living pressures and government policy disagreements. The 2023 finance bill protests brought large crowds onto the streets of central Nairobi and several people were killed by police gunfire (Human Rights Watch, July 2023).

Kenyan police respond to unrest with tear gas as a first measure and have used live ammunition on multiple occasions. Election periods are particularly volatile. The 2017 post-election period saw weeks of protest and sporadic violence across Nairobi.

For travellers, the risk is disruption and collateral exposure, not targeted attack. Avoid the CBD during announced protest days. Keep away from political rallies. If you encounter a demonstration, leave the area immediately. Do not attempt to photograph police or security forces during unrest.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Jomo Kenyatta International Airport (NBO) is the main entry point. Use pre-arranged transfers with known operators. Unofficial taxis at the airport are associated with robbery and overcharging (OSAC Kenya Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

Carjacking is a serious risk on Nairobi's roads, particularly on Mombasa Road and Thika Road after dark. Criminals target vehicles at traffic lights and during the heavy congestion that affects most major routes during rush hours. Keep doors locked, windows up and valuables out of sight.

"Matatus" (minibus taxis) are the standard form of public transport. They are overcrowded, poorly maintained and regular targets for pickpockets and armed robbers. Matatus are not recommended for foreign visitors. Ride-hailing apps offer a better option, though drivers should be verified before entering the vehicle.

Local security

Police and local security forces

The Kenya Police Service has a poor reputation for professionalism and accountability. Corruption is widespread. Officers at checkpoints and traffic stops routinely expect payment, and refusing can result in delays or fabricated charges (US State Department Kenya Travel Advisory, January 2026).

Response times are unreliable, particularly outside the wealthier suburbs. In areas like Westlands and Karen, police may arrive within 15 to 30 minutes. In other parts of the city, response can take hours. Do not count on a rapid police response in an emergency.

Private security is the norm for businesses and residences in Nairobi. Companies like G4S, KK Security and Wells Fargo (Kenya) operate armed response units that often arrive faster than police.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Nairobi

Nairobi is a high-risk city with a specific terrorism dimension that separates it from most African capitals. The Al Shabaab threat means that hotels, malls and transport routes carry risks that go beyond ordinary street crime.

Business travellers staying within the Westlands, Karen or Gigiri corridors and moving only during daylight hours may manage with a vetted security driver and sensible precautions. This is the minimum. Anyone moving across the city, attending events at hotels or conference centres, or operating on a published schedule should have close protection.
