Kenya security risk overview

Kenya is East Africa's primary commercial hub, but it carries a terrorism threat that has produced mass-casualty attacks within the last decade. The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to border areas and parts of the coast. Street crime in Nairobi is persistent and often violent.

Security landscape

Security situation in Kenya

Kenya's threat environment is shaped by two factors: al-Shabaab's persistent cross-border operations from Somalia, and high levels of urban crime concentrated in Nairobi, Mombasa, and Kisumu. The 2013 Westgate mall attack (67 killed) and the 2019 DusitD2 hotel complex attack (21 killed) demonstrated that commercial and hospitality targets in central Nairobi are not immune. Al-Shabaab has stated intent to continue targeting Kenya for its military presence in Somalia under AMISOM (now ATMIS).

Street crime is the more likely daily threat. Muggings, carjackings, and phone snatching are routine across Nairobi. Matatu routes and pedestrian overpasses are known hotspots. Armed home invasions occur in residential areas, including those considered upmarket. The police response is inconsistent. In some cases it is non-existent.

Political violence flares around election cycles. The 2017 and 2022 elections both produced localised unrest, roadblocks, and clashes between opposition supporters and security forces. Travellers should plan around the political calendar. The northeast border counties (Garissa, Mandera, Wajir) remain high-threat areas where al-Shabaab conducts IED attacks and ambushes targeting security forces and civilians.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Kenya

Kenya's private security industry is one of the largest in Africa, with over 500,000 registered security guards across the country. The sector is regulated by the Private Security Regulatory Authority (PSRA), established under the Private Security Regulation Act of 2016. Before the PSRA, the industry operated with minimal oversight. Regulation is improving, but enforcement remains uneven.

The PSRA uses a grade system (A through G) to classify different security roles and their requirements. Close protection operatives fall under the higher grades and require specific training certifications. Foreign security firms must register locally through the PSRA before conducting any operations. This applies equally to close protection assignments and static guarding contracts.

Major international operators including G4S Kenya, KK Security, and Securex maintain significant operations in the country. These firms generally meet international training and vetting standards. Smaller domestic firms vary widely in quality. Kenya does not permit private security operatives to carry firearms as standard, though exceptions exist for specific licensed roles and high-threat assignments.
