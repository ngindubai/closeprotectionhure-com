Philippines security risk overview

The Philippines presents a split security picture. Metro Manila and Cebu function as major business centres with manageable urban crime, while Mindanao and the Sulu Archipelago remain active conflict zones where Islamist militant groups operate with lethal capability. The FCDO advises against all travel to western and central Mindanao and the Sulu Archipelago.

Security landscape

Security situation in the Philippines

The Philippines' security environment is shaped by two distinct threat profiles. In Luzon and the Visayas (including Metro Manila), the primary concerns are street crime, petty theft, and occasional politically motivated violence. In Mindanao and the Sulu Archipelago, the threat shifts to armed insurgency, kidnap-for-ransom operations, and terrorist attacks by groups including Abu Sayyaf, the Bangsamoro Islamic Freedom Fighters (BIFF), and remnants of the Maute Group that besieged Marawi in 2017.

Manila itself is a city of sharp contrasts. Makati and Bonifacio Global City (BGC) are heavily secured with private guards, metal detectors, and bag checks at entrances to malls, hotels, and office buildings. This level of visible security speaks to the baseline threat. Outside these commercial enclaves, areas like Tondo, Quiapo, and parts of Manila's port district have significantly higher crime rates. Pickpocketing, robbery, and opportunistic targeting of foreigners are common concerns for travellers without local orientation.

Kidnapping is a persistent risk throughout the archipelago. In Mindanao, it is a funding mechanism for militant groups. In Metro Manila and Cebu, express kidnapping targets both Filipino nationals and foreign business visitors. The Philippine National Police (PNP) Anti-Kidnapping Group reported continued operations against syndicates in 2024 (PNP Annual Report, 2024). Typhoon season (June to November) is an additional operational factor, disrupting transport infrastructure and creating secondary security vulnerabilities during evacuations.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in the Philippines

Private security in the Philippines is regulated by the Philippine National Police Supervisory Office for Security and Investigation Agencies (PNP-SOSIA). Security agencies must hold a valid License to Operate, and individual guards require a Security Guard License. The industry employs an estimated 600,000 licensed personnel (PNP-SOSIA, 2024), making it one of the largest private security workforces in Southeast Asia.

Armed security is legal and widespread. Guards at shopping centres, hotels, banks, and residential compounds routinely carry sidearms. For executive protection assignments, licensed agencies can deploy armed close protection officers, though weapons must be registered and operators must hold the appropriate firearms permits under Republic Act 10591 (Firearms and Ammunition Regulation Act).

Quality varies significantly across the industry. Well-established agencies in Manila with corporate clients maintain training and vetting standards. Smaller provincial operators may not. Foreign security companies cannot operate directly without local licensing, and working with unregistered providers creates serious legal exposure under Philippine law.

The legal framework is RA 5487, as amended by RA 11917 in 2022. All guards must hold a valid PNP-SOSIA licence, pass NBI clearance, and complete annual in-service training.

The Philippines applies a 60-40 ownership rule under its constitution. Foreign investors cannot hold a majority stake in a Philippine security company. Foreign firms must partner with a Filipino-owned entity to operate. This restriction is actively enforced.
