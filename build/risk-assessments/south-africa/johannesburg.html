Crime

Crime in Johannesburg

Johannesburg has one of the highest violent crime rates of any city worldwide. Carjacking is the defining threat. The FCDO specifically warns about criminals following vehicles from OR Tambo International Airport along the M3 and N2 highways (FCDO South Africa travel advice, March 2026). Travellers have been ambushed within minutes of leaving the airport.

Armed robbery is pervasive. ATM muggings are common, particularly at standalone machines or those in poorly lit areas. Smash-and-grab attacks target vehicles stopped at traffic lights. Criminals break side windows and seize bags, laptops and phones from the passenger seat before the light changes.

Home invasions affect both high-end suburbs and middle-class areas. In Sandton and surrounding neighbourhoods, armed gangs breach perimeter walls and electronic security systems. The violence in these incidents is often extreme. Victims are tied up and threatened at gunpoint while the property is stripped.

Load shedding (scheduled power outages) creates a distinctive and dangerous dynamic. When the electricity cuts, electric fences, CCTV cameras, alarm systems and traffic lights all go dark simultaneously. Criminal activity spikes during these windows. The South African Police Service (SAPS) recorded over 9,000 carjacking incidents in Gauteng province in the 2023/24 reporting year (SAPS Annual Crime Statistics, September 2024).

The inner city (Hillbrow, Joubert Park, the CBD after dark) is effectively ungoverned from a street safety perspective. Foreign nationals are targeted regularly. Do not walk in these areas.

Terrorism

Terrorism and political violence

Terrorism risk in Johannesburg is low. South Africa has not experienced a significant terrorist attack in recent years, and no groups currently demonstrate the intent and capability to strike in Johannesburg. The US State Department does not cite terrorism as a primary concern for South Africa (US State Department South Africa Travel Advisory, January 2026).

That said, the country's porous borders and the presence of various international groups in the region mean the risk is not zero. For practical purposes, though, terrorism should not be a primary planning consideration in Johannesburg. The overwhelmingly dominant threats are criminal.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping in Johannesburg is increasing. SAPS reported a year-on-year rise in kidnapping incidents nationally, with Gauteng province (which includes Johannesburg) accounting for the largest share (SAPS Annual Crime Statistics, September 2024).

Three types of kidnapping operate in the city. Criminal ransom kidnapping targets business owners, professionals and anyone perceived as wealthy. Ransom demands are typically negotiated down from initial figures, but the process can last days or weeks. Violence during captivity is common.

Express kidnapping follows the standard pattern: victims are forced to withdraw cash from multiple ATMs before release. This is opportunistic and can happen to anyone.

Carjack-kidnapping is a distinctly Johannesburg variant. The victim is taken along with the vehicle, driven to a secondary location, and robbed of everything. Sometimes this escalates into a ransom situation if the criminals believe the victim has significant resources. The routes from OR Tambo airport and areas around Sandton are the highest-risk zones for this type of incident.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

South Africa has a strong protest culture. In Johannesburg, service delivery protests (over water, electricity and housing shortages) are frequent and can block major roads with burning tyres and debris. These are typically limited to township areas but can spill onto main highways without warning.

Xenophobic violence is a recurring pattern. In July 2021, widespread looting and unrest across Gauteng and KwaZulu-Natal caused over 300 deaths and billions of rand in damage (South African Human Rights Commission report, 2022). While triggered by a specific political event, the unrest exposed deep social tensions that remain unresolved.

Load shedding frustration adds another layer. Communities losing power for extended periods have blocked roads and targeted Eskom infrastructure. For travellers, the risk is being caught in roadblocks rather than being directly targeted. Monitor local news and maintain alternative route options.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Carjacking defines the transport risk in Johannesburg. Traffic light stops are ambush points. Criminals position themselves at red lights and approach vehicles, often armed. The FCDO warns against stopping at red lights in isolated areas after dark (FCDO South Africa travel advice, March 2026). Some locals treat red lights as yield signs after dark for this reason.

The route from OR Tambo International Airport to the city and northern suburbs is a known hunting ground for follow-from-airport criminals. They identify arriving passengers with luggage and follow them to a quieter location. Pre-arranged pickup by a security-conscious driver is not optional. It is the baseline.

The Gautrain (rapid rail) is the safest form of public transport, connecting the airport, Sandton, Rosebank and Pretoria. It is well-managed and heavily secured. Minibus taxis are dangerous and not recommended. Metrorail (commuter rail) has experienced armed robberies on board and at stations. Avoid it.

Local security

Police and local security forces

The South African Police Service (SAPS) is under-resourced and stretched thin. Response times in Johannesburg are slow, often exceeding 30 minutes even for violent crime in progress. In less affluent areas, police may not respond at all (OSAC South Africa Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

South Africa's private security industry is one of the largest in the world. The Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA) regulates over 2 million registered security officers, outnumbering the police and military combined. Armed response companies operate across Johannesburg and typically arrive faster than SAPS.

This is a city where private security is not an upgrade. It is the expected standard of care. Most residential properties and commercial premises rely on private security as their primary protection, with SAPS as a backup at best.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Johannesburg

Johannesburg is a city where private security is not a luxury. It is the norm. Over 2 million registered private security officers operate across South Africa precisely because the police cannot provide adequate protection. The question is not whether you need security, but what level is appropriate for your movements.

At minimum, every business traveller should use a vetted security driver for all airport transfers and city movements. The follow-from-airport carjacking threat alone justifies this. If your itinerary includes evening events, movements outside the Sandton/Rosebank corridor, or you are carrying visible equipment or assets, close protection is the right call.

Load shedding schedules are unpredictable and change daily. During outages, the entire security landscape shifts. Having a professional who understands the local pattern, knows which routes become dangerous when the lights go out, and can adapt in real time is a material advantage. Common questions

Frequently asked questions: Johannesburg security

Johannesburg is rated critical risk in our assessment framework. South Africa has one of the highest violent crime rates globally, and Johannesburg is the country's crime epicentre. The SAPS Annual Crime Statistics 2024/25 recorded over 27,000 murders nationally, with Gauteng province accounting for a disproportionate share.

Johannesburg and Cape Town compete for the highest crime numbers depending on the category. For business travellers, Johannesburg presents the greater carjacking and armed robbery risk. Cape Town's murder rate is driven by gang activity in the Cape Flats, which rarely overlaps with business travel routes. Johannesburg's crime is more dispersed across areas that visitors actually use.

Carjacking is the signature crime risk. Gauteng province records the highest carjacking numbers in South Africa (SAPS Annual Crime Statistics, 2024/25). Follow-from-airport attacks are a specific concern: criminals monitor OR Tambo International arrivals and follow vehicles to their destination. Targeting happens at compound gates, traffic lights and highway off-ramps.

Kidnapping has escalated significantly in South Africa over the past five years. While much of the activity targets local business owners, foreign nationals are not exempt. Both express kidnapping (ATM withdrawal, release) and ransom kidnapping occur. The risk is highest during transit between locations, making airport transfers the most exposed periods.

Load shedding has a direct impact on the security environment. When power cuts hit, traffic lights fail, creating chaotic junctions ideal for carjacking. Electric fences and gates at residential compounds lose power. Street lighting fails, extending the effective hours of darkness for criminal activity. Criminals understand these patterns and time their operations accordingly.

Sandton, Rosebank and the Melrose Arch precinct offer the most controlled environments for business travellers. These areas have private security patrols, CCTV coverage and rapid armed response. Fourways and Bryanston are also relatively lower risk. However, armed robbery and carjacking occur in all Johannesburg suburbs. The Sandton CBD has seen a notable increase in street crime since 2022.

For most business visits, a vetted security driver is the minimum recommended measure. The follow-from-airport carjacking threat alone justifies professional transport. A driver who knows which routes are compromised, which traffic lights to approach with caution, and how to respond to a carjacking attempt adds material protection. Self-driving in an unfamiliar city with critical-level crime is an avoidable risk.

The Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA) regulates all private security under the Private Security Industry Regulation Act. All companies and individual operatives must be registered. South Africa has over 2 million registered private security personnel, more than the police and military combined. Foreign security personnel require specific authorisation to operate.
