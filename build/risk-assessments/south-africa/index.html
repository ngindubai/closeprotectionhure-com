South Africa security risk overview

Security landscape

Security situation in South Africa

South Africa records approximately 27,000 murders per year (SAPS Annual Crime Statistics, 2024/25). That figure is not a typo. It translates to roughly 45 per 100,000 population, placing the country among the most violent in the world outside active conflict zones. Armed robbery, carjacking, and home invasion are not occasional risks. They are statistical certainties for long-term residents.

Johannesburg is the highest-risk city for business travellers. Carjacking is concentrated in Gauteng province, which accounts for over a third of national incidents. The modus operandi is direct: armed approach at traffic lights, driveways, or parking areas. Resistance is met with lethal force. Cash-in-transit heists involve military-grade weapons and are a daily occurrence. Express kidnapping, where victims are held briefly while bank accounts are emptied, has increased sharply since 2021.

The July 2021 civil unrest in KwaZulu-Natal and Gauteng provinces (triggered by the arrest of former President Zuma) resulted in 354 deaths and an estimated R50 billion in economic damage (South African Insurance Association, 2021). Private security companies and community defence groups, not the police or military, secured most commercial and residential areas during the unrest. Load shedding (rolling power blackouts) compounds security risk by disabling alarms, CCTV, and electric fencing.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in South Africa

South Africa's private security sector is regulated by the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority (PSIRA), established under the Private Security Industry Regulation Act 56 of 2001. With over two million registered security guards, the country has the largest private security industry in the world. The industry employs more people than the South African Police Service and the national defence force combined.

Close protection operatives are licensed to carry firearms while on duty, subject to individual competency certification under the Firearms Control Act. This is a practical necessity given the threat environment. South Africa also has one of the world's largest fleets of armoured vehicles in civilian use. Armoured transport is standard for high-value corporate movements in Johannesburg and Pretoria.

Foreign security operators must register with PSIRA before conducting any work in the country. The registration process includes background checks and verification of qualifications. PSIRA has enforcement powers including the ability to conduct inspections and revoke licences. Compliance standards are generally higher among larger, established firms. Smaller operators sometimes fall below acceptable thresholds for training and vetting.
