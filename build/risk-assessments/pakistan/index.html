Security landscape

Security situation in Pakistan

Pakistan faces overlapping security threats that vary dramatically by region. Balochistan and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa remain active conflict zones where militant groups, including Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP) and Baloch separatist organisations, carry out regular attacks on security forces, infrastructure, and civilian targets. The FCDO advises against all travel to these areas. Islamabad and Lahore are comparatively stable but have experienced large-scale terrorist attacks in recent years, including bombings targeting religious gatherings and government buildings.

Karachi is Pakistan's commercial capital and its most challenging security environment. The city of over 16 million people has a violent crime rate driven by political factionalism, ethnic tensions, extortion rackets, and gang activity. Targeted killings, particularly of business owners who refuse extortion payments, are documented regularly by the Citizens-Police Liaison Committee (CPLC Karachi, annual crime statistics). Carjacking is common. Kidnap-for-ransom operations target both Pakistani nationals and foreigners, with syndicates operating across Sindh province.

For foreign business travellers, the security calculus in Pakistan is straightforward: you need locally connected protection. The threat environment requires operators who understand which routes are currently safe, which areas to avoid on which days (political and religious calendar awareness is critical), and who have the relationships with local authorities to manage checkpoints, No Objection Certificates, and emergency response. Operating without this local knowledge is not a calculated risk; it is an operational failure.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Pakistan

Pakistan's private security sector is regulated at the provincial level under the respective Private Security Companies Ordinances. Licensing requirements vary between Sindh, Punjab, KPK, and Balochistan, but all provinces require company registration and individual operator credentials. The industry draws heavily from former Pakistan Army and paramilitary (Rangers, Frontier Corps) personnel, and many operators have intelligence (ISI or IB) backgrounds.

Armed security is standard for executive protection in Pakistan. Licensed operators can carry sidearms, and for higher-threat movements, armed escort vehicles are common. No Objection Certificates (NOCs) may be required from provincial authorities for certain travel routes, particularly in Balochistan, southern KPK, and parts of interior Sindh. These NOCs require advance notice and coordination with local police or military authorities.

Foreign security firms must work through locally licensed partners. Operating without proper licensing, or without the required NOCs for travel outside major cities, risks detention, equipment confiscation, and criminal charges. Given the political sensitivity around foreign security operations, all arrangements should be coordinated with locally connected firms who maintain active relationships with relevant authorities.
