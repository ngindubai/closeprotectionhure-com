Karachi risk assessment: security analysis for travellers and business visitors

Karachi is Pakistan's financial capital and largest city, home to roughly 16 million people. It is also one of the most dangerous major cities in the world for travellers. The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to parts of the country, and across Karachi the threat picture is severe on every axis: terrorism, kidnapping, crime, and political violence all score at or near the maximum. Five separate militant organisations operate in or around the city. Street crime, including armed carjacking and target killings, is a daily occurrence. British nationals of Pakistani origin face elevated targeting because they are perceived as wealthier. This is not a city where standard travel precautions are enough.

Crime

Crime in Karachi

Street crime in Karachi is relentless. Armed robbery, carjacking, and phone snatching happen across the city, including in upscale areas. Target killings linked to political, ethnic, and sectarian disputes added hundreds of fatalities in recent years. The city has an active black market in forged and stolen passports, and credit card fraud is widespread (OSAC Pakistan Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

British nationals of Pakistani origin face specific targeting. They are perceived as wealthier than local residents and more likely to carry cash or valuables. The FCDO flags this explicitly (FCDO Pakistan travel advice, April 2026). Criminals monitor airport arrivals for passengers on flights from the UK and Gulf states.

Extortion is systematic. Businesses pay protection money. Individuals receive threats. Some criminal networks operate with political cover, making official recourse unreliable. Lyari, once the centre of gang warfare between rival political-criminal factions, has seen military-led operations reduce open conflict, but the underlying networks remain. Carjacking is common enough that many residents of Defence Housing Authority and Clifton drive with doors locked and windows up at all times, even in daylight.

Terrorism

Terrorism and political violence

Multiple groups have the intent and demonstrated capability to carry out attacks in Karachi. Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP), the Balochistan Liberation Army (BLA), Islamic State Khorasan Province (ISKP), al-Qaeda affiliates, and various sectarian organisations including Tehrik-i-Jafria Pakistan (TJP) all operate in or have struck targets in the broader Sindh province (FCDO Pakistan travel advice, April 2026).

The attack tempo is high. In February 2026, a suicide bombing at a mosque in Islamabad killed 36 people. In January 2026, the BLA carried out coordinated attacks across Balochistan that killed at least 48 people. In October 2024, a suicide attack near Karachi's Jinnah International Airport killed four, including two Chinese nationals. In March 2025, the BLA hijacked the Jaffar Express train, killing 64 passengers (multiple news sources, 2024-2026). The FCDO advises avoiding all railway travel in Pakistan.

Targets include government and military installations, religious gatherings (both Sunni and Shia mosques), transport infrastructure, Chinese nationals and Chinese-funded projects (CPEC corridor), and Western interests. Hotels used by international visitors, the airport perimeter, and crowded markets are all assessed as vulnerable. The threat is not seasonal or cyclical. It is constant.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping in Karachi operates across three categories, all of them active. Terrorist kidnapping targets foreign nationals for political gain or ransom. Criminal kidnapping for ransom targets anyone perceived as wealthy, with British nationals of Pakistani origin particularly at risk. Express kidnapping (short-duration, forced ATM withdrawals) is opportunistic and can happen anywhere (FCDO Pakistan travel advice, April 2026).

The UK government policy is clear: it does not make substantive concessions to hostage takers, including payment of ransoms. This is worth understanding before travel. If kidnapped, the resolution timeline may be long and the outcome uncertain.

Kidnapping syndicates in Karachi are organised and sometimes connected to political parties or criminal networks with official protection. The risk is city-wide, though it increases outside gated communities like DHA and Bahria Town. Low-profile behaviour, unpredictable routines, and pre-vetted transport arrangements are baseline precautions. For any visitor whose presence or identity could attract attention, close protection with local intelligence capability is not optional.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Political rallies, strikes, and protests in Karachi are frequent. They often shut down major roads with little or no warning. Sectarian violence between Sunni and Shia communities flares periodically, particularly around religious observances. Ethnic tensions between Muhajir, Sindhi, Pashtun, and Baloch communities add another layer of instability.

Mobile data and internet services are cut without notice during periods of unrest or ahead of planned protests. This is government policy, not a technical failure, and it can leave travellers unable to communicate or navigate. During elections, the shutdown can last days. Major arterial roads are blocked by protesters, security forces, or both. What starts as a peaceful march can become violent quickly.

The practical impact for visitors is significant. A political event on the opposite side of the city can strand you in traffic for hours. A sectarian killing can trigger retaliatory violence across multiple neighbourhoods within the same day. Situational awareness and local intelligence reporting are more important here than in almost any other city on this list.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Driving in Karachi is chaotic. Lane discipline does not exist. Night driving amplifies every risk, including carjacking. The FCDO advises against using public transport, including the Karachi Metro Bus (FCDO Pakistan travel advice, April 2026). Avoid e-taxi services. Use pre-vetted private drivers with local knowledge.

Jinnah International Airport saw a suicide attack nearby in October 2024, and security has been heightened since. The airport perimeter and approach roads are considered vulnerable. Pre-arranged airport transfers with a security-aware driver are a basic precaution. For high-profile travellers, armored vehicles are available through specialist providers.

Railway travel is off the table. The March 2025 BLA hijacking of the Jaffar Express, which killed 64 people, demonstrated that trains are not defensible targets in Pakistan's current threat environment. A No Objection Certificate (NOC) may be required from authorities for travel to certain areas, particularly for foreign nationals. Your security provider should manage this.

Areas

Areas of Karachi: where is safer, where is not

Lower-risk areas

  • Defence Housing Authority (DHA), gated, military-secured perimeter, checkpoints
  • Clifton, upscale residential area with security checkpoints at key access points
  • Bahria Town, gated community with private security and controlled access

Higher-risk areas

  • Lyari (history of gang warfare, factional violence, still volatile)
  • Orangi Town (one of the largest informal settlements, high crime)
  • Korangi Industrial Area after dark (robbery, minimal policing)
  • Old City / Saddar after dark (petty and violent crime, poor lighting)
  • Anywhere near political or religious gatherings (terrorism, crowd violence)
Local security

Police and local security forces

Sindh Police in Karachi are widely regarded as corrupt and sometimes complicit in crime. Complaints of police involvement in extortion, kidnapping, and staged "encounters" (extrajudicial killings) are documented by human rights organisations (Human Rights Watch Pakistan reports, 2024). Filing a police report as a foreign national may or may not produce meaningful follow-up.

The Pakistan Rangers (Sindh), a paramilitary force under federal control, supplement police operations in Karachi. They conduct security operations, man checkpoints, and respond to terrorist incidents. Rangers have broader authority than police and may use significant force. Their presence has reduced some categories of crime but introduced its own risks for civilians.

Private security is the norm for anyone with resources. Gated communities, hotels, commercial buildings, and diplomatic compounds all operate independent security arrangements. For foreign visitors, relying on police protection is not a viable strategy. Your security provider and your embassy are your first two calls in any serious incident.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Karachi

There is no scenario where a foreign national should travel to Karachi without professional security arrangements. This is not a city with a "safe enough" zone for unprotected visitors. Even DHA and Clifton, the most controlled areas, sit within a city where terrorism, kidnapping, and armed crime are daily realities. The threat ratings speak for themselves: 5/5 across crime, terrorism, and kidnapping.

At minimum, any visit requires pre-vetted private transport (armored for high-profile visitors), a security-aware driver with local knowledge, pre-arranged airport transfers, a communications plan that accounts for mobile network shutdowns, and direct contact details for your embassy's emergency line.

For corporate executives, journalists, NGO workers, or anyone whose identity or organisation might attract attention, close protection with armed operators is standard practice. British nationals of Pakistani origin travelling for family or business face specific targeting and should not assume familiarity with the city provides adequate protection. The threat environment has changed. Local knowledge helps, but it does not replace professional security.
