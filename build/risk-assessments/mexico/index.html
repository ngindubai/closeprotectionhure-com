Security landscape

Security situation in Mexico

Mexico recorded over 30,000 homicides in 2024 (Secretariado Ejecutivo del Sistema Nacional de Seguridad Publica). The violence is driven by cartel competition for territory and trafficking routes. Sinaloa, Jalisco, Chihuahua, Guerrero, and Tamaulipas states account for a disproportionate share of incidents. Cartel conflicts produce collateral civilian casualties, and some states have seen near-complete erosion of local law enforcement capacity.

Mexico City is a different environment from the cartel-affected states. The capital has a large and visible police presence, and homicide rates are significantly lower than the national average. That said, street crime is common. Phone theft, pickpocketing in metro stations, and taxi-related robbery (particularly in unofficial cabs) are daily occurrences. Express kidnapping operates in the capital, and cases involving business travellers are documented each year by OSAC.

The 2026 FIFA World Cup (co-hosted with the US and Canada, with matches at Estadio Azteca) is expected to increase demand for executive protection and security driver services in Mexico City. Large international events historically attract opportunistic crime alongside the influx of visitors. Road travel between cities carries elevated risk, particularly after dark. Highway robbery and cartel checkpoints (retenes) occur on routes in affected states. Domestic flights are the recommended alternative where available.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Mexico

Mexico's private security industry is regulated at both federal and state levels, which creates a fragmented licensing landscape. Each of Mexico's 32 states maintains its own regulatory framework for private security companies, and licensing requirements vary. The Federal Law on Private Security (Ley Federal de Seguridad Privada) covers companies operating across multiple states, but most enforcement happens at the state level.

Armed bodyguards are common among Mexico's business community and are legal when properly licensed. Many close protection operators are former military, particularly ex-members of the Fuerzas Especiales or the now-disbanded Estado Mayor Presidencial (Presidential Guard). Armoured vehicles are widespread in executive protection work, and major cities have established providers offering both vehicle supply and trained security drivers.

The National Guard (Guardia Nacional), created in 2019, handles federal security operations but does not regulate private security firms. Foreign operators should work through locally licensed companies. Operating without proper state-level credentials risks detention and equipment confiscation. The World Cup 2026 is expected to tighten enforcement and increase regulatory scrutiny of security providers operating in host cities.
