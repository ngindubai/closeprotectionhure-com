Mexico City risk assessment: security analysis for travellers and business visitors

Mexico City is a megacity of over 21 million people where extreme wealth and extreme poverty share the same postcodes. The central colonias popular with business travellers and tourists are manageable. Step outside those corridors, especially into the outer delegaciones after dark, and the risk profile shifts sharply. Express kidnapping, armed robbery, and carjacking are the primary threats. With the FIFA World Cup 2026 approaching, foreign visitors will likely face increased targeting.

Crime

Crime in Mexico City

Crime in CDMX is stratified by neighbourhood, time of day, and target profile. In the upscale colonias (Polanco, Condesa, Roma), daytime street crime is mostly opportunistic: phone snatching, pickpocketing, distraction theft. After dark, armed robbery becomes more common, even in these areas.

The FCDO warns specifically about unlicensed taxis as a vehicle for robbery and kidnapping (FCDO Mexico travel advice, April 2026). Criminals operate vehicles that look like legitimate taxis but are not registered. Passengers are robbed at gunpoint, driven to ATMs, or held for hours. This is not rare. It is a pattern that has persisted for years.

Drink spiking occurs in nightlife areas, particularly bars in Condesa and Zona Rosa. The method is simple: a spiked drink, then robbery while the victim is incapacitated. Going out with people you trust and watching your drinks is the baseline, not overcaution.

Carjacking happens across the city but concentrates at traffic lights and stop signs in quieter residential streets, especially after dark. Drug-related violence exists in CDMX but is less visible than in northern border states. The cartel presence here is more about logistics and money laundering than open gunfights. That said, the OSAC 2025 report rates Mexico City's overall crime environment as "Critical."

The bottom line: Mexico City rewards situational awareness and punishes complacency. Keep to known areas, travel smart, and do not display wealth.

Terrorism

Terrorism and political violence

Terrorism risk in Mexico City is low. There are no active insurgent groups targeting the capital in the way that cartels operate in other states. Cartel violence is classified as organised crime rather than terrorism, and it does not generally target foreign business travellers or tourists directly.

The distinction matters for risk planning. You are not facing ideologically motivated attacks on transport or public gatherings in CDMX. The violence that exists is criminal, transactional, and largely avoidable by staying out of cartel-adjacent activities and locations. That said, collateral exposure is possible if you are in the wrong place during a targeted incident.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping is one of Mexico City's most serious security concerns. The OSAC 2025 report identifies three types operating in the metropolitan area.

Express kidnapping is the most frequent. Victims are taken from the street or from unlicensed taxis, driven to ATMs, and forced to withdraw cash repeatedly until their accounts are empty. This usually lasts a few hours. Nightlife areas and the outer delegaciones are the highest-risk zones.

Virtual kidnapping is a phone scam. Criminals call a victim's family claiming to hold them hostage, demanding immediate wire transfers. No physical abduction occurs, but the pressure tactics work often enough to sustain the business. Traditional ransom kidnapping still occurs, though it targets wealthier Mexicans more than foreigners.

With Mexico hosting FIFA World Cup 2026 matches, security analysts expect increased targeting of foreign visitors around match days and fan zones. Higher visibility plus unfamiliarity with the city makes tourists easier marks. Pre-trip security briefings and verified transport will matter more than usual during that window.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Mexico City is the country's political centre, and protests are frequent. Teacher union actions, marches commemorating the 2014 Ayotzinapa student disappearances, and women's rights demonstrations regularly fill Paseo de la Reforma and the Zocalo. Most are peaceful. Some are not.

When protests turn violent, the damage concentrates around government buildings, bank branches, and commercial shopfronts. The Zocalo and surrounding blocks become impassable. Tear gas deployment by police is common during escalations.

The practical impact for business travellers is disruption. A large march on Reforma can shut down cross-city transport for an entire day. If your hotel or meeting site is on a protest route, you need an alternative plan. Monitor local news, follow @ABOREDA_CDMX on social media for real-time road closures, and keep flexible scheduling when possible.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Mexico City's traffic is punishing. Average commute times rank among the longest in the world, and gridlock creates exposure windows for crime. Carjacking at red lights is a known pattern, particularly in quieter streets after dark.

At AICM (Benito Juarez International Airport), use only authorised taxi services booked from the official kiosks inside the terminal. The FCDO explicitly warns against unlicensed taxis from the airport (FCDO Mexico travel advice, April 2026). Rideshare apps (Uber, DiDi) are widely available and generally safer than street taxis, though drivers occasionally cancel in airport queues.

The Metro is functional but crowded. Lines 1, 2, and 3 are the busiest and see the most pickpocketing. Women-only carriages are available during peak hours. Avoid the Metro entirely after 10pm. For business travellers with a full itinerary, a dedicated driver is the most practical and safest option in Mexico City.

Areas

Areas of Mexico City: where is safer, where is not

Lower-risk areas

  • Polanco (corporate hotels, restaurants, heavy private security)
  • Condesa / Roma (daytime: restaurants, cafes. After dark: more caution needed)
  • Santa Fe (business district, gated compounds, newer development)
  • Lomas de Chapultepec (embassy district, residential, well-patrolled)

Higher-risk areas

  • Tepito (open-air stolen goods and drug market, extremely dangerous)
  • Doctores after dark (high robbery and assault rates)
  • Iztapalapa (outer delegacion, high violent crime)
  • Ecatepec (in Estado de Mexico, one of the highest murder rates in the country)
  • Metro system after 10pm (personal safety risk, pickpocketing)
Local security

Police and local security forces

Policing in Mexico City is complicated. Multiple forces operate: local police, federal police, and the National Guard (Guardia Nacional), which has taken on an increasing domestic security role since its formation in 2019. Tourist police patrol Zona Rosa and Polanco and are generally helpful for directions and minor issues.

Corruption is a documented, persistent issue across Mexican law enforcement (US State Department Human Rights Report, Mexico, 2025). Cases of police colluding with criminals, including in kidnapping operations, are not hypothetical. They are in the case files. If you are the victim of a serious crime, contact your embassy or consulate immediately in addition to filing a local report. Do not share detailed travel plans or accommodation information with anyone you do not fully trust, including officials.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Mexico City

Many business travellers visit Polanco and Santa Fe for short trips without close protection. Stick to known hotels, use rideshare or a pre-booked driver, and avoid moving around after dark on foot, and the exposure is manageable for low-profile visits.

Security support becomes a serious consideration for airport transfers (the single highest-risk moment), itineraries that cross multiple colonias, evening events or dinners outside hotel grounds, visits during high-profile events like the FIFA World Cup 2026, and anyone whose profile or role could attract targeting. A security driver familiar with Mexico City's geography, traffic patterns, and known hotspots is the most practical first step. For higher-exposure visits, close protection adds deterrence and response capability.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions: Mexico City security

Mexico City is rated high risk in our assessment framework. The US State Department issues a Level 2 advisory for Mexico with additional warnings for specific states (2026). The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to parts of Mexico and notes crime risks in the capital (FCDO Mexico advice, April 2026). Express kidnapping, armed robbery and carjacking drive the elevated rating.

Mexico City's central colonias have lower homicide rates than border states like Tamaulipas or Sinaloa. This comparison can mislead. CDMX recorded over 1,300 homicides in 2024, and crimes affecting business travellers (robbery, express kidnapping, carjacking) occur at rates that justify the high-risk classification. Risk varies enormously by neighbourhood and time of day.

Express kidnapping remains a persistent threat. The typical pattern involves victims grabbed from taxis or on foot, forced to withdraw cash from ATMs, and released after several hours. ATM withdrawals near entertainment districts at night are a known targeting pattern. Actual incidence likely exceeds reported figures, as many victims do not file reports (OSAC Mexico Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

Mexico City is not a cartel battleground like Sinaloa or Tamaulipas. However, cartel-linked groups operate in the city, particularly in drug retail, extortion and some kidnapping operations. The Jalisco New Generation Cartel and Sinaloa Cartel both maintain networks in CDMX. Cartel violence is rarely directed at foreign visitors, but collateral exposure exists in areas where criminal operations are active.

Tepito, La Merced, Doctores and parts of Iztapalapa carry the highest crime rates and should be avoided. Gustavo A. Madero has elevated robbery and carjacking rates. The lower-risk corridor includes Polanco, Condesa, Roma Norte and Coyoacan. These areas still experience crime but at lower frequency and with better police response. Risk increases across all areas after dark.

Use established app-based services (Uber, DiDi) or pre-booked cars from your hotel. Do not hail street taxis. The metro is efficient but carries high pickpocketing risk, particularly on Lines 1, 2 and 3 during peak hours. For itineraries involving multiple locations, a dedicated security driver who knows the traffic patterns and risk zones is the most practical measure.

Mexico City hosts FIFA World Cup 2026 matches at the Estadio Azteca. Large international events typically increase tourist-targeting robberies and opportunistic crime around venues and fan zones. Increased police and military presence may deter some crime but will not eliminate it. Business travellers visiting during the tournament should expect heightened targeting of foreign nationals and plan accordingly.

Private security in Mexico is regulated at both federal and state levels. In CDMX, companies must be registered with the Secretaria de Seguridad Ciudadana and hold a valid operating permit. Armed security requires additional federal licensing through SEDENA (Secretaria de la Defensa Nacional). Foreign nationals cannot independently operate as security personnel in Mexico.
