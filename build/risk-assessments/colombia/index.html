Colombia security risk overview

Colombia's security environment is shaped by over 50 years of armed conflict, narcotics trafficking, and the uneven implementation of the 2016 peace accord. Bogota and Medellin are now major business destinations, but kidnapping, armed robbery, and politically motivated violence remain real operational risks outside secured commercial districts.

Security landscape

Security situation in Colombia

Colombia's 2016 peace agreement with the FARC reduced large-scale guerrilla warfare, but it did not end the violence. FARC dissidents who rejected the accord now control significant territory in departments like Cauca, Narino, and Norte de Santander. The ELN (National Liberation Army) remains active and has escalated operations in recent years, including bombings in urban areas. Narco-trafficking organisations (including groups descended from the AUC paramilitaries) contest control of coca-growing regions and smuggling corridors.

For corporate travellers, Bogota presents a mixed picture. The city's northern commercial districts (Zona T, Usaquen, Parque 93) are relatively secure with visible police presence. South Bogota, parts of the centre, and areas around bus terminals carry higher risk. Street crime is opportunistic and often involves motorcyclists snatching phones and bags. "Scopolamine" drugging (using burundanga to incapacitate victims before robbing them) is a documented tactic that targets both locals and foreigners, particularly in nightlife areas.

Kidnapping has decreased from the peak years of the early 2000s but has not disappeared. Express kidnapping (secuestro express) remains a concern, particularly in Bogota, Cali, and Bucaramanga. Virtual kidnapping (phone-based extortion using faked evidence of abduction) is increasingly common. Protest activity can disrupt transport networks for days, particularly on the main highway corridors between cities.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Colombia

Colombia's private security industry is regulated by the Superintendencia de Vigilancia y Seguridad Privada (SuperVigilancia), a government agency with authority over licensing, inspections, and enforcement. The industry is mature, shaped by decades of conflict that created both the demand and the supply of trained security professionals. Many bodyguards and close protection operators in Colombia have military special forces backgrounds, typically with the Ejercito Nacional or Armada de Colombia.

Armed escorts are common and legal for licensed operators. SuperVigilancia issues specific credentials for armed security work, and companies must maintain records of all weapons assigned to personnel. Armoured vehicles are widely available from established providers and are standard for high-profile corporate movements in Bogota, Medellin, and Cali.

Foreign security firms must obtain licensing through SuperVigilancia before operating in the country. The process involves verification of corporate registration, personnel backgrounds, and operational plans. Working without proper licensing carries criminal penalties, not just administrative fines. Given the regulatory maturity, engagement with an unlicensed provider is both a legal risk and a strong indicator of poor operational standards.
