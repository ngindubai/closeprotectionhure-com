Bogota risk assessment: security analysis for travellers and business visitors

Bogota sits at 2,640 metres in the Andes, a sprawling capital of over 8 million people where express kidnapping, scopolamine drugging, and street-level opportunistic crime remain persistent threats. The security picture splits sharply by neighbourhood and time of day. Northern districts and select commercial zones are manageable with basic precautions. Move south or travel after dark without local guidance and the risk profile changes fast.

Crime

Crime in Bogota

Street crime in Bogota is opportunistic, persistent, and often well-organised. The FCDO warns specifically about scopolamine (locally called "burundanga"), a drug blown into faces, slipped into drinks, or applied to business cards (FCDO Colombia travel advice, April 2026). Victims become compliant and are robbed of everything. Some are taken to ATMs repeatedly. It happens in bars, restaurants, and on the street.

Express kidnapping is the other standout threat. Criminals posing as taxi drivers pick up passengers, then drive to ATMs and force cash withdrawals over several hours. Fake taxis are the most common vehicle for this. The OSAC 2025 Crime and Safety Report rates Bogota as "Critical" for overall crime.

Pickpocketing and phone snatching are constant in crowded areas. Motorcycle-borne crime is common: two riders pull alongside, the passenger grabs a bag or phone, and they disappear into traffic. Resistance during any robbery significantly increases the chance of violence. Criminals in Bogota frequently carry knives or firearms, and the calculation for them is simple: your phone is not worth your life.

The pattern is predictable. Crime concentrates around TransMilenio stations, La Candelaria after dark, and the southern barrios. Northern commercial districts see far less, though nowhere is immune.

Terrorism

Terrorism and political violence

Colombia's 50-year armed conflict is not over. The 2016 FARC peace agreement fractured the movement, leaving FARC dissident factions active alongside the ELN (Ejercito de Liberacion Nacional) and various armed criminal groups known as GAOs (Grupos Armados Organizados).

The ELN carried out a car bomb attack on a police academy in Bogota in January 2019, killing 22 cadets and injuring 68 (BBC News, 17 January 2019). That attack demonstrated that urban targets in the capital remain within reach, even if most insurgent violence concentrates in rural departments. The ELN has conducted sporadic attacks in urban areas since, and peace negotiations have stalled and restarted repeatedly.

For business travellers, the direct terrorism risk in Bogota's northern commercial districts is lower than in border regions or rural conflict zones. But it is not zero. Large gatherings, government buildings, and security installations are possible targets. Travel near military or police facilities warrants extra awareness.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping in Bogota operates at three levels. Express kidnapping ("paseo millonario") is the most common: a victim is forced into a vehicle, driven to multiple ATMs over several hours, and released once accounts are drained. The FCDO notes this frequently involves scopolamine to incapacitate the victim (FCDO Colombia travel advice, April 2026).

Virtual kidnapping is a phone-based scam where criminals call claiming to hold a family member, demanding immediate payment. No actual abduction takes place, but the psychological pressure is effective. Traditional ransom kidnapping still occurs in Colombia, though it has decreased in Bogota compared to the early 2000s.

Foreigners are targets because they are perceived as wealthy. The highest risk window is airport arrival: criminals watch for international passengers then follow or intercept them in unmarked taxis. Pre-arranged transport from El Dorado International Airport through a verified provider is not optional. It is a baseline precaution.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Bogota sees frequent large-scale protests. Triggers include economic inequality, disputes over the peace process with armed groups, indigenous land rights, student grievances, and labour disputes. When demonstrations escalate, they can shut down entire districts for hours or days.

The 2021 national strikes paralysed Bogota for weeks, with road blockades, tear gas, and clashes between police and protesters (Reuters, May 2021). Plaza Bolivar and the area around the Congreso are focal points. Police response can be heavy-handed, with riot control units deploying tear gas and water cannon with limited warning.

Stay away from protest routes. Monitor local media. If your hotel or meeting location is near a planned march, have an alternate route mapped in advance.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Traffic in Bogota is severe. Journey times are unpredictable, and motorcycle-borne crime adds a layer of risk during any road movement. The TransMilenio bus rapid transit system is functional but should be avoided after dark due to pickpocketing and personal safety incidents at stations.

Street taxis are involved in a significant proportion of express kidnappings. The FCDO advises against hailing taxis on the street (FCDO Colombia travel advice, April 2026). Use only rideshare apps (Uber, InDriver, DiDi) or pre-booked vehicles from verified providers. At El Dorado airport, authorised taxi stands are available inside the terminal. Do not accept rides from anyone approaching you in arrivals.

For business visitors with multi-site itineraries, a dedicated security driver who knows the city reduces both transit time and exposure. This is standard practice for corporate travel programmes in Bogota.

Areas

Areas of Bogota: where is safer, where is not

Lower-risk areas

  • Zona T / Zona Rosa (commercial, nightlife, higher police presence)
  • Usaquen (upscale dining district, quieter streets)
  • Chapinero Alto (mid-range residential, manageable daytime)
  • Parque 93 (corporate area, restaurants, controlled access)
  • North Bogota (gated residential compounds, lower street crime)

Higher-risk areas

  • La Candelaria after dark (tourist area by day, robbery hotspot by night)
  • El Bronx (extremely dangerous, active drug market)
  • Los Martires (high crime, avoid entirely)
  • Ciudad Bolivar (southern slum, no business reason to visit)
  • Southern Bogota generally (elevated crime, limited police coverage)
  • TransMilenio stations late at night (pickpocketing, personal safety)
Local security

Police and local security forces

The Policia Nacional maintains a visible presence in commercial and tourist districts. Tourist police units operate in La Candelaria and Zona Rosa and can be helpful for minor incidents. Response times are reasonable in northern Bogota but degrade significantly in southern barrios.

Corruption within the police force remains a documented issue (US State Department Human Rights Report, Colombia, 2025). Officers may solicit bribes during traffic stops or minor interactions. For serious incidents involving foreign nationals, contact your embassy or consulate in parallel with filing a police report. Do not rely solely on local law enforcement for resolution of theft, assault, or kidnapping cases.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Bogota

Bogota is workable for experienced travellers who stick to northern districts, use pre-booked transport, and maintain situational awareness. Many business visitors manage short trips to known locations without close protection.

Professional security becomes worth considering for airport arrivals and departures (the highest-risk transit window), multi-day itineraries with movement across the city, meetings in unfamiliar or mixed-risk neighbourhoods, and any travel that involves carrying visible valuables or equipment. A trained driver who knows the city is the most cost-effective measure available. For higher-profile visits, a close protection officer adds a layer of deterrence and response capability.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions: Bogota security

Bogota is rated high risk in our assessment framework. The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to specific departments in Colombia and maintains general travel warnings (FCDO Colombia advice, April 2026). The US State Department issues a Level 2 advisory. While northern commercial districts are more manageable than other Colombian cities, express kidnapping, armed robbery and scopolamine-assisted crime keep the rating elevated.

Colombia's national security picture has improved since the 2016 peace agreement with FARC, but the gains are uneven. Bogota has seen reduced murder rates compared to the early 2000s. Street crime, express kidnapping and scopolamine attacks have not followed the same downward trend. Dissident FARC factions and ELN activity continue to affect the country. The practical risk for business travellers remains high outside established commercial corridors.

Express kidnapping is a short-duration abduction where victims are held for hours rather than days. The typical pattern involves forced ATM withdrawals or calls to family members for immediate payment. In Bogota, criminals operate from vehicles or use compromised taxi services. Victims are usually released within 12 to 24 hours once funds are extracted (OSAC Colombia Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

Scopolamine attacks are well-documented in Bogota and present a genuine threat. The drug is derived from borrachero trees, which grow widely in Colombia. It is typically administered through spiked drinks, food, or blown as powder. Victims become compliant and suggestible, allowing criminals to empty bank accounts or steal belongings. These attacks occur in bars, restaurants and social settings, frequently targeting foreign visitors.

Southern districts including Ciudad Bolivar, Bosa and parts of San Cristobal carry the highest crime rates. The La Candelaria historic centre is manageable during daytime but risky after dark. Santa Fe and Los Martires are red zones at all hours. The lower-risk corridor runs through Zona T, Usaquen, Chapinero Alto and the northern business districts along the Septima and Autopista Norte.

Do not hail taxis from the street. This is the single most actionable precaution. Street taxis are the primary vehicle for express kidnapping. Use pre-booked transport through your hotel, a vetted car service, or app-based services like Uber. For business travel, a dedicated driver who knows the city is the recommended approach. Avoid the TransMilenio bus system during peak hours.

Risk increases sharply after dark in virtually all areas. The period between 22:00 and 05:00 carries the highest crime frequency. Early morning hours (05:00 to 07:00) also present elevated risk as streets are quiet and response times are slower. Even in northern commercial districts, walking after dark is not recommended. Use door-to-door vehicle transport for evening commitments.

Private security companies in Colombia are regulated by the Superintendencia de Vigilancia y Seguridad Privada (SuperVigilancia). All companies must hold a current operating licence and personnel require credentialing. Armed protection services require additional licensing. Foreign nationals cannot independently operate as security personnel without affiliation to a locally licensed firm.
