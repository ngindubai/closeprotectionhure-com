Crime

Crime in Riyadh

Street crime in Riyadh is rare by any international standard. The Kingdom's strict Islamic law enforcement, heavy police presence and severe penalties create an environment where petty theft, mugging and violent confrontation stay at low levels. Visitors from high-crime cities often remark on the difference immediately.

That said, crime is not zero. Petty theft occurs in shopping malls and souks, particularly targeting unattended bags. Fraud and financial scams affect business visitors, especially in real estate and commercial deals. Road rage incidents, while not classified as traditional crime, occasionally escalate to physical confrontation on Riyadh's highways (OSAC Saudi Arabia Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

The main legal risks for foreign visitors are cultural, not criminal in the conventional sense. Saudi Arabia enforces strict laws on alcohol (prohibited), drugs (severe penalties including death), public behaviour and dress codes. What would be a minor social infraction in London or New York can result in detention and deportation here. Foreign workers involved in employment disputes may face travel bans preventing them from leaving the country (FCDO Saudi Arabia travel advice, March 2026).

For most business travellers staying within Riyadh's commercial districts, violent crime is not the concern. Understanding local laws is.

Terrorism

Terrorism and missile threats

Riyadh sits within range of Houthi rebel long-range missiles and armed drones launched from Yemen. This is not a theoretical risk. Houthi forces have targeted Riyadh directly with ballistic missiles on multiple occasions, including intercepted attacks on King Khalid International Airport and the city centre. Drone and missile strikes on Saudi oil facilities at Abqaiq and Khurais in September 2019 demonstrated the group's reach and capability (Saudi Press Agency, September 2019).

Saudi air defences, primarily the Patriot missile system, have intercepted incoming projectiles over Riyadh. Interceptions are effective but not guaranteed. Debris from intercepted missiles has caused casualties on the ground in previous incidents.

Islamic State remnants also present a domestic threat, though diminished from their peak. IS cells previously targeted Saudi security forces and conducted attacks on Shia mosques in the Eastern Province. The Saudi security services have disrupted multiple planned attacks in recent years, but the FCDO maintains that "terrorists are very likely to try to carry out attacks in Saudi Arabia" (FCDO Saudi Arabia travel advice, March 2026).

For corporate travellers, the terrorism rating of 3 reflects this combination: an external ballistic threat that reaches the capital, plus a suppressed but not eliminated domestic extremist presence.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping in Riyadh is extremely rare. The combination of pervasive surveillance, harsh judicial penalties (including execution for kidnapping) and effective security forces makes this one of the lowest-risk cities globally for abduction.

Neither ransom kidnapping nor express kidnapping feature as meaningful threats within the city. Cases that do occur tend to involve domestic disputes or migrant worker exploitation rather than criminal targeting of foreign business visitors.

The southern border areas near Yemen present a different picture entirely, and the FCDO advises against all but essential travel to those regions. But within Riyadh itself, kidnapping risk is negligible.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Public demonstrations are illegal in Saudi Arabia. The government suppresses dissent through surveillance, internet monitoring and swift detention. There is no meaningful tradition of street protest in Riyadh, and the few attempts at public assembly in recent decades were dispersed rapidly.

Vision 2030 economic reforms have introduced social changes (women driving, entertainment venues, reduced religious police powers) that carry broad public support. However, the reforms also create economic pressures, particularly for Saudi nationals affected by subsidy reductions and workforce nationalisation (Saudization) requirements.

The practical risk to business travellers from civil unrest in Riyadh is minimal. This is not a city where you need to plan routes around potential protest locations. The authorities maintain tight control over public gatherings, and any emerging tension is addressed before it reaches the streets.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Road accidents are the primary physical risk in Riyadh. Saudi Arabia has one of the highest road fatality rates in the Middle East (WHO Global Status Report on Road Safety, 2023). Driving standards are poor. High-speed tailgating on the King Fahd Road, running red lights, and aggressive lane changes are routine. Speed limits are frequently ignored on the wide urban highways.

King Khalid International Airport (RUH) is well-managed with good security. The arrival experience is straightforward, but the drive into the city centre takes 30 to 45 minutes depending on traffic, and that journey is where the risk sits.

The Riyadh Metro opened in late 2024, adding six lines across the city. It is clean, modern and safe, though coverage is still expanding and many business destinations require a car for the last mile. Private vehicles remain dominant for corporate travel.

Use a professional driver with local experience. The standard of driving in Riyadh means that an unfamiliar visitor behind the wheel is taking on unnecessary risk. Pre-booked transfers from the airport are standard practice for corporate visitors.

Local security

Police and local security forces

Saudi police and security services are well funded, well equipped and generally effective. Corruption within the force is low relative to the region. Response times in Riyadh are good, and the presence of police patrols, CCTV and traffic cameras across the city acts as a visible deterrent.

The challenge for foreign visitors is not police effectiveness but the legal framework they enforce. Saudi Arabia applies strict Islamic (Sharia) law. Zero tolerance on drugs (penalties include death). Alcohol is prohibited entirely. Public behaviour, dress codes and social media posts are subject to regulation. Men and women may still encounter restrictions depending on context. Criticism of the government, royal family or religion can lead to arrest (US State Department Saudi Arabia Travel Advisory, 2026).

Foreign workers in commercial disputes should be aware that Saudi employers or sponsors can request a travel ban, preventing departure from the country until the matter is resolved. This has affected business visitors and contractors.

Police score: 4/5. Reliable, well resourced, low corruption. But the legal system itself creates risks that business travellers from Western countries may not anticipate.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Riyadh

Riyadh's low crime environment means that many business travellers operate safely without close protection. A security driver familiar with local roads and customs is often sufficient for routine corporate visits to the Diplomatic Quarter, KAFD or Al Olaya.

Professional security becomes appropriate in specific circumstances. High-profile visits where your presence is publicly known attract attention in a city with an active, if suppressed, terrorism threat. Events coinciding with periods of heightened Houthi activity (which can escalate without warning) justify having a team that monitors real-time threat intelligence and can adjust movements accordingly.

The legal environment also creates situations where professional support adds value. A security consultant with local knowledge can brief you on behavioural norms, help you avoid inadvertent legal violations, and provide discreet liaison with local authorities if problems arise. For female executives travelling to Riyadh, a local security advisor can navigate the rapidly changing but still complex social landscape.
