Security landscape

Security situation in Saudi Arabia

Saudi Arabia maintains a comparatively stable internal security environment. The UK FCDO advises against all but essential travel to areas within 10km of the Yemeni border due to cross-border missile and drone attacks (FCDO Travel Advice: Saudi Arabia, updated February 2026). The US State Department holds it at Level 2, urging increased caution due to terrorism and missile threats (US State Department Travel Advisory: Saudi Arabia, January 2026).

Riyadh carries a low overall risk score of 2/5. OSAC rates countrywide crime as LOW (OSAC Crime and Safety Report: Saudi Arabia, 2025). Street crime is minimal. Violent crime against foreign nationals is rare. The kingdom's extensive internal security apparatus, managed by the Ministry of Interior, maintains visible policing and surveillance across major cities.

Terrorism risk sits at 3/5 for Riyadh. While the threat from ISIS and affiliated groups has been substantially reduced since the 2015-2016 attack cycle, Saudi security services continue to disrupt cells periodically. Houthi drone and missile attacks from Yemen have reached as far as Riyadh, though the Saudi air defence network intercepts the majority.

Kidnapping risk is very low at 1/5. Saudi Arabia's strict legal system and pervasive security infrastructure make abduction operations extremely difficult. The primary residual risks for foreign nationals are road traffic accidents (a leading cause of injury and death in the kingdom) and cultural or legal misunderstandings that can escalate into detention.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Saudi Arabia

Private security in Saudi Arabia is regulated by the Ministry of Interior and the High Commission for Industrial Security. All companies must be Saudi-registered and hold valid operating licences.

Vision 2030 has expanded the market. NEOM, the Red Sea project, Diriyah Gate, and a packed calendar of entertainment and sporting events have driven demand for trained close protection officers (Saudi Vision 2030, giga-projects update, 2025). Foreign operators face strict requirements: they cannot operate independently and must partner with Saudi-licensed firms.

Firearms are prohibited for private security personnel. EP operations rely on unarmed officers working alongside coordinated police support. Saudi authorities maintain tight control over security operations, and all foreign security personnel require proper work visas and clearances through the licensed Saudi partner company.
