Crime

Crime in Mumbai

Mumbai has a moderate crime profile for a city of 21 million people. Violent crime against foreign business travellers is rare. The threats that exist are persistent but predictable.

Pickpocketing and bag snatching concentrate around crowded train stations (Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Terminus, Dadar, Andheri), tourist areas near the Gateway of India, and markets in Colaba. Scams targeting foreign visitors operate at these same locations: inflated taxi fares, fake tour guides, gem and carpet purchase frauds. These are not dangerous, but they are constant.

Sexual assault is a serious concern. The FCDO specifically warns female travellers about the risk of sexual assault in India, including on public transport and in taxis (FCDO India travel advice, March 2026). Women travelling alone should avoid local trains during off-peak hours and use pre-booked ride services rather than street-hailed taxis or auto-rickshaws after dark.

Cybercrime and phone scams are widespread. Credit card skimming at ATMs occurs, particularly at standalone machines in less-monitored locations. Use bank ATMs inside branches.

Dharavi and Kamathipura have higher crime rates than the business districts but are not areas that most corporate travellers would visit. Violent crime against foreigners is unusual in Mumbai, but petty crime and sexual harassment are genuine, daily risks.

Terrorism

Terrorism and political violence

Mumbai is a priority target for terrorist groups. The November 2008 attacks killed 166 people across multiple locations: the Taj Mahal Palace Hotel, the Oberoi Trident, Leopold Cafe, and Chhatrapati Shivaji Terminus station. Ten Lashkar-e-Taiba (LeT) operatives arriving by sea from Pakistan carried out the coordinated assault over four days (Indian Government and multiple international reports, November 2008).

That attack reshaped Mumbai's security landscape. Hotels now have airport-style screening. The National Security Guard (NSG) established a permanent hub in Mumbai for rapid response. But the underlying threat has not gone away.

LeT remains operational in Pakistan. Jaish-e-Mohammed (JeM) continues to target Indian interests. Al-Qaeda in the Indian Subcontinent (AQIS) has called for attacks on Indian cities. IS-inspired individuals have been arrested in Maharashtra in recent years. The FCDO states that "terrorists are very likely to try to carry out attacks in India" and specifically names Mumbai as a target (FCDO India travel advice, March 2026).

Hotels, transport hubs, markets and religious sites remain the most likely targets. The terrorism rating of 4 reflects the combination of proven capability (2008 demonstrated what is possible), persistent intent from multiple groups, and Mumbai's continued symbolic value as India's financial capital.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping of foreign nationals in Mumbai is rare. The city's density, CCTV coverage in commercial areas, and police presence make abduction and concealment difficult in the districts where business travellers operate.

Kidnapping is a more significant threat in rural India and in northern states (Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Jharkhand) where organised gangs operate ransom kidnapping as a business. Within Mumbai itself, cases that occur tend to involve domestic disputes, business extortion within Indian communities, or child custody matters.

Express kidnapping does not feature as a pattern in Mumbai. However, ride-hailing scams where drivers take passengers to unfamiliar locations and demand payment are reported. These are opportunistic rather than organised, and using established platforms (Ola, Uber) with GPS tracking reduces exposure.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Mumbai experiences regular civil unrest. The triggers are varied: religious tensions between Hindu and Muslim communities, caste-based disputes, political rallies, labour actions, and "bandh" (shutdown) calls by political parties or trade unions.

Bandh days can paralyse the city. Shops close, public transport stops, and roads are blocked. These shutdowns are sometimes called at short notice, typically by opposition parties or following a contentious political event. During a bandh, vehicles on the road may be targeted for stone-throwing, and businesses that remain open can face intimidation (OSAC India Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

Religious riots have affected Mumbai historically. The 1992-93 communal riots killed over 900 people. While large-scale communal violence has not recurred at that level, smaller incidents of religious tension flare periodically. Election periods bring increased political rallies and occasional clashes between party supporters.

For business travellers, the practical risk is disruption rather than targeting. Monitor local news for bandh announcements. Have contingency plans for days when the city shuts down. Avoid large gatherings whether they appear peaceful or not; the shift from protest to violence happens without much warning in Indian cities.

Transport

Transport and road safety

India has one of the highest road accident death rates in the world. The FCDO warns that "road travel can be hazardous" and notes that driving standards are "poor throughout India" (FCDO India travel advice, March 2026). Mumbai concentrates this national problem into a dense urban environment with added hazards: motorcycles weaving through traffic, pedestrians crossing multi-lane highways, and auto-rickshaws making unpredictable turns.

Mumbai's local train network carries over 7.5 million passengers daily. It is not recommended for business travellers. Overcrowding is extreme. People fall from moving trains. During rush hours, passengers are physically crushed into carriages. Women face groping and harassment, particularly in general compartments (ladies-only carriages exist but are also crowded).

Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj International Airport (BOM) is functional but chaotic. Pre-booked transfers from the airport are the only sensible option. The taxi queue system at the airport works, but drivers may not know specific addresses and communication can be difficult.

Use private cars with experienced local drivers. Ola and Uber operate widely and provide GPS tracking and fare estimates. Avoid auto-rickshaws and local taxis for anything beyond short daytime trips in familiar areas. Night driving in Mumbai is higher risk due to poor lighting, drunk drivers, and pedestrians sleeping on roads in some areas.

Local security

Police and local security forces

Mumbai Police have improved significantly since 2008. The Anti-Terrorism Squad (ATS) and Force One rapid response unit were established or upgraded directly in response to the attacks. Counter-terrorism capability is now meaningfully better, with faster response times and better equipment for handling complex armed assaults.

Regular policing is a different story. Corruption is widespread. The OSAC rates police corruption in India as a serious concern (OSAC India Crime and Safety Report, 2025). Officers may solicit bribes during routine interactions. Filing a police report (First Information Report, or FIR) for theft or assault can be a slow and frustrating process, with officers sometimes reluctant to register complaints.

Response times vary wildly. In South Mumbai's business districts, police presence is visible and response is reasonable. In suburban areas and northern Mumbai, it can take significantly longer. Traffic police are visible but traffic enforcement is inconsistent.

For foreign business travellers: do not rely on Mumbai Police as your primary security layer. The counter-terrorism infrastructure has improved, but day-to-day policing remains unreliable. Private security fills this gap for most corporate operations in the city.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Mumbai

Mumbai's medium overall risk rating masks a split threat profile. Crime and civil unrest are manageable for experienced travellers. The terrorism rating of 4 is not.

Any business traveller staying at a high-profile hotel in South Mumbai is operating in an area that was directly targeted in 2008 and remains on threat lists. This does not mean avoiding these hotels (their security has improved dramatically), but it does mean that close protection teams with counter-terrorism training add genuine value for high-profile visitors.

If your itinerary includes movement across the city, from BKC to Colaba, from Powai to Lower Parel, the chaotic traffic and unpredictable conditions make a professional security driver with local knowledge a practical necessity rather than a luxury. Travel time in Mumbai can double without warning due to bandh activity, monsoon flooding (June to September), or traffic incidents.

Female executives travelling to Mumbai should consider professional security for evening movements and any travel outside the core business districts. The FCDO warning on sexual assault is not hypothetical.

For corporate travel programmes with duty-of-care requirements, the combination of terrorism threat level, unreliable policing and civil unrest potential makes professional security advisable for most executive visits to Mumbai.
