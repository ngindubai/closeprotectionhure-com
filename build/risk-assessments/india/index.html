Security landscape

Security situation in India

India presents a complex security picture shaped by its size, population density, and the diversity of threat types across regions. The UK FCDO issues specific warnings for several states, including Jammu and Kashmir, northeastern states, and areas affected by Naxalite insurgency (FCDO Travel Advice: India, updated March 2026). The US State Department maintains a Level 2 advisory urging increased caution (US State Department Travel Advisory: India, January 2026).

Mumbai carries a medium overall risk score of 3/5. OSAC rates countrywide crime as HIGH (OSAC Crime and Safety Report: India, 2025). Street crime in Mumbai is moderate, with petty theft, scams, and opportunistic crime common in tourist and commercial districts. The city's density makes crowd management a constant consideration for any protection detail.

Terrorism risk is scored at 4/5 for Mumbai. The 2008 attacks remain the defining event in the city's modern security planning. Multiple coordinated attacks across hotels, a railway station, and a Jewish centre killed 166 people (multiple sources, November 2008). Indian security forces have expanded counter-terrorism capabilities since then, but the threat from both domestic and cross-border groups persists.

Kidnapping risk is 2/5. Targeting of foreign nationals for ransom is uncommon in Mumbai, though express kidnapping and extortion occur. Large public gatherings, religious festivals, and political demonstrations can escalate quickly. India's traffic conditions and infrastructure gaps add logistical complexity to any executive protection operation.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in India

The Private Security Agencies Regulation Act (PSARA) of 2005 governs security companies across India. All agencies must be registered under PSARA and comply with state-level implementation rules, which vary between states (Ministry of Home Affairs, Government of India).

Firearms are heavily restricted for private security. The vast majority of EP operations are unarmed. When armed protection is necessary, it typically involves police liaison or government-authorised armed escorts rather than privately armed officers.

The Indian EP market is growing rapidly. Demand comes from Bollywood and entertainment figures, corporate India's expanding C-suite travel requirements, and visiting HNWIs. International security firms must partner with PSARA-registered Indian companies. Most operators employ a mix of trained close protection officers and local support staff with strong knowledge of city infrastructure and police coordination channels.

Common questions

India security: frequently asked questions

The Private Security Agencies Regulation Act (PSARA) of 2005 is the primary framework. It is administered at state level, which means a company operating across multiple states needs separate licences from each one. Enforcement standards vary considerably between states.

Foreign companies must establish an Indian subsidiary or joint venture and register under PSARA at state level. FDI caps apply to the security sector. Operational close protection must be delivered by Indian-licensed personnel, though foreign professionals can work in advisory or management roles.

India's private security market is predominantly unarmed. Armed protection typically involves off-duty or serving police or CRPF personnel assigned through official channels. Private companies cannot independently field armed operatives without specific state-level permissions, which are rare.

The FCDO rates a terrorist attack in India as "very likely." Mumbai remains a priority target, as demonstrated by the 2008 coordinated attacks across multiple locations that killed 166 people. Groups including Lashkar-e-Taiba and IS-inspired cells maintain intent and capability to strike commercial and hospitality targets.

Kidnapping in Mumbai itself is relatively low risk compared to other high-threat cities. The threat is higher in rural areas and certain northern states. Corporate travellers in urban centres face more exposure from street crime and scams than from abduction.

India has one of the highest road accident death rates globally (FCDO, April 2026). Mumbai traffic is chaotic, with potholes, unpredictable driving behaviour, and pedestrians sharing the road. Pre-booked private drivers with local route knowledge are the standard recommendation for visiting executives.
