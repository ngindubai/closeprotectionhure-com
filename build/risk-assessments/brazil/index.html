Brazil security risk overview

Brazil operates one of the largest private security markets in the world, driven by persistently high rates of violent crime and kidnapping. Sao Paulo and Rio de Janeiro are major commercial hubs, but armed robbery, carjacking, and express kidnapping remain daily realities for corporate travellers moving outside secured business districts.

Security landscape

Security situation in Brazil

Brazil recorded over 47,000 homicides in 2023, according to the Brazilian Forum on Public Security (FBSP Annual Report, 2024). That number has declined from peak levels, but it still places Brazil among the most violent countries outside active conflict zones. The threat is not evenly distributed. Rio de Janeiro's favela communities see regular armed confrontations between police and organised criminal factions like Comando Vermelho and Primeiro Comando da Capital (PCC). Sao Paulo's commercial centre is comparatively safer, but express kidnappings and armed carjackings persist in areas around Guarulhos airport and along main highway corridors.

Organised crime militia groups now control significant territory in Rio de Janeiro state, running protection rackets and controlling utilities in areas the state has largely abandoned. For corporate visitors, the practical risk is getting caught in crossfire during police operations. These are unannounced and can close highways and airport access roads without warning.

Brazil's armoured vehicle fleet exceeds 100,000 units (Brazilian Association of Armoured Vehicles, ABRABLIN, 2024). That statistic alone indicates the threat level that executives and high-net-worth individuals face daily. Armed executive protection is standard practice for business travel in Sao Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, and Brasilia. Travelling without it, particularly with visible indicators of wealth, is a decision that experienced operators would strongly advise against.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Brazil

Brazil's private security sector is regulated by the Federal Police (Policia Federal) under Law 7.102/83 and subsequent decrees. The industry is one of the largest in Latin America, employing over 500,000 registered security professionals (FENAVIST, 2024). EP operatives are typically required to hold a valid security credential issued by the Federal Police, and many hold former military or Policia Militar backgrounds.

Armed protection is legal and common for licensed operators. Companies must register all firearms with the Federal Police and maintain detailed logs of weapon assignments. Armoured vehicle providers operate under separate licensing requirements. Foreign security firms cannot operate independently in Brazil; they must partner with a locally licensed company or obtain their own Brazilian credentials, a process that involves corporate registration, background checks, and Federal Police approval.

Enforcement of these regulations varies by state. In Sao Paulo and Rio de Janeiro, oversight is relatively consistent. In northern and northeastern states, unlicensed operators are more common. Working with an unlicensed provider carries criminal liability and removes any legal protection if an incident occurs.

Brazil security: frequently asked questions

The Federal Police (Policia Federal) oversees the private security sector through its Private Security Division. Companies must obtain Federal Police authorisation and meet capital, facility, and personnel requirements. Different categories exist for personal security, property guarding, cash-in-transit, and armed escort.

Foreign companies must establish a Brazilian legal entity (LTDA or S.A.) and register with the Federal Police. There is no reciprocity for foreign qualifications. Operational EP must use Brazilian-licensed armed personnel, though foreign consultants may work in advisory roles.

Express kidnapping (sequestro relampago) involves victims being forced to withdraw money from ATMs over several hours before release. It is endemic in Brazilian cities, and the FCDO has issued specific warnings about it. Victims are often selected based on visible wealth, and violence during the ordeal is common.

OSAC rates the crime threat in Brazil as critical. Armed robbery, carjacking at traffic lights (particularly after dark and in tunnels), and phone snatching are daily realities. The FCDO warns that attackers are often armed and under the influence of drugs. Do not resist.

Cracolandia (an open drug market) is extremely dangerous. The Bras and Luz districts carry high crime risk, as does Centro after dark. Carjacking is a particular concern at traffic lights in tunnels. Jardins, Itaim Bibi, and the Faria Lima corridor are the lower-risk commercial districts.
