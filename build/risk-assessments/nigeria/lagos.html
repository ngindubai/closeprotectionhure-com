Crime

Crime in Lagos

Lagos has one of the highest violent crime rates of any major African city. Armed robbery is the primary threat to foreign visitors. Criminals operate in organised teams, often targeting vehicles stuck in the city's notorious traffic congestion. Carjacking is common along the Lekki-Epe Expressway and on approaches to the Third Mainland Bridge, particularly after dark (OSAC Nigeria Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

Street crime concentrates in specific areas. Oshodi market, Mushin, Ajegunle and the Mile 2 corridor are high-frequency zones for mugging, pickpocketing and armed confrontation. Lagos Island becomes markedly more dangerous after sunset. Apapa port and its surrounding roads see regular hijackings of commercial vehicles and their occupants.

Express kidnapping is a particular concern. Victims are seized and forced to withdraw cash from ATMs before being released. This tactic requires no ransom negotiation and is over within hours, making it attractive to low-level criminal gangs. The FCDO warns specifically about the threat of robbery and kidnapping in Lagos (FCDO Nigeria travel advice, updated March 2026).

Cybercrime also operates at scale from Lagos, though this primarily affects targets outside Nigeria. For travellers on the ground, the physical threats of armed robbery and express kidnapping are the most immediate concerns.

Terrorism

Terrorism and political violence

The primary terrorist threat in Nigeria comes from Islamic State West Africa Province (ISWAP) and Boko Haram (Jama'atu Ahlis Sunna Lidda'awati wal-Jihad, or JAS). Both groups operate mainly in northeast Nigeria, but Nigerian security services have warned that their operational reach extends to Lagos and other southern cities (FCDO Nigeria travel advice, March 2026).

Soft targets are the main concern: hotels, shopping centres, transport hubs and places of worship. No major attack has occurred in Lagos in recent years, but the US State Department maintains Nigeria at Level 3 ("Reconsider Travel") partly due to terrorism risk nationwide. The FCDO advises against all travel to several northeastern states and warns of a general terrorism threat across the country.

Foreign nationals and venues associated with Western business interests carry a higher target profile. Corporate travellers should maintain awareness of this background threat, even in areas like Victoria Island where day-to-day security is better managed.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping in Lagos is rated critical. Three distinct types operate in the city. Traditional ransom kidnapping targets high-net-worth individuals, expatriates and prominent business figures. Gangs conduct surveillance on potential targets for days before acting. Ransoms can reach hundreds of thousands of dollars (OSAC Nigeria Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

Express kidnapping is faster and more opportunistic. Victims are grabbed, driven to ATMs, and released once accounts are drained. This can happen anywhere, but the Lekki corridor and approaches to the Third Mainland Bridge are known hotspots.

Maritime kidnapping in the Gulf of Guinea affects workers in the offshore oil industry and individuals using watercraft around Lagos ports. The International Maritime Bureau recorded 35 crew kidnappings in the Gulf of Guinea in 2023 (IMB Piracy Report, January 2024), a decline from previous years but still a serious threat.

Targets are not exclusively wealthy. Anyone perceived as having access to foreign currency is at risk. Travelling with visible security and using low-profile vehicles reduces (but does not eliminate) exposure.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Lagos has a recent history of major civil unrest. The EndSARS protests of October 2020 resulted in fatalities when security forces fired on demonstrators at the Lekki toll gate (Amnesty International, October 2020). Economic grievances, fuel subsidy removal and election disputes regularly trigger large-scale protests.

When protests erupt, they can escalate quickly. Road blockades halt traffic for hours. Commercial areas shut down. Security forces have used tear gas and, in some cases, live ammunition to disperse crowds. The transition from peaceful march to violent confrontation can happen with little warning.

For business travellers, the main risk is being caught in disruption rather than targeted directly. Avoid protest areas. Monitor local news. Have contingency routes planned for any journey across the city.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Murtala Muhammed International Airport (LOS) presents immediate risks on arrival. Luggage theft, aggressive solicitation by unofficial taxi drivers and scams targeting arriving passengers are reported frequently (OSAC Nigeria Crime and Safety Report, 2025). Pre-arranged pickup by a known driver is the baseline recommendation.

Road conditions are poor across much of Lagos. Potholes, minimal street lighting, and aggressive driving create constant hazards. "Danfo" minibuses and BRT vehicles are frequent targets for petty criminals. Carjacking peaks during evening traffic congestion, when vehicles are stationary for extended periods.

Use only vetted private drivers with local knowledge. Avoid the Third Mainland Bridge after dark. Keep windows up and doors locked at all times. If moving between Victoria Island and the mainland, plan journeys for daylight hours where possible.

Local security

Police and local security forces

The Nigeria Police Force (NPF) is widely regarded as unreliable for foreign visitors. Corruption is endemic. Officers at roadblocks routinely solicit bribes from motorists, and payment is generally expected, not requested (US State Department Nigeria Travel Advisory, January 2026).

Response times to emergency calls are slow, sometimes exceeding one hour even in high-priority areas like Victoria Island. Investigative follow-up on reported crimes is rare. For this reason, most businesses, hotels and residential compounds in Lagos employ private security contractors. Armed private guards are a standard feature of daily life, not a luxury.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Lagos

With a critical overall risk rating, professional security is appropriate for most business travellers to Lagos. This is not a city where awareness alone is sufficient. The combination of armed crime, kidnapping threat and unreliable policing means that a trained close protection operative reduces your exposure significantly.

If you are attending meetings on Victoria Island and staying within the Ikoyi/VI corridor, a security driver with local knowledge may be adequate. If your itinerary includes mainland locations, the Apapa port area, or any movement after dark, a full close protection team is the appropriate level of cover.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions: Lagos security

Lagos carries a critical risk rating based on current threat assessments. The UK FCDO advises against all but essential travel to several Nigerian states and notes persistent security concerns across Lagos (FCDO Nigeria advisory, March 2026). Armed robbery, kidnapping and civil unrest combine to create one of the most challenging operating environments on our assessment list.

Foreign business travellers face elevated risk because they are perceived as high-value targets. Criminals monitor airport arrival routes and hotel zones to identify foreign nationals. Visible wealth indicators (watches, phones, branded luggage) increase targeting probability. The kidnap-for-ransom economics favour targeting foreigners who are assumed to have greater financial resources.

Kidnapping remains one of the most persistent threats in Lagos. Both express kidnapping (short-duration, ATM-focused) and longer-term kidnap-for-ransom operations affect the city. Routes from Murtala Muhammed International Airport to Victoria Island and Ikoyi are known targeting corridors (OSAC Nigeria Crime and Safety Report, 2025). Most incidents involve armed groups operating in vehicles.

Armed robbery is the most frequent violent crime affecting business travellers. Street-level robberies occur in commercial districts during business hours, while carjackings concentrate on the Third Mainland Bridge and airport approach roads. Armed groups use motorcycles to approach vehicles stalled in traffic. Nigerian Police Force response times in Lagos traffic can exceed 30 minutes.

The terrorism threat in Lagos is lower than in northern Nigeria, but it is not absent. Boko Haram and ISWAP have historically focused on the north-east, though security alerts for Lagos as a symbolic target have been issued. The FCDO advisory covers potential attacks throughout Nigeria (FCDO Nigeria advisory, March 2026). Crowded public spaces, transport hubs and government buildings should be treated as potential targets.

Mainland areas including Oshodi, Mushin, Ajegunle and the Apapa port corridor carry the highest risk profiles. Most business travellers operate in the Victoria Island, Ikoyi and Lekki Phase 1 corridor, which has a lower but not negligible threat level. Risk increases significantly after dark across all areas. Airport transfer routes are high-risk regardless of time of day.

Pre-booked vehicles with vetted drivers are the recommended method. Public minibuses (danfo) should be avoided entirely. Rideshare apps operate in Lagos but do not provide driver vetting or route security. A dedicated security driver who knows the city's traffic patterns, criminal hotspots and alternative routes is the most practical risk-reduction measure available.
