Security landscape

Security situation in Nigeria

Nigeria's security picture is fractured. The north faces an ongoing Islamist insurgency, with Boko Haram and ISWAP active across Borno, Yobe, and Adamawa states. The Niger Delta region has its own set of problems: pipeline vandalism, oil theft, and armed groups that operate with near impunity in remote waterways. The Middle Belt suffers from farmer-herder violence that kills more people annually than the insurgency makes international headlines.

For corporate travellers, the picture in Lagos and Abuja is different but not simple. Street crime in Lagos is persistent and often violent. Express kidnapping (grab, withdraw cash from ATMs, release) is a known tactic. Armed robbery peaks after dark, particularly on the mainland. Targeted kidnap-for-ransom operations have increased since 2020, and they no longer focus only on expatriates. Wealthy Nigerians are the primary targets now, though foreign nationals still carry elevated risk.

Protests can turn volatile without warning. The 2020 EndSARS movement, which started as peaceful demonstrations against police brutality, escalated to lethal force by security services at the Lekki toll gate (Amnesty International, October 2020). Road blockades and tyre fires remain common during periods of political tension.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Nigeria

Nigeria's private security sector is regulated under the Private Guard Companies Act of 1986. The Nigeria Security and Civil Defence Corps (NSCDC) oversees licensing and compliance for guard companies operating nationally. There are over 1,500 registered private security firms in the country, though the actual number including unregistered operators is likely higher.

Foreign security companies cannot operate independently. They must partner with a Nigerian-registered firm. This is not a formality. The registration requirement is actively enforced, and operating without a local partner creates both legal exposure and operational risk. Armed security requires additional licensing beyond standard guard company registration. Corporate clients in Lagos and Abuja commonly employ armed close protection teams, but the licensing paperwork must be in order before any deployment.

Vetting standards vary widely between firms. Some of the larger companies maintain training standards that approach international benchmarks. Many smaller operators do not. Due diligence on any provider is essential before engagement.
