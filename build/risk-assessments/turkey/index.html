Security landscape

Security situation in Turkey

Turkey's security environment reflects its geographic position between stable Western Europe and volatile Middle Eastern conflict zones. The UK FCDO advises against all but essential travel to parts of the country, particularly areas near the Syrian border (FCDO Travel Advice: Turkey, updated March 2026). The US State Department maintains a Level 2 advisory, citing risks from terrorism and arbitrary detention (US State Department Travel Advisory: Turkey, January 2026).

Istanbul, the country's commercial centre, faces a medium overall threat level. OSAC rates countrywide crime risk as HIGH (OSAC Crime and Safety Report: Turkey, 2025). Terrorism remains the most significant concern, scored at 4/5 for Istanbul. Multiple attacks in the past decade have targeted transport hubs, tourist areas, and government buildings. The PKK, its affiliates, and ISIS-linked cells have all conducted operations on Turkish soil.

Street crime in Istanbul is moderate. Petty theft and scam operations target visitors in Sultanahmet, Taksim, and Grand Bazaar areas. Kidnapping risk is low at 2/5, primarily linked to political or organised crime contexts rather than opportunistic targeting of foreign nationals.

Since the 2016 coup attempt, Turkey has maintained an expanded security and surveillance posture. State of emergency provisions, although formally lifted, have been codified into law. This means heightened police presence and periodic security operations, particularly in eastern provinces. Earthquake preparedness remains a growing concern for security planning following the February 2023 disaster that killed over 50,000 people (USGS, February 2023).

Regulations

Security industry regulations in Turkey

Turkey's private security sector operates under Law No. 5188, which governs licensing, training requirements, and operational boundaries for security companies (Turkish Ministry of Interior, Private Security Department). All close protection officers must hold a valid private security licence issued by provincial governors' offices.

The executive protection market has grown since 2016. Licensed firms operate under Turkish law and face regular audits. Foreign operators cannot work independently. They must partner with or be employed by a Turkish-licensed security company.

Firearms permits for private security are available but tightly controlled. Most EP operations in Istanbul use unarmed officers supported by vehicle security and advance route planning. Companies serving foreign clients typically maintain liaison relationships with local law enforcement to coordinate armed support when threat assessments require it.
