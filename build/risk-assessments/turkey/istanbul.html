Istanbul risk assessment: security analysis for travellers and business visitors

Istanbul straddles two continents and carries a security profile unlike any other city on this site. Violent crime rates are lower than Latin American or African cities on our list. But the terrorism threat is real and recent, with multiple attacks since 2015 by PKK, Daesh, and DHKP/C operatives. Add seismic risk (a magnitude 6.2 earthquake struck in April 2025), political sensitivities, and heavy-handed policing of dissent, and the picture requires careful reading.

Crime

Crime in Istanbul

Violent crime in Istanbul is lower than in most cities on this site. You are far less likely to face armed robbery here than in Bogota or Mexico City. The threats are different: pickpocketing, bag snatching, drink spiking, and financial scams aimed at tourists.

Pickpocketing concentrates around Sultanahmet, the Grand Bazaar, and on crowded trams (particularly the T1 line between Kabatas and Sultanahmet). Techniques are standard: distraction, crowding, and bump-and-grab. Phone snatching by passing motorcyclists happens but is less frequent than in Latin American cities.

Scam bars are an Istanbul-specific problem. Men are approached on Istiklal Avenue or around Taksim by a friendly local who suggests a bar. Once inside, drinks appear, and the bill arrives at several thousand euros. Bouncers block the exit until payment is made, often by card under duress. The FCDO specifically warns about this (FCDO Turkey travel advice, April 2026). Drink spiking in nightlife venues follows a similar pattern: incapacitation followed by card fraud.

Street robbery with violence is uncommon in tourist and business districts but occurs in Tarlabaasi and the back streets of Fatih after dark. These areas have higher concentrations of poverty and less police presence.

Terrorism

Terrorism and political violence

This is where Istanbul's risk profile is most serious. The city has been hit repeatedly by attacks from multiple groups with different ideologies and targets.

The November 2022 bombing on Istiklal Avenue killed 6 people and injured 81 (BBC News, 13 November 2022). Turkish authorities attributed the attack to PKK-linked operatives, though the PKK denied involvement. In 2024, Turkey experienced a courthouse attack, a church attack, and an attack on an aerospace defence facility, all attributed to different groups (OSAC Turkey Crime and Safety Report, 2025).

Three organisations are the primary concern. The PKK (Kurdistan Workers' Party) has conducted urban attacks targeting police and military. Daesh (ISIS) carried out the 2017 Reina nightclub attack on New Year's Eve (39 dead) and has maintained recruiting networks in Turkey. The DHKP/C (Revolutionary People's Liberation Party/Front) is a Marxist-Leninist group that has targeted government and security force facilities.

Heavy security is now standard at major transport hubs, shopping centres, and tourist sites. Armed police and barriers are visible at Istanbul Airport (IST) and Taksim Square. This presence reduces but does not eliminate risk. The FCDO assesses that terrorist attacks in Turkey are "likely" (FCDO Turkey travel advice, April 2026).

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping risk in Istanbul is low. Express kidnapping of the type seen in Latin American cities does not operate here at any meaningful scale. The OSAC 2025 report does not flag Istanbul as a kidnapping concern for foreign nationals.

The risk is higher near the southeastern border with Syria and Iraq, where PKK activity and conflict-zone dynamics create different conditions entirely. For business travellers operating only in Istanbul, kidnapping should not be a primary planning concern. Standard precautions around transport and accommodation are sufficient for this threat category.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Turkey has a complex relationship with public protest. The 2013 Gezi Park demonstrations in Taksim Square drew millions of people and were met with tear gas, water cannon, and mass arrests. Since the failed coup attempt of July 2016, authorities have maintained broad emergency-style powers, and tolerance for public dissent has narrowed considerably.

Protests related to Kurdish political issues, political opposition, labour disputes, and women's rights occur in Istanbul. Taksim Square and Istiklal Avenue are the traditional focal points. Police response is often swift and disproportionate to the scale of the gathering. Tear gas deployment happens with little warning.

For business visitors, the main risk is being caught in a dispersal operation rather than targeted by protesters. Stay away from Taksim during planned demonstrations. Monitor local news and Turkish social media for planned actions.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Istanbul's traffic is aggressive and congested. Driving standards are poor by European norms, with lane discipline treated as optional and speed limits as suggestions. Cross-Bosphorus commutes during rush hour can take well over two hours.

Istanbul Airport (IST) is modern, well-secured, and efficiently run. Transport from IST to the city is straightforward via Havaist bus, taxi, or pre-booked transfer. Licensed yellow taxis use meters, but some drivers run scams on airport routes (longer routes, "broken" meters). Agree on the fare or confirm the meter is running before departure. Rideshare options are limited in Turkey; BiTaksi is the local taxi app and is more reliable than hailing from the street.

The Metro and tram network is generally safe. Pickpocketing on crowded T1 trams is the main concern. Keep bags in front of you and phones secured. After dark, transport risk is more about aggressive driving than personal safety on public transit.

Areas

Areas of Istanbul: where is safer, where is not

Lower-risk areas

  • Beyoglu / Nisantasi (upscale shopping, dining, business hotels)
  • Kadikoy (Asian side, active market district, lower crime)
  • Besiktas (residential, university area, waterfront, moderate police presence)
  • Bebek (affluent Bosphorus neighbourhood, low street crime)

Higher-risk areas

  • Istiklal Avenue (terrorism target, pickpocketing, scam bars)
  • Tarlabaasi (high poverty, street crime, drug activity, avoid after dark)
  • Fatih back streets after dark (lower police presence, opportunistic crime)
  • Sultanahmet (tourist scams, pickpocketing, overcharging. Safe for violent crime)
Local security

Police and local security forces

Turkish police maintain a heavy presence in Istanbul, particularly since the 2016 coup attempt. Armed officers, armoured vehicles, and security checkpoints are common near government buildings, transport hubs, and tourist sites. Tourist police units operate in Sultanahmet and can assist with scam-related complaints and lost property.

Corruption within Turkish law enforcement exists but is less of a direct threat to foreign visitors than in Latin American contexts. The primary concern for foreigners is the breadth of Turkish law: social media posts critical of the state, photographing military installations, and certain political activities can result in detention. The FCDO warns about the risk of arrest for actions that would be legal in many other countries (FCDO Turkey travel advice, April 2026). Keep political opinions private, avoid photographing security infrastructure, and do not engage in protests.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Istanbul

Istanbul is more accessible than many high-risk cities. Most business travellers manage visits to the commercial districts without close protection, provided they take the basics seriously: pre-booked transport, awareness of the scam bar problem, and avoiding demonstration areas.

Professional security is worth considering when the visit involves high-value individuals with a public profile (the terrorism threat makes low-profile movement more important here than in lower-threat cities), extended stays with movement across multiple districts including less central areas, events or meetings near known target zones like Istiklal or Taksim, and travel that coincides with politically sensitive dates or planned protests. A local security advisor who understands Turkish policing, the political landscape, and the terrorism threat picture adds value that goes beyond physical protection. In Istanbul, the intelligence component matters as much as the close protection.

Istanbul does not fit neatly into the European category. Its terrorism threat is higher than Western European capitals, with multiple attacks since 2015 by PKK, Daesh and DHKP/C operatives. Street crime is lower than London or Paris for most categories. Earthquake risk adds a dimension that few European cities share at similar scale. The result is a moderate overall profile with spikes in specific threat categories.

The terrorism threat remains the defining security concern. PKK-linked groups, Daesh and the leftist DHKP/C have all conducted attacks, targeting the Ataturk Airport (2016) and Istiklal Avenue (2022) among other locations. Turkish security forces have disrupted numerous plots, but the capability for further attacks persists. Scam bars (meyhane scams) are well-documented in tourist districts, particularly around Taksim and Istiklal Avenue. The pattern involves a friendly local suggesting a nearby bar, followed by bills running to thousands of US dollars and physical intimidation if victims refuse to pay. While not violent crime in the traditional sense, incidents can escalate. Avoiding unsolicited social approaches in entertainment districts is the primary countermeasure.

Istanbul sits on the North Anatolian Fault, and seismologists have warned that a major earthquake affecting the city is overdue. The April 2025 magnitude 6.2 earthquake provided a direct reminder. For business travellers, this means selecting hotels with post-2000 seismic-compliant construction, knowing evacuation routes, and having a communications plan for potential infrastructure disruption.
