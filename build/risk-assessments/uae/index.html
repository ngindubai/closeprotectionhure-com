Security landscape

Security situation in the UAE

The UAE's internal security environment is tightly controlled and generally stable. The UK FCDO issued updated advice in April 2026 citing regional tensions, advising against all but essential travel depending on the evolving situation (FCDO Travel Advice: UAE, updated April 2026). The US State Department maintains Level 2 for the country (US State Department Travel Advisory: UAE, January 2026).

Dubai carries a low overall risk score of 2/5. OSAC rates countrywide crime as LOW (OSAC Crime and Safety Report: UAE, 2025). Violent crime is rare. Petty crime exists but at levels well below most international cities. The emirate's extensive CCTV coverage and responsive police force contribute to a controlled environment.

Terrorism risk sits at 2/5 for Dubai. While the UAE has not experienced a major terrorist attack on its soil, its regional position and high-profile status mean the threat cannot be dismissed. Houthi attacks on UAE infrastructure in January 2022 demonstrated that the country is not beyond the reach of regional conflict (Reuters, January 2022). Regional escalation in April 2026 has increased this concern.

Kidnapping risk is minimal at 1/5. The primary security considerations for visiting executives and HNWIs are: surveillance and information security (particularly around sensitive business dealings), road safety, and reputational risk in a jurisdiction where legal norms differ from Western expectations. Alcohol-related incidents and social media posts have led to detention of foreign nationals.

Regulations

Security industry regulations in the UAE

The Security Industry Regulatory Agency (SIRA) governs private security in Dubai. All security firms must be SIRA-licensed and comply with emirate-level regulations. Abu Dhabi operates under a separate but similar regulatory framework.

Foreign operators cannot work independently in the UAE. They must partner with locally registered and licensed entities. Firearms are strictly prohibited for private security personnel, with no exceptions. EP operations are entirely unarmed, relying on trained personnel, vehicle security, and coordination with local police when threat levels escalate.

The market is growing. Corporate headquarters relocations, COP28 legacy events, Dubai's expanding conference calendar, and the influx of HNWIs to the emirate have all increased demand for vetted close protection officers (Dubai Department of Economy and Tourism, 2025). SIRA maintains a public registry of licensed operators.
