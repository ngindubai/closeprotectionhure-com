Crime

Crime in Dubai

Dubai is one of the safest major cities in the world for conventional crime. Petty theft is extremely rare. Mugging, violent assault and armed robbery are almost nonexistent in the areas where business travellers operate. Heavy surveillance (CCTV networks, facial recognition, extensive police presence) and severe penalties suppress criminal activity effectively.

The crimes that do occur tend to be financial: property fraud, business scams, and cybercrime. Visitors involved in commercial disputes can find themselves detained or subject to travel bans under UAE civil law. This is more common than most Western visitors expect.

The real legal risks for visitors are behavioural, not criminal in the traditional sense. Dubai enforces strict laws on alcohol consumption (only in licensed venues), drugs (zero tolerance, including residual traces), public decency, cohabitation, and social media posts. Tourists have been arrested for swearing in WhatsApp messages, making complaints about hotels on social media, and public displays of affection (FCDO UAE travel advice, April 2026). Alcohol-related incidents, particularly around the Bur Dubai and Deira nightlife areas, lead to arrests each year.

For business visitors: conventional crime is not your concern in Dubai. The legal framework is.

Terrorism

Terrorism and regional security

Dubai itself has not experienced a major terrorist attack. The UAE's counter-terrorism capability is strong, well resourced and technologically advanced. Domestic threat actors are effectively suppressed through surveillance and intelligence operations.

The risk comes from the region. In April 2026, the FCDO issued an advisory against all but essential travel to the UAE, citing heightened regional tensions. Houthi maritime attacks in the Red Sea and Gulf of Aden have escalated since late 2023, and the broader Middle East security environment remains volatile. The FCDO has specifically noted potential threats to Israeli and Jewish targets in the region (FCDO UAE travel advice, April 2026).

The UAE's proximity to Iran (roughly 150km across the Strait of Hormuz) places it within range of Iranian ballistic missiles and drones. While the UAE maintains strong air defences and diplomatic channels, the regional escalation cycle creates a background threat to commercial infrastructure, aviation, and population centres.

For most business travellers, the day-to-day terrorism risk in Dubai is low. The FCDO advisory reflects regional instability rather than an imminent threat to the city itself. But regional conditions can change quickly, and travel plans should be reviewed against current advisories.

Kidnapping

Kidnapping risk

Kidnapping in Dubai is virtually nonexistent. The combination of blanket surveillance, rapid police response, strict penalties and a tightly controlled population makes this one of the lowest-risk cities in the world for abduction.

No pattern of ransom kidnapping, express kidnapping or criminal abduction targeting foreign nationals exists in Dubai. High-profile custody disputes between nationals of different countries occasionally make headlines, but these are civil matters, not security threats to business travellers.

Civil unrest

Protests and civil unrest

Public protests are effectively prohibited in the UAE. The government maintains control through a combination of legal restrictions, surveillance technology, and a population structure where over 85% of residents are expatriates with work visas tied to their employers.

There is no recent history of street protests, political demonstrations or organised civil unrest in Dubai. Labour disputes in the construction sector have occasionally resulted in work stoppages at industrial sites (Sonapur, Al Quoz), but these are localised and do not affect business districts.

The risk of being caught in civil unrest in Dubai is the lowest of any city in this assessment series.

Transport

Transport and road safety

Road accidents are the primary physical hazard in Dubai. Sheikh Zayed Road, the arterial highway connecting most of the city's business and hotel districts, sees aggressive driving at high speeds. Tailgating at 140km/h is common. Lane discipline is poor. The road fatality rate, while improved from a decade ago, remains above Western European averages (Dubai Roads and Transport Authority annual statistics, 2025).

Dubai International Airport (DXB) is well managed with strong security protocols. The arrival process is efficient and the airport itself presents no unusual risk. Al Maktoum International (DWC) handles growing traffic but is further from central business districts.

The Dubai Metro and tram system are clean, safe and well policed. Taxis are metered, regulated and generally trustworthy. Ride-hailing services (Uber, Careem) operate legally and provide GPS-tracked journey records. There is no transport equivalent of the chaotic, high-risk scenarios seen in Lagos or Mumbai.

For corporate travellers: use a private driver or taxi for door-to-door journeys. The Metro is efficient for routes it covers. The main risk is other drivers on the road, not the transport infrastructure itself.

Local security

Police and local security forces

Dubai Police are among the most effective law enforcement agencies in the Middle East. Corruption is very low. Response times are fast. The force is technologically advanced, with AI-powered surveillance, drone patrols, and predictive policing systems deployed across the city. Tourist police units operate in major commercial and leisure areas.

For visitors, the concern is not police competence but the strictness of the laws they enforce. Dubai applies UAE federal law, which has been modernised in recent years but remains strict by Western standards. Key points for business travellers:

  • Drugs: zero tolerance. Possession of even trace amounts (including in bloodstream) can result in a four-year prison sentence.
  • Alcohol: legal only in licensed venues. Public intoxication is a criminal offence.
  • Social media: defamatory, insulting or critical posts about individuals, businesses or the government can result in fines and imprisonment.
  • Photography: taking photographs of government buildings, military installations or individuals without consent can lead to detention.

The April 2026 FCDO advisory on regional tensions means the security environment is under heightened monitoring. Dubai Police capabilities are strong, but the regional backdrop adds a layer of uncertainty that did not exist two years ago.

Police score: 5/5. Among the most capable and least corrupt forces in this assessment series.

Recommendation

When to consider professional security in Dubai

Dubai's low crime rates lead many organisations to skip security planning entirely. That is a mistake for certain profiles. Low crime does not mean zero need for executive protection, particularly when the client is a visiting VIP, public figure, or executive whose presence attracts attention.

The April 2026 FCDO advisory on regional tensions changes the calculation. Organisations with duty-of-care obligations should be conducting risk assessments for any travel to the UAE under current conditions. A security team provides real-time threat monitoring, route planning that accounts for regional developments, and rapid extraction capability if the situation deteriorates.

High-profile events (conferences at DIFC, product launches, investor meetings) benefit from discreet close protection that manages crowd exposure and access control. Reputational protection is a legitimate driver: ensuring that a VIP's movements in Dubai are managed without incident, particularly given the strict legal environment around public behaviour and media.

For routine corporate travel to Dubai under normal conditions, a security driver and pre-trip legal briefing are usually adequate. Under the current regional advisory, organisations should consider upgrading to professional close protection with threat intelligence monitoring.
