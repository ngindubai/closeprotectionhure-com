The Port of Spain security environment

The UK FCDO advises travellers to Trinidad and Tobago to exercise a high degree of caution, citing gang-related firearms violence and carjacking as the primary risks. Trinidad and Tobago has one of the Caribbean’s highest per-capita murder rates, driven by territorial gang conflict in communities including Laventille, Sea Lots, and the East-West Corridor. While the most intense violence is concentrated in specific communities outside the primary corporate operational zones, carjacking and armed robbery targeting high-value vehicles and individuals affect the Westmoorings, Goodwood Park, and St Clair zones where most international principals operate.

The connection between Trinidad’s criminal landscape and Venezuelan narcotics transit routes sustains the gang firearms capacity that underlies the overall crime environment. The TTPS and SIA framework provides a state security architecture that is more developed than in many Caribbean states, and coordination with police and security services is a standard component of EP programme design.

Trinidad and Tobago’s energy sector, built on natural gas, LNG, and petrochemicals, draws a substantial and regular flow of corporate executives from the UK, USA, and across the energy industry. These principals constitute the core of Port of Spain’s EP client base, alongside diplomatic staff and regional financial sector principals.

What executive protection covers in Port of Spain

An EP programme in Port of Spain covers the principal’s full movement cycle: Piarco Airport arrival and transfer on the Churchill Roosevelt Highway, hotel and accommodation assessment in the Westmoorings or St Clair zones, movements to the International Waterfront Centre and Ministry of Energy, advance work at meeting venues, and any overland travel to energy sites at Point Fortin or Pointe-a-Pierre. Armed or low-profile CP is determined by the threat assessment for the specific principal and engagement.

Medical planning identifies Westshore Medical or Medical Associates as the primary receiving facility for acute emergencies, with Miami at approximately 4 hours as the medevac destination for cases requiring higher-level care.

Licensing and Regulatory Framework in Trinidad and Tobago

Private security in Trinidad and Tobago is regulated under the Private Security Industry Act 2014 and its amendments, administered by the Licensing Authority within the Ministry of National Security. Security companies and their individual officers must hold current licences from the Licensing Authority, and armed security officers require separate authorisation under the Firearms Act. The Trinidad and Tobago Police Service (TTPS) retains oversight responsibility for security industry compliance and coordinates with the Trinidad and Tobago Security Intelligence Agency (SIA) on matters involving higher-risk operational contexts. Trinidad and Tobago's private security market is mature relative to most Caribbean states: a well-established domestic industry exists alongside subsidiaries of international security companies, and EP providers with documented close-protection experience are available in the market. Clients should verify that their provider holds a current Licensing Authority licence, that armed personnel carry valid authorisations under the Firearms Act, and that the company has demonstrable close-protection experience in Trinidad and Tobago's specific urban security environment. The Defence Force of Trinidad and Tobago and the TTPS Special Operations Response Team (SORT) are the state's primary armed response assets; coordination protocols with these agencies are a component of EP programme planning for higher-risk engagements.

Gang Violence and Firearms Crime

Trinidad and Tobago has one of the Caribbean's highest per-capita murder rates, driven predominantly by gang-related firearms violence concentrated in specific urban communities. The UK FCDO advises travellers to exercise a high degree of caution in Trinidad and Tobago and identifies gang violence and firearms crime as the primary threats. The TTPS Crime and Problem Analysis Branch data consistently shows the majority of homicides concentrated in communities including Laventille, Sea Lots, Carenage, and parts of the East-West Corridor, which are generally outside the primary EP operational zones used by international corporate principals. However, carjacking, armed robbery targeting perceived high-value individuals, and opportunistic crime affect the broader Port of Spain metropolitan area including Westmoorings, Goodwood Park, and the St Clair diplomatic zone. High-value vehicle targeting in upmarket residential and commercial areas has been documented. EP teams counter these risks through vehicle selection, route discipline, anti-surveillance, and avoidance of predictable patterns in the principal's movement. Nighttime movements carry elevated risk across Port of Spain's wider metropolitan area and are managed with additional support and routing care. The criminal landscape in Trinidad and Tobago is also linked to its proximity to Venezuela and to the trans-shipment of South American narcotics, which funds armed gangs and sustains their capacity for violence.

Operational EP Protocols in Port of Spain

Port of Spain's primary EP operational zones are the city centre around Independence Square and the Queen's Park Savannah, the St Clair and Newtown diplomatic and residential zones, the Westmoorings and Goodwood Park upmarket residential and commercial corridors on the western side of the metropolitan area, and the Port of Spain waterfront where the International Waterfront Centre houses major corporate and government tenants. Piarco International Airport (POS) is located approximately 26 kilometres east of the city centre along the Churchill Roosevelt Highway. The airport transfer is the longest high-exposure movement in a typical Port of Spain EP programme: the Churchill Roosevelt Highway runs through areas with documented gang activity in its eastern sections, and the transfer vehicle and timing are managed accordingly. EP programmes typically include a close-protection officer with the principal and a follow vehicle for higher-risk principals. Armoured vehicles are available but less commonly used for the Port of Spain market than in higher-risk African or Middle Eastern environments; the assessment of whether to deploy armoured vehicles is made on the basis of the specific principal profile and threat assessment. Advance work at hotels, the International Waterfront Centre, and meeting venues is standard. The programme includes coordination with the client organisation's own security function and with relevant embassy security advisers.

Energy Sector Principal Profile

Trinidad and Tobago's petroleum and petrochemical sector is the dominant driver of international corporate travel to Port of Spain. The country holds significant natural gas reserves and is one of the Western Hemisphere's leading liquefied natural gas (LNG) exporters: Atlantic LNG operates the Point Fortin liquefaction facility, and BP, Shell, BHP, and other international operators hold upstream assets in Trinidad's offshore blocks. Energy sector executives visiting for government negotiations at the Ministry of Energy and Energy Industries, commercial discussions with NGC (National Gas Company of Trinidad and Tobago), or operations management at Point Fortin and the Petrotrin refinery site at Pointe-a-Pierre require EP programmes calibrated to their specific movement profile. Point Fortin is located approximately 90 kilometres southwest of Port of Spain and overland movements to LNG and refinery sites require route assessment beyond the capital's operational zones. The energy sector's historical dominance of the Trinidad economy means that EP clients from this sector have a relatively high profile in Port of Spain, and the social and business intelligence that EP operators bring to principal briefings is as important as the physical security component of the programme. Financial and corporate services principals from the banking and insurance sectors, which are significant in Port of Spain as a regional financial centre, form a secondary EP client segment.

Medical and Emergency Evacuation Planning

Trinidad and Tobago has the best medical infrastructure in the English-speaking Caribbean, with the Port of Spain General Hospital providing a public emergency service and private facilities including Westshore Medical Private Hospital and Medical Associates Hospital in St Joseph offering a higher standard of care for emergency cases. Private hospital services in Port of Spain are capable of managing many acute emergencies and minor trauma, making them a meaningful medical asset compared to the limited capability available in many other jurisdictions where EP programmes operate. However, for complex trauma, specialist surgical cases, or conditions requiring intensive care at an international standard, medical evacuation to Miami is the planning standard. Miami is approximately 4 hours by air from Piarco International Airport, served by multiple daily American Airlines and Caribbean Airlines flights. Jackson Memorial Hospital and Mount Sinai Medical Center in Miami are common receiving facilities for medical evacuations from the Caribbean. EP programmes operating in Port of Spain should identify the primary local receiving hospital and confirm travel insurance coverage for medical evacuation to Miami. A first-aid-trained CPO is standard for all Port of Spain EP programmes. For site visits to Point Fortin or Pointe-a-Pierre, where response times are longer, a dedicated medic may be warranted.
