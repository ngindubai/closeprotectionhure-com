Executive protection for Saint Petersburg is a programme decision, not a booking, and the first item in that programme is the FCDO’s advisory: against all travel to Russia, a country-wide position that gives Saint Petersburg no exemption on account of its museums or its imperial history. Anyone weighing a visit needs to treat that advisory as the starting input for planning, not a line to note and move past.

Saint Petersburg adds a specific complication of its own on top of the national picture. Both the British and US consulates general in the city closed in 2018, and neither has reopened. A Moscow-based executive at least has an embassy to call on; a Saint Petersburg-based one does not, and any escalation runs through the Embassy in Moscow or the Consulate in Ekaterinburg instead. That gap belongs in the risk assessment from the outset, alongside the detention risk the FCDO names explicitly for British nationals across the whole country, and the fact that Saint Petersburg has itself suffered a fatal terrorist attack, the April 2017 metro bombing that killed 15 people.

Where a genuinely essential, legally reviewed visit does proceed, information security sits alongside physical protection as a primary planning item: travel-dedicated devices, encrypted communications, and the assumption that hotel rooms and meeting venues may be monitored. Financial and transit planning has to account for a country where Western bank cards do not work and where Pulkovo has no direct Western flight connection, meaning cash roubles and third-country routing are baseline requirements rather than contingencies.

What this covers

Operational detail for Saint Petersburg

The Advisory Position, Stated Plainly

The FCDO advises against all travel to Russia. This is a national position, not a Moscow-specific one, and it covers Saint Petersburg on identical terms: risk of arbitrary detention for British nationals, active conflict-related instability, and a level of UK government support that is already limited even where an embassy operates. Executive protection planning for Saint Petersburg begins with this advisory as the primary input, not as a caveat added afterward.

The Added Complication: No Local Consular Presence

The British Consulate General in Saint Petersburg closed in 2018, following the diplomatic expulsions after the Salisbury poisoning; the US Consulate General closed the same year on Russian government order. Neither has been restored. This puts Saint Petersburg in a materially worse position than Moscow for any executive requiring emergency consular contact: the nearest UK support is the Embassy in Moscow, reachable only by flying, or the Consulate in Ekaterinburg further still.

Who This Service Is Realistically For

Since 2022, we do not treat Saint Petersburg as a corporate destination in any conventional sense. The residual demand we see is narrow: dual nationals managing family or legal matters that cannot be conducted remotely, or organisations with legacy asset or wind-down obligations requiring a documented, board-approved, legally reviewed visit. This page exists to inform that narrow group, not to encourage a wider audience toward Saint Petersburg.

Terrorism and Detention Risk, Applied Locally

Saint Petersburg is not a passive backdrop to Russia's national risk picture; it has been directly targeted. The 3 April 2017 metro bombing killed 15 people, including the bomber, and injured more than 45, and eleven men were convicted over the attack in 2019. The FCDO's current assessment that a terrorist attack in Russia is highly likely applies with equal weight here, and the detention risk explicitly named for British nationals is a national, not a Moscow-specific, feature.

Information Security for Any Essential Visit

As in Moscow, any residual engagement in Saint Petersburg requires travel-dedicated devices, encrypted communications on independently assessed platforms, and the working assumption that hotel rooms, meeting spaces, and vehicles may be monitored. This is not an optional add-on to physical protection; it is a primary component of the assessment, and it should be built in from the first planning conversation.

Financial and Transit Planning

No direct Western commercial flights serve Pulkovo Airport since 2022, and Western-issued bank cards, UK cards included, do not function anywhere in Russia. Any executive protection plan for Saint Petersburg needs a fully cash-based financial plan in roubles and a transit plan built around third-country routing, not standard European travel assumptions.
