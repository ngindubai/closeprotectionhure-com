Executive protection in Havana is not a conventional deployable programme; MININT controls all security functions, so duty of care here means structured state-mediated support rather than an independent protection detail. This page covers what a documented programme actually looks like: pre-visit briefings on surveillance and communications security, local liaison officers managing logistics and movement, and a risk framework built around Cuban legal and political constraints rather than a standard corporate template.

Operational detail for Havana

Threat and Risk Assessment: Havana

Havana carries a moderate risk rating for visiting executives, shaped by a combination of rising petty crime, state surveillance of foreign nationals and structural limitations in the available medical and commercial infrastructure. FCDO Cuba 2024 notes increased petty crime in Habana Vieja and Centro Habana linked to economic deterioration following the compounding effects of the US embargo tightening and pandemic-period revenue collapse; jinetero scam activity (overcharging, guiding to unofficial businesses, currency fraud) is the most common contact vector for visiting executives. FCDO Cuba 2024 further notes that the Ministry of Interior (MININT) monitors foreign nationals operating in Cuba, particularly business visitors; internet access is restricted and monitored; communications should not be assumed to be private. Currency and payment infrastructure is a practical operational risk: US dollars are not accepted; UK and European bank cards are unreliable in Cuba due to correspondent banking restrictions; Euro cash in sufficient quantity for the full stay is essential (FCDO Cuba 2024). Limited medical facilities relative to the health needs that could arise -- the US embargo constrains pharmaceutical supply and equipment -- mean that medical evacuation insurance and planning is a higher priority in Havana than in most other moderate-risk cities.

Licensing and Vetting in Cuba

Cuba has no independent commercial private security industry. The Ministry of Interior (MININT) controls all security operations under MININT Order 89/2011; there is no commercial CPO licensing framework equivalent to those in other jurisdictions. Foreign organisations operating in Cuba must work through state-sanctioned security arrangements, typically via the relevant state enterprise, joint venture partner, or approved ministry channel. Visiting executives who require protective security in Havana work through state-approved security protocols coordinated with the relevant Cuban counterpart organisation. Our operational framework for Havana deployments therefore centres on advance coordination with the host-side Cuban institution, thorough pre-travel briefing for the principal on Cuban operational norms, and the use of operators with documented experience of navigating the MININT framework. Independent commercial CPO deployment as practised in other jurisdictions is not available in Cuba.

Principal Movement and Route Planning in Havana

Havana executive movement divides across four primary zones: Habana Vieja (Old Havana, UNESCO World Heritage area -- tourism, historic commercial, corporate entertainment); Vedado (modern commercial and embassy district, primary executive hotels including Hotel Nacional de Cuba and Meliá Cohiba, as well as theatre, arts and university areas); Miramar (diplomatic missions, international company representative offices and joint ventures -- 5th Avenue / Avenida 5ta corridor, the primary zone for international business operations); and Playa (quieter residential, some embassy staff accommodation). Route pre-surveys cover main arterial roads and their condition (Havana's road infrastructure has significant maintenance deficits), vehicle access to diplomatic mission buildings in Miramar and the location of MININT checkpoints. In Habana Vieja, vehicle access is restricted in sections of the historic core; vehicle staging and extraction points are identified for walking meetings in the colonial zone. State surveillance is a contextual factor for route planning: movement patterns that could be interpreted as intelligence-gathering (proximity to sensitive government or military installations) are avoided.

Airport Arrival and Departure Security: Aeropuerto Internacional José Martí (HAV)

Aeropuerto Internacional José Martí (IATA: HAV) is located 20km southwest of Habana Vieja, with a transfer time of 20-30 minutes. The airport is the primary international gateway for Cuba, serving flights from Europe, Canada, Latin America and the Caribbean. US-registered aircraft and most US-originating travel remains subject to US Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) travel restrictions; most visiting executives arrive from European or Latin American hub connections. CPOs or approved state-coordinated security representatives meet principals at the international arrivals hall. The arrivals process at HAV can be extended due to Cuban immigration processing (baggage and document scrutiny is thorough; allow 90 minutes from touchdown to kerbside). Transfer vehicles are pre-positioned at the arrivals pick-up zone. Euro cash for initial payments (airport taxi supplement, porter tips) should be carried by the principal before the flight, as no reliable ATM or card facility is available at the airport.

Residential and Venue Security in Havana

Primary executive accommodation in Havana is in the Vedado district: Hotel Nacional de Cuba (Calle O y 21, Vedado -- the historical landmark property with strong security infrastructure and established government liaison) and Meliá Cohiba (Paseo y 1ra, Vedado, on the Malecón waterfront). Both properties have active relationships with MININT and maintain their own security resources. In Miramar, some executives working on joint ventures or diplomatic-adjacent engagements use the Hotel Meliá Habana (Avenida 3ra, Miramar). CPOs or state-coordinated security conduct a venue security survey on arrival, identifying emergency exits, medical routing and vehicle access. Meeting venues at Miramar Trade Centre (Avenida 3ra, Miramar) and at joint venture company offices on Avenida 5ta are surveyed before principal arrival. Communications security: hotel room conversations and telephone calls should not be treated as private in the Cuban context; meeting content of a commercially sensitive nature should be managed accordingly.

Medical and Emergency Protocols: Havana

Primary medical facility for foreign nationals: CIREN International Hospital (Centro Internacional de Restauracion Neurologica), Avenida 25 entre 158 y 160, Cubanacan, telephone +53 7 273 8102. Secondary facility: Clínica Central Cira García (the designated clinic for foreign nationals in Cuba), Calle 20 No.4101 esq. 41, Miramar, telephone +53 7 204 2811. Both facilities are staffed by Cuban medical professionals with specialist qualifications; however, pharmaceutical supply constraints due to the US embargo mean that some medications and medical consumables may not be available in Cuba. Medical evacuation insurance with confirmed evacuation capability to Mexico City or Miami is mandatory for all principals operating in Havana; evacuation routing and insurance documentation should be confirmed before travel. CPOs carry a first-aid kit and any critical medications required by the principal for the full stay. Emergency numbers: 106 (police -- PNR, Policia Nacional Revolucionaria), 104 (ambulance). Consular support: the Swiss Embassy in Havana handles British interests at +53 7 204 2611; for out-of-hours emergencies, the FCDO London 24-hour number is +44 20 7008 5000.
