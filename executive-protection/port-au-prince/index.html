Executive protection in Port-au-Prince is not a deployable programme in the conventional sense; gang networks control most of the metropolitan area and both FCDO and the US State Department advise against all travel. This page covers what a duty-of-care approach actually requires: a full armed protection package with armoured vehicles, live gang intelligence, medevac cover, and a pre-exercised extraction plan, reserved for essential travel with no functioning national regulatory authority to rely on.

No effective regulatory framework

Haiti has no functioning national private security regulatory authority. EP operators rely on individually vetted local networks. Any operator who cannot document individual vetting and armed authorisation for each assigned officer should be declined.

Port-au-Prince EP: the minimum viable standard

The minimum viable EP standard for Port-au-Prince is: armed personnel in armoured vehicles, three-vehicle formation for all movements, live gang-movement intelligence updated before each movement, medevac contract confirmed before travel, pre-positioned safe room, and two rehearsed extraction options for every principal venue. Everything below this standard is inadequate for this environment.

Executive Risk Profile: Port-au-Prince

Port-au-Prince is the most dangerous capital city in the Western Hemisphere for foreign nationals as of 2026. FCDO advises against all travel to Haiti (FCDO Haiti travel advice, 2024). The US State Department designates Haiti as Level 4: Do Not Travel (2026). The United Nations Integrated Office in Haiti (BINUH) reported in 2024 that armed gang networks control approximately 85 percent of the Port-au-Prince metropolitan area, with the G9/G-Pep coalition and rival networks engaged in active armed conflict. Mass kidnapping is a documented gang tactic; between January and June 2023 alone, BINUH recorded over 2,400 kidnapping incidents in Haiti. Executives, foreign nationals, NGO workers, and diplomatic personnel have all been targeted. There is no safe profile in Port-au-Prince: any foreign national is a target.

Pre-Advance and Venue Assessment

Pre-advance for Port-au-Prince operates differently from all other EP environments. There are no unconditionally safe venues. Pre-advance must be conducted by a locally embedded operator with real-time gang-movement intelligence updated within 24 to 48 hours of the principal's arrival. Venue assessment covers only locations that have a vetted, armed security presence (diplomatic compounds, NGO secured facilities, vetted hotel properties in Petionville or Tabarre). All movement between venues is treated as a high-risk operation requiring a full armed escort. The advance must also confirm the operational status of Toussaint Louverture International Airport with the airline and the relevant embassy before any travel commitment is made.

Protective Intelligence in Haiti

Protective intelligence for Port-au-Prince deployments must draw on live, locally embedded intelligence from vetted networks, not published advisories alone. FCDO and State Department advisories establish the baseline - both advise against all travel or designate Level 4 - but gang territorial boundaries in Port-au-Prince shift on a weekly or even daily basis. The BINUH reporting cycle, UN Security Council updates, and vetted local sources provide the most current gang-movement intelligence. Any intelligence indicating changes to gang control in areas on the principal's planned routes must halt movement until an alternative is assessed and approved.

Motorcade and Movement Security

Port-au-Prince EP movements require armed personnel in armoured vehicles at all times, with no exceptions. A minimum three-vehicle formation is recommended for any principal movement outside a secured compound: a lead vehicle to check routes, the armoured principal vehicle, and a follow vehicle with armed response capability. No movement should occur without live gang-movement intelligence confirming the planned route is not currently controlled or contested. Counter-surveillance is difficult in the current environment because gang network surveillance of vehicle movements is itself a documented tactic; vehicle route variation, unmarked vehicles, and timing unpredictability are essential.

Haiti EP Operator Licensing

Haiti has no effective national private security regulatory framework as of 2026. International EP operators in Port-au-Prince rely on individually vetted local networks whose personnel are background-checked by the operator, not by a functioning national authority. Any operator claiming Haitian government licensing should have that claim independently verified; the claim itself cannot be relied upon in an environment of state collapse. EP personnel must be armed, must have been individually vetted by the international operator, and must have documented emergency evacuation training. Engaging operators who cannot provide documentation of individual vetting should be declined.

Crisis Response and Evacuation Planning

Crisis response and evacuation planning for Port-au-Prince EP is the most critical element of any engagement - not an add-on. Before any principal enters Port-au-Prince, the following must be in place: a medevac contract with a provider that can extract from Haiti (options are severely limited; verify the provider's current capability before signing), a pre-positioned safe room at the principal's compound, an extraction plan with two rehearsed options for every principal venue, a satellite communications capability (mobile networks are unreliable), and an emergency contact at the principal's national embassy confirming current embassy operating status. Without all of these in place, the engagement should not proceed.
