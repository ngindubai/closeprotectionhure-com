What this covers

Operational detail for Caracas

Maiquetia Airport Transfer: The Highest-Risk Movement in Caracas

The road between Simon Bolivar International Airport (Maiquetia, CCS) and eastern Caracas is consistently identified as the highest-risk single element of any Caracas visit in FCDO Venezuela advisories and security sector reporting. The route descends from the coastal range through areas with documented robbery, illegal checkpoints operated by criminal and colectivo-aligned groups, and ambush incidents targeting foreign nationals travelling without security cover. No principal should travel this route unaccompanied, in an unmarked vehicle, or in a standard taxi. Pre-arranged, SENAS-licensed, armed transport is the non-negotiable baseline.

SENAS-Licensed Transfer Operators

Venezuela's private security companies and individual operators are licensed by SENAS (Servicio Nacional Autonomo de Seguridad Privada). Venezuela's economic crisis has disrupted some operators' SENAS compliance, creating a risk of engaging unlicensed or improperly authorised providers. All transfer operators used for Caracas engagements hold current SENAS company licensing and, for armed personnel, valid individual operator authorisations. SENAS documentation is provided to clients before the transfer date.

Vehicle Selection and Hardening

Transfer vehicles for the Maiquetia-to-Caracas corridor are hardened or armoured vehicles with experienced drivers who have current knowledge of the route, colectivo patrol patterns, and checkpoint activity. Standard estate cars and hotel taxis do not provide appropriate protection for this corridor. Vehicle selection is confirmed during pre-transfer planning based on the principal's profile and the current threat picture on the route. Armoured vehicles (B4 minimum) are available for principals with documented elevated targeting profiles or for periods of heightened threat on the corridor.

Colectivo Route Intelligence

Colectivos operate in multiple areas along the Maiquetia-to-Caracas route and on approaches to the eastern city districts. The colectivo threat on this corridor is not static: patrol patterns, checkpoint locations, and activity levels change with political conditions, particularly around elections, government announcements, and periods of anti-government protest. Transfer route planning incorporates current colectivo intelligence from local sources and from FCDO and OSAC reporting. Route modifications are applied if colectivo activity levels increase in the days before a scheduled transfer.

Arrivals Hall Collection Protocol

SENAS-licensed transfer teams are positioned inside the arrivals hall at CCS before the principal exits the secured arrivals area to the public concourse. The collection point is agreed with the principal before travel, including a physical description of the transfer officer and a contact protocol in the event of a communications failure. Principals are briefed not to accept assistance from any unsolicited individual in the arrivals area. The transfer vehicle is positioned for immediate departure; no waiting time in the public concourse is acceptable at CCS.

Operations Controller and Emergency Protocols

An operations controller monitors all Maiquetia-to-Caracas transfers throughout. The controller maintains current intelligence on route conditions, communicates with the driver at regular intervals, and activates the emergency protocol on any deviation from plan, unscheduled stop, or communications failure. Emergency contacts held by the controller include the principal's organisation, FCDO Venezuela emergency contacts, and any pre-identified medical facility contact for the Caracas end of the route. The communications failure protocol is activated within five minutes of loss of contact with the transfer vehicle.

SENAS, Servicio Nacional Autonomo de Seguridad Privada, is Venezuela’s national private security regulator. SENAS licences both companies and individual operators and sets the legal basis for armed private security services in Venezuela. Venezuela’s economic crisis has disrupted some operators’ compliance, meaning that unlicensed or improperly authorised providers are active in the market. SENAS verification is therefore a critical due diligence step, not a formality. All transfer operators we engage for Caracas hold current SENAS company and, where applicable, individual operator licensing.
