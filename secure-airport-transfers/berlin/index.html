Operational detail for Berlin

Berlin Brandenburg Airport Terminal Handling

Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER), which opened on 31 October 2020 after an extended construction period, operates as a single unified passenger terminal building that replaced both Tegel (TXL, closed 2020) and Schönefeld (SXF) airports. The main terminal, Terminal 1, handles the majority of commercial passenger traffic. Terminal 2, designed as a lower-cost satellite, opened in 2022 to handle additional capacity. BER is located approximately 18km south-east of central Berlin in the Schönefeld municipality of Brandenburg, adjacent to the former Schönefeld airport site. The single-terminal configuration simplifies collection logistics relative to multi-terminal hub airports; however, the terminal is still large enough to require precise kerbside staging by zone and departure level.

No Major Private Aviation Hub in Berlin

Unlike Paris (Le Bourget), London (Farnborough), or New York (Teterboro), Berlin does not have a major dedicated private aviation hub within reasonable transfer distance of the city centre. Limited private and general aviation operations can be accommodated at BER through coordination with its general aviation handling services, but the facility is not configured as a full FBO in the sense of direct apron vehicle access or dedicated private terminal. For HNWI and corporate principals arriving in Berlin on private or charter aircraft, pre-arrival coordination with BER's handling agents and the German aviation authority (LBA - Luftfahrt-Bundesamt) is required. Alternative arrangements via Hamburg or Leipzig with onward high-speed rail connection may be appropriate for some itineraries.

Section 34a GewO Licensed Drivers

All Berlin transfer drivers engaged for security or guarding functions hold a qualification under Section 34a of the Gewerbeordnung (GewO), the German Trade Regulation, which sets the legal baseline for private security personnel in Germany. The Section 34a qualification, administered through the Industrie- und Handelskammer (IHK), requires completion of an approved examination covering legal framework, first aid, conflict de-escalation, and related competencies. For higher-level security driver roles, the IHK Gepruefter Schutz- und Sicherheitsdienst qualification provides a more comprehensive framework. Drivers also hold the relevant EU driving licence category and, for commercial passenger transport, the applicable Personenbeforderungsschein (P-Schein) under Section 48 of the FeV.

A113 and A100 Stadtring Route Planning

The transfer from Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER) to central Berlin uses the A113 autobahn, which connects the airport to the A100 Berliner Stadtring (Berlin inner ring road). The A100 Stadtring circles the south-west of Berlin and provides access to the city centre via junctions at Treptow, Tempelhof, Schoneberg, and Charlottenburg. Journey time from BER to Mitte, Charlottenburg, or Potsdamer Platz is typically 35 to 50 minutes under normal conditions. During morning peak (07:30 to 09:30) and evening peak (16:30 to 18:30), congestion on the A100 Stadtring, particularly at the Innsbrucker Platz and Kaiserdamm junctions, can extend this to 60 to 70 minutes. The BER Airport Express rail (FEX) connects to Berlin Ostbahnhof and Hauptbahnhof in approximately 30 minutes.

Berlin Transfer Routes to Key Districts

Berlin's key destination districts for corporate and government-adjacent principals require different route choices from the A113 approach. For government district (Regierungsviertel) destinations near the Reichstag and Federal Chancellery, the route via A100 to Charlottenburg and then the Tiergarten corridor is the typical approach. For Mitte and Unter den Linden, the A100 to Tempelhof and then Tempelhofer Damm or the Mehringdamm corridor provides access. For Prenzlauer Berg or Friedrichshain, the A113 to the A10 and then Berliner Allee is an alternative. Transfer route selection is assessed against current BVG (Berlin Verkehrsbetriebe) road condition data and real-time autobahn monitoring from the VIFG traffic information portal.

Security Environment and Awareness in Berlin

Berlin's ambient security environment is assessed as low in the context of the broader European risk picture. The German domestic intelligence service (BfV - Bundesamt fur Verfassungsschutz) maintains assessment on extremism and politically motivated threats in the capital, and BfV annual reports are incorporated into the security awareness context for transfer operations in Berlin. The government district and diplomatic mission areas of Mitte are subject to an elevated but low-profile police and security presence. For HNWI, diplomatic, and senior corporate principals, transfer operations in Berlin focus on operational precision and discretion, with operations controller tracking maintained from landing through to confirmed accommodation drop-off.

BER’s unified terminal structure simplifies some aspects of transfer coordination compared to multi-terminal airports. There is a single arrivals hall for all commercial services, which means terminal confusion - a common issue at airports like JFK or CDG - is not a material risk. However, BER’s terminal building is sizeable, and kerbside collection zones are divided by zone and level. Pre-arrival confirmation of the correct collection zone, combined with operations controller monitoring of immigration clearance status, ensures the driver is staged at the correct point as the principal exits the arrivals hall. The BER S-Bahn and Airport Express connections are available for uncomplicated arrivals without security requirements.

Section 34a of the Gewerbeordnung (GewO) is the German legal provision that governs the operation of private security services, including security drivers. The Section 34a Sachkundeprufung (competency examination), administered by the Industrie- und Handelskammer (IHK), covers the legal powers and limits of private security personnel, handling of persons (Umgang mit Menschen), first aid, data protection, and prevention of criminal offences. Passing this examination is the minimum legal requirement to work in a security role in Germany. For security driver roles at the level relevant to HNWI and diplomatic principal transfers, operators typically hold the more advanced IHK Gepruefter Schutz- und Sicherheitsdienst certification in addition to the basic Section 34a qualification.

Pre-arrival coordination with BER’s general aviation handler, plus advance LBA coordination, is required.
