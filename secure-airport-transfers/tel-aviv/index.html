What this covers

Operational detail for Tel Aviv

Ben Gurion Airport Familiarity

Ben Gurion International Airport (TLV) operates under Israeli Airport Authority (IAA) security protocols that are widely regarded as among the most rigorous in the world. Terminal 3 handles the majority of international arrivals. Our drivers hold full familiarity with IAA landside checkpoints, vehicle staging zones, and the approved meet-and-greet procedures at Terminal 3 Arrivals Hall. Correct positioning at Ben Gurion reduces dwell time at the kerb, which is the period of greatest exposure for a newly arrived principal. Transfers into Tel Aviv CBD and the Hayarkon waterfront hotel corridor are pre-planned and briefed before each collection.

Route 1 and Ayalon Highway Planning

The primary transfer corridor from Ben Gurion to Tel Aviv uses Route 1 (Kvish 1) entering the city via the Ayalon Highway (Highway 20). The journey is typically 20 to 30 minutes outside peak periods. During heightened security periods following the October 2023 escalation, route plans are reviewed against current IDF Home Front Command guidance and FCDO Israel travel advice (updated 2024). Alternate approach routes via the Reading interchange or Glilot junction are pre-mapped to allow rapid deviation. All drivers receive a security briefing before each run covering the current rocket-alert status.

Rocket Alert and Shelter-In-Place Protocols

Israel's national rocket alert system (Red Alert / Tzeva Adom) activates with minimal warning. Drivers operating in the Tel Aviv metropolitan area are trained in the shelter-in-place protocol required under Israeli Home Front Command directives: pulling to the kerb, moving the principal to the roadside of the vehicle (away from glass), and remaining low until the all-clear. Principals travelling with children or with reduced mobility receive a specific pre-journey briefing. The IDF Home Front Command app is monitored actively on every run. These procedures reduce risk; they cannot eliminate the hazard posed by active conflict conditions.

Vehicle Profile and Counter-Surveillance

Vehicles used for Tel Aviv transfers carry no corporate livery, security markings, or visible equipment. The vehicle profile matches the local executive car market (typically Mercedes-Benz E-Class or Volvo XC60) to avoid drawing attention at Ben Gurion's departures and arrivals kerbside. Counter-surveillance awareness is standard for all drivers operating in the post-October 2023 environment. Principals who have received prior threat reporting or whose travel has been publicised are assessed for a two-vehicle arrangement. The transfer booking process includes a brief threat-context intake so that vehicle and driver allocation can be appropriately calibrated.

Corporate and Diplomatic Arrivals

Tel Aviv serves a significant corporate and diplomatic visitor population, including personnel arriving for technology sector business, government liaison, and international NGO work. Ben Gurion Terminal 3 handles all non-domestic traffic. Corporate arrivals requiring port-to-port continuity, including liaison with in-country security teams or hotel security desks at properties on Herbert Samuel Promenade or Dizengoff Street, can be coordinated through our operations centre. Discreet transfer handover to an in-country close protection team is a standard service element and is documented in the transfer brief.

Licensing and Operator Standards

Security services operating in Israel fall under the authority of the Israel Police National Unit for Licensing and Inspection. Private security companies must hold a licence issued under the Security Organisations Law (1977). Transfer operators working with foreign principals at Ben Gurion are required to comply with both IAA landside access conditions and the general licensing framework. Our Tel Aviv transfer network partners hold current licences and are vetted by our operations team before client assignments. Documentation of operative credentials is available on request before travel.
