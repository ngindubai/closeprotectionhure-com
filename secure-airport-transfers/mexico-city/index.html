The Benito Juarez International Airport arrival is the moment of highest concentrated security risk for most business visitors to Mexico City. OSAC Mexico City crime reports and FCDO advisory notices both identify the airport transfer window as a priority security concern, specifically referencing the risk of express kidnapping from unregulated taxi services and unvetted vehicles. The transit from the terminal to the first secured accommodation is the window where planning decisions have the highest return.

The Mexico City airport transfer risk environment

Benito Juarez International (AICM) is one of the busiest airports in Latin America. Its arrivals zones – both T1 and T2 – are active locations for unregulated taxi operations, and OSAC reporting documents express kidnapping incidents linked to unregistered drivers who approached foreign arrivals. The official airport taxi rank provides some security improvement over street-level unregistered vehicles, but neither provides the pre-arranged, SEGOB-registered driver standard with operations controller tracking that a professional secure transfer delivers.

Felipe Angeles International and domestic connections

Felipe Angeles International Airport (NLU) opened in 2022 and handles increasing domestic traffic. It is a longer transfer from the city’s main hotel zones than AICM, and the route north of the city carries different risk characteristics. Pre-arranged SEGOB-registered transfers from NLU follow the same protocol as AICM arrivals.

What this covers

Operational detail for Mexico City

Benito Juarez International Collection Protocol

Benito Juarez International Airport (AICM / MEX), located in the Venustiano Carranza district, operates two terminals: T1 (most international airlines) and T2 (Aeromexico and affiliates). Pre-arranged collection inside the arrivals hall is the correct protocol. The AICM kerb zone has a documented history of unregulated taxi activity targeting foreign arrivals. Authorised taxi services exist but do not provide the same vetted driver and operations controller standard as pre-arranged transfer.

Felipe Angeles International Airport Transfers

Felipe Angeles International Airport (NAICM / NLU) in Zumpango, State of Mexico, is the newer supplementary airport approximately 50 km north of central Mexico City. Transfer from NLU to Polanco or Santa Fe is significantly longer (60 to 90 minutes under normal conditions) and passes through areas with different risk characteristics from the AICM approach routes. Pre-arranged SEGOB-registered drivers are the correct approach for NLU arrivals.

Express Kidnap Counter-Protocols

Express kidnapping targeting airport arrivals -- including forced ATM withdrawals following pick-up -- is documented in OSAC Mexico City crime reporting and FCDO advisory notices. Transfer protocols include: inside-terminal-only collection (no kerb pickup for strangers), driver identity pre-confirmation, operations controller tracking throughout, and destination confirmed in advance rather than communicated in transit.

SEGOB-Registered Drivers and Vehicles

All Mexico City transfer drivers operate under a SEGOB-registered private security company. Vehicle selection is appropriate to the threat profile: standard vetted executive vehicles for most corporate transfers, armoured vehicles for principals for whom the threat assessment specifically warrants it. Armoured vehicle hire is available in Mexico City through SEGOB-licensed specialist operators.

Polanco, Santa Fe, and Reforma Route Planning

The AICM-to-Polanco corridor (via Circuito Interior or Periferico Norte), the AICM-to-Santa Fe route (via Viaducto and Periferico), and the AICM-to-Reforma corridor (via Insurgentes Sur) each have distinct traffic and security characteristics. Route selection takes current incident data, time of day, and traffic conditions into account, with contingency routing for when primary routes are affected.

Departure Transfer Protocols

The departure transfer to AICM or NLU is provided on the same security terms as the arrival transfer. Departure transfers allow for Mexico City's traffic unpredictability: the operations controller confirms real-time traffic before departure and adjusts the planned departure time accordingly. For AICM departures, adequate lead time for terminal check-in and security queues is built into the departure transfer plan.
