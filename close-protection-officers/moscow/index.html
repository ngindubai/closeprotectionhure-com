FCDO Advisory and Operational Context

The UK Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office (FCDO) advises against all travel to Russia as at 2024, citing the ongoing Russia-Ukraine conflict and the legal risks to foreign nationals created by Russia's domestic legislation. Close protection officer services in Moscow are provided for organisations and individuals maintaining a necessary presence in the city: diplomatic missions, international organisations, essential commercial operations under existing contractual obligations, and humanitarian organisations. This page is not intended to encourage travel to Russia contrary to current UK government advice.

Rosgvardia CPO Licensing

Private close protection in Russia operates under Federal Law No. 2487-I of 11 March 1992 on Private Detective and Security Activities, as amended. Both private security companies (chastnye okhrannye organizatsii, ChOO) and individual security guards must be licensed by Rosgvardia (Federal Service of the National Guard). Guards may be unarmed or armed (requiring a higher-category licence with specific weapons authorisation). Moscow's private security market is large and long-established. For foreign organisations engaging CPO services in Moscow, the operating company's relationship with Russian state structures is a due-diligence factor that goes beyond the formal licence check.

Counter-Intelligence Awareness for Foreign Principals

Moscow CPO operations for foreign corporate or diplomatic principals operate in an environment characterised by active domestic intelligence service (FSB) monitoring of foreign national activities. CPOs operating for foreign principals in Moscow must be briefed on counter-intelligence awareness: the recognition of surveillance behaviour by state-level actors (distinct from criminal surveillance), communications discipline (avoiding sensitive business or personal discussions in hotel rooms, meeting rooms, or vehicles), and the legal constraints on CPO counter-surveillance activities under Russian law. This is materially different from CPO operational planning in any Western market.

Threat Profile for Western Principals in Moscow

Foreign corporate and diplomatic principals in Moscow face a threat environment that combines conventional security risks (property crime, vehicle-related incidents in high-traffic areas) with state-level risks specific to the current political situation: selective law enforcement pressure, broadly-worded legal provisions that can be applied to foreign nationals conducting business or media activities, and a diplomatic climate that increases the consequences of any incident involving a foreign national. The CPO pre-engagement brief for Moscow addresses the full spectrum: conventional physical security, state-level legal risk awareness, and emergency departure planning.

Embassy and Diplomatic Community Coordination

For diplomatic mission staff and international organisation personnel in Moscow, CPO operations are coordinated with the relevant embassy security attache or organisation security manager. Most major diplomatic missions in Moscow maintain active security staff who can provide current threat assessment and support emergency response. The UK Embassy Moscow security team and the EU Delegation security staff are resources for British and EU nationals respectively. CPO operations in the current environment should be conducted with awareness of embassy emergency protocols and the communication chain for foreign national incidents.

Emergency Departure Planning

Emergency departure planning is a mandatory component of CPO operations in Moscow under the current FCDO advisory. The CPO pre-engagement brief includes: confirmed departure routes through Sheremetyevo (SVO) or Domodedovo (DME) airports, alternative departure options if primary airports are restricted, documentation and essential items pre-staged for rapid departure, communication protocol with the relevant embassy or organisation if an emergency arises, and the threshold criteria for initiating the departure plan. The CPO role in Moscow includes maintaining awareness of these protocols throughout the engagement.

CPO operators in Russia are licensed by Rosgvardia (Federal Service of the National Guard) under Federal Law No. 2487-I. Both companies (ChOO) and individuals hold licences, and both can be verified with Rosgvardia. For foreign organisations, the additional due-diligence step is understanding the operating company’s relationship with Russian state structures: in the current political environment, certain companies have closer FSB or Rosgvardia connections than others, which has operational implications for how they handle client information and their responsiveness to state authority requests.

CPO operations in Moscow for foreign principals should incorporate: communications security (no sensitive discussions in hotel rooms, vehicles, or meeting rooms not swept for surveillance devices), movement discipline (variation of routes and timing rather than predictable patterns), social media discipline (no posting of locations, meetings, or schedule information), awareness of surveillance behaviour by state-level actors (distinct from the criminal surveillance pattern recognised in other markets), and a clear protocol for reporting any contact with Russian law enforcement or security services to the relevant embassy. These are standard precautions for high-risk, state-intelligence-active environments.

In a Moscow emergency, the CPO’s primary responsibilities are: physical principal protection during any incident; communication with the operations controller and the relevant embassy security attache; implementation of the agreed emergency departure plan if evacuation is the appropriate response; documentation of the incident for post-event reporting; and, if law enforcement contact occurs, facilitating but not obstructing the principal’s right to communicate with their embassy under the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations. The CPO must also maintain awareness of the specific legal constraints on private security personnel under Russian law to avoid creating additional legal exposure for the principal.
