This page is about how close protection support actually works in Havana: there is no independent private security licensing framework, and all security functions are ultimately controlled by MININT and the Revolutionary Armed Forces. It covers the local liaison model used instead, how joint-venture hotel operations provide a security layer, and the extra due-diligence and communications-security preparation applied given the absence of a conventional CPO market.

The sectors that drive international business visits to Havana include the tourism and hospitality industry (Cuba’s primary foreign-exchange earner, attracting cruise industry and hotel investment discussions), the energy sector through CUPET state oil company partnerships, BioCubaFarma’s internationally recognised pharmaceutical and vaccine research programmes, and diplomatic engagements that draw on Cuba’s neutral and non-aligned positioning for bilateral meetings. As US sanctions policy evolves, the landscape of commercially permissible activity for US-adjacent businesses and their counterparts changes periodically, making legal review alongside security planning an essential pre-travel step. The economic crisis that has deepened since 2020, including widespread shortages, power outages, and the erosion of purchasing power for Cuban citizens, has created a social environment in which the visible affluence of foreign visitors is more salient than it was previously, with documented consequences for petty crime and harassment in the tourist zones (FCDO Cuba, 2024).

Operationally, the pre-visit planning model for Havana concentrates risk reduction in the preparation phase. This includes communications security briefing for the principal and travelling party, currency and financial logistics planning, hotel selection in the Miramar district (the most controlled environment for senior visitors), advance coordination with Cuban government counterparts where applicable for any MININT-approved escort arrangements, and pre-confirmation of medical evacuation insurance and the specific Cira García clinic contact. State surveillance is a planning given rather than a contingency: FCDO Cuba (2024) advises treating all communications and accommodation environments as potentially monitored, and principals attending commercially sensitive meetings should apply the communications security disciplines appropriate to a high-surveillance environment. These are disciplines that experienced international business travellers adapt to for other state-surveillance environments, and they are manageable with proper preparation. What this covers

Operational detail for Havana

Regulatory Framework and Legal Constraints

Havana presents a fundamentally different regulatory environment from any other city in this CPO network. Cuba has no independent private security industry: all security activity is controlled by MININT (Ministry of the Interior, Ministerio del Interior) under MININT Order 89/2011. Foreign organisations and individuals cannot operate a commercial close protection service in Cuba -- there is no licensing framework through which an international security company can deploy personnel on a conventional CPO basis. All security arrangements for foreign principals in Havana must instead be channelled through state-sanctioned options: hotel security managed by MININT-supervised staff, MININT-approved escort services for specific categories of foreign visitor, or corporate-level agreements negotiated between the visiting organisation and Cuban authorities in advance. What this means in practice is that pre-visit planning and MININT engagement -- rather than conventional CPO deployment -- is the primary security risk management lever available to visiting executives. Any international security consultant advising on a Havana visit must be transparent about these constraints and must not misrepresent their ability to deploy conventional CPO personnel on Cuban soil.

Petty Crime and Jinetero Activity

FCDO Cuba (2024) identifies petty crime and jinetero activity as the primary security concerns for foreign visitors in Havana, with a specific note that economic deterioration since 2020 has increased the prevalence of opportunistic crime. Jinetero activity -- a well-established category of persistent touts, scammers, and fixers who target foreign visitors in tourist areas -- is concentrated in Habana Vieja, Centro Habana, and the Malecon waterfront. Techniques range from persistent offers of private taxis, accommodation, and tours, through to more sophisticated confidence scams involving fake officials, currency exchange manipulation, and bait-and-switch commercial arrangements. While jinetero activity is primarily financial in character, it creates a pressure environment for foreign visitors that can lead to poor decisions and financial loss. Physical assault in Havana is not the dominant risk for foreign visitors, but muggings in poorly lit streets in Habana Vieja and Centro Habana during late evening are documented. The security situation has deteriorated relative to pre-2020 conditions as Cuba's economic crisis has deepened (FCDO Cuba, 2024), and the social pressure environment for foreign visitors -- who are visibly more affluent than the majority of Havana's population -- should be factored into all movement planning.

Business Zone and Operational Coverage

Havana's operational geography for business visitors is structured around two primary zones. Vedado is the modern commercial and embassy district, containing the major international hotels (Hotel Nacional, Melia Cohiba, Tryp Habana Libre), the offices of international companies permitted to operate in Cuba, and several foreign diplomatic missions. Miramar, to the west along the Avenida 5ta, is the primary diplomatic and international business district: it houses the majority of foreign embassies and consulates, the offices of joint ventures and foreign-invested entities operating in Cuba, and the Miramar Trade Center. Miramar is regarded as the most operationally appropriate base for senior business visitors and offers a more controlled environment than either Vedado or Habana Vieja. Habana Vieja (UNESCO World Heritage) contains tourism sector commercial activity and some cultural venue meetings and is appropriate for daytime hospitality in the context of a managed itinerary. Movement planning between Vedado, Miramar, and Habana Vieja should account for the variable traffic and road conditions of Havana's ageing transport infrastructure.

State Surveillance and Communications Security

State surveillance is the most significant operational security challenge unique to Havana. FCDO Cuba (2024) explicitly notes that foreign nationals may be monitored by MININT and advises that all communications -- including mobile phone calls, emails, and in-person conversations -- should be treated as potentially intercepted. Cuba's internet infrastructure is state-controlled and subject to monitoring: the ETECSA (state telecommunications operator) network is the sole ISP, and international roaming connections route through Cuban state infrastructure. Executives attending commercially sensitive negotiations or diplomatic meetings in Havana should treat hotel rooms, meeting rooms, and vehicles provided by Cuban entities as potentially monitored. Device hygiene for Havana visits is more demanding than in most other cities: using travel-specific devices containing no sensitive commercial data, avoiding the discussion of sensitive matters on any Cuban-networked device, and using end-to-end encrypted communications via roaming or satellite data where critical communications must be made. The pre-visit briefing for any principal travelling to Havana must include a specific communications security protocol agreed with their organisation's security team.

Airport and Transit Security

Aeropuerto Internacional José Martí (HAV) is located approximately 20 kilometres southwest of Habana Vieja, with a transfer time of 20 to 30 minutes to the Vedado or Miramar districts depending on traffic. The airport has separate international terminals (Terminal 2 for most non-US international flights; Terminal 3 for some charter and US connections). Arrivals management at HAV requires awareness that Cuban immigration and customs processes can be thorough and time-consuming for foreign nationals, particularly those arriving for the first time. Vehicle provision in Cuba is constrained by the limited availability of well-maintained vehicles -- the state-approved taxi and transfer services available to foreign visitors vary considerably in condition. Pre-booking a vehicle through the hotel or through a state-approved ground service provider is essential; independent kerb approaches from unlicensed drivers should be declined. Currency logistics at the airport must also be planned in advance: US dollars are not accepted, Euro cash is the most reliable currency for foreign visitors, and UK and EU bank cards are unreliable at Cuban ATMs (FCDO Cuba, 2024).

Emergency Response and Medical Support

Medical facilities present one of the most significant practical planning challenges for senior executives visiting Havana. Cuba's healthcare system has historically been internationally recognised for its trained medical professionals, but the US embargo constrains pharmaceutical and medical equipment supply, and the economic crisis since 2020 has further depleted hospital resource availability. FCDO Cuba (2024) recommends comprehensive travel insurance with medical evacuation cover as a non-negotiable requirement for all visitors. For executive-level visitors, the most appropriate medical facilities are the Clínica Central Cira García (+53 7 204 2811) in Miramar, which is Cuba's primary international clinic and serves foreign visitors with a higher standard of resource than public hospitals, and CIREN International Hospital (+53 7 273 8102), which covers specialist neurological and rehabilitative cases. For serious trauma, cardiac events, or conditions requiring intensive care, medical evacuation to Mexico City (approximately 2.5 hours by air) or Miami (approximately 50 minutes -- subject to current US-Cuba flight availability) is the standard pathway. Emergency contacts: 106 (police), 104 (ambulance). British consular interests in Cuba are handled by the Swiss Embassy in Havana (+53 7 204 2611); FCDO London for emergencies: +44 20 7008 5000.
