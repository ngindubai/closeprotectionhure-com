What this covers

Operational detail for Sharm el-Sheikh

FCDO Travel Advice and Sinai Context

FCDO advises against all travel to North Sinai and notes a high terrorism threat across Egypt, including Sharm el-Sheikh, which is situated on the southern tip of the Sinai peninsula. FCDO travel advice for Egypt highlights the threat from Islamic State Sinai Province (ISSP) activity, which is primarily concentrated in North Sinai but has demonstrated the capability to conduct attacks in wider areas of the peninsula and in mainland Egyptian cities. The 2005 hotel bombings in Sharm el-Sheikh, the 2004 Taba attacks, and the 2015 destruction of Metrojet Flight 9268 at Sharm el-Sheikh International Airport are documented historical incidents that have shaped how international governments and security professionals assess the resort's risk profile. Any deployment to Sharm el-Sheikh requires a current threat assessment that distinguishes between the resort zone's security and the broader Sinai context.

Regulatory Framework and Licensing

Private security companies in Egypt must register with the Ministry of Interior (MOI) and comply with Egyptian Security Services Law. The MOI maintains oversight of commercial security operators through its licensing directorate, and all security personnel working commercially must hold valid documentation from the relevant authority. Foreign security personnel cannot operate as commercial close protection officers in Egypt without appropriate Egyptian legal authorisation. Our Egypt network engages only MOI-registered operators whose credentials are verified before deployment. Clients should note that informal security arrangements are common in resort areas of Egypt; these carry no legal standing and should not be relied upon as professional close protection.

Terrorism Threat Assessment: Sharm el-Sheikh

The terrorism threat in Egypt is assessed as high by FCDO, with Islamic State Sinai Province (ISSP) responsible for a sustained insurgency in North Sinai that has included attacks on security forces, infrastructure, and, historically, locations connected to tourism and foreign interests. Sharm el-Sheikh is geographically distant from the main ISSP operational area in North Sinai, located at the southern tip of the peninsula approximately 350 kilometres from the North Sinai border. However, the 2015 Metrojet incident demonstrated ISSP capability in Sharm el-Sheikh itself. The current threat picture should be obtained from FCDO travel advice, the client organisation's own threat intelligence function, and the deployed security team's current local assessment before deployment decisions are finalised.

Resort Zone Operations

Sharm el-Sheikh's tourism and business infrastructure is concentrated in Naama Bay (the main resort and entertainment district), Hadaba (administrative and residential), and the newer Nabq Bay development further north. The resort zone has its own physical security infrastructure including perimeter controls at major hotel compounds, armed police presence at high-profile venues, and tourist police units that are a visible feature of the Naama Bay area. Close protection officers in Sharm el-Sheikh operate within this layered resort security environment, liaise with hotel security teams and tourist police, and supplement the institutional security with personal protection tailored to the specific principal and their programme.

Airport Security and Transfer Protocols

Sharm el-Sheikh International Airport (SSH) is the primary arrival and departure point for the resort. Following the 2015 Metrojet incident, Egyptian authorities, in cooperation with UK and international aviation security advisers, implemented enhanced security measures at SSH including additional screening and access controls. Close protection teams assess the current airport security environment at the time of deployment, coordinate vehicle positioning for arrivals and departures to minimise kerb-side exposure, and incorporate the airport into the overall journey management plan. Clients should follow airport security instructions without exception and be prepared for extended processing times during peak periods or elevated security phases.

Medical and Emergency Support

Sharm el-Sheikh has developed medical facilities compared to a typical Egyptian provincial city, reflecting the resort economy's requirement to support a large international visitor population. Sharm el-Sheikh International Hospital and several private clinics in Naama Bay handle routine medical needs and some trauma cases. For serious medical emergencies requiring specialist care, medical evacuation to Cairo is the primary option for mainland Egypt, and international evacuation to European medical centres is accessible by charter or air ambulance. Egyptian emergency services use 122 for police and 123 for ambulance. Tourist police can also be reached through hotel reception. Close protection teams carry trauma first aid equipment and maintain standing contacts with international medical evacuation providers operating in the Egypt and Red Sea region.

As of June 2026, FCDO advises against all travel to North Sinai and notes a high terrorism threat in Egypt as a whole, including the Sinai peninsula. FCDO travel advice for Egypt does not advise against all travel to Sharm el-Sheikh, which is located at the southern tip of Sinai, but does note that the terrorism risk affects the whole country and specifically references the Sinai context. FCDO advice is updated in response to changes in the security situation, and clients should check the current published FCDO travel advice at the time they are planning travel, as the assessment can change. Any deployment to Sharm el-Sheikh should be preceded by a review of the current FCDO advisory, US State Department Egypt assessment, and an independent threat assessment from the security team.

Several significant terrorist incidents have occurred in and around Sharm el-Sheikh over the past two decades. The 2004 Taba hotel bombing killed approximately 34 people at the Hilton Taba on the Israeli border. The July 2005 Sharm el-Sheikh bombings killed 88 people at the Ghazala Gardens hotel and in the Naama Bay market area. In October 2015, Metrojet Flight 9268, departing Sharm el-Sheikh International Airport (SSH), was destroyed by an improvised explosive device, killing all 224 people aboard; Islamic State Sinai Province claimed responsibility. These incidents are documented in Egyptian judicial proceedings and international aviation investigation reports. They define the security posture that professional close protection in Sharm el-Sheikh must account for.

Sharm el-Sheikh’s major resort hotels operate substantial internal security infrastructure including perimeter controls, armed security personnel, baggage screening at entrances, and armed police presences at some properties. This institutional security provides a baseline protective environment for guests. Close protection adds a layer that is personal and mobile: officers protect a specific principal rather than a venue population, travel with the principal outside the hotel perimeter, provide journey management covering the airport, vehicle movements, and external venues, and conduct advance work on locations the principal intends to visit. For business visitors whose programme includes meetings outside a single hotel property, close protection is the only mechanism that maintains protection continuity across all movements.

Organisations sending personnel to Sharm el-Sheikh under a duty-of-care framework should conduct a formal risk assessment that addresses: the current FCDO travel advisory, the specific nature of the business requiring travel (whether it could be conducted remotely), the individual threat profile of the personnel involved, the security support that will be in place for the duration, and the emergency and evacuation plan if the security situation deteriorates. The Corporate Manslaughter and Corporate Homicide Act 2007 and the general duty-of-care framework under UK employment law require employers to take reasonable steps to protect employees sent to high-risk environments. A critical risk designation means the baseline security provision must be more extensive, not that travel cannot occur; but the decision and the rationale must be clearly documented.

Sharm el-Sheikh requires officers with experience in terrorism-affected environments, not only conventional corporate close protection. Relevant experience includes prior deployments in environments with high terrorism threat, familiarity with counter-terrorism enhanced awareness protocols, understanding of explosive device indicators and avoidance, and the professional judgement to advise mission-abort when the threat level warrants it. Officers should have current awareness of ISSP activity patterns and the standard operating procedures for operating in a heightened-terrorism environment. Standard corporate close protection officers without this background are not appropriate for Sharm el-Sheikh, and we assess each enquiry individually to confirm the experience level available before committing to an engagement.
