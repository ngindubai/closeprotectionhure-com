Operational detail for Beijing

PSB-Licensed Security Companies: The Operating Framework

China does not operate an individual CPO licensing model analogous to the UK's SIA system or Australia's Class 1B. Under the State Council Regulations on the Administration of Security Services (2009), individual security officers are employees of security companies that hold a PSB (Public Security Bureau) operating licence. The PSB licence is granted at the provincial or municipal level, with Beijing-operating companies supervised by the Beijing Municipal Public Security Bureau. Security companies must meet PSB-stipulated staffing, training, and operational standards. For clients requiring close protection in Beijing, the operating company's PSB licence is the primary credential to verify, not an individual officer's personal licence number.

No Private Freelance CPO Market in China

The 2009 State Council regulations explicitly structure the security industry around licensed companies rather than individual practitioners. There is no freelance or independent CPO market in mainland China. An individual who performs security functions commercially without being employed by a PSB-licensed company is operating outside the regulatory framework. For international principals, this means the client relationship is with the licensed security company, not with a named individual officer, and the company's regulatory standing is the material quality indicator. This is a stricter company-led model than even Japan's equivalent framework.

International CPOs in Beijing: The Legal Boundary

International CPOs accompanying principals to Beijing cannot legally operate in a commercial security capacity under the 2009 regulations without being employed by a PSB-licensed Chinese security company. In practice, international CPOs who travel with a principal to Beijing typically operate as personal assistants, executive support staff, or travel coordinators rather than contracted security personnel, keeping their function within the scope of a non-commercial support role. This is a legal boundary with material implications: for corporate and HNWI clients who require formal close protection coverage in Beijing, the correct model is to engage a PSB-licensed Beijing operator for the in-country period, with the international CPO transitioning to a support rather than security function.

Armed Protection in Beijing

Private close protection in Beijing is unarmed. China's regulatory framework does not provide a licensing route for private security companies or their staff to carry firearms commercially for close protection purposes. The People's Armed Police and police forces carry arms; private security does not. For the vast majority of international business principals visiting Beijing, this is not a functional limitation - the threat environment for foreign executives in Beijing is characterised by very low violent crime, with the relevant risks more likely to involve corporate intelligence activity, surveillance, and data security than physical violence.

Threat Profile: What CPO Cover Addresses in Beijing

The relevant threat dimensions for foreign principals requiring close protection support in Beijing differ from those in high-crime cities. Physical security risks from violent crime are low. The operationally significant considerations include: corporate intelligence and surveillance activity targeting senior executives in technology, energy, defence, and financial sectors; the risk of device and communication compromise in hotel environments (use of hotel Wi-Fi, meeting room surveillance); and the regulatory environment, including the 2017 National Intelligence Law's provisions that may affect how local staff interact with foreign principals. A competent CPO brief for Beijing addresses these dimensions alongside conventional threat assessment.

Working With a PSB-Licensed Beijing Operator

For clients requiring close protection support in Beijing, the engagement should be with a PSB-licensed security company operating in the Beijing area. Verification of the PSB licence is conducted through the Beijing Municipal Public Security Bureau. The key questions when evaluating a Beijing operator are: what is the company's PSB licence status and renewal date, what is the demonstrated experience of their staff with international business principal assignments, what language capabilities do their CPO-level staff hold, and what protocols do they follow for the specific counter-surveillance and communications security concerns relevant to Beijing. Companies with demonstrable experience in international executive assignments - including an understanding of the protocol expectations of Western corporate clients - are a smaller sub-set of the wider PSB-licensed market.

A CPO holding a UK SIA licence, US DCJS registration, or any other foreign security credential cannot operate commercially as a security officer in Beijing under the 2009 State Council regulations. Commercial security services in China must be provided by PSB-licensed Chinese companies employing regulated staff. For international principals travelling to Beijing with an existing CPO team, the compliant structure is to engage a PSB-licensed Beijing operator for formal close protection duties, with the international CPO operating in a support or advisory capacity rather than a contracted security role. The specific structure should be agreed with legal advice, as the boundary between a permitted support function and a regulated security function is a matter of Chinese law.

For most foreign executives visiting Beijing on commercial business, violent crime is a very low risk. The operationally significant concerns are more likely to be: corporate intelligence and surveillance activity, particularly for executives in sectors identified as strategic priorities by Chinese state-linked entities; digital security risks in hotel environments and business meetings; and the regulatory environment under laws such as the 2017 National Intelligence Law. A pre-Beijing CPO or security brief should address device security protocols, use of personal devices versus clean travel devices, meeting room sweep procedures, and awareness of surveillance indicators. These considerations do not require high-visibility physical security but do require specialist advisory input.

A PSB (Public Security Bureau) licence is the mandatory operating credential for security companies in China under the 2009 State Council regulations. Operating without a PSB licence is illegal. The licence requires the company to meet staffing, training, and operational standards set by the PSB at the provincial or municipal level. For Beijing-operating companies, this means approval from the Beijing Municipal Public Security Bureau. The PSB licence is the equivalent of the SIA approval for UK operating companies - it is the regulatory baseline that distinguishes a legitimate, accountable security company from an unregulated operator. Clients should request the PSB licence reference for any security company they are considering engaging in Beijing.
