The regulatory framework: working through Nepali firms

All CPO services in Kathmandu are delivered through registered Nepali companies with current District Administration Office licensing. This is not a limitation in practice: the registered Nepali operator pool includes experienced, capable firms with demonstrated track records in corporate and diplomatic protection assignments. The Act’s framework provides accountability and quality standards that benefit clients.

What this covers

Operational detail for Kathmandu

Security Personnel and Private Security Organizations Act 2017

Nepal's Security Personnel and Private Security Organizations Act 2017 provides the regulatory framework for the private security sector. The Act requires companies to register with the District Administration Office and prohibits foreign operators from deploying their own personnel directly: all CPO assignments must be conducted through registered Nepali firms. Armed close protection is tightly controlled under the Act and requires police liaison approval. CPOs on our Kathmandu panel hold current District Administration Office registration under the 2017 Act. Documentation is provided to clients before deployment.

Unarmed CP Standard with Police Liaison

The standard CPO configuration for Kathmandu is unarmed close protection. Nepal's 2017 Act tightly controls armed CP, requiring police liaison authorisation beyond the baseline unarmed framework. For the majority of corporate principals visiting Kathmandu, unarmed CP with police liaison coordination is appropriate to the threat level and consistent with the regulatory framework. Principals with documented elevated risk profiles, or those travelling to Nepal for high-profile events that generate political or media attention, should discuss the police liaison authorisation process with the security planning team.

Bandh Monitoring and Contingency Cover

Nepal's political environment generates bandhs (general strikes) called by parties, trade unions, and community organisations that can shut all transport across Kathmandu Valley at short notice. CPO assignments in Kathmandu include a bandh monitoring protocol active from 72 hours before the principal's arrival, with continuous monitoring throughout the visit. If a bandh is called, the principal moves to a pre-confirmed secure holding location and remains there until the bandh lifts and movement is confirmed as viable. Bandh monitoring is a standard service element, not an additional cost.

Seismic Emergency Protocol

Kathmandu Valley is in one of Asia's most active seismic zones. The 2015 Gorkha earthquake (7.8 magnitude) killed approximately 9,000 people and demonstrated the valley's vulnerability to large seismic events. CPO assignments include a seismic emergency protocol covering: immediate response actions if a significant tremor occurs during a principal movement or in-hotel period; identification of the nearest structurally sound open space relative to the principal's accommodation; and communication protocols with the principal's organisation and the operations controller. The protocol is briefed to the principal before the assignment begins.

Tribhuvan Airport Collection Protocol

Tribhuvan International Airport (KTM) has a documented taxi scam environment where unofficial drivers, false porters, and credential fraudsters target arriving foreign visitors in the terminal building. CPO teams for Kathmandu are positioned inside the arrivals hall with a confirmed vehicle registration and driver contact, meeting the principal before they exit to the public taxi area. Principals are briefed before travel on the KTM arrival environment and instructed not to accept any unsolicited assistance in the terminal.

Lazimpat and Jhamsikhel Operating Zone

CPO assignments in Kathmandu are based in the Lazimpat embassy district, Jhamsikhel, Sanepa, and Bouddha. These areas have the best combination of hotel infrastructure, physical security, and operating environment for corporate principals. The Thamel tourist district is not used as a base for CPO assignments: its petty crime environment, crowded pedestrian access, and vehicle management difficulty make it unsuitable as a principal's accommodation or operating zone. Movement to Thamel for tourism purposes is managed with a specific petty crime awareness briefing and close accompaniment.

Armed close protection in Nepal is tightly controlled under the 2017 Act and requires police liaison approval. It is not the default configuration for standard corporate CPO assignments in Kathmandu. Nepal’s political culture means bandhs are a regular feature of the Kathmandu operating environment. General strikes can be called by political parties, trade unions, community groups, or religious organisations and can shut all transport across the valley at short notice. The frequency varies with the political cycle, but no Kathmandu visit of more than 24 hours should be planned without a bandh contingency. CPO assignments include a 72-hour pre-arrival monitoring window and a continuous bandh watch throughout the visit, with a pre-confirmed holding location for use if a bandh is called.

The 2015 Gorkha earthquake demonstrated the valley’s seismic vulnerability and left a building stock of uneven structural quality across Kathmandu. CPO planning for Kathmandu includes a seismic emergency protocol covering immediate response to a significant tremor, pre-identified open areas near the principal’s accommodation, and communication protocols with the principal’s organisation. Accommodation is recommended in modern reinforced concrete hotels in Lazimpat, Jhamsikhel, or Sanepa rather than older masonry structures in the historic core.

Kathmandu’s risk level is elevated rather than critical. The primary security planning variables are political bandhs, seismic risk, and petty crime in tourist areas; the terrorism threat, while present in Nepal’s broader security picture, is not the dominant operational variable for standard corporate CPO assignments in Kathmandu Valley. The security planning requirement is genuine and should not be underestimated, but the threat environment is materially different from that in Karachi, Kabul, or Caracas. The appropriate security configuration for most corporate principals is unarmed CP, bandh contingency, and a seismic emergency protocol.
