Operational detail for Berlin

SS34a Gewerbeordnung: The Individual Legal Requirement

Individual security officers operating commercially in Germany, including those performing close protection functions, are regulated under Paragraph 34a of the Gewerbeordnung (Trade Regulation Act). Under this framework, individuals providing security services must complete a Sachkundeprufung (expertise examination) administered by the Industrie- und Handelskammer (IHK, the Chamber of Commerce and Industry). The examination covers legal frameworks, powers of security personnel, first aid, handling of special situations, and data protection. The Sachkundeprufung is the baseline individual qualification for professional security work in Germany. Individual security officers must also be registered with the Ordnungsamt (public order office) in their operating area.

Ordnungsamt Registration: Individual Compliance

In addition to the Sachkundeprufung, individual security officers in Germany must be registered with the Ordnungsamt of the relevant municipal authority. In Berlin, this is the Ordnungsamt of the Berlin Senate Administration. The registration involves a background check and confirmation that the officer holds the relevant qualifications. Clients verifying a Berlin-based CPO should request both the IHK Sachkundeprufung certificate and confirmation of current Ordnungsamt registration. An individual holding the Sachkundeprufung but not registered with the Ordnungsamt is not fully compliant. The registration requirement is an ongoing obligation, not a one-time credential.

40-Hour Refresher Training: The Ongoing Requirement

Under the legal framework governing security personnel in Germany, individual security officers are required to complete a 40-hour refresher training programme every three years. This continuing professional development requirement maintains the officer's knowledge of updated legal frameworks, threat environments, and security techniques. For clients evaluating a Berlin-based CPO operator, the refresher training records of individual officers are a legitimate verification point. An officer who completed their Sachkundeprufung five years ago but cannot demonstrate the required refresher training is not in full regulatory compliance with the ongoing maintenance obligation.

CPO vs Bodyguard: The German Terminology

German usage does not employ the anglophone close protection officer (CPO) designation as a distinct regulatory category. The operative term is Bewachungsgewerbe (security trade) and individual officers are Sicherheitsmitarbeiter (security staff) or Personenschutz (personal protection) specialists. The Sachkundeprufung is the entry-level legal minimum; dedicated Personenschutz training programmes, which are not separately regulated but are offered by specialist training providers, provide the additional skills expected of a close protection professional: threat assessment, advance work, defensive driving, and emergency response. When evaluating a Berlin operator, confirm whether their proposed CPO-level staff have completed Personenschutz-specific training beyond the Sachkundeprufung baseline.

Armed Protection in Berlin

Armed private close protection in Germany is legally possible but subject to strict conditions. Under German weapons law (Waffengesetz), a private security officer carrying a firearm commercially requires a specific authorisation tied to a demonstrable need. In practice, armed CPO deployments in Berlin are rare and are reserved for situations where a documented threat assessment supports an application for weapons authorisation. The overwhelming majority of close protection in Berlin - including for high-profile corporate and HNWI clients - is unarmed. German CPO culture places strong emphasis on threat avoidance, advance work, and conflict de-escalation rather than the armed-deterrent model more common in some other markets.

Berlin Operational Profile: Government, Corporate, and Cultural

Berlin's operational environment for close protection combines a concentration of federal government institutions (Bundestag, Bundesrat, Federal Ministries in Mitte) with a significant corporate and diplomatic community, major tech and startup sector in Prenzlauer Berg and Mitte, and a high-profile cultural scene. CPO engagements in Berlin typically involve government-adjacent principals on business in Mitte and Tiergarten, corporate executives in the City West (Kurfurstendamm corridor) and Potsdamer Platz zones, and visiting HNWI clients attending cultural or art-market events. The advance for a Berlin engagement covers the principal's hotel in Mitte or City West, primary meeting venues, and government-zone access procedures where the itinerary involves Bundestag or ministerial buildings.

The Sachkundeprufung under Paragraph 34a Gewerbeordnung is the legally required baseline examination for any individual providing commercial security services in Germany. It covers the legal, first-aid, and situational foundations of security work. Personenschutz (personal protection) training is a specialist vocational programme offered by private training providers - it is not a separate legal licence category but provides the advanced skills required for close protection work: threat and risk assessment, advance work, principal handling, and emergency response. A professional Berlin CPO should hold both: the Sachkundeprufung as the legal minimum, and recognised Personenschutz training as the specialist qualification.

Berlin’s ambient threat environment is generally low for corporate and HNWI principals in terms of violent crime or targeted criminality. Berlin’s position as the German capital means high-profile political and diplomatic events create periodic elevated security environments that a competent CPO brief addresses. For most corporate executive assignments, Berlin presents a manageable, low-threat operational environment.
