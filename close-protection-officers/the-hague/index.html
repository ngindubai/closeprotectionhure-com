Operational detail for The Hague

Regulatory Framework and Licensing

Close protection in The Hague is governed by the Wet particuliere beveiligingsorganisaties en recherchebureaus (WPBR, 1997, as amended). All firms must register with Justis (Ministry of Justice and Security screening bureau); individual operatives require Toestemming screening. Armed protection requires Politie authorisation and is rarely granted commercially; diplomatic protection where warranted may involve Korps Nationale Politie armed officers under Dutch diplomatic protection protocols. EU-licensed officers may operate subject to WPBR registration; non-EU nationals must work through a Justis-registered Dutch firm. A critical distinction from other Dutch cities: firms providing close protection services near international organisations - the International Criminal Court (ICC), International Court of Justice (ICJ), OPCW, Europol, or Eurojust - may be subject to additional background vetting by those organisations' own security departments before access is permitted. Coordinate all assignments near international organisation buildings with the client institution's security directorate well in advance of deployment.

Threat Environment in The Hague

FCDO Netherlands 2024 rates The Hague as low risk in general terms, but the city presents specific targeted risks absent in other Dutch cities. Petty crime occurs in the Centraal Station area and at Scheveningen seafront during summer. Protest activity near the Peace Palace, ICC buildings, and Europol is documented - primarily anti-ICC protests by national delegations and protests connected to specific cases including Netherlands-Indonesia historical litigation and Russia-Ukraine ICJ proceedings (Dutch media 2022-2024). The most significant contextual risk for The Hague is foreign intelligence activity: the AIVD annual report 2023 explicitly documents foreign intelligence services operating in the Netherlands with a focus on international organisations and the diplomatic quarter. Certain principals connected to ICC or ICJ proceedings (state actors, parties to cases, witnesses in high-profile matters) may face specific personal risk not reflected in the city's general crime statistics.

Principal Hotel and Business Zone Coverage

Hotel des Indes (Lange Voorhout 54) is The Hague's premier 5-star property, positioned adjacent to the embassy row on Lange Voorhout; it is the reference property for state, legal, and international organisation delegations and requires professional lobby and entrance management given the guest profile. Kurhaus Hotel Scheveningen (Gevers Deynootplein 30) is suitable for leisure principals in the coastal resort environment and provides a lower-profile alternative to the city centre. Mercure Hotel Den Haag Central provides a business-standard city centre option. The primary business zones are Bezuidenhout (Haagse Arc, near Centraal Station), Benoordenhout (residential embassy district), and the international organisation campus on Johan de Wittlaan, which concentrates the ICC, Europol, and Eurojust buildings. Unlike Rotterdam or Amsterdam, The Hague functions as a government, diplomatic, and international legal centre; most corporate meetings involve government agencies, law firms, or international institutions.

Airport and Transit Security

Amsterdam Schiphol Airport (AMS) is approximately 50km northwest with a normal transit time of around 40 minutes via the A4 and N14 under normal conditions, and is the primary gateway for intercontinental delegates. Rotterdam The Hague Airport (RTM) is approximately 15km south with a transit time of around 20 minutes, handling regional EU routes. Private aviation typically uses RTM, where advance ground handling coordination is required. A direct intercity rail service runs from Schiphol to Den Haag Centraal in approximately 45 minutes; principals with counter-surveillance requirements should not use public transport, but the regular public rail movement affects route planning for accompanying ground transport vehicles during parallel transit.

Operational Considerations

Dutch is the formal working language; English fluency is very high across the international community in The Hague. Mobile coverage on KPN, Vodafone, and T-Mobile 4G networks is solid throughout the city. Counter-surveillance route planning in The Hague requires more thorough advance work than in Rotterdam: the older street grid around the international organisation quarter has narrower streets and more complex one-way patterns. Anti-surveillance awareness is particularly relevant given the AIVD-documented intelligence activity in the city (2023). The large number of diplomatic vehicles with immunity plates in the diplomatic quarter creates specific protocol considerations for any incident involving diplomatic quarter movements; teams should be briefed on immunity vehicle identification and escalation procedures. All assignments near ICC, ICJ, OPCW, Europol, or Eurojust facilities require advance coordination with those institutions' security departments.

Emergency Response and Medical Support

The primary city emergency department is HMC Westeinde (Lijnbaan 32, telephone +31 88 979 7900). The Hague does not have a level-1 trauma centre within the city limits; serious trauma cases are directed to LUMC Leiden University Medical Centre (Albinusdreef 2, Leiden, +31 71 526 9111, approximately 25km north) or Erasmus MC Rotterdam (approximately 30km south). Emergency numbers: unified emergency 112, police non-emergency 0900 8844. Consular contacts: British Embassy The Hague +31 70 427 0427; US Embassy The Hague +31 70 310 2209. Both the British and US embassies are based in The Hague, making consular access more direct than in most Dutch cities.
