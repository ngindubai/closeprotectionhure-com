CNAPS Carte Professionnelle: The Legal Requirement

Every individual close protection officer operating commercially in France must hold a carte professionnelle issued by the Conseil National des Activites Privees de Securite (CNAPS). The requirement derives from Loi n°83-629 of 12 July 1983, as substantially amended by the LOPPSI 2 reforms. CNAPS issues the card following a background check conducted in coordination with the French Interior Ministry, review of professional qualifications, and confirmation that no disqualifying criminal or professional history exists. The card specifies the holder's authorised activity domains, including surveillance humaine et gardiennage or, for CPO-specific work, protection rapprochee (personal protection).

French Law and the CPO Function

French security law draws a clear line between the protection rapprochee function and general surveillance or security-guarding. A CPO in France is not simply a garde du corps in the colloquial sense - the role requires a CNAPS card authorising personal protection activity, distinct from a card authorising site guarding or event security. This distinction matters when selecting a French operator: confirm that the individual CPO's CNAPS card explicitly covers protection rapprochee, not merely a general security category.

No UK SIA Reciprocity in France

There is no mutual recognition or reciprocity arrangement between the UK's SIA Close Protection licence and the French CNAPS carte professionnelle. A British CPO holding a current SIA licence cannot legally provide commercial close protection services in France on the strength of that licence alone. For UK-based principals visiting Paris, continuity of protection requires either a CNAPS-authorised French operator or a cross-border arrangement where the UK CPO accompanies as a personal assistant rather than in a commercial security capacity. We can advise on compliant cross-border structures for international principals.

Operational Profile: Discretion in Paris

Paris CPO engagements for corporate and HNWI clients operate at a consistently discreet profile. The cultural environment of business Paris - meetings at Eighth Arrondissement offices, dinners at high-profile restaurants in Saint-Germain or the First, hotel stays at property on Rue de Rivoli or Avenue Montaigne - requires close protection that is indistinguishable from a senior executive's business travel companion. French CPOs working at this level combine operational competence with the social fluency to operate across these environments without signalling the security function.

Threat Context for Paris Engagements

The UK Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office and the French Direction Generale de la Securite Interieure (DGSI) both identify France as facing an elevated and persistent terrorism threat, sustained by the SGDSN Vigipirate system at niveau urgence attentat (the highest level) across national events and major venues. Separately, protest action and civil disorder events in Paris can affect route planning and venue access. A current CPO brief for Paris includes Vigipirate status, any active protest notifications from the Prefecture de Police de Paris, and route alternatives through the central arrondissements.

Advance Work in Paris: Hotels and Venues

Paris CPO pre-advance covers the principal's hotel (checking in under a neutral name where privacy is required), primary meeting venues in the Eighth, Sixteenth, and business districts, and restaurant-level access and egress. The CPO confirms the nearest relevant emergency services, including the Brigade de Sapeurs-Pompiers de Paris response times for the principal's operational zones. For HNWI clients with extended Paris stays, the pre-advance also covers residential property security and any chauffeur-route coordination with the principal's driver.
