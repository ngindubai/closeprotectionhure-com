Licensing: the GPG standard

The Guardia Particolare Giurata (GPG) qualification is Italy’s regulated standard for close protection officers. Both the individual GPG authorisation and the operating company’s Prefettura licence must be current and verifiable. The Prefettura di Milano issues licences for the Province of Milan, and its register provides the authoritative verification source.

Operational detail for Milan

Italian GPG Licensing: TULPS and DM 269/2010

Private security in Italy is regulated under Article 134 of the Testo Unico delle Leggi di Pubblica Sicurezza (TULPS, R.D. 773/1931) and the implementing Decreto Ministeriale 269/2010 (DM 269/2010). Operating companies (Istituti di Vigilanza) must hold a licence issued by the Prefettura (Prefecture) of the province in which they operate. Individual close protection officers must be qualified as Guardie Particolari Giurate (GPG) through an examination conducted by the Prefettura and hold an individual authorisation certificate. GPG status carries specific legal powers and obligations under Italian law, including an oath of office. The Milan Prefettura issues licences for operations in the Province of Milan. Clients should confirm that both the operating company and the individual GPG officer hold current Prefettura authorisations before committing to an engagement.

Milan's Threat Environment: Low Ambient Violence, HNWI Targeting

Milan has a low violent crime rate by European standards. The FCDO Italy travel advice (2024) notes pickpocketing and bag-snatching in tourist-heavy areas including the Duomo, Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II, and Stazione Centrale, and on crowded public transport. For CPO purposes, the primary threat to HNWI and corporate principals in Milan is not street violence but a more sophisticated profile: surveillance by organised criminal groups, financial fraud targeting high-net-worth individuals, social engineering, and vehicle-based theft of valuables. The design, luxury goods, and finance industries that drive Milan's economy attract a concentration of high-value targets, and the threat picture reflects this. Petty crime in tourist zones is a nuisance; the relevant threat for a protected principal operates at a different level.

Fashion, Luxury, and Finance Industry Context

Milan's economy is dominated by three sectors with high CPO relevance: fashion and luxury goods (with the quadrilateral della moda centred on Via Montenapoleone, Via della Spiga, Via Manzoni, and Corso Venezio); financial services (Piazza Affari, the Milan Stock Exchange, and the Porta Nuova business district); and industrial and manufacturing holding companies. CPO clients in Milan are typically HNWI principals associated with fashion houses, luxury brands, private equity, or family offices with Italian industrial holdings. The operational profile is consistently low visibility: business attire, discreet close work at hotel lobbies and restaurant-level venues, and anti-surveillance awareness appropriate to the principal's profile.

Porta Nuova, Brera, and City Centre Coverage

The principal operational zones for Milan CPO engagements are: Porta Nuova (the modern financial and residential district around Unicredit Tower and Piazza Gae Aulenti); the quadrilateral della moda (the fashion retail and brand office zone in the 1st and 2nd arrondissements); and the historic centre around Duomo and Scala. For residential HNWI principals, cover extends to the Brera, Magenta, and Navigli districts. Hotel pre-advances cover the Four Seasons Milan (Via Gesù), Armani Hotel Milan, Park Hyatt Milan, and Bulgari Hotel Milan as the principal HNWI accommodation venues. Conference and trade show CPO cover for Fiera Milano (Rho) is a recurring engagement type during Fashion Week and the Salone del Mobile.

Malpensa and Linate Airport Transfer Operations

Milan is served by two main commercial airports: Malpensa International Airport (MXP), 49 kilometres northwest of the city in the Varese province, handling most long-haul and international traffic; and Linate Airport (LIN), 7 kilometres east of the city centre, handling primarily domestic and short-haul European services. Malpensa to central Milan transfers take 50 to 80 minutes by road via the A8/A9 Autostrade. Linate transfers run 20 to 35 minutes. Pre-arranged collection by a vetted driver operating under the CPO team's oversight is standard for both airports. Bergamo Orio al Serio (BGY) serves budget carriers and is approximately 55 kilometres east; it is less commonly used for principal transfers but must be planned for when relevant.

Counter-Surveillance and Discreet Profile

Milan CPO operations are almost exclusively low-visibility. The environment, both socially and commercially, does not accommodate visible close protection in most contexts. HNWI and corporate principals in Milan require CPOs who can blend into the city's fashion, business, and hospitality environments without triggering any reaction from venue staff, other guests, or bystanders. Counter-surveillance capability is more operationally relevant in Milan than physical intervention: identifying surveillance behaviour near the principal's hotel, at regular routes between hotel and meeting venues, and at repeated venue choices is the primary protective discipline. CPO selection for Milan engagements weights discreet-profile competence, Italian language capability, and familiarity with the city's operational environment.
