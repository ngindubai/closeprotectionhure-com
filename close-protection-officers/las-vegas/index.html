Operational detail for Las Vegas

Regulatory Framework and Licensing

Close protection and private security in Nevada is regulated by the Nevada Private Investigators Licensing Board (PILB). Security companies operating as guard or protection providers must hold a Private Patrol Operator (PPO) licence, while individual officers must carry a valid Guard Card issued by PILB after completing the required training hours and background check. For close protection officers carrying firearms, a Firearm Permit is required in addition to the Guard Card, with separate live-fire qualification standards. Las Vegas also operates an LVMPD (Las Vegas Metropolitan Police Department) off-duty officer programme that allows sworn police officers to provide supplemental venue and event security on a contracted basis -- this is a common arrangement for large convention security packages and major entertainment venue deployments. Clients should request sight of PPO licence, individual Guard Cards, and Firearm Permits where applicable before deployment.

Large-Venue and Crowd Risk

The 1 October 2017 Route 91 Harvest shooting -- in which 60 people were killed and over 400 injured by a gunman firing from the Mandalay Bay hotel onto an outdoor concert crowd on Las Vegas Boulevard South -- remains the defining event in Las Vegas's venue security posture. LVMPD and Clark County substantially revised crowd safety, venue ingress protocols, elevated-position monitoring, and inter-agency response procedures in the aftermath. All major Strip hotels, convention facilities, and entertainment venues now operate substantially upgraded security infrastructure including enhanced screening, real-time communications with LVMPD, and dedicated security operations centres for large events. For principals attending major conventions such as CES (January, 170,000+ attendees, Consumer Technology Association), NAB Show (April), AWS re:Invent (November-December), or SEMA, the crowd density during peak attendance periods is an operational planning variable in its own right. CPO details managing convention principals must plan entry and exit timing to avoid peak ingress crowds, identify venue-specific emergency exits, and maintain continuous communication with principal and advance team throughout.

Business District and Convention Coverage

Las Vegas Convention Center (LVCC) covers approximately 6.9 million square feet, making it one of the largest convention complexes in the world, with multiple halls, concourses, and a connected underground transport loop. Convention principals requiring close protection need advance work at the specific hall and session locations for each day, as the scale of the LVCC means that public areas within it can be extremely dense and disorienting during peak attendance. The Strip hotel corridor -- running from Mandalay Bay in the south to the Wynn/Encore resort group at the north end -- concentrates corporate hospitality, bilateral meetings, and private entertainment alongside very high public footfall. Summerlin, to the northwest of the city, is the primary corporate residential and office zone for senior Las Vegas-based executives and operates at a materially different risk profile from the Strip. Downtown (Fremont Street area) has higher crime rates and is less commonly used as a principal base but may be relevant for site visits or specific sector meetings.

Street Crime and Environmental Risk

LVMPD crime data records above-average robbery and larceny rates in the visitor corridors of Las Vegas Boulevard South compared to US national averages. The 3-kilometre stretch between Flamingo Road and Tropicana Avenue is where the majority of visitor-affecting street crime is concentrated, including phone and wallet theft, distraction-based robbery, and occasional violent robbery in the less-illuminated approaches between major hotel properties. Drug activity involving fentanyl-adulterated narcotics is documented in the Fremont Street area and parts of the east Strip (Clark County public health data). Environmental risk during summer is significant: temperatures exceed 40 degrees Celsius from June through August, and outdoor exposure during principal movement must be minimised during peak heat hours (1100-1700). Close protection details manage environmental risk through vehicle-to-venue transfers that eliminate unnecessary outdoor exposure, booking vehicles with consistent air conditioning, and planning any outdoor movements for early morning or evening.

Airport and Transit Security

Harry Reid International Airport (LAS) is located approximately 8 kilometres south of the middle Strip, typically a 10-to-20-minute transfer depending on traffic. The airport is one of the busiest in the United States during major convention periods, when tens of thousands of delegates arrive and depart within compressed windows. For CES in January, NAB in April, and AWS re:Invent in November, transfer times from airport to Strip hotel can extend significantly during peak arrival days. CPO meet-and-greet at LAS involves staging in the arrivals hall, managing baggage and porter coordination, and a controlled kerb transfer to a pre-positioned vehicle. Private and charter arrivals use the Atlantic Aviation or Signature Flight Support FBOs on the west side of the airport, which offer considerably more controlled and discreet arrival environments. For principals travelling at a schedule that coincides with a major convention peak, advance vehicle booking and staggered arrival timing are strongly recommended.

Emergency Response and Medical Support

University Medical Center (UMC, +1 702 383 2000) is the Clark County trauma centre and the primary hospital for serious injuries and medical emergencies. Sunrise Hospital and Medical Center (+1 702 731 8000) is the nearest major facility to the north Strip. Las Vegas has a well-resourced emergency medical system (EMS) with fast response times across the Strip corridor, in part reflecting the post-October 2017 investment in emergency response capacity. For chest pain and cardiac events -- a statistically elevated concern in a city that involves significant alcohol consumption, disrupted sleep, and physical overexertion among convention delegates -- all major Strip hotels maintain AED equipment and trained first responders. Emergency contact: 911. CPO teams are expected to hold current CPR and AED training, and ideally Emergency First Response (EFR) certification, as standard for Las Vegas deployments.
