The scale of pilgrimage activity in Medina is genuinely extraordinary during peak periods. Hajj 2023 attracted approximately 1.8 million pilgrims to Makkah alone, with many more visiting Medina as part of the broader religious journey; Umrah throughout the year brings additional millions. The city’s infrastructure, including its roads, hotels, and emergency services, is designed to accommodate this volume, which is a reassurance in terms of capacity. Operational detail for Medina

Regulatory Framework and Licensing

The private security framework in Medina follows the national Saudi Arabian structure: the General Directorate of Public Security and the National Security Council framework govern all licensed security activity, and Council of Ministers Resolution No. 58 (2020 revision) sets out the licensing requirements for commercial security companies. All security companies must hold Ministry of Interior licences and individual officers must hold personal authorisations. Commercial close protection in Medina, as elsewhere in Saudi Arabia, is generally conducted unarmed; armed security remains state-controlled. Foreign firms must operate through a Saudi-licensed entity with appropriate sponsorship arrangements for foreign personnel. One constraint that is specific to Medina and does not arise elsewhere in Saudi Arabia is the Haram boundary restriction: non-Muslim security personnel are not permitted within the Haram area, the restricted zone surrounding Al-Masjid an-Nabawi (the Prophet's Mosque). This restriction is enforced at checkpoints and is applied consistently by Saudi authorities. Any protection brief that requires a CPO to accompany the principal into the Haram must be staffed with Muslim CPOs only; non-Muslim CPOs must remain at the Haram boundary and hand off protection to Muslim colleagues or to trusted mosque security personnel for the duration of the principal's visit to the holy site. This access restriction must be addressed explicitly in the operational plan before any Medina assignment begins.

Threat Environment in Medina

The FCDO Saudi Arabia travel advisory (2024) includes an elevated terrorism warning applicable across Saudi Arabia, and Medina is included within that assessment. Saudi Arabia's security services maintain a very active counter-terrorism posture in the holy cities, given the extreme sensitivity of any security incident near Al-Masjid an-Nabawi. Historical incidents targeting the Madinah area, including a 2016 attack near the Prophet's Mosque perimeter, underscore that the threat is real and that Saudi authorities treat security here as a national priority. Non-Muslim visitors to Medina are not permitted within the Haram area and therefore do not face the crowd density risk of the mosque interior, but the broader commercial and hotel district surrounding the Haram experiences very high population density during Ramadan, Hajj, and Umrah seasons, when millions of pilgrims transit the city. Saudi Arabia's strict Islamic social law applies with particularly close enforcement in Medina, reflecting its status as one of Islam's holiest cities; dress requirements, gender interaction norms, and behavioural standards are applied more rigorously here than in Riyadh or Jeddah. Road conditions in the inner city during peak pilgrimage seasons are extremely congested, and emergency vehicle access can be delayed significantly by crowd volumes.

Principal Hotel and Business Zone Coverage

The commercial and hotel district of Medina is located outside the Haram boundary, and this is the zone accessible to non-Muslim visitors for accommodation and business activity. The Haramain High Speed Railway (HHR), connecting Medina to Jeddah and Makkah since 2018, has significantly improved logistics connectivity to the city; the Medina HHR station is located west of the city centre, providing a practical alternative to road transfer for inter-city movements. Prince Mohammad bin Abdulaziz Airport provides international air access. Hotel properties near the outer Haram perimeter include the Anwar Al Madinah Mövenpick Hotel, Sheraton Medina Hotel, Oberoi Medina, and Hilton Madinah. Non-Muslim visitors, including non-Muslim CPOs, will be accommodated at these outer-perimeter hotels, and all business and meeting activity for non-Muslim principals will take place in the commercial district outside the Haram. The Medina Knowledge Economic City (MKEC), a major development project under the Vision 2030 framework, is growing as a commercial zone and technology business destination. CPOs should be aware that the scale of the hotel cluster adjacent to the Haram means that the approach roads experience extreme vehicle congestion during pilgrimage peaks.

Airport and Transit Security

Prince Mohammad bin Abdulaziz Airport (IATA: MED) is located approximately 14 kilometres south of the city centre, with a normal transfer time of approximately 20 minutes in non-peak conditions. During Hajj and Umrah seasons, transfer times can increase very significantly as the airport handles pilgrimage traffic well above its standard throughput. The airport has been expanded and upgraded in recent years to accommodate the volume of religious visitors, but arrivals hall congestion remains a planning factor during peak periods. International flights serve the airport from across the Middle East, South Asia, Southeast Asia, and a growing number of European destinations (including UK direct services). The Haramain High Speed Railway provides an alternative arrival route: passengers arriving by train from Jeddah King Abdulaziz International Airport can reach Medina HHR station in approximately 2 hours at speeds up to 300km/h, which may be more practical than a flight to MED during peak periods when MED is capacity-constrained. Vehicle positioning at MED follows standard Saudi airport protocols; the arrivals lay-by is the appropriate collection point, with private aviation handling at a separate general aviation area.

Operational Considerations

The defining operational consideration unique to Medina is the Haram boundary restriction for non-Muslim security personnel. This requires explicit planning at the operational design stage, not as an afterthought. Where the principal is Muslim and intends to visit Al-Masjid an-Nabawi, the CPO team must include sufficient Muslim officers to provide coverage inside the Haram, with non-Muslim team members positioned at the nearest accessible perimeter point and communication protocols established between the teams. Where the principal is non-Muslim, the Haram restriction does not affect the assignment (as the principal similarly cannot enter the Haram), but CPOs must be aware that their principal may have business interactions with Muslim counterparts who will be entering the Haram at prayer times, creating temporary pauses in meetings. The broader Saudi social and religious framework applies in full: dress standards, gender interaction norms, and the prayer-time schedule all affect operational tempo. Ramadan and Hajj/Umrah seasons generate extreme crowd density across the hotel and commercial zone outside the Haram and significantly affect vehicle movement, emergency access, and the predictability of the principal's schedule. Advanced liaison with the Saudi partner firm on pilgrimage season dates and their operational implications is essential. Mobile communications are good across all networks in the city; the MKEC area is served by developing 5G infrastructure.

Emergency Response and Medical Support

Medical facilities in Medina are well-developed by Saudi standards, reflecting the city's obligation to serve millions of pilgrims annually. King Salman Hospital in Medina (+966 14 824 0000, confirm current number on assignment) is the primary public hospital and is equipped to handle high volumes of emergency cases during pilgrimage seasons. The Security Forces Hospital in Medina provides an additional facility. Private medical clinics in the commercial district provide non-emergency care and English-speaking staff. Emergency services are reached on 999 (integrated dispatch), 997 for ambulance, and 998 for civil defence. The Saudi health system in Medina is geared to high-volume pilgrimage emergencies; standard trauma management is available. For complex cases requiring specialist intervention not available locally, transfer to Riyadh or Jeddah is the standard protocol. The British Embassy in Riyadh (+966 11 488 0077) provides consular services for British nationals in Medina; there is no British representation in the city. The US Embassy in Riyadh (+966 11 488 3800) provides consular services for US nationals. Consular staff should be notified of any significant security incident involving a national at the earliest practical opportunity.
