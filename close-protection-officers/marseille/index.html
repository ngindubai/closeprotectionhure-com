The risk environment is more complex than Marseille’s standing as a major western European city might suggest. French Interior Ministry statistics identify Marseille as the city with the highest concentration of organised crime in France, concentrated in the northern arrondissements where drug trafficking networks operate as de facto territorial authorities. Gang-related violence, while primarily intra-criminal, has produced bystander casualties documented in French media and police reporting. Simultaneously, France’s sustained Vigipirate: Urgence attentat terrorism alert level – maintained continuously since January 2015 and renewed repeatedly (FCDO France, 2024) – places Marseille in the same risk category as Paris and Lyon for jihadist attack planning. These two risk vectors operate largely independently of each other but require separate CPO responses: neighbourhood-awareness route planning for organised crime, and public-space immediate-action protocols for terrorism. Neither risk is unmanageable with a properly designed programme, but both require active engagement rather than background awareness.

The operational picture for visiting executives is in practice manageable. The La Joliette Euroméditerranée zone is the appropriate base for most business activity, offering a modern corporate environment with controlled access to the port facilities that most visiting executives need. The Vieux-Port hospitality area is the standard venue for client entertainment and works well with a plainclothes CPO approach during daytime and early evening. The primary exposure moments are transfers from Aéroport Marseille Provence (MRS), where the motorway approach to the city from the northwest requires deliberate routing to avoid the highest-risk northern city zones, and any evening movement that extends into the late night in less-assessed parts of the city. Operational detail for Marseille

Regulatory Framework and Licensing

Private security in France is regulated by the Conseil National des Activités Privées de Sécurité (CNAPS), established by Loi 2012-304. All close protection officers must hold a valid CNAPS Professional Card, which requires a clean criminal record, a completed training programme approved by CNAPS, and periodic renewal. Security companies operating in Marseille must hold CNAPS company authorisation from the CNAPS regional directorate covering the Provence-Alpes-Côte d'Azur region. Armed protection -- where applicable -- requires a separate Agence de Direction de la Securite (ADS) authorisation, which is subject to substantially stricter criteria and applies primarily to principals at documented elevated risk. The CNAPS framework is well-administered and the Professional Card is straightforward to verify: clients should request the card number and confirm it against the CNAPS public register before deployment begins. The regulation covers both uniformed and plainclothes officers, and CNAPS authorisation is required regardless of whether the detail is operating in a visible or discreet capacity.

Organised Crime and Neighbourhood Risk

French Interior Ministry statistics consistently identify Marseille as having the highest concentration of organised crime activity in France. The northern arrondissements -- the 13e through 16e, north of the Boulevard Michelet -- are operating territory for drug trafficking networks, and these zones record gang-related violence including shootings at a rate substantially higher than any other major French city. While this violence is primarily intra-criminal and targeted rather than indiscriminate, bystander casualties are documented, and the proximity of some port and industrial logistics facilities to these areas means that supply-chain and maritime executives cannot treat the northern arrondissements as simply off-limits without consequence to their operational programme. For executives whose meetings are in or adjacent to these zones, CPO route planning must account for neighbourhood boundaries, vehicle profiles that avoid conspicuousness, and immediate departure protocols. The primary business zones for visiting executives -- the Euroméditerranée La Joliette district and the Vieux-Port -- are substantially removed from the highest-risk arrondissements but are not entirely insulated from the city's wider crime environment.

Business District Coverage

The Euroméditerranée zone at La Joliette, on the northern waterfront, is Marseille's primary corporate district and the base for most senior visiting executive activity. The Arenc tower cluster, Les Docks Villages commercial complex, and the offices of maritime sector companies serving the Grand Port Maritime de Marseille are concentrated in this regenerated waterfront zone. The Vieux-Port area, a few minutes south along the waterfront, is the principal hospitality and client entertainment zone, with a range of restaurants and corporate dining venues overlooking the old harbour. Fos-sur-Mer, approximately 50 kilometres west along the A55, is a separate operational environment for executives visiting the petrochemical complex: Elengy's LNG terminal, Naphtachimie, and Kem One operations draw supply chain, engineering, and commercial executives whose visits are centred on the Fos industrial zone rather than Marseille city itself. CPO support for Fos visits requires separate advance work on the industrial zone access protocols and a distinct route plan from the city-based programme.

Terrorism Awareness and Vigipirate Protocol

France has maintained its highest sustained terrorism alert level -- Plan Vigipirate: Urgence attentat -- since January 2015, renewed repeatedly in response to the sustained jihadist threat environment in France (FCDO France, 2024). Marseille operates under the same Urgence attentat posture as Paris and Lyon. This means that all high-footfall public spaces -- transport hubs, markets, major tourist areas including the Vieux-Port and the MuCEM museum complex -- carry heightened attack risk that is not reducible to zero by any security arrangement. CPO protocols for terrorism awareness in Marseille follow the same framework applicable across France: avoiding predictable routine movements through high-footfall areas, maintaining awareness of crowd-density changes that may indicate a developing incident, and having an immediate-action plan for each transit through a public space that includes a rally point and primary exit route. Saint-Charles railway station is a particular awareness point given its volume and its role as the principal's likely arrival point for train-based access from Paris or Lyon.

Airport and Transit Security

Aéroport Marseille Provence (MRS) is located approximately 25 kilometres northwest of the city centre, near Marignane. Transfer time to the La Joliette business district or Vieux-Port is typically 25 to 40 minutes, depending on traffic on the A55 and A7 motorway approaches. The airport has a VIP terminal for private and charter arrivals, which offers a more controlled arrival environment and reduced public exposure compared to the main terminal. CPO meet-and-greet at MRS involves staging at the arrivals hall or, for private arrivals, at the FBO apron, with controlled vehicle transfer to a pre-positioned secure vehicle. The motorway approach to Marseille from MRS passes through the northern city outskirts, and route selection must account for the avoidance of the highest-risk northern arrondissements. The TGV connection from Paris Saint-Charles to Marseille Saint-Charles station (approximately 3 hours) is a common alternative to flying for principals travelling from Paris, and Saint-Charles station arrivals require a street-level vehicle transfer that involves a brief exposure period on one of Marseille's busier public squares.

Emergency Response and Medical Support

Marseille has two major public hospitals relevant to serious medical emergencies. Hôpital La Timone (+33 4 91 38 00 00) is the primary university hospital and trauma centre for Marseille and the Provence region, located in the 5e arrondissement. Hôpital Nord (+33 4 91 96 55 55) provides additional capacity in the northern city. Both operate to full French public health standards, which are excellent by European comparison. The SAMU (Service d'Aide Médicale Urgente, 15) is the medical emergency dispatch service and coordinates ambulance response; response times in the La Joliette and Vieux-Port areas are generally within 10 minutes. Emergency contacts: 15 (SAMU/medical), 17 (police), 18 (fire brigade), 112 (pan-European). The British Consulate in Marseille (+33 4 91 15 72 10) provides consular services for UK nationals and should be notified on arrival for high-profile principals. Medical evacuation to Paris or London is available for specialist conditions not treatable in Marseille, though the city's hospital infrastructure makes this an unusual requirement.

CNAPS card numbers can be checked against the CNAPS online public register (cnaps.fr). Armed protection additionally requires ADS authorisation documentation. Any provider that cannot produce both company authorisation and individual cards on request should not be used.

For CPO operations, this translates to heightened situational awareness at all high-footfall transit points, avoidance of predictable routines through public areas, immediate-action planning for each public transit including rally points and exit routes, and brief principal on venue-specific awareness (crowded market areas, the Vieux-Port waterfront during events). The military and police presence resulting from Vigipirate provides a degree of deterrence, but does not eliminate the threat that the alert level exists to address.

The A55 and A7 motorway corridor between Marseille and Fos-sur-Mer (approximately 50km west) passes through the Marseille western outskirts and the Martigues industrial zone. The route itself is well-maintained motorway and is not a high-crime zone, but it passes near areas of the city with elevated social tension, and HGV and tanker traffic from port and petrochemical operations is constant. For executives visiting Elengy, Naphtachimie, or Kem One operations at Fos, the access protocols at each industrial site should be confirmed in advance with site security: industrial zone entry typically requires pre-registered access permits, and the CPO team’s vehicles and personnel must be on the site’s authorised access list. A site-specific advance visit is recommended before the principal’s first visit to any Fos facility.

Marseille’s hospital system is comprehensive by European standards, with Hôpital La Timone (+33 4 91 38 00 00) providing Level I trauma and specialist care, and SAMU (15) coordinating emergency medical response citywide. Principals should carry EHIC or equivalent European health insurance for EU residents, or comprehensive private health insurance for non-EU visitors, as SAMU treatment is covered under reciprocal health arrangements for EU nationals but non-EU visitors without insurance face billing at the point of care. The CPO detail should carry a basic trauma kit and all officers should hold current first-aid certification. For conditions beyond Marseille’s hospital capability, medical transfer to Paris (approximately 3 hours by air) or London (approximately 2 hours by air) is the standard pathway.
