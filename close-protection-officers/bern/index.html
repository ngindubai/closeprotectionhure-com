Operational detail for Bern

Regulatory Framework and Licensing

Private security in Switzerland is governed by the Concordat on Private Security (CIPES), a cantonal-level regulatory framework under which each of Switzerland's 26 cantons maintains its own licensing authority. Bern falls under the Canton of Bern's cantonal authority, and all close protection companies and individual officers operating here must hold valid cantonal licences. Armed protection officers require an additional cantonal firearms permit, which is issued separately and requires demonstrated competency and specific authorisation for each engagement. All security companies must be Switzerland-registered -- foreign companies cannot simply deploy personnel into Switzerland without meeting the CIPES requirements. Clients should verify that any security provider holds current cantonal company authorisation and that individual officers carry valid personal licences. The licensing framework is well-administered and provides meaningful accountability, making Bern a location where credential verification is reliably achievable.

Intelligence and Espionage Risk

Switzerland's Federal Intelligence Service (NDB -- Nachrichtendienst des Bundes) has consistently reported elevated foreign state-sponsored espionage activity on Swiss soil, with Bern specifically identified as a priority target given its concentration of diplomatic missions, federal government institutions, and intergovernmental organisations. The NDB's annual threat assessment notes that foreign intelligence services exploit Switzerland's open, neutral environment to target both governmental and commercial principals attending sensitive meetings. For executives attending bilateral negotiations using Switzerland's neutral status as a meeting environment, or for diplomats attending intergovernmental sessions, the intelligence risk is materially higher than the city's low physical crime profile might suggest. CPO teams in Bern are therefore as much an operational security asset as a physical one: controlling who has contact with the principal, managing venue selection and counter-surveillance protocols, and advising on communications hygiene during sensitive engagements.

Diplomatic District and Government Zone Coverage

Bern's operational geography for close protection divides into distinct zones. The Bundeshaus district -- the Federal Parliament, Federal Council chambers, and federal department offices -- is the primary venue for government-level meetings and requires advance coordination with Federal Police (Fedpol) for access to sensitive areas. The Kirchenfeld district south of the Aare houses the majority of foreign embassies, consulates, and intergovernmental missions, and is the most common operational zone for diplomatic principals. The Länggasse district to the west of the city centre hosts pharmaceutical sector offices and university-linked research facilities. The Hotel Bellevue Palace on Kochergasse is the standard venue for high-level diplomatic entertaining and requires its own advance work given its proximity to the Bundeshaus and the security posture it operates during diplomatic events. CPO teams operating across these zones must be comfortable with both the formal access protocols of government buildings and the discreet operating profile expected in Bern's hotel and restaurant environments.

Counter-Surveillance and Digital Operational Security

Given Bern's function as a confidential meeting environment for sensitive diplomatic and commercial engagements, counter-surveillance and digital operational security are core CPO competencies for this city. Officers conduct venue advance work that includes identifying surveillance positions, assessing camera coverage, and planning routes that vary approach and departure patterns. For principals attending particularly sensitive negotiations, technical counter-surveillance sweeps of meeting rooms can be arranged through specialist providers. Digital hygiene advice for Bern deployments covers device management -- using travel-only devices without sensitive data, ensuring no sensitive material is stored locally, and using encrypted communications tools throughout the visit. Switzerland's strong rule of law makes overt monitoring of foreign nationals legally constrained, but the NDB's own reporting confirms that foreign intelligence services operate in Bern in ways that Swiss law cannot always intercept before an exposure event occurs.

Airport and Transit Security

Bern Airport (BRN) is a small regional airport located 9 kilometres southeast of the city centre with limited international connections, primarily to leisure and regional destinations. Most senior principals travelling to Bern arrive at Zurich Airport (ZRH, approximately 120 kilometres northeast, reachable in approximately 75 minutes by direct rail or 90-100 minutes by secure vehicle), or Geneva Airport (GVA, approximately 165 kilometres southwest, approximately 100 minutes by rail or 110-120 minutes by secure vehicle). The ZRH route is more commonly used for commercial flights, while GVA is standard for principals connecting from western Europe. CPO meet-and-greet at ZRH or GVA involves airside coordination with ground handlers where possible, controlled kerb transfer, and a secure drive on well-maintained Swiss motorway to Bern. The A1 motorway between Zurich and Bern is one of Europe's best-maintained inter-city routes and offers predictable transfer times outside of peak commuter hours (0730-0900 and 1700-1900 on weekdays).

Emergency Response and Medical Support

Bern has one of Europe's most capable hospital systems. Inselspital (Bern University Hospital, +41 31 632 21 11) is a Level I trauma and tertiary referral centre with comprehensive specialist capabilities. Switzerland's ambulance system (emergency: 144) operates to a high standard with fast response times in the city. For serious medical emergencies, air ambulance capability is available domestically through REGA (Swiss Air Rescue, +41 333 333 333), which operates throughout Switzerland and internationally. The quality and accessibility of medical care in Bern means that medical evacuation to another country is rarely necessary -- the primary planning concern is ensuring the principal has the correct European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) or equivalent private health coverage, as Swiss hospital costs without insurance are very high. The British Embassy in Bern (+41 31 359 77 00) provides full consular services for UK nationals. Emergency contacts: 117 (police), 144 (ambulance), 118 (fire), 112 (pan-European emergency).
