Security Service Law: How CPO Licensing Works in Japan

Japan's regulatory framework for close protection differs fundamentally from the UK, Australian, or US individual-licence model. The Security Service Law (Keibi-Gyoho, Law No. 117 of 1972, as amended) licences security businesses as entities, not individuals as independent practitioners. Individual close protection officers (CPOs) in Japan are employees of a licensed security company, and their authority to operate derives from their employer's company licence, not from a personal licence held in their own name. This means there is no freelance or self-employed CPO market in Japan analogous to the UK model. A CPO in Tokyo is, by regulatory design, employed by and operating under the oversight of an authorised security business.

NPA Oversight and National Standards

The National Police Agency (NPA) oversees the security industry in Japan, with prefectural police forces (the Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department, or Keishicho, for the Tokyo area) responsible for local enforcement and company licensing. Security companies must meet NPA-stipulated training standards, staffing requirements, and operational protocols. The Security Service Law specifies minimum training requirements for security officers, including those performing close protection functions. The structure creates accountability through the employer company rather than through individual credentials, which is a different but comparably rigorous approach to the individual-licence systems used in common-law jurisdictions.

No Individual Freelance CPO Model in Tokyo

The absence of a freelance or independent CPO model in Japan is the most significant structural difference from Western markets. For international principals accustomed to engaging a named CPO as an individual contractor, the Tokyo model requires a different approach: the operating company is the licensed entity, and the CPO is an employee within that company's authorised structure. This has practical implications for how overseas clients should structure Tokyo engagements. The client relationship is with the licensed security company, and the operator assigns qualified staff. For international principals arriving with their own CPO team, those individuals cannot operate commercially in a security capacity in Japan without being employed by a Japanese licensed security operator.

Armed Protection in Tokyo

Private close protection in Japan is unarmed. Japan maintains one of the most restrictive civilian firearms frameworks of any developed country - private firearm ownership is extraordinarily rare, and there is no mechanism for private security operators to carry firearms commercially. This is not a material limitation in Tokyo's threat environment, where violent crime affecting foreign principals is exceptionally rare. The operational emphasis is entirely on protocol-based protection: advance work, route selection, crowd management, and the ability to operate discreetly across Tokyo's dense commercial and hotel environments.

Tokyo CPO engagements span the principal business zones (Marunouchi, Otemachi, Shinjuku, Shibuya), high-end hotel environments (Chiyoda and Minato ward hotels along the Ark Hills to Azabu corridor), and HNWI residential areas in Minato, Setagaya, and Shibuya wards. The advance for a Marunouchi-based executive covers underground station exits, building lobby security layers, and the pedestrian links between Tokyo Station and the commercial towers. For engagements involving Ginza restaurant and cultural venues, the CPO's advance maps the narrow street-level access points and confirms driver waiting zones given Tokyo's complex vehicle stopping rules in central zones.

Working With a Licensed Japanese Operator

For international clients requiring close protection in Tokyo, the engagement should be with an NPA-licensed Japanese security company that has demonstrable experience in executive and personal protection at the required level. The company licence can be confirmed through the Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department. The key qualification questions are: what is the company's operating licence status, what specific training framework do their CPO-category staff complete, and what is the individual officer's operational background and English-language capability for international principal assignments. Bilingual CPOs with experience of international executive protocols are available in Tokyo but represent a specific sub-pool within the broader licensed operator market.
