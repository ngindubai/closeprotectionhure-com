Residential security in Port-au-Prince operates in one of the most challenging urban environments in the Western Hemisphere. Gang alliances control significant portions of the city, including major transit routes, and the national police force operates with severely degraded capacity. The FCDO advises against all travel to Haiti, and the US State Department classifies Haiti at Level 4 (Do Not Travel) as of 2026. Organisations maintaining a presence in Port-au-Prince do so with Haiti-specialist security providers, compound-grade protection in Petion-Ville, and tested evacuation plans. There is no standard residential security programme applicable to Port-au-Prince; every programme is built to the specific compound location and organisational risk tolerance.

The residential security environment in Port-au-Prince

Petion-Ville provides the closest approximation to a viable residential zone in Port-au-Prince: higher elevation, greater distance from the most gang-controlled neighbourhoods, and a concentration of secured compounds used by the diplomatic and international NGO community. Even in Petion-Ville, armed compound guarding, generator power, water storage, and a 72-hour self-sufficiency capability are prerequisites rather than enhancements. The gang environment shifts regularly, and route surveys to Toussaint Louverture International Airport (PAP) must be conducted and updated at minimum monthly. Sources: FCDO Haiti travel advice (2026); US State Dept Haiti Level 4 advisory (2026).

A Port-au-Prince residential programme covers Haiti-specialist armed compound guarding, compound hardening assessment, generator and water supply planning, domestic staff vetting, and primary and secondary evacuation planning including overland routes to the Dominican Republic. The Security Framework in Haiti

Haiti's private security sector operates in a severely degraded state environment. The FCDO advises against all travel to Haiti as of 2026. The US State Department classifies Haiti as Level 4 (Do Not Travel). For organisations maintaining a presence in Port-au-Prince, residential security operates through a small number of specialist operators with Haiti-specific experience. Any residential programme must be built on the assumption of very limited government security response capacity.

Residential Locations in Port-au-Prince

The Petion-Ville area, at higher elevation above central Port-au-Prince, provides the most viable residential zone for international personnel who must maintain a presence in the city. Petion-Ville has a higher proportion of secured compounds with armed guarding, generator power, and water storage, and is separated by elevation and distance from the most gang-controlled areas in Cite Soleil, Martissant, Delmas, and Croix-des-Bouquets. Laboule, further up the hillside, is used by some diplomatic and aid community personnel.

Compound Security Requirements

Residential security in Port-au-Prince requires compound-grade protection: armed guarding with Haiti-experienced operators (typically former military or national police), high perimeter walls with barbed wire or broken glass, generator power for all security systems and habitability, water storage with a minimum of 72 hours' supply, vehicle access control via heavy metal gates, and a documented evacuation plan to Toussaint Louverture International Airport (PAP).

Armed Guarding and Weapons Policy

Armed residential security is the standard requirement for any compound in Port-au-Prince that needs to be maintained. Armed guards must hold weapons under the relevant Haitian legal framework, but in practice the regulatory oversight of the security sector is severely limited. International organisations typically coordinate armed guarding through contracted operators with UN and diplomatic community experience in Haiti. Regular audits of guard deployments are essential in an environment where operator quality is highly variable.

Emergency Evacuation Planning

Every residential security programme in Port-au-Prince requires a primary and secondary evacuation plan. Primary evacuation routes from Petion-Ville to Toussaint Louverture International Airport (PAP) must be pre-surveyed and updated regularly as gang territorial boundaries shift. A secondary plan involving overland evacuation to the Dominican Republic border crossing at Malpasse or Ouanaminthe must be documented. Medevac is the only medical response option for anything beyond first aid, given the near-collapse of Port-au-Prince's hospital system.
