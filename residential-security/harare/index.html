Harare’s residential security environment reflects Zimbabwe’s prolonged economic crisis, post-Mugabe political instability, and documented armed robbery and home invasion risks that affect expatriate, NGO, and senior corporate households in the city’s northern suburbs. A structured programme combining compound hardening, ZRP-licensed static guarding, and emergency planning is the baseline for residential operations in the Zimbabwean capital.

The residential environment in Harare

The FCDO maintains a high-risk designation for Zimbabwe, citing crime and economic volatility as the primary drivers affecting residential security. Zimbabwe’s economy has experienced repeated currency crises, fuel shortages, and power supply failures that directly shape the operational environment for residential security teams. Economic hardship creates elevated opportunistic crime risk and increases the insider threat from household staff under financial pressure.

Harare’s northern suburbs, particularly Borrowdale and Highlands, house the bulk of the city’s expatriate population in compound arrangements that reflect the city’s historical residential security culture. Legacy perimeter infrastructure is common but of variable quality, and programme design must include a thorough survey before relying on existing walls and access controls.

Carjacking at residential gates, home invasion, and armed robbery are the primary residential crime threats. Zimbabwe’s power supply deficit, managed through ZESA load-shedding schedules that can affect northern suburbs for up to 18 hours per day, creates systematic gaps in electronic security systems that must be addressed through generator and battery backup.

What residential security covers in Harare

A residential security programme in Harare covers perimeter surveys, static guarding, armed response retainer, CCTV and alarm monitoring, domestic staff vetting through ZRP and civil registry channels, and emergency response planning. Generator backup for all security-critical electrical systems is a standard component, not an optional upgrade.

For principals at elevated personal risk, programmes include pattern-of-life review, gate and vehicle access protocols, a tested emergency communications plan, and pre-arranged medevac coordination with Johannesburg. Staff vetting is integrated into the programme setup rather than treated as a separate exercise.

Operational detail for Harare

Private Security Licensing Under the ZRP Framework

Private security companies operating in Zimbabwe must be registered and licensed by the Zimbabwe Republic Police (ZRP) under the Private Investigators and Security Guards (Control) Act. This legislation requires all security companies to register with the ZRP, submit to periodic inspections, and ensure that guards are trained to a prescribed standard. Armed security personnel require individual weapons permits issued through the ZRP Firearms Registry; unarmed guard deployment is more common at residential properties, with armed response capability provided by a licensed response company rather than static armed guards. The post-Mugabe period since 2017 has not substantially changed the licensing framework, though economic pressures have increased the number of informal and unregistered operators in Harare's residential security market. Principals engaging a provider should demand evidence of current ZRP registration, individual guard identification documentation, and a copy of the company's public liability insurance. Unregistered operators are common in Harare's informal sector and carry no regulatory accountability. Source: Zimbabwe Private Investigators and Security Guards (Control) Act and FCDO Zimbabwe advisory (2025).

Expatriate Residential Zones in Harare

Harare's expatriate and senior corporate residential market concentrates in a set of established suburbs north and north-east of the city centre. Borrowdale is the primary zone, with large plots, established compound infrastructure, and a concentration of diplomatic and NGO residences. Highlands, to the east, is favoured by longer-established expatriates and senior mining-sector staff. The Avenues area, between the city centre and the northern suburbs, houses a mixture of diplomatic staff and NGO personnel in smaller compounds. Msasa Park and Chisipite are secondary zones used by corporate and mining-sector principals. All these suburbs are characterised by mature perimeter walling, in many cases dating from the period when Harare had higher residential crime rates; however, the quality of existing walls varies and should be assessed as part of any property survey. Generator backup is essential in all zones given Zimbabwe's chronic power supply deficit, which the Zimbabwe Electricity Supply Authority (ZESA) attributes to inadequate generation capacity and infrastructure deterioration. Source: FCDO Zimbabwe advisory (2025).

Armed Robbery, Carjacking, and Home Invasion Risks

The FCDO records armed robbery, carjacking, and home invasion as documented risks in Harare, driven by economic hardship and high unemployment rates that have persisted through Zimbabwe's prolonged economic crisis. Carjacking incidents at or near residential gates are a recorded pattern, with attackers targeting returning vehicles in the brief period between gate opening and entry. Home invasion involving armed attackers has been reported in Borrowdale and Highlands, typically targeting properties with high-value vehicles, generators, or fuel storage visible from the road. Zimbabwe's economic volatility creates pressure on lower-paid domestic and security staff and increases the insider risk from domestics and guards with knowledge of property layouts. Properties that display visible wealth signals, including high-specification vehicles, satellite equipment, and illuminated perimeters, attract disproportionate targeting attention. Residential security programmes should incorporate strict gate and vehicle access protocols, randomised pattern variation for residential arrivals, and a tested emergency response plan. Source: FCDO Zimbabwe advisory (2025) and US State Department Zimbabwe travel advisory (2025).

Property Security Survey and Hardening

A residential security survey in Harare covers perimeter integrity, access control, CCTV coverage, lighting, communications, and generator capacity. Many Harare residential properties have legacy perimeter infrastructure of variable quality; walls that appear adequate may be structurally degraded or have concealed access points that require addressing. CCTV systems must be generator-backed to maintain coverage during ZESA outages, which can run to 12-18 hours per day in affected areas. Access control at vehicle gates should use an intercom-and-camera challenge system rather than manual observation alone. Guard patrols of the perimeter at randomised intervals are standard for higher-risk properties. Window and door reinforcement on ground-floor openings reduces forced-entry vulnerability. Alarm systems should be connected to a licensed armed response company rather than a bells-only configuration. A safe room with independent communications and first-aid supplies is advisable for principals identified as elevated personal-risk targets. Source: FCDO Zimbabwe advisory (2025).

Domestic Staff Vetting and Medevac Planning

Domestic staff vetting in Harare should use a combination of ZRP police clearance checks, civil registry identity verification, and reference checks from previous employers. The ZRP clearance process, conducted at district level, returns a certificate confirming whether the applicant has a criminal record; the process takes two to four weeks and should be completed before staff enter the property. Civil registry checks confirm identity through birth and identity documentation. At least two independent employer references are a baseline requirement; verbal reference calls rather than written references are more effective at surfacing concerns. A probationary period of at least 60 days with restricted access to security-sensitive areas of the property should follow before full access is granted. Medevac planning from Harare routes to Johannesburg, South Africa, at approximately one hour's flight time from Robert Gabriel Mugabe International Airport. Johannesburg's Netcare and Mediclinic networks provide advanced trauma and specialist care capability. Harare's own private hospital sector, including Avenues Clinic, can manage initial stabilisation but cannot replace international evacuation for complex cases. Principals should hold an active medevac policy explicitly covering Zimbabwe. Source: FCDO Zimbabwe advisory (2025).
