Residential Security in Basra

Residential security for energy sector personnel in Basra. Compound security, staff vetting, and emergency protocols for oil and gas assignees in southern Iraq.

Basra is Iraq’s principal oil and gas hub, hosting operations for BP (Rumaila), ExxonMobil (West Qurna), TotalEnergies, and Lukoil, among others. The security environment is high-risk: FCDO advises against all but essential travel to Basra Governorate, and the US State Dept rates Iraq at Level 4: Do Not Travel. FCDO specifically flags kidnapping risk for foreign nationals and documents mortar and rocket attacks on energy infrastructure and compound areas. The 2018 protests that targeted energy company offices and compounds in Basra demonstrated that periods of civil unrest can escalate to direct attacks on energy-sector infrastructure. For the full Basra city security briefing, see our dedicated city page.

Residential security in this environment is a specialist discipline that bears little resemblance to its equivalent in lower-risk locations. The question is not whether to use a security compound but which compound, with what specifications, and with what tested protocols. Purpose-built compounds and oil company operational bases are the only suitable residential configurations; any deployment based on standard hotel or domestic residential accommodation in Basra represents an unacceptable risk level for foreign national personnel. Compound assessment, compound-staff vetting, and a fully tested medical-evacuation and emergency-extraction plan are the three non-negotiable components of any Basra residential security arrangement. For close protection coverage of movements between compound and operational sites, our executive protection in Basra service provides armoured vehicle provision and close-protection team deployment appropriate to the threat level.

All providers we engage in Basra hold current PSCL authorisation from the Ministry of Interior under Order 3 of 2005. Operations are coordinated with Basra Operations Command as required. What this covers

Operational detail for Basra

Property Security Survey

Standard residential premises in Basra are not appropriate for foreign national assignees. Security surveys in this environment focus on purpose-built security compounds and oil company operational bases with integrated residential facilities. Assessment criteria include perimeter blast and ballistic standards, access-control architecture (vehicle exclusion zone, airlock entry, biometric access), CCTV and guard-tower coverage, quick-reaction force capacity and response time, safe-room specification, and emergency evacuation route integrity. Compounds are assessed against the ASIS International Physical Security Standard and relevant oil-company operator security baselines.

Regulatory Framework in Iraq

Private security companies operating in Iraq require authorisation from the Private Security Companies Licensing Bureau (PSCL) at the Ministry of Interior. Armed security provision requires Ministry of Interior authorisation under Order 3 of 2005 (as amended). In Basra Governorate, compound security operations must additionally coordinate with Basra Operations Command and local police. Foreign nationals employed in a security capacity require individual Ministry endorsement. Non-compliant operators face weapons confiscation and expulsion; clients of non-compliant operators face operational disruption and potential legal exposure.

Domestic Staff Vetting

All Iraqi national staff employed within a compound, including cleaners, caterers, translators, and maintenance personnel, require structured vetting given the threat environment. Vetting in the Basra context covers tribal and community affiliation review (relevant to PMF/Hashd al-Shaabi proximity), employment reference verification with previous employer companies or organisations known to the client, national identity card (CSID) verification, and a structured interview by a trained investigator with local language and cultural competence. All staff access should be documented, restricted by role, and subject to periodic re-vetting.

Neighbourhood and Zone Assessment: Basra

No standard Basra residential district is appropriate for unescorted foreign national living. Assignees are housed in one of two approved configurations: purpose-built security compounds (typically in the corridor near the Al Basrah hotel, along the river, or on the outskirts towards Rumaila and other oilfield access routes), or oil company operational bases with integrated residential facilities. Both configurations must maintain vehicle exclusion zones, armed access control, and a tested quick-reaction force. FCDO advises against all but essential travel to Basra Governorate (2024); US State Dept rates Iraq at Level 4: Do Not Travel. Mortar and rocket attacks on energy infrastructure and compound areas are documented in FCDO reporting.

Emergency Protocols and Consular Contact

Emergency contacts in Basra: police 104, ambulance 115. Primary medical facility: Basra Teaching Hospital (limited capacity for complex trauma; medical evacuation is the standard protocol for serious cases). Medical evacuation routing: Erbil is the primary destination within Iraq; Kuwait City and Amman are the established international alternatives. British Embassy Baghdad: +964 790 192 6280 (not present in Basra; Baghdad is the consular point of contact). Emergency protocols must include a tested evacuation plan with at least two airport/extraction routes from the compound, a communication-loss protocol, and a hostage-recovery protocol aligned with company kidnap-and-ransom insurance requirements.

Digital and Communications Security

Basra's operational security environment requires a communications protocol more stringent than standard corporate practice. Measures appropriate to this environment include: strict schedule and location confidentiality (no social media posting of compound location, routing, or personnel identification); encrypted satellite communications as a backup to standard mobile networks (Iraqi mobile networks have documented outage risk during unrest periods); device security protocols for all personnel entering and leaving the compound; and vetting of any local service-provider personnel given physical-access requirements (IT maintenance, satellite-equipment servicing).
