The Russian private security licensing framework

Federal Law No. 2487-1 (1992, as amended) governs private security and detective activities in Russia. The 2016 establishment of Rosguard (National Guard of Russia, Rosgvardia) transferred the primary licensing function for private security companies from the MVD, and Rosguard now issues the Federal Guard Licence required for company operations. Individual personnel hold охранника удостоверение (guard certificates) at grades 4, 5, or 6, with grade 6 covering armed operations under specific authorisation. Operating within this framework requires familiarity with Rosguard’s regional administration processes and the documentation requirements for foreign-linked operations.

Operational detail for Moscow

Russian Federal Private Security Licensing Framework

Private security in Russia is governed by Federal Law No. 2487-1 On Private Detective and Security Activities in the Russian Federation (1992, as amended). Security companies must hold a Federal Guard Licence issued by Rosguard (the National Guard of Russia, Rosgvardia, established 2016), which replaced MVD licensing for private security companies following that organisational restructuring. Individual guards hold охранника удостоверение (guard certificate) in grades 4, 5, or 6, with grade 6 permitting armed operations under specific Rosguard authorisation. Company and personnel licensing is administered through Rosguard's regional departments.

FCDO Against-All-Travel Advisory - Current Position

The FCDO advises against all travel to Russia as of its current travel advice (2024 onwards, following the February 2022 escalation of conflict in Ukraine). This advisory reflects the materially changed security environment for Western principals, including the risk of arbitrary detention, the closure of airspace to many Western carriers, and the broader conflict context. Principals with specific operational requirements in Moscow should treat this advisory as the primary planning input and discuss their specific circumstances with specialist security advisers before making any travel decision.

Pre-2022 CPO Demand Context in Moscow

Prior to February 2022, Moscow's CPO demand was shaped by the corporate security environment of a major energy-sector capital: organised crime risk for business principals (the krysha protection system historically affecting commercial operations), business dispute-related violence, and the documented FSB and intelligence service activity around foreign business operations. Western companies with Moscow offices routinely engaged CPO cover for senior visiting executives. The current environment involves these pre-existing factors overlaid by conflict-related considerations that are qualitatively different from a conventional business security assessment.

Moscow City and Rublyovka Operational Areas

Historically, Moscow CPO operations have centred on the Moscow International Business Centre (MIBC or Moscow City), the Rublyovka corridor (the principal HNWI residential and dacha zone west of the city), and the Tverskaya Street and Garden Ring business corridor. The Rublyovka area has a long-established profile as the most significant concentration of high-net-worth residential security requirements in Russia. Pre-advance work for the Moscow Kremlin vicinity, Gorky Park, and major hotel properties (Baltschug Kempinski, Marriott Grand, Four Seasons Moscow) was standard for diplomatic and corporate programmes.

Airport Transfer Logistics - Sheremetyevo and Domodedovo

Moscow's principal international airports are Sheremetyevo (SVO), which handles the majority of international traffic including Aeroflot's hub operations, and Domodedovo (DME), which serves a range of international carriers. A third airport, Vnukovo (VKO), primarily handles domestic routes and charter operations. The airport-to-city transfer is a significant element of the Moscow CPO engagement given road congestion and the specific access conditions of each airport's approach. Current flight connectivity to Moscow from Western European hubs has been materially reduced since February 2022.

Intelligence Service Activity and Counter-Surveillance

Moscow has one of the most intensive domestic intelligence environments of any major world city, with FSB activity around foreign business operations thoroughly documented. Prior to 2022, counter-surveillance awareness, device security, and meeting venue selection were standard components of corporate security briefings for senior executives operating in Moscow. Communications security, avoidance of informal meetings with government-connected individuals, and strict digital hygiene protocols are elements of the specialist briefing required for any engagement in the current environment. These factors are materially elevated relative to the pre-2022 baseline.
