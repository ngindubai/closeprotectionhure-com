What this covers

Operational detail for Beirut

Lebanon Private Security Licensing

Private security companies in Lebanon operate under Ministry of Interior operating licences. Individual close protection officers require Ministry of Interior authorisation. The regulatory framework is less standardised than Western licensing bodies such as the UK SIA, making pre-engagement diligence on operator licensing and personnel vetting documentation essential for every Beirut CPO deployment. Our Lebanon partners hold current Ministry of Interior registration and provide all documentation to clients before deployment.

Hezbollah Territory and Zone Avoidance

The southern suburbs of Beirut (Dahieh), including Haret Hreik, Bourj al-Barajneh, and the areas adjacent to the Palestinian camps, are zones of Hezbollah territorial control and are categorically excluded from all principal itineraries. Israeli air operations in 2024 targeted multiple Dahieh sites; the ongoing militia and political environment renders these areas inappropriate for corporate or HNWI visitors. CPO route planning in Beirut explicitly avoids Dahieh on all airport and inter-district transfers. Source: FCDO Lebanon travel advice, 2025.

Economic Collapse and Operational Environment

Lebanon's financial system has collapsed progressively since 2019, with the Lebanese Lira losing over 90 per cent of its value by 2024. Cash availability constraints, intermittent bank closures, and fuel supply disruptions have materially degraded the security operational environment: hospital capacity has contracted, ambulance reliability is below historical norms, and opportunistic crime has increased as economic desperation has grown across the city. CPO engagements in Beirut incorporate a specific medical protocol referencing the American University of Beirut Medical Centre (AUBMC) as the primary private facility.

Port Explosion Aftermath and Infrastructure

The August 2020 Beirut Port explosion destroyed or severely damaged large sections of the Gemmayzeh, Mar Mikhael, and port district. Reconstruction is ongoing but incomplete; road conditions and building stability in the port area remain unpredictable. CPO route planning avoids the port district for non-essential movements and routes east-west transfers through Hamra and Achrafieh, bypassing the Mar Mikhael zone.

Rafic Hariri International Airport Transfer

Rafic Hariri International Airport (BEY) is located approximately 8 kilometres south of central Beirut, immediately adjacent to the Dahieh southern suburbs. The airport exit routing must specifically avoid Hezbollah-controlled areas on the approach to Hamra, Downtown Solidere, and Achrafieh. Journey time under normal conditions is 20 to 35 minutes; checkpoint delays and road conditions can extend this. Inside-terminal CPO collection is standard for all Beirut principal arrivals.

Medical Evacuation and Emergency Protocol

AUBMC and Hotel-Dieu de France are the two most capable private medical facilities in Beirut. For cases requiring specialist treatment beyond Beirut's reduced local capacity, Cyprus (Nicosia) is the nearest European-standard destination by air, approximately 45 minutes from BEY. Evacuation coordination with Larnaca or Tel Aviv as alternates is included in the Lebanon medical protocol. All Beirut CPO engagements carry a documented medical evacuation plan given the degraded local ambulance system.
