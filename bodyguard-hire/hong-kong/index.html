Hiring a bodyguard in Hong Kong is typically about navigating National Security Law exposure and a close surveillance environment rather than conventional crime, which is low. The SGSIA licensing framework

Hong Kong’s Security and Guarding Services Industry Authority (SGSIA) administers the licensing framework under the Security and Guarding Services Ordinance (Cap. 460). Security company licences and individual security guard licences are both administered by SGSIA. The framework applies to all commercial security work in Hong Kong including close protection. SGSIA licence verification is the primary due diligence step when engaging any Hong Kong security operator.

The post-NSL operating environment

The National Security Law has changed specific elements of the intelligence environment for senior executives in Hong Kong. Communications security awareness, caution around discussions of politically sensitive topics in mixed company, and awareness of which activities may attract legal scrutiny under the NSL are now standard elements of CPO briefing for Hong Kong engagements at the senior executive level.

Operational detail for Hong Kong

SGSIA Licensing Under Cap. 460

Private security in Hong Kong is regulated under the Security and Guarding Services Ordinance (Cap. 460). Security companies must hold a Security Company Licence from the Security and Guarding Services Industry Authority (SGSIA). Individual security personnel must hold a Type I Licence (Security Guard) or Type II Licence (Armoured Car Guard) with the appropriate category for close protection work.

HNWI and Corporate Executive Protection

Hong Kong is one of Asia's principal HNWI and UHNWI concentrations, with a significant private banking and family-office sector. CPO demand in Hong Kong's Peak, Mid-Levels, Repulse Bay, and Deep Water Bay residential areas is driven by the wealth concentration rather than ambient crime. Corporate CPO demand is driven by the financial services, private equity, and technology sectors headquartered in Central and Wan Chai.

Political and Intelligence Environment

The political environment in Hong Kong following the 2019 protests and the implementation of the National Security Law (NSL) in June 2020 has introduced specific intelligence-environment considerations for senior executives and HNWI principals. CPO briefings for Hong Kong include awareness of the current legal and political context, particular attention to communications security in sensitive business situations, and awareness of the specific provisions of the NSL relevant to the principal's sector and activities.

Central, Wan Chai, and Kowloon Coverage

Operational familiarity with Hong Kong's principal corporate and HNWI zones: Central (financial district), Wan Chai (corporate and government), TST (Tsim Sha Tsui commercial), the Peak and Mid-Levels (HNWI residential), and Admiralty (government and legal). Each area has distinct venue security standards and access protocols.

HKIA Chek Lap Kok Transfer Integration

Hong Kong International Airport (HKIA) on Lantau Island is connected to the city centre by the Airport Express rail service and road via the Tsing Ma Bridge. CPO transfer cover from HKIA to Central, Wan Chai, or Kowloon hotels follows the SGSIA-licensed driver and operations controller standard. The Airport Express is not the appropriate transport for principals requiring CPO cover; pre-arranged vehicle transfer is the correct approach.

Discreet Profile Requirement

Hong Kong corporate and HNWI culture values discretion in close protection, particularly given the political sensitivity of visible security presence in the post-NSL environment. CPOs for Hong Kong engagements are selected for discreet-profile capability and awareness of the current political context.
