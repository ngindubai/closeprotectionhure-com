Operational detail for Dublin

Threat Assessment: Dublin

Dublin carries a low risk rating for corporate visitors. The FCDO advises normal precautions for Ireland in 2026, and the US State Department maintains a Level 1 designation. The primary risks for close protection planning are petty theft and bag snatching in the O'Connell Street and Henry Street shopping corridor (north inner city), and organised crime enforcement activity: the Garda Siochana annual report for 2025 notes continued enforcement against criminal networks, with some high-profile incidents in the north inner city that are unconnected to corporate visitors and not present in the Ballsbridge, Sandymount, or IFSC Docklands zones used by visiting executives. The Kinahan-Hutch feud, once a significant organised crime dynamic, has substantially diminished. The overall environment is well-managed for the substantial volume of multinational corporate traffic Dublin receives.

Ireland Close Protection Licensing and Vetting

Private security services in Ireland are regulated under the Private Security Services Act 2004, overseen by the Private Security Authority (PSA), an independent statutory regulator. This is a distinctive feature of the Irish model: the PSA operates separately from the Garda Siochana and is specifically mandated to licence and regulate the private security sector. Companies providing close protection must hold a current PSA licence, and individual officers must hold a PSA licence card specific to the activity they are undertaking (close protection is a separately licensed category). Licensing requires completion of a PSA-approved training programme, criminal background clearance (checked by the Garda Siochana), and medical fitness assessment. Principals should request sight of the provider's PSA company licence and the assigned officers' individual PSA cards before engagement.

Airport and Arrival Security

Dublin is served by Dublin Airport (DUB), located approximately 10 km north of the city centre via the M1 motorway and M50 junction, with a standard transfer time of 20 to 40 minutes depending on traffic (morning and evening peaks on the M50 approach can extend this significantly). The airport operates two terminals: Terminal 1 handles most European, transatlantic, and low-cost routes; Terminal 2 handles US pre-clearance flights and some Star Alliance services. US pre-clearance at DUB allows transatlantic principals to arrive in Dublin as domestic passengers, which reduces customs and border exposure. Pre-arranged, vetted collection at the arrivals hall of the relevant terminal is the standard protocol.

Movement and Route Security

Dublin's key corporate zones (Ballsbridge embassy and corporate district, IFSC Docklands, and the Grand Canal Dock technology quarter) are well-connected but require route planning to manage the M50 and city-centre congestion, particularly during the morning and evening peaks. For repeated journeys to fixed destinations such as KPMG or LinkedIn on South Dock or embassy meetings in Ballsbridge, route variation is standard practice. O'Connell Street north and Henry Street should be avoided in principal route planning where alternatives exist, due to elevated petty theft conditions in that corridor. Protective drivers should be familiar with Dublin's port tunnel access for northside journeys and the key alternates around the Grand Canal.

Residential and Hotel Security

Hotels appropriate for HNWI and corporate principals in Dublin include the Merrion Hotel (Upper Merrion Street, Georgian quarter, government-district adjacent), the Westbury Hotel (Grafton Street, city centre), and the InterContinental Dublin (Simmonscourt Road, Ballsbridge, preferred for principals focused on embassy and corporate Ballsbridge meetings). The Merrion and InterContinental offer the most controlled-access environments. Advance security surveys should confirm access control arrangements, CCTV coverage, and emergency egress. For longer stays, Ballsbridge and Sandymount provide well-managed residential environments well-suited to close protection deployments.

Emergency Response and Medical Cover

The primary public trauma facility in Dublin is the Mater Misericordiae University Hospital (Eccles Street, north inner city), the city's main trauma and acute care centre. St Vincent's University Hospital (Elm Park, Ballsbridge-adjacent, south Dublin) is the primary academic teaching hospital and well-positioned for principals staying in the Ballsbridge and Sandymount area. Private alternatives with English-language capability include Blackrock Clinic (Rock Road, Blackrock) and Mater Private Hospital (Eccles Street, adjacent to the public Mater). For medical emergencies requiring specialist care beyond Dublin's capacity, medevac within Ireland or to the UK (30 to 60 minutes by air) is the standard routing. The Irish emergency number is 999 or the European 112.
