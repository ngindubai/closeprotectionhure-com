Chiang Mai’s bodyguard hire market looks quite different to Bangkok’s. There’s no political-protest history to plan around and no terrorism precedent, and the city sits well clear of the insurgency the FCDO flags for Thailand’s deep south. What actually shapes a Chiang Mai detail is seasonal air quality and road conditions, not crowd control or crisis response.

Burning season is the sharpest example. From roughly mid-February through April, agricultural burning across northern Thailand, Myanmar and Laos fills the mountain basin the city sits in with smoke. A 29 March 2026 reading of 188 micrograms of PM2.5 per cubic metre, cited in coverage of that season, sat at more than four times the WHO’s 24-hour guideline. A properly run detail treats that as a scheduling input: checking daily air-quality readings, moving outdoor engagements indoors where forecasts are poor, and paying particular attention to principals with respiratory conditions.

Operational detail for Chiang Mai

Licensing Framework

Private security in Thailand is regulated under the Security Guard Business Act, administered through the Ministry of Interior and the Royal Thai Police. Companies must hold valid business registration and individual officers need personnel licensing before working a detail. Firearms are restricted nationally, so close protection in Chiang Mai runs unarmed in the overwhelming majority of assignments, built around planning, awareness and route management rather than armed response.

Threat Environment

Chiang Mai's day-to-day threat picture is calmer than Bangkok's: no political-protest history, no terrorism precedent, and no proximity to the deep south insurgency the FCDO flags separately for Pattani, Yala and Narathiwat. The two factors that actually shape a Chiang Mai detail are seasonal air quality and road safety. During burning season, roughly mid-February to April, PM2.5 readings have reached over four times the WHO's guideline in recent years; a 29 March 2026 reading of 188 micrograms per cubic metre, cited in coverage of that year's crisis, illustrates the scale.

Key Operational Areas

The Old City moat district, with its night markets and Sunday Walking Street, carries the highest concentration of tourist footfall and the most consistently reported pickpocketing risk. Nimmanhaemin, the city's cafe and co-working hub, is where much of the digital-nomad and remote-business community operates day to day, and generally presents a calmer working environment.

Close Protection Services

Officers here manage crowd positioning around the Old City markets much as they would in any dense tourist district, but the more distinctive skill set is seasonal: building air-quality forecasts into a principal's schedule during burning season, and adjusting outdoor engagements, event timing or exercise routines accordingly. Route planning around Doi Suthep and other mountain roads is the other recurring requirement, given the accident risk those routes carry.

Airport and Transit Cover

Chiang Mai International Airport (CNX) sits close to the city, with a transfer to the Old City typically running 15 to 20 minutes by private car. Officers meet principals airside or at the terminal exit and manage the short onward leg, factoring in reduced visibility on the worst burning-season days when that applies.

Communications and Contingency

Thailand's police and emergency number is 191, with 1155 for the Tourist Police, 1669 for ambulance and medical emergencies, and 199 for fire. Bangkok Hospital Chiang Mai (+66 52 089 888) runs a 24-hour emergency and trauma centre and is the standard reference hospital for serious incidents in the city.
