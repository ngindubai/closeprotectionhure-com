Medan is the capital of North Sumatra province and, by 2020 Indonesia census figures, the country’s largest city outside Java, with a city population above 2.4 million and a wider metropolitan area exceeding 4.7 million. It ranks alongside Jakarta, Surabaya, and Makassar as one of Indonesia’s four principal urban centres, but its commercial character is distinct from either Jakarta’s finance-and-government base or Surabaya’s manufacturing and port trade. Medan grew out of a colonial-era plantation economy, and it remains North Sumatra’s hub for processing and exporting palm oil, rubber, and coffee, a genuinely different corporate profile from the country’s other major cities.

Security planning for Medan combines Indonesia’s national threat backdrop with two features more specific to this city and province. The FCDO’s assessment that terrorists are likely to try to carry out attacks in Indonesia applies here as it does nationally, and the US State Department rates the country Level 2, Exercise Increased Caution. Layered on top of that is a genuine volcanic hazard: FCDO advice advises against all travel within 5km of the crater of Mount Sinabung, an active volcano in Karo Regency around 70km from Medan, which erupted in 2021 and showed renewed activity in May 2026. Petty crime, largely motorbike-borne bag and phone snatching in crowded markets and transport hubs, is the more routine concern for day-to-day movement.

Medan’s economic identity is worth understanding on its own terms. Companies including Asian Agri run substantial palm oil operations across North Sumatra, Riau, and Jambi from a Medan base, and the city hosts the PALMEX trade event for the sector each year. Corporate visits here more often involve plantation or mill site work in the surrounding regencies than a single head-office meeting, which changes both the itinerary and the security planning involved. Medan is also the standard gateway for travel to Lake Toba, the world’s largest volcanic lake, and to the Berastagi highlands, and executives who combine business with a Lake Toba side trip should plan it as a distinct, several-hour road movement rather than a short add-on.

Threat Intelligence

Threat Profile

Volcanic and Seismic Hazards

Indonesia sits on the Pacific Ring of Fire, and North Sumatra carries a genuine volcanic risk that Jakarta and Surabaya do not share to the same degree. FCDO travel advice for Indonesia advises against all travel within 5km of the crater of Mount Sinabung, an active volcano in Karo Regency, roughly 70km south of Medan on the Berastagi road. Sinabung erupted in 2021 and saw renewed volcanic activity in May 2026, according to Indonesian geological monitoring reported by Tempo.co. Medan itself sits outside the exclusion zone, but any itinerary extending toward Berastagi or the Karo highlands needs current alert status checked first.

Petty Crime and Motorbike Snatching

Bag and phone snatching from moving motorbikes is a recognised pattern across Indonesian cities, and Medan follows it. Traditional markets, bus terminals, and other crowded transport hubs carry the highest concentration of opportunistic theft. Violent crime aimed specifically at foreign visitors is uncommon, though not absent, and the same caution that applies in any unfamiliar Indonesian city after dark applies here.

Terrorism (National Backdrop)

The FCDO assesses that terrorists are likely to try to carry out attacks in Indonesia, with hotels, restaurants, shopping malls, and other places where foreigners gather among the nationally identified target categories, and risk assessed as heightened around religious holidays and elections. Medan has not experienced an attack on the scale of Jakarta's Marriott or Thamrin incidents, but the national threat picture applies here too. The US State Department places Indonesia at Level 2, Exercise Increased Caution, citing terrorism and natural disasters.

Traffic, Infrastructure, and Seasonal Haze

Medan's road network carries less capacity than Jakarta's, and uneven surfaces plus inconsistent enforcement of traffic rules make ground movement a genuine planning factor rather than routine logistics. In the dry season, typically June to September, agricultural and forest fires elsewhere in Sumatra can produce haze that cuts visibility, sometimes below one kilometre in severe episodes, disrupting both road travel and flight schedules at Kualanamu.

Security Regulations

Key regulatory requirements for operating security services in Medan.

Firearms Policy

Firearms are strictly controlled in Indonesia, nationally and in Medan. Armed private security requires POLRI authorisation and is limited to narrow contexts; close protection here is conducted unarmed in the large majority of assignments.

Licensing

Private security is regulated by POLRI (the Indonesian National Police) and the Ministry of Manpower, the same national framework applied in Jakarta and Surabaya. Companies require valid registration and individual operators require certification.

Foreign Operators

Foreign operators require KITAS work permits and local registration. Engagements in Medan are staffed through POLRI-licensed Indonesian operators rather than direct foreign deployment.

Local Intel

Zone Intelligence

Lower-Risk Areas

  • Medan Polonia, around the site of the former Polonia airport: an established upmarket district with international hotels and a lower crime profile than the city average.
  • Kesawan and the Merdeka Walk district: the colonial-era commercial core, home to banks, government offices, and a well-lit evening dining strip with a consistent security presence.
  • Setia Budi and the newer southern business corridors: modern commercial development with lower pedestrian density than the older city centre.

Elevated-Risk Areas

  • Pasar Petisah and other crowded traditional markets, plus bus and rail terminals: the highest concentration of opportunistic petty crime and motorbike-borne bag snatching.
  • Karo Regency and the road toward Berastagi, within 5km of Mount Sinabung's crater: FCDO advises against all travel into this exclusion zone; wider Karo highland day trips should track current volcanic alert status first.
  • Isolated or unlit roads on the city's outskirts after dark: standard urban caution, consistent with secondary Indonesian cities generally.
