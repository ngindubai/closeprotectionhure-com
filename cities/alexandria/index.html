Alexandria is Egypt’s second-largest city and its principal Mediterranean port, handling the large majority of the country’s seaborne trade. It sits roughly 220km northwest of Cairo along the desert road, a different city in character from the capital: less government and tourism traffic, more shipping, refining, and petrochemical business. Six refineries and three petrochemical complexes operate in and around the city, representing a substantial share of Egypt’s national refining capacity. For corporate visitors, that industrial base shapes the itinerary as much as the city’s ancient history does.

The historical draw is real and distinct from Cairo’s pyramid-led tourism. The Bibliotheca Alexandrina, built on the site of the ancient Library of Alexandria, the 15th-century Qaitbay Citadel on the site of the old lighthouse, and Pompey’s Pillar all sit within the city rather than requiring a day trip out. Corporate visits often combine business meetings with a short cultural itinerary along the Corniche, the city’s waterfront boulevard, and security planning needs to account for both the business and the heritage-site legs of a single trip.

The security picture is calmer than Cairo’s on routine crime but carries its own specific concern. Two shootings, in May 2023 and October 2024, targeted individuals with Israeli association in Alexandria, a pattern documented by the FCDO. Neither was connected to the Sinai-based terrorism threat that dominates national risk assessments; both indicate a targeted threat that can reach tourist and public locations in the city itself. This changes the calculus for any itinerary with an Israeli-linked element, regardless of how routine the rest of the visit looks.

Alexandria has no operating British Consulate at present. Consular support for British nationals runs through the British Embassy in Cairo, a meaningful distance away by the desert road, and this needs to be built into any incident-response plan rather than assumed to be a local resource.

Threat Intelligence

Threat Profile

Targeted Shootings

Alexandria has recorded two documented shootings targeting foreign nationals in the current period. In May 2023, a gunman at a checkpoint near a police station shot and killed two Israeli tourists and wounded four others. In October 2024, a separate shooting killed an Israeli-Canadian national. Both incidents are catalogued in FCDO travel advice for Egypt. They mean Alexandria carries a specific attack risk for individuals with Israeli or dual-national association that does not reduce simply because the city sits outside the Sinai conflict zone.

Terrorism (National Backdrop)

The FCDO's country-wide assessment is that terrorists are likely to try to carry out attacks in Egypt. The active group, Islamic State Sinai Province, is concentrated in North Sinai, roughly 400km from Alexandria. Alexandria itself has not seen IS-SP activity, but the national threat level still shapes venue selection and advance planning for any visit.

Port and Industrial Site Access

Alexandria handles a large share of Egypt's seaborne trade and hosts several of the country's refining and petrochemical complexes. Port and industrial facilities operate under Ministry of Interior and, in places, military-adjacent security protocols. Photography at or near port infrastructure, refineries, or the Suez Canal system is illegal under Egyptian law, including from a moving vehicle, and enforcement is active around Alexandria's harbour district.

State Surveillance and Detention Risk

Egypt's security services maintain broad surveillance of foreign nationals, and social media content critical of the government carries a documented detention risk that applies in Alexandria as much as in Cairo. Business visitors attending energy-sector or government-adjacent meetings should be briefed on communications discipline before arrival.

Security Regulations

Key regulatory requirements for operating security services in Alexandria.

Firearms Policy

Armed close protection in Egypt is tightly restricted, and Alexandria follows the national model. Private operators work unarmed as standard. Armed protection is generally reserved for government-authorised details at diplomatic or senior state level. For corporate assignments in Alexandria, unarmed close protection with police liaison is the working standard.

Licensing

Private security in Egypt is regulated nationally by the Ministry of Interior, and this applies in Alexandria without local variation. Operators require a valid licence, and enforcement carries genuine legal consequences for firms and clients using unlicensed providers.

Foreign Operators

Foreign security operators need Ministry of Interior authorisation to work anywhere in Egypt, Alexandria included. The practical route for international clients is an Egyptian-licensed operator or a registered partner firm rather than bringing in an unauthorised foreign team.

Zone Intelligence

Lower-Risk Areas

  • Corniche waterfront (central stretch near Bibliotheca Alexandrina): well-trafficked, visible policing, established hotel presence
  • San Stefano and Smouha districts: newer business and residential areas with a controlled environment and international hospital access
  • Kafr Abdou and Roushdy: established residential districts with a lower crime profile, favoured by expatriate professionals

Elevated-Risk Areas

  • Port and industrial zones east of the city: restricted photography, active security checks, not a general-access area for casual movement
  • Manshiya and the older commercial core after dark: reduced footfall and lighting once markets close
  • Any Egypt-wide location connected to an Israeli-linked itinerary, given the 2023 and 2024 Alexandria shootings

Important Warnings
