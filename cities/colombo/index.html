Colombo is Sri Lanka’s commercial capital and primary business hub. The city’s strategic location in the Indian Ocean, the development of the Colombo Port City project, and Sri Lanka’s growing role as a logistics and financial services hub have attracted significant corporate interest. The security environment is shaped by two landmark events – the 2019 Easter Sunday bombings and the 2022 political crisis – and professional security planning accounts for both dimensions.

The 2019 Easter Sunday bombings and their legacy

The April 2019 Easter Sunday bombings killed 269 people in simultaneous attacks on three high-end hotels and three churches in Colombo. The attacks were carried out by an IS-affiliated local network and targeted venues specifically because of their association with foreign nationals and Christian worshippers. The coordinated, sophisticated nature of the attacks established a clear targeting template: international hotels and places of worship attended by Western visitors.

Colombo’s hotel security infrastructure has been substantially upgraded since 2019. Vehicle searches at hotel entrances, security screening of visitors, and enhanced access control are now standard at international hotel properties. For corporate visitors, the practical implication is to take venue security measures seriously – vehicle entry checks, bag screening, and access protocols at hotels are genuine mitigation measures rather than theatre.

The 2022 political crisis and economic fragility

Sri Lanka’s 2022 crisis, during which economic collapse led to fuel and food shortages and ultimately to protesters storming the presidential palace, demonstrated the fragility of the country’s political stability. An IMF stabilisation programme has improved the immediate situation, but the underlying economic pressures have not been fully resolved. Corporate visitors on extended assignments should maintain political and economic monitoring as a standard practice, with contingency plans for accommodation and transport if civil unrest recurs.

Infrastructure investment and Port City

Colombo is increasingly relevant for infrastructure and logistics investment, centred on the Colombo Port City development zone and the broader port expansion. Executives visiting project sites should note that travel outside Colombo, particularly to the Northern and Eastern Provinces, requires specific route and site assessment. The former conflict areas in the north and east have a security profile that is distinct from the capital.

Threat Intelligence

Threat Profile

Terrorism

The April 2019 Easter Sunday bombings killed 269 people, including foreign nationals, at high-end hotels and churches in Colombo. The attacks demonstrated that IS-affiliated groups had both intent and capability to carry out simultaneous, sophisticated attacks against targets frequented by Western visitors. The FCDO assesses an elevated terrorism threat remains. Sri Lanka's counter-terrorism capability has been substantially upgraded since 2019.

Political Instability

Sri Lanka experienced a severe economic and political crisis in 2022, during which protesters stormed the presidential palace and the prime minister's residence. The crisis reflected deep structural economic fragility. While stability has improved since, the underlying economic pressures have not been fully resolved, and civil unrest could recur.

Crime

Petty crime including bag snatching, phone theft, and vehicle break-ins occurs in commercial areas and at transport hubs. The overall crime rate is low by regional standards. Residential burglary targeting expatriate properties occurs at documented frequency in upscale districts.

Road Safety

Road traffic fatalities in Sri Lanka are high relative to the country's income level. Bus and motorcycle accidents are common. Driving standards and enforcement vary significantly. Vetted transport with a competent driver is the appropriate standard.

Compliance

Security Regulations

Key regulatory requirements for operating security services in Colombo.

Firearms Policy

Sri Lanka's Firearms Ordinance governs all weapons. Private security companies must be licensed by the Department of Police. Armed security requires specific police authorisation.

Licensing

Private security agencies must be registered under Sri Lanka's regulatory framework. Individual security officers require police vetting. Foreign operators must work through locally licensed partners.

Foreign Operators

Foreign security personnel must operate through Sri Lankan-registered partner organisations. The Sri Lanka Police maintain oversight of all private security operations.

Local Intel

Zone Intelligence

Lower-Risk Areas

  • Colombo 3 (Colpetty) and Colombo 5 (Havelock Town): Primary expatriate residential areas with established security infrastructure.
  • Colombo 7 (Cinnamon Gardens): Diplomatic and upscale residential area with lower crime rates and embassy presence.
  • Port City Colombo: New development zone with modern security infrastructure and managed access.

Elevated-Risk Areas

  • Colombo 10-16 and peripheral areas: Higher crime rates and less established security infrastructure.
  • Public transport hubs and bus stations: Higher petty crime risk in crowded environments.
  • Former Northern Province conflict areas: Require specific security assessment; different risk profile from Colombo.

Important Warnings

  • Hotels and venues frequented by foreign nationals are the primary terrorism target category established in 2019. Follow security protocols rigorously at hotels, comply with vehicle entry checks, and apply heightened awareness at any major gathering.
  • Monitor the political and economic situation before and during visits. Sri Lanka's underlying economic pressures can escalate to public unrest with limited warning. Identify an alternative accommodation option if staying near government buildings.

Colombo’s security environment has been substantially revised since the 2019 Easter Sunday bombings. Security screening at hotels, public buildings, and major venues is now significantly more thorough. Sri Lanka’s security services have undertaken major counter-terrorism work, and the National Counter Terrorism Authority was strengthened. The underlying terrorism threat has not been removed, but the response capability and the physical security at target venues is materially better than pre-2019.

Sri Lanka’s 2022 crisis was driven by severe economic mismanagement, foreign exchange shortages, and resulting supply failures for fuel, food, and medicines. The immediate political crisis, during which protesters stormed the presidential palace, was resolved by a change of government and an IMF stabilisation programme. The underlying economic fragility remains a monitoring item. Signs of resurgent economic pressure or political deadlock can precede further unrest.

Sri Lanka is a focus for infrastructure investment, particularly in its strategic port and logistics facilities. The Colombo Port City development and Hambantota port are attracting significant commercial interest. Executives visiting project sites outside Colombo should obtain route and site assessments appropriate to the specific locations. Travel in the Northern and Eastern Provinces, which were conflict-affected areas, requires specific security assessment.

Port City Colombo is a new development zone with modern security infrastructure and managed vehicle access. It provides a relatively well-controlled environment for meetings and business activity. The appropriate baseline for visits is standard corporate precautions rather than the elevated measures that apply at higher-risk sites. Standard vetted transport for arrival and departure is appropriate.

The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to certain areas. For Colombo and the main commercial areas, the FCDO does not prohibit travel but maintains an elevated terrorism threat rating and advises vigilance at tourist and transport sites. Areas of the Northern Province near the Kilinochchi, Mullaitivu, and Mannar districts have specific advisories. Check current guidance before any travel outside the Colombo-Galle-Kandy corridor.
