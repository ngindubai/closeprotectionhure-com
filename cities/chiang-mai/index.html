Chiang Mai is northern Thailand’s largest city and a well-established hub for digital-nomad and remote-work communities, regional business and logistics, and long-stay expatriates. Its security profile differs sharply from Bangkok’s: political unrest and terrorism, the defining risks in the capital, are largely absent here, while the Old City’s moat district, night markets and mountain surroundings bring their own, quieter set of planning considerations.

The city’s most distinctive risk factor is not crime but air quality. Every February through April, agricultural burning across northern Thailand and neighbouring Myanmar and Laos fills the mountain basin Chiang Mai sits in with smoke that has nowhere to disperse. During the 2026 season, IQAir-sourced readings put PM2.5 levels at 188 micrograms per cubic metre in late March, well over four times the WHO’s guideline, with authorities recording thousands of fire hotspots and running an extended open-burning ban. For any principal, expatriate household or event planner based in Chiang Mai during those months, this is a real operational factor rather than a footnote, and it needs to be built into scheduling in the same way heat or flooding would be planned around in other markets.

Road safety is the second consistent concern. Thailand’s national road death rate sits among the highest the WHO records worldwide, and Chiang Mai’s combination of scooters, songthaews and winding routes to outlying attractions such as Doi Suthep adds local texture to that national picture. A trained security driver remains the standard mitigation, just as in Bangkok, though the underlying risk here is accidents and congestion rather than the political unrest that shapes Bangkok assignments.

Crime against visitors is comparatively low-key: pickpocketing and scams cluster around the Old City’s markets, much as they do in any major Thai tourist centre, but violent crime targeting foreign business travellers is uncommon. Chiang Mai also sits well clear of both the Cambodian border tensions and the deep south insurgency that the FCDO flags separately for far-southern Thailand; those risks belong to a different part of the country and should not be read across onto a Chiang Mai itinerary.

Threat Intelligence

Threat Profile

Burning Season Air Quality

Every year from roughly mid-February through April, agricultural burning across northern Thailand, Myanmar and Laos fills the Chiang Mai basin with smoke that the surrounding mountains trap in place. IQAir data cited in coverage of the 2026 season recorded PM2.5 readings of 188 micrograms per cubic metre on 29 March, over four times the WHO's 24-hour guideline of 15 micrograms per cubic metre, with the city's air quality index reaching 263 that day and staying in the 'Very Unhealthy' band through the following days. Northern Thai authorities logged more than 2,090 fire hotspots between 1 January and 27 March 2026 and imposed an open-burning ban from 1 February to 31 May. This is a genuine, well-documented health and logistics factor for anyone based in Chiang Mai during those months, distinct from the crime and terrorism risks that dominate planning in Bangkok.

Road Traffic Accidents

Thailand ranked ninth of 175 countries assessed in the WHO's Global Status Report on Road Safety, with a national road death rate of 25.4 per 100,000 people in 2021, one of the highest in Asia. Motorcyclists accounted for 83.8% of recorded traffic deaths. Chiang Mai's mix of scooters, songthaews (shared pickup taxis) and mountain roads to outlying districts such as Doi Suthep makes ground movement a real safety consideration rather than routine logistics.

Petty and Opportunistic Crime

Bag snatching, scams and pickpocketing occur around the Old City's night markets, Sunday Walking Street, and the Nimmanhaemin cafe district, largely mirroring patterns reported across Thailand's tourist centres. Violent crime against foreign visitors and business travellers is comparatively rare, and Chiang Mai does not carry the political-protest history or terrorism precedent recorded in Bangkok.

Border-Adjacent Insurgency (Not Local to Chiang Mai)

The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to Thailand's deep south (Pattani, Yala, Narathiwat and parts of Songkhla) owing to a long-running separatist insurgency. This threat is geographically distant from Chiang Mai and northern Thailand and should not be conflated with the city's own profile; the FCDO's 2026 guidance continues to treat Chiang Mai as an area with no additional travel restriction.

Security Regulations

Key regulatory requirements for operating security services in Chiang Mai.

Firearms Policy

Firearms are restricted in Thailand. Private armed security is limited and tightly controlled; close protection in Chiang Mai, as in Bangkok, is predominantly unarmed and relies on planning and awareness rather than armed response.

Licensing

Private security is regulated by the Ministry of Interior under the Security Guard Business Act. Companies must hold valid registration and personnel require licensing through the Royal Thai Police.

Foreign Operators

Foreign operators require work permits and Thai security certification. Assignments in Chiang Mai typically use Thai-licensed operators with local knowledge of the city and its outlying districts.

Local Intel

Zone Intelligence

Lower-Risk Areas

  • Nimmanhaemin: The city's business, cafe and co-working hub, popular with the digital-nomad and remote-work community. Good infrastructure and consistent foot traffic.
  • Hang Dong and the eastern ring road business corridor: Lower-density commercial area used for logistics and light industry, with straightforward vehicle access.
  • Old City (moat district) outside peak market hours: Calm and manageable through the day and evening; busier and more crowded during the Sunday Walking Street market.

Elevated-Risk Areas

  • Old City night markets and Sunday Walking Street: Highest concentration of tourist foot traffic and the area most consistently flagged for pickpocketing and bag snatching.
  • Doi Suthep mountain road and other outlying routes: Steep, winding roads with a higher accident rate, particularly for scooters and songthaews after dark.
  • City-wide during burning season (mid-February to April): Not a security threat in the conventional sense, but the period when PM2.5 levels most consistently exceed WHO guidance by a wide margin, with practical implications for outdoor movement and event planning.

Advisory

Important Warnings

  • During burning season (roughly mid-February to April), monitor daily air quality readings and plan outdoor engagements, event logistics and any medically vulnerable travellers' schedules around forecast peaks. Northern Thai authorities recorded over 2,090 fire hotspots between January and late March 2026 and ran an open-burning ban from 1 February to 31 May.
  • Use a vetted security driver for road movements, particularly on mountain routes such as the Doi Suthep road and for any journey after dark. Thailand's road death rate is among the highest recorded by the WHO, and motorcyclists make up the large majority of fatalities.
  • The FCDO advises against all but essential travel to Thailand's deep south provinces. This does not apply to Chiang Mai or northern Thailand, but any itinerary extending south of Bangkok toward the Malaysian border needs a separate, specific security assessment.
  • Be alert to scams and pickpocketing in the Old City night markets and around Sunday Walking Street. Use only pre-arranged or reputable metered transport.
  • Do not become involved in any dispute over commercial or personal matters that could be referred to local authorities. Thailand's lese-majeste laws carry serious penalties and apply nationally, including in Chiang Mai.
